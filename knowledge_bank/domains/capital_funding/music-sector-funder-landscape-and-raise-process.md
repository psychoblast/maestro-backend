# Music-Sector Funder Landscape & Capital Raise Process

The capital-stack doctrine, source-ranking methodology, and non-dilutive
sequencing in the founding files define the analytical framework for capital
decisions. This file covers who the actual funders are in the music and adjacent
media/tech sector, what each type looks for, and how to run the capital raise
process from preparation through close. Understanding the landscape allows the
sequencing doctrine to be applied to real providers, not abstract tiers.

All timelines and advance-sizing parameters below are illustrative; confirm
current market data before they drive a recommendation or a capital plan.

---

## The music-sector funder landscape

The music sector has a more distinct capital-provider landscape than most
industries: specialist IP-finance vehicles, catalog-backed lenders, sector grant
bodies, label and distributor advance providers, and an emerging class of
music-focused private equity sit alongside general-purpose credit providers.
Understanding which type of funder aligns to a given use, stage, and structure
determines whom to approach — and in what sequence.

### Tier 0: Non-dilutive public funders

Arts councils, cultural agencies, collective-society foundations
(performing-rights and neighbouring-rights society grant arms), and government
export-market programs constitute Tier 0. They are the cheapest capital available
when the enterprise is eligible.

**What they assess:**
- Program eligibility on every stated criterion — residency, entity type,
  activity category, content threshold, co-funding level. A single failed
  criterion makes the entire application ineligible.
- Artistic and commercial merit as framed by the program's own evaluation rubric;
  successful applicants study prior funded projects and align their language to
  the program's stated priorities.
- Demonstrated capacity to execute: prior completed projects, team credentials,
  existing market presence.

**Key operational facts:**
- Grant bodies do not operate on demand — they run fixed cycles, and a missed
  deadline means waiting for the next one (often 6–12 months). The capital-raise
  calendar must be built around grant cycles, not the other way around.
- These sources are assessed on program eligibility, not investment merit. The
  evaluation language, materials, and positioning appropriate for a grant body
  are categorically different from what a private investor or lender expects.

### Tier 0: Tax credit programs

Sound recording, digital media production, and R&D tax credits are government
incentives administered by revenue authorities or sector agencies. The enterprise
claims against eligible production expenditure; the capital flows either as a
tax offset (non-refundable) or a cash payment (refundable). Tax credit bridge
lenders are the intermediary who advance against a pending credit claim.

**What program administrators assess:**
- Eligibility on the specific program criteria (labor content, jurisdiction,
  content, entity type, timing)
- Eligible-expenditure base: only documented, in-category expenditures count

**What bridge lenders assess:**
- Existence of a preliminary or conditional eligibility determination from the
  program administrator
- Quality of the cost certification (independent accountant review)
- Certainty of the credit receipt (refundable programs are much more bridgeable
  than non-refundable)

### Revenue-based and royalty advance providers

Platforms and specialist funds that advance capital against a named revenue
stream — streaming income, sync licensing, catalog mechanical income — with
repayment as a percentage of that stream to a cap multiple of the advance.
These providers underwrite the revenue stream itself, not the enterprise as a
whole.

**What they assess:**
- Demonstrable revenue history on the pledged stream: minimum 12 months of
  streaming or licensing data at threshold levels; without history the advance
  cannot be sized
- Clean IP ownership on the pledged stream: split documentation, ownership
  confirmation, absence of competing claims
- Revenue trajectory: growing or stable income streams are advanced against
  more favorably than declining ones; a structurally declining stream is
  discounted or declined
- Absence of prior encumbrances on the same revenue stream

**Underwriting process:** typically data-driven and faster than traditional
credit — the provider pulls distributor and streaming data, models the
repayment period under base and conservative scenarios, and issues terms
within days to a few weeks. The enterprise should pre-assemble clean ISRC
records, consolidated streaming dashboards across all distribution outlets,
and ownership and split documentation before initiating discussions — delays
in data provision extend the process and can signal disorganization.

**Critical pricing note:** the effective IRR on royalty advances is frequently
and materially higher than the headline revenue-share percentage suggests,
especially when the revenue stream underperforms the base-case projection and
repayment extends beyond the modeled period. Always model the effective cost
under base, bear, and bull trajectories — not just the headline percentage —
before accepting a term sheet.

### Catalog-backed lenders and IP finance funds

Specialized banks, specialty finance firms, and IP-focused funds that advance
against the value of a catalog of owned rights as collateral — typically master
recordings and/or publishing copyrights generating contractual royalty income.
This is a senior secured instrument; the catalog is pledged as the primary
collateral.

**What they assess:**
- Clear, documented, and unencumbered ownership of the pledged rights: chain-of-
  title documents from creation through all transfers, producer agreements,
  sample clearances, absence of prior security interests
- Royalty history demonstrating revenue durability: minimum 24–36 months of
  verifiable royalty statements from all collection pipelines; a lender's
  underwriting relies on the stability and predictability of that history
- Concentration analysis: a catalog where 2–3 works generate the large majority
  of income is discounted versus a diversified catalog; concentration risk is
  scored explicitly because loss of a single work materially impairs the
  collateral value
- Income trajectory: a catalog in structural decline (streaming volume falling
  year-on-year without new licensing activity to offset it) is harder to finance
  than a stable or growing one; the lender's recovery on enforcement depends on
  the income the catalog will generate after any default
- Debt-service coverage ratio: can the catalog's income reliably service the
  proposed debt at base and conservative scenarios

**Typical advance sizing:** lenders typically size advances as a multiple of
trailing 12-month net income from the catalog, or as a percentage of the
catalog's assessed value. Loan-to-value ratios for catalog-backed lending
are typically 40–60% of assessed catalog value for a first-lien instrument,
applied to a conservative valuation. These are illustrative parameters;
current market levels must be confirmed.

**Underwriting timeline:** 60–120 days from initial approach to funding is
typical for a standard catalog-backed facility — significantly longer than
revenue-based advance providers. The timeline includes title search and
ownership verification, royalty statement review (usually 3 years), engagement
of an independent royalty auditor for larger facilities, catalog valuation
analysis, covenant negotiation, and security documentation. An enterprise that
needs funds in 30 days is not a candidate for catalog-backed financing.

**Encumbrance consequence:** pledging the catalog removes it from the menu of
future financing and sale options for the term of the loan. The optionality
cost is real and belongs in the effective-cost calculation even when no default
occurs. A catalog pledged for 5 years cannot be sold during that period without
lender consent and release, and cannot be re-pledged for a second facility
without the first lender's consent.

### Label and distributor advance providers

Major and independent labels, distribution companies, and label-services
companies that advance capital against future royalties in exchange for
licensing, distribution, or services rights. These are a primary financing
source for recording artists but are often the most expensive conventional
capital in the stack when the effective cost is modeled.

**What they assess:**
- Demonstrated streaming velocity and social engagement: the label's economic
  risk is creation expenditure that fails to generate enough royalties to recoup
  the advance, so evidence of an existing audience and engagement trajectory is
  the central underwriting signal
- Genre and commercial positioning compatible with the label's distribution
  network and marketing capabilities
- Team and prior release track record: management quality, prior campaign
  performance, relationship network
- Clear ownership of master recording rights: no prior security interests,
  no split disputes, no unresolved producer or featured-artist claims

**Capital-structure implication:** a label advance is not free capital — the
enterprise relinquishes a portion of royalty income on the funded works (and
potentially other works, if cross-collateralization applies) until the advance
and all chargeable costs are recouped. The effective cost can be very high when
modeled against a realistic royalty trajectory; this source belongs near the
bottom of the dilutive tier in the sequencing analysis, not treated as a neutral
cash injection.

**Cross-collateralization:**  when advances from multiple releases are
cross-collateralized, the unrecouped balance on one release is charged against
royalties from all others. An artist generating consistent streaming income
across five releases can remain "unrecouped" in the accounting indefinitely
because each release's balance is charged against the pool. Model cross-
collateralization explicitly; a deal that looks affordable on a single-release
basis may have a materially different effective cost when the full cross-
collateralized position is modeled.

### Music and media venture capital and growth equity

Venture funds, growth equity firms, and family offices with music-sector exposure
that invest in music enterprises — labels, artist-services companies, catalog
acquisition vehicles, music-technology businesses, live-entertainment companies.
Less active at the individual artist or single-project level; more active at the
scalable-enterprise level with a defined exit pathway.

**What they assess:**
- Enterprise-level investment thesis: market size, defensibility, differentiation,
  competitive position — not a single project's commercial prospects
- Management team with a verifiable track record in the relevant domain
- Evidence of operating leverage: unit economics that improve as the enterprise
  scales
- A credible exit pathway within the fund's hold period (typically 5–10 years
  for growth equity; shorter for venture)

**Capital-structure implication:** venture and growth equity carries the highest
cost of capital in the stack and is the last tier in the non-dilutive-first
sequence. It is structurally appropriate for enterprise-level raises where the
business itself compounds returns over time — not for project-level production
financing where the return profile is single-cycle. Reaching for equity to fund
a recording project, when the production could be funded by a combination of
grants, credits, and a royalty advance, is the most common capital-sequencing
error in the music sector.

### Sector-specialized banks and credit unions

Several jurisdictions have banks, credit unions, and specialty finance
institutions with dedicated creative-sector portfolios. These providers
understand royalty-backed collateral, tolerate the lumpy income profiles of
cultural enterprises better than generalist commercial lenders, and can engage
productively with royalty statements as income evidence.

**What they assess:** standard commercial credit criteria — income, collateral,
character/track record — but with a credit-officer base that can read and
underwrite royalty statements without translating them into more familiar
income formats. An enterprise presenting a royalty statement history to a
sector-experienced credit officer encounters materially different analysis
than presenting the same documents to a generalist commercial lender.

**Positioning:** this tier is often overlooked — enterprises jump from grant
programs directly to specialized IP funds or equity when a sector bank or credit
union would have provided a conventional secured loan at a lower effective cost
on a shorter timeline. Include this tier in every source-ranking exercise.

## What funders assess: underwriting criteria by type

| Funder type | Primary underwriting signal | Key document | Critical risk |
|-------------|----------------------------|--------------|---------------|
| Grant body / arts council | Program eligibility + artistic merit | Eligibility docs, project description, co-funding evidence | Single eligibility failure invalidates the application |
| Tax credit program | Eligible-expenditure base + refundability | Pre-approval filing, accountant cost certification | Non-refundable credit has no cash value pre-profit |
| Revenue-based lender | Revenue history + trajectory on pledged stream | 12+ months distributor data, ownership docs | Revenue underperformance extends repayment; effective IRR rises |
| Catalog lender / IP fund | Catalog valuation + title ownership + concentration | 36 months royalty statements, title chain, encumbrance schedule | Title defects, concentration risk, declining trajectory |
| Label / distributor | Streaming velocity + team + ownership clarity | Release history, streaming data, team profile | Cross-collateralization obscures true effective cost |
| Venture / growth equity | Enterprise model + exit path + team | Cap table, financials, market size, team track record | High cost; dilution; equity is structurally last resort |
| Sector bank / credit union | Debt service coverage + collateral | Financials, royalty statements, collateral schedule | Standard credit criteria apply; income must service the debt |

## Running the capital raise process

A successful raise follows a disciplined process with distinct preparation,
outreach, and closing phases. Failures in preparation are the most common cause
of failed raises or expensive, poorly structured outcomes.

### Phase 1: preparation

**Define the precise capital need.** An amount tied to a specific, documented
use of proceeds and a stated runway. A use-of-proceeds statement that reads
"$320,000 for recording and market development — $180,000 recording over 6
months, $80,000 tour support in 2 international markets, $60,000 digital
marketing over 12 months" gives a funder and a compliance auditor the
information they need; "we need $500,000 to grow" does not.

**Complete the non-dilutive scan before approaching any private funder.**
Every grant, credit, and subsidy program with plausible eligibility must be
evaluated and formally dispositioned before dilutive or debt capital is
presented to private funders. Skipping the non-dilutive scan is a structural
error with a material capital cost; it also, in some grant programs, affects
eligibility when private capital is already in place.

**Assemble the data room before outreach begins.** The delay between a funder's
data request and the enterprise's response is a negotiating disadvantage and a
competence signal. Assembling materials reactively under funder scrutiny
introduces errors and delays that cost price and terms. Prepare the data room
in full before the first approach.

Standard data room contents by funder type:

*All funder types:*
- Corporate structure and ownership documentation; cap table (if equity or
  convertible debt is involved)
- Three fiscal years of financial statements (or full operating history if less);
  management accounts if interim financials are unavailable
- A clean encumbrance schedule: every existing security interest, lien, royalty
  pledge, or contractual payment commitment on every material asset

*Debt and catalog lenders:*
- Royalty statements from all collection pipelines, 24–36 months
- Chain-of-title documentation for all pledged assets, from creation through
  every transfer
- Prior-period covenant compliance certificates if existing debt is in place

*Grant programs:*
- Eligibility documentation: residency, citizenship, entity registration
- Prior grant history: most programs consider the track record of prior awards
- Project description mapped explicitly to the program's eligible-activity
  categories
- Budget broken out by eligible-expenditure category per program definitions

*Equity investors:*
- Business plan or investment memo
- Financial model showing base, bear, and bull scenarios
- Market analysis with competitive positioning
- Team biographies with documented prior track record

**Resolve entity and ownership issues before the first funder conversation.**
Title ambiguities, undocumented ownership splits, informal revenue-sharing
arrangements, or expired clearances are diligence red flags. Resolving them
under funder scrutiny costs more time and negotiating leverage than resolving
them in advance. A pre-raise title and encumbrance review is a standard
preparatory step for any catalog-backed or equity raise.

### Phase 2: funder outreach and pipeline management

**Build the funder list.** Categorize every plausible funder by type; map each
to the sequencing doctrine (non-dilutive first); assess each for realistic
accessibility at the enterprise's current stage. A first-cycle artist
approaching institutional venture capital is misaligned; a catalog owner
approaching a specialized IP lender without 24 months of royalty history is
misaligned. The funder list should contain realistic targets.

**Apply the sequencing discipline.** Non-dilutive applications must be in
active process or formally dispositioned before dilutive or debt conversations
are opened. The common error is opening equity conversations while grant
applications are pending — some grant programs treat existing private investment
as disqualifying, and others reconsider the co-funding structure if dilutive
capital is confirmed post-application. The non-dilutive phase completes before
the private capital outreach opens.

**Maintain an active pipeline tracker.** A living document with one row per
funder and these minimum fields:

| Funder | Type | Amount | Stage | Last contact | Next action | Deadline | Est. close |
|--------|------|--------|-------|-------------|-------------|---------|-----------|

Update after every contact. The tracker reveals whether the raise is on pace
and where concentration risk exists — if only one conversation is active, the
enterprise has no leverage and no fallback.

**Format the ask for the funder type.** The opening contact format must match
what the funder type expects:
- Grant body: formal application through the prescribed channel — no pitch deck,
  no investor memo
- Debt or catalog funder: a one-page teaser (capital need, purpose, proposed
  security, enterprise summary) before any formal information memorandum
- Equity investor: a pitch deck (10–15 pages) before the data room

Sending an investor pitch deck to a grant body, or a grant-program application
format to an equity investor, signals that the enterprise does not understand
the funder's process — an immediate credibility deficit.

**Manage lead-lag across funder types.** Different provider types close on
fundamentally different timelines:
- Grant award cycle: 8–20 weeks from submission to award
- Tax credit: fiscal year following expenditure (plus bridge if applicable)
- Revenue-based advance: 2–4 weeks from data submission to term sheet
- Catalog-backed loan: 60–120 days from initial approach to funding
- Equity — angel or seed: 4–12 weeks to term sheet; 4–8 weeks to close
- Equity — institutional growth: 3–6 months to term sheet; 6–12 weeks to close

A capital plan that assumes grant awards and equity close on the same timeline
will fail. Stage these in the capital plan and fund the gaps explicitly.

### Phase 3: diligence and term negotiation

**Respond to data requests promptly and completely.** Diligence delay is the
enterprise's worst negotiating position — it signals disorganization and gives
the funder grounds to reprice or withdraw. A pre-assembled data room allows
complete, organized responses in days. A funder who waits two weeks for basic
royalty statements will price the organizational risk into the terms.

**Do not share data rooms indiscriminately across competing funders.** A catalog
lender's due-diligence package containing full royalty data should not be shared
with a label or distributor exploring a distribution advance — the same data is
commercially sensitive in both contexts. Manage data-room access with named-
recipient controls and purpose restrictions.

**Engage counsel before signing anything.** A term sheet is mostly non-binding,
but the final agreement is fully binding and contains the covenant package,
default provisions, and every clause the term sheet summarized. The fatal-term
taxonomy applies most critically here — the full agreement, not the term sheet,
is where IP alienation clauses, uncapped ratchets, and automatic change-of-
control triggers tend to appear in their legally operative form. Legal review of
the full agreement before execution is not optional.

**Maintain alternatives until a term sheet is signed.** A single-funder raise
with no live alternative is the weakest negotiating position. Even when one
funder is clearly preferred, keeping other discussions active until a term sheet
is executed preserves leverage on price, terms, and timing. "We have another
term sheet pending" is a real negotiating fact, not a posture — which requires
having another real discussion in process.

---

### Phase 4: close and post-close discipline

**Conditions precedent.** Most closings are conditional: approval by counsel
for both parties, execution of security documents, release of prior
encumbrances, delivery of legal opinions, satisfaction of co-funding
requirements. Each condition must have a named owner and a deadline; a missed
condition delays the close. Track conditions precedent with the same discipline
as the pipeline tracker.

**Directed-payment arrangements.** Lenders requiring direct payment from a
royalty payer, tax credit authority, or distributor need an executed payment
direction from that payer before the loan proceeds are released. These direction
letters require the payer's processing time — typically 1–3 weeks — that is
frequently overlooked in closing timelines.

**At close: update the encumbrance schedule and activate the compliance
calendar.** Every new instrument adds encumbrances and obligations that must
be tracked from day one of the facility. A financing that closes and is not
actively monitored is a covenant breach waiting to happen. The compliance
calendar per the terms-and-covenant analysis must be live, with named owners
for each obligation, before the close date — not assembled after the first
missed reporting deadline.

## Common process failures

**Starting grant applications too late.** Grant cycles are fixed; missing a
deadline means waiting 6–12 months for the next cycle. The enterprise that
begins researching grant programs when capital is urgently needed will find
cycles closed and the non-dilutive tier effectively unavailable. Grant
applications must be planned 6–9 months before the capital is needed.

**Running only one conversation.** A pipeline with a single active funder
provides no leverage on price or terms and no fallback if that funder declines
or reprices. Maintain minimum two to three live funder conversations at each
stage until a term sheet is signed.

**Sequencing dilutive before non-dilutive.** An equity raise executed before
available grants and credits are evaluated and formally dispositioned is a
capital-allocation error with permanent consequences — the equity dilution
cannot be recovered by a grant that was never applied for. The sequence is
binding.

**Underestimating due diligence timelines.** First-time founders routinely
expect catalog loans and equity closings to complete in 2–4 weeks; the actual
range is 60–120 days for a catalog facility and 3–9 months for institutional
equity. Cash management must cover the gap between commitment and close;
high-cost emergency bridging because due diligence ran long is avoidable with
a realistic timeline.

**Accepting terms without modeling the effective cost.** A revenue-based advance
priced as a flat revenue percentage is consistently mispriced by enterprises
that do not model the IRR. The fatal pattern: accept a term sheet on the
headline percentage without running the bear-case repayment model; the actual
effective cost proves to be 2–3× the initial intuition when the stream
underperforms.

**Relying on verbal indications of intent.** A grant program officer's
enthusiasm is not an award; an investor's positive meeting is not a commitment;
a distributor's oral interest is not a signed term sheet. Cash planning that
treats verbal indications as committed capital fails when, as often happens,
those indications do not convert. Only executed agreements are capital.

**Closing the raise with an unclean data room.** Title defects, undisclosed
prior encumbrances, unresolved split disputes, informal revenue-sharing
arrangements discovered mid-diligence — each is harder and more expensive to
resolve under funder scrutiny than before the raise begins. A pre-raise title
and encumbrance review costs a fraction of the negotiating leverage lost when
a defect surfaces in the middle of a closing.
