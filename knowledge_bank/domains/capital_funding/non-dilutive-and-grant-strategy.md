# Non-Dilutive & Grant Strategy

Non-dilutive capital is evaluated first, every time. This file covers grant
strategy, matching-fund math, clawback-risk analysis, and the treatment of
sponsorship/partnership and revenue/royalty financing as funding sources. The
goal is to exhaust cheaper, less-constraining capital before any dilutive or
encumbering source is recommended.

Program-specific amounts, deadlines, and eligibility criteria change constantly.
Treat any specific figure as an estimate to be verified against current program
documentation before it drives advice; never assert grant eligibility without a
stated basis.

---

## The non-dilutive tier in music and media

Music and adjacent media enterprises sit on an unusually deep non-dilutive tier,
driven by public support for cultural production, collective-society
distributions, and sector tax regimes. The categories:

| Tier | Subcategory | Applicability |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| **Grants** | Federal/national cultural-production grants | Recording, business innovation, artist support, market development |
| **Grants** | Provincial/regional cultural support | Distinct eligible-expenditure categories per jurisdiction |
| **Grants** | Collective-society / charitable foundations | Performing-rights and neighbouring-rights foundations, sector charities |
| **Tax credits** | Sound-recording / video-production credits | Content, eligible-expenditure, and labor-percentage requirements |
| **Tax credits** | R&D / innovation credits | Music-tech or platform components may qualify; fact-specific |
| **Subsidies** | Export / market-development support | Showcase, touring, and market-entry programs |
| **Sponsorship as capital** | Non-equity brand partnerships | Activation/co-branding sized here; deal handed to business development |
| **Revenue financing** | Advance against royalty/licensing revenue | Against predicted streaming, licensing, or sync income |
| **Royalty financing** | Royalty-right pledge or revenue share | Catalog-secured advance on master or publishing income |

The depth of this tier is exactly why skipping it is expensive: each grant or
credit dollar displaces a higher-cost equity or debt dollar.

## Grant strategy and the disposition discipline

Every potentially eligible program must be dispositioned, not just the ones
pursued:

- **Eligible and pursuing** — meets eligibility; named in the application pipeline
  with a deadline and an owner.
- **Eligible and declined** — meets eligibility but declined, with a stated
  reason (e.g., the compliance burden outweighs the net capital, or a string is
  incompatible with the plan).
- **Ineligible** — does not meet eligibility, with the specific failing criterion
  stated (e.g., content threshold not met, activity outside eligible categories,
  applicant type excluded).

A grant pursued without a documented eligibility basis is not a plan. Document
the basis at application with specificity; if a lineup, producer, or
collaborator change is anticipated that could affect an eligibility
determination, pre-confirm with the program before making the change.

## Matching-fund math

Many grants and subsidies require the recipient to contribute or raise matching
capital. The match is a real cost and changes the economics:

- **Net capital received** is the grant minus the match the enterprise must fund
  itself, adjusted for the cost of raising that match. A heavily matched grant
  can be more expensive in effective terms than it first appears.
- **Match-source eligibility** varies by program. Common restrictions:
  - **No stacking the same dollar** — two government grants generally cannot
    each count the same dollar as match. Stacking grants across a project may
    still be allowed; double-counting a single dollar is not.
  - **Arm's-length requirement** — match must be genuine external capital, not
    related-entity money dressed up as third-party funding.
  - **In-kind match** — some programs accept in-kind contributions (discounted
    studio time, below-market services) with specific valuation and documentation
    rules. Verify in-kind eligibility before relying on it in the matching plan.
- **Timing of the match** — match capital is often spent before or alongside
  grant disbursement, creating a cash-flow gap the enterprise must bridge. The
  bridge has a cost; include it.

Always model the **total package** (grant + match + bridge), not the headline
grant amount.

## Eligible-expenditure strings

Programs define eligible and ineligible expenditure categories with specificity.
Eligible commonly includes recording time, session-musician fees, producer and
engineer fees, mastering, video production, defined marketing/promotion, defined
touring costs, and export-market development. Ineligible commonly includes
general overhead, capital equipment for ongoing enterprise use, expenditures
before the project start date, and — in many programs — **management
commissions** charged against the funded budget.

The management-commission exclusion is consequential: the enterprise must absorb
commission costs from non-grant funds for the funded activity, or restructure the
arrangement for the funded period. Misapplying grant funds to ineligible
management fees is a classic clawback determination.

## IP, attribution, and exploitation strings

Some programs condition how funded work is exploited:

- **Distribution/release restrictions** — funded works may need release through a
  qualifying distributor, label, or publisher; an exclusively non-qualifying
  channel can breach the condition.
- **Credit and attribution** — funded works must credit the program; missing
  credits are a compliance failure on the record, minor in isolation but real.
- **Assignment restrictions** — some programs prohibit assignment of funded work
  or require consent. A catalog sale or IP transfer touching funded works can
  trigger this — route any such transaction to legal review first.
- **Non-commercial-use restrictions** — rare, but a condition that prevents
  commercial exploitation of funded music is a *fatal* condition and must be
  flagged before acceptance (see the terms-and-covenant analysis).

## Clawback-risk analysis

A grant is the non-dilutive analog of debt only on the downside: there is
nothing to repay, but a clawback right lets the funder demand the money back on a
trigger. Clawback feasibility — not service burden — is what determines whether a
grant clears the repayment/clawback gate.

Map every clawback trigger:

| Trigger | Probability (estimate) | Amount at risk | Mitigation | Residual risk |
|---------|------------------------|----------------|------------|---------------|
| [specific condition] | Low / Med / High | Full / Partial | [action] | Low / Med / High |

State probability directionally (Low/Med/High), not as a false-precision
percentage. A grant whose conditions are achievable under normal operations
clears the gate no matter how demanding those conditions are; a grant whose
conditions cannot realistically be met — or whose clawback fires on ordinary
business activity such as commercial release — does not.

When several instruments carry correlated clawback triggers (a revenue shortfall
that fires both a grant clawback and a debt covenant breach), model the
correlation explicitly. Do not treat triggers driven by a shared cause as
independent.

## Sponsorship and partnership as a capital source

Sponsorship and partnership are evaluated **as funding sources** here — the
amount, the structure type, the activation and licensing cost, and where they
sit in the sequence. Common structures: activation fees, co-branding
arrangements, and tour or release sponsorship contributed as capital rather than
equity.

The boundary is firm: this domain **sizes and sources** the sponsorship as
capital and hands the **deal structure and negotiation** to the brand/business-
development function. Do not prescribe partnership terms or negotiate the
relationship from the funding seat. The value here is placing sponsorship
correctly in the non-dilutive sequence and stating its effective cost
(activation + licensing + any use restriction), not running the deal.

## Revenue and royalty financing (non-dilutive, encumbering)

These sit below sponsorship and above debt in the sequence. They do not dilute
ownership, but they **encumber a revenue stream** and reduce future free cash
flow:

- **Revenue-based financing** — an advance repaid as a percentage of a named
  revenue stream until a cap (a multiple of the advance) is reached. The stated
  percentage is not the cost; model the actual stream, collection timing, and the
  early-payoff option to derive the effective IRR.
- **Royalty financing / royalty advance** — capital advanced against a royalty
  right (master, publishing, or sync income) recouped from that stream. The
  effective cost is the total income relinquished versus capital received, modeled
  under base, bear, and bull royalty trajectories.

Because they reduce future free cash flow the same way equity does, treat their
effective cost as a real component of WACC. Their advantage over equity is that
they are recouped and then end, returning the stream to the enterprise; their
risk is recoupment feasibility under a weak revenue trajectory, which is scored
on the repayment/recoupment/clawback dimension.

## A worked sequencing walk-through (framework, not a case)

An emerging music company needs capital for an album campaign and market
development. The sequence:

1. **Grants** — is there an eligible program for recording, market development,
   or touring? Assess each program's eligibility basis; disposition each.
2. **Tax credits** — is the recording eligible for sound-recording credits? Is
   there a qualifying labor component? Factor credit timing (often refunded a
   fiscal year later) into the cash-flow model.
3. **Sponsorship/partnership capital** — is there an aligned brand category that
   could contribute activation capital for co-branding rights? Size it here;
   hand the deal to business development.
4. **Revenue/royalty financing** — is there a contracted or predictable stream
   (sync income, catalog mechanicals, prior-release streaming) that can support
   an advance against the revenue right, not equity?
5. **Debt** — after non-dilutive sources are maximized, is catalog or contracted
   revenue sufficient to support secured debt at a reasonable coverage ratio?
6. **Equity** — only after the above are evaluated and dispositioned, and only
   for the gap that cannot be filled more cheaply from earlier tiers.

The sequence is a binding analytical requirement. The effective-cost comparison
across tiers makes the economic case for it explicit and falsifiable.
