# Multi-Entity Integrity and Cost Allocation

Intercompany integrity, cost-allocation methodology, consolidated elimination, and
entity-wall enforcement. General frameworks followed by music-specific modules.

---

## GENERAL FRAMEWORKS

### Entity walls are absolute

Costs, revenues, and balances belonging to one entity never migrate to another
without a documented, arm's-length intercompany transaction. This is not a
preference — it is a structural requirement. A cost incurred by Entity A and
recorded in Entity B without an intercompany transaction misstates both entities:
B's results are overstated by the cost it did not incur, A's are understated by the
cost it absorbed silently, and neither set of books can be independently relied on.
Entity-wall verification is a required close step, signed off like any other.

The discipline applies equally to the controller's own work product: an assessment
produced for one entity's books never references another entity's data, balances,
or figures. Mixing entity contexts contaminates both.

### Cost allocation methodology

Where costs are genuinely shared across entities, departments, or functions
(shared services, common overhead, group-level functions), they are allocated by a
**documented, consistently applied methodology** with a stated basis — headcount,
revenue, usage, square footage, or another named driver appropriate to the cost.
The standard is not which basis is chosen but that the basis is documented,
defensible, applied consistently across periods, and re-applied without
manipulation to shift results between entities.

- **Documented driver.** Each allocation pool names its driver and the rationale
  for it. A driver chosen because it produces a convenient result, rather than
  because it reflects the cost's actual consumption, is a manipulation risk.
- **Consistency over periods.** Allocation methodology is stable. A change requires
  controller approval, a stated reason, prospective application, and disclosure to
  affected entities — logged in a formal change log.
- **Annual review.** The methodology is reviewed for continued appropriateness at
  least annually (the budget cycle is a natural trigger), because cost structures
  and drivers shift over time.

### Intercompany reconciliation and elimination

Intercompany accounts must reconcile to zero on a consolidated basis — every
intercompany receivable in one entity has a matching payable in the counterparty
entity. A non-zero consolidated intercompany balance is an out-of-balance condition
that artificially inflates or deflates the consolidated balance sheet.

- **Matched two-sided booking.** Every intercompany transaction is booked on both
  sides with matching amount and period; a one-sided booking is the most common
  source of intercompany breaks.
- **Documented arm's-length rationale.** Intercompany charges (management fees,
  shared-service charges, internal licensing) carry transfer-pricing documentation
  supporting the rate as arm's-length, consistent with any applicable transfer-
  pricing requirements.
- **Elimination as a close deliverable.** Intercompany eliminations are prepared and
  reviewed at consolidation with a sign-off trail; any intercompany profit in
  inventory or capitalized balances is eliminated with documentation.
- **Break aging.** Intercompany breaks are aged and escalated like any reconciling
  break; chronic small breaks left unresolved beyond 60 days indicate a process
  failure, not immateriality.

## MUSIC MODULES

### Music-enterprise entity structures

Music groups commonly operate across multiple legal entities — a recording entity,
a publishing entity, a distribution entity, territorial sub-entities, and
special-purpose entities for specific catalogs or projects. Rights income and
costs flow between them, which makes intercompany integrity and entity-wall
discipline more consequential than in a single-entity operating company, not less.

- **Intercompany rights flows.** Where one group entity licenses rights to
  another (a publishing entity licensing to a recording entity, a master-rights
  entity licensing to a distribution entity), the internal licensing transaction is
  booked on both sides at a documented, arm's-length rate, and eliminated on
  consolidation. An internal rights flow recorded on only one side overstates group
  revenue.
- **Royalty flows across entities.** Royalties earned in one entity and remitted
  through another (a collection entity distributing to operating entities) must
  preserve the entity wall: each entity's share is recorded against its own books,
  not commingled in a single pool that obscures which entity earned what.
- **Shared-service allocations.** Group-level A&R, marketing, and administrative
  functions serving multiple entities are allocated on a documented driver (roster
  headcount, revenue share, project attribution). An allocation that loads
  disproportionate cost onto one entity to flatter another's margin is an
  entity-wall and allocation-integrity failure simultaneously.
- **Territorial sub-entity consolidation.** Territorial entities reporting in local
  currency are translated and consolidated with FX handled consistently; the
  intercompany positions between a territorial entity and the parent reconcile to
  zero after translation, with translation differences taken to the appropriate
  reserve, not buried in an intercompany break.

### The entity-wall close checkpoint

At each close, the controller confirms — as an explicit, signed-off step — that no
cost, revenue, or balance belonging to one entity has been recorded in another
without a documented intercompany transaction, and that all intercompany positions
reconcile to zero on consolidation or carry documented open items. This checkpoint
is named in the close package; its absence is itself a finding.
