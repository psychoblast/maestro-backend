# Prioritization & Forecasting

Every decision is a prioritization act, and every prioritization rests on a
forecast. This file covers opportunity-cost-driven prioritization under binding
constraints, portfolio construction, kill-decision discipline, and the forecasting
and scenario methods that make the underlying projections honest.

All directional figures, base rates, and projection ranges are ESTIMATE / NOT
QUOTABLE until confirmed against measured outcomes.

---

## PART A — PRIORITIZATION

### Opportunity Cost as the Governing Lens

Evaluating an opportunity in isolation — asking only whether it is worth pursuing —
is the wrong analysis. The right analysis asks whether it is worth more than the
next-best use of the same scarce resources. The difference is the difference between
a yes/no binary and a genuine ranking, and only the ranking produces sound
prioritization under constraint.

Apply prioritization to ALL competing uses of a scarce resource simultaneously, not
to individual opportunities in sequence. Evaluating A, then B, then C one at a time
produces three binary approvals, none of which acknowledges that the enterprise may
lack the resources to do all three well. The correct method is a comparative
ranking: given available capital, attention, and capacity, which ordering maximizes
expected enterprise value? That ranking immediately surfaces the opportunity cost of
each position — what is forfeited by placing B above C is the value of C.

When opportunities are approved without naming what was forfeited, the enterprise
accumulates commitments without accounting for their mutual opportunity cost. Each
approval feels individually justified; the aggregate exceeds capacity, leaving every
commitment under-resourced. This is the mechanism by which initiative sprawl is
created — not one bad decision, but a series of individually defensible ones. The
cure is comparative ranking applied before each commitment, not after.

### Ranking Under a Binding Constraint

The enterprise always operates under at least one binding constraint; identify which
one is actually binding for the decision at hand.

- **When capital is binding** (typical in high-growth phases): rank by expected
  return per dollar deployed, not by absolute return. The marginal dollar is the
  reference point.
- **When executive attention is binding** (typical in mature/scaled phases): rank
  by expected return per unit of attention. Attention is rarely modeled and so its
  opportunity cost is systematically underestimated; high-touch commitments
  (frequent reviews, high uncertainty, intensive relationship management) carry a
  higher attention cost that must be named.
- **When organizational capacity is binding** (typical in high-uncertainty phases):
  rank by execution-quality-adjusted return — expected return discounted by the
  probability the enterprise can staff, manage, and deliver at the standard the
  projection assumed, given everything already committed.

Misidentifying the binding constraint produces systematically wrong prioritization —
optimizing for capital efficiency when attention is actually binding yields a
capital-efficient portfolio no one has the bandwidth to execute well.

### Compare Against the Next-Best Alternative, Not Against Zero

The most common evaluation error is comparing the proposal against zero (doing
nothing), which produces an artificially favorable comparison — almost any action
beats inaction. The correct benchmark is the next-best alternative: the most
valuable thing the enterprise would do with the same resources if this opportunity
did not exist. A proposal returning 1.5× capital is attractive only if the next-best
alternative returns less than 1.5×. Name the next-best alternative explicitly,
estimate its expected value (ESTIMATE), and state why the proposal is preferred.
Reframe inaction not as zero but as the next-best use — this surfaces the cases the
zero-comparison would have suppressed.

### Portfolio Construction

A portfolio is not the sum of individually evaluated opportunities; its aggregate
properties are emergent. Attend to three simultaneously:

- **Correlation.** Two opportunities depending on the same underlying assumption are
  correlated bets — when the assumption fails, both fail together. A portfolio
  diversified across surfaces (geography, segment, type) may carry concentrated
  exposure at the assumption level. Map the dependency structure: what assumptions,
  if wrong, would cause multiple positions to fail at once?
- **Time-horizon balance.** Too heavily weighted toward long-duration bets and the
  portfolio cannot fund its own operating costs; too heavily toward short-duration
  and it generates current cash but no future pipeline. Confirm near-term
  obligations are met within the current configuration.
- **Risk-return calibration.** Maximize risk-adjusted return given the capacity to
  absorb loss, not raw expected return. Size each bet in proportion to its edge,
  adjusted by outcome asymmetry: larger bets when upside is asymmetric and loss is
  bounded and survivable; smaller bets when the loss scenario threatens core
  operations.

Portfolio construction is ongoing — as bets resolve, the aggregate shape changes,
and a successful position can become a dominant one. Reassess at regular intervals.

### Sunk Cost vs. Forward Value

Sunk costs are irrelevant to forward decisions — widely stated, frequently violated,
because resources already deployed create emotional and organizational investment.
The corrective question at each decision point: given everything known now, would we
start this commitment from scratch today? If no, the case for continuing rests on
sunk-cost psychology, not forward value.

Distinguish **sunk-cost escalation** (committing more because of what was spent,
without a model of how the marginal capital changes the expected outcome — always
irrational) from **rational recommitment** (continuing or increasing because new
information has changed the forward expectation — justified by the model, not the
history). The test: can the recommitment be justified entirely on forward terms,
ignoring everything spent? Beware the commitment-to-completion fallacy — that
reaching a milestone justifies continuing regardless of forward economics. The cost
of completion is a forward deployment justified by its expected return, not by the
ratio of completion to prior investment.

### Kill-Decision Discipline

The kill decision is the most underinvested discipline because it is
organizationally costly — it signals an earlier bet was wrong and makes freed
resources visible. The antidote is to design kill criteria at the point of
commitment, not at review. Kill criteria are pre-specified, measurable conditions at
defined intervals that, if unmet, automatically trigger a kill review — the minimum
conditions required to justify continued investment, not performance targets. They
make kills procedural rather than discretionary (triggered by a metric, not a
person) and compress the time from underperformance signal to decision.

The three kill verdicts: **kill now** (forward economics negative regardless of
additional investment, even in optimistic scenarios); **wait** (forward economics
uncertain but resolvable within a defined window at acceptable cost of waiting); and
**never** (the underlying market/regulatory/competitive conditions have made the
original thesis permanently invalid — an immediate kill regardless of milestone
proximity).

### Prioritization Failure Modes

- **Yes to everything** — the most common failure with growth momentum and available
  capital; commitments accumulate faster than execution capacity, producing a wide,
  shallow portfolio visible in execution quality, not financial projections.
- **Recency and loudest-voice bias** — priority reflects what was most recently or
  loudly advocated rather than expected return per constrained resource. Counter with
  a structured ranking process using common criteria and format.
- **Ignoring opportunity cost** — the analytic failure underlying the above; without
  naming the cost of the yes, approvals accumulate because each appears costless.
- **Refusing to kill** — resources stay tied up in underperformers; the kill is
  deferred because its cost is felt acutely while the opportunity cost of not killing
  is invisible. Counter with pre-specified kill criteria applied as rigorously as
  investment criteria.

### Music Applications — Prioritization

A music enterprise allocates simultaneously across roster development, advances,
release production, and marketing (capital); active artists, candidates, and catalog
(attention); release-calendar slots; and operational bandwidth — interdependent
constraints. In most music contexts the binding constraints, in rough order of
scarcity, are A&R and senior executive attention, then release-calendar capacity,
then capital. Capital is the most visible constraint but rarely the most binding,
because committed capital can often be staged while attention and calendar slots
cannot.

Evaluate a signing not just for its capital cost (advance plus expected marketing)
but for its **attention cost**: how much A&R and executive involvement it will
require, and what that displaces. An artist needing intensive development support is
a high-attention commitment, and that cost should be weighed against the alternatives
forgone. Compare across domains — roster vs. release slate, marketing vs. A&R,
catalog vs. roster development — using a common unit: expected enterprise value per
unit of constrained resource.

**Roster capacity and release-calendar slots are the most underpriced constraints.**
Capital can be raised and attention partially delegated, but the roster and calendar
are physically finite. The opportunity cost of a roster position is the expected
value of the highest-ranked unsigned candidate who would occupy it if freed; an
existing position generating below-expected returns should be evaluated against that
opportunity cost, not just its own forward model. Release-calendar slots operate
identically — a major campaign window forecloses every other release that could have
occupied it. An early-development artist who will not be release-ready for many
months consumes both a roster slot and a future calendar slot; make that compound
commitment explicit.

**A roster is a portfolio of correlated bets.** Map the dependency structure: two
artists in different genres are correlated if both depend on the same discovery
mechanism (e.g., algorithmic placement on one platform). Outcomes are power-law
distributed, so the portfolio must be large enough for the expected-value
calculation to be meaningful (a handful of positions is too small), and structured
so the most asymmetric positions receive the most favorable terms and deepest
support when traction emerges. Balance time horizons deliberately rather than
signing whatever is available.

**The kill decision in music** carries a human dimension absent in most enterprise
contexts — a dropped artist experiences it as personal rejection, discussed within
the talent community in ways that affect the enterprise's brand as a partner. Separate
the analytical verdict (continue investment or not, on forward-value terms) from its
execution (the timing, framing, and handling of the relationship transition). The
forward-value analysis combines three inputs: streaming/sales trajectory (the trend
line, not the current level), the A&R team's assessment of next-project potential
(a genuine step change vs. a continuation that has not worked), and roster-slot
opportunity cost. Negative trajectory + no creative step change + a higher-value
alternative waiting for the slot = a clear kill. Negative trajectory + a genuine
creative step change = a conditional wait to a defined milestone with no further
extensions. The same logic applies to over-budget recordings, depleted campaigns,
and deteriorated catalog acquisitions.

## PART B — FORECASTING & SCENARIOS

### The Outside View: Base Rates Before Narrative

Begin every forecast from the outside view — what has typically happened to
comparable decisions across a large population — before the inside view (the
specifics of this case) enriches it. The base rate is a prior, not a ceiling; the
case for deviating from it must be explicit, evidence-based, and stated as a testable
claim. Three steps before inside-view analysis: identify the reference class (genuinely
comparable, not cherry-picked for optimism); determine the base rate within it; assess
what adjustments the specific case justifies, tied to evidence rather than the
emotional salience of the opportunity.

### Scenario Construction: Bull / Base / Bear

Scenario planning is not three tints of optimism on one projection — it is an
investigation of the assumptions whose uncertainty most drives outcome uncertainty.
Begin with assumption mapping: identify the three to five critical uncertainties —
variables that are genuinely uncertain and consequential (high sensitivity). The
bull scenario is the favorable resolution of those assumptions (not the theoretical
maximum); the bear is their unfavorable resolution (not a worst-case catastrophe);
the base is the most probable combination (not a mechanical average). Each scenario
needs three elements: the specific assumption combination that characterizes it; a
narrative of how the world got there (which makes it testable through observable
early signals); and a quantified outcome range with a stated confidence level.

### Probabilistic Thinking and Calibration

A probability is a claim that can be tracked. A well-calibrated forecaster who
assigns 70% confidence is correct about 70% of the time across that set; persistent
overconfidence is detectable in the record. Log probability estimates and evaluate
them. Be explicit about uncertainty: an 80% credible interval that is narrow claims
high predictability; a wide one acknowledges meaningful uncertainty. The appropriate
width is determined by the evidence, not by a preference for appearing decisive. A
narrow interval without evidence is overconfidence; a wide interval with no central
estimate is epistemic abdication.

### Point Forecasts vs. Ranges

A point forecast is a central tendency stripped of its uncertainty context. Two
decisions with the same expected value but different dispersion are different
decisions; a point forecast presents them identically. Range-based forecasting states:
"under base-case assumptions, the outcome falls between X and Y, most probably near
Z." The range's width is itself an information signal about dependence on untested
assumptions. The critical step point forecasts obscure: whether the downside end of
the range is survivable within the capital structure, and whether the upside
materially changes the expected-value calculation. Both require the range.

### Sensitivity Analysis

Vary each assumption across its plausible range, holding others at base case, and
record the change in outcome. High-sensitivity assumptions are the load-bearing ones —
if wrong, the central estimate is materially wrong; low-sensitivity assumptions are
robustness parameters. Two outputs affect the decision: a ranking of assumptions by
impact (where to invest in more evidence), and whether the downside of the
highest-sensitivity assumption falls above or below the no-go threshold (if above,
the decision is robust to that assumption; if below, the decision hinges on it — say
so). Convert each high-sensitivity assumption into an observable leading indicator so
the forecast becomes a living, decision-monitoring model rather than a static
projection evaluated only after the fact.

### Optionality and Staging as Responses to Uncertainty

Forecast uncertainty is not a reason to avoid deciding; it is a reason to structure
decisions to preserve the ability to respond. An option is a decision right —
contingent on conditions at a future time — and it has value because uncertainty has
value. The classic error is confusing the option to proceed with the obligation to
proceed: committing fully upfront when conditions justified only a staged first step
that creates the option. Staging defines commitment phases — limited early capital to
test core assumptions, incremental later capital if results confirm them — limiting
downside without forgoing the opportunity. The decision to stage is a calculated
judgment about the ratio of option value to structure cost, not a default response to
uncertainty. Staging without pre-defined trigger conditions is commitment laundering:
the appearance of optionality with no mechanism to act, which reverts to full
commitment.

### Forecasting Failure Modes

- **Single-point overconfidence** — point estimates with unjustified precision and no
  interval; clustered bull/base/bear cases. Counter with mandatory interval reporting
  calibrated to the evidence.
- **Anchoring** — the first number exerts disproportionate influence; adjustments are
  insufficient. Counter by doing the base-rate analysis before engaging inside-view
  anchors.
- **Base-rate neglect** — building forecasts entirely from inside-view specifics,
  producing consistently optimistic results. Counter by making the reference class and
  base rate explicit steps.
- **Narrative-driven projection** — numbers selected to support a pre-existing
  conclusion. Diagnostic: what evidence would cause a downward revision? If none, the
  projection is narrative-driven.
- **Assumption concealment** — critical assumptions embedded without labeling. Counter
  with mandatory assumption labeling, source, and sensitivity.

### Music Applications — Forecasting

Music is a hit-rate industry: outcome distributions are heavily skewed — a small
fraction of releases and signings generate most returns, and the majority return at
or below breakeven on a fully loaded basis. Modeling music outcomes as if they were
normally distributed around a comfortable mean forecasts an enterprise that does not
exist. Expected value is dominated by (probability of hitting the upper tail) ×
(magnitude in the tail) minus (probability of bottom-tier) × (cost of bottom-tier).
A mean-only model undervalues high-optionality bets and undervalues downside
protection where the bottom-tier outcome is material to solvency.

Develop a running reference class for recurring decisions. For a signing: of artists
signed at a comparable stage, investment profile, and environment, what fraction
achieved recoupment-associated streaming thresholds; what fraction returned in excess
of advance; what fraction ended unrecouped? These rates (ESTIMATE) calibrate the
probability weights on bull/base/bear. The base rate is not a veto — but deviation
from it must be a stated, testable claim about specific differentiating factors, and
some weight must always remain on the modal outcome.

**Release scenario construction.** The critical assumptions cluster around five
variables: audience reach, conversion rate, per-stream/unit economics (usually
low-sensitivity), editorial and algorithmic discovery, and catalog durability
(decay rate and long tail). For an established artist, the high-sensitivity assumption
is typically discovery (an input the enterprise does not control — say so explicitly).
For a developing artist, it is reach (no proven audience to anchor a floor). Express
the revenue range with stated assumption drivers, never as an arbitrary spread — for
each endpoint, explain exactly which assumptions made it high or low. Unexplained
spreads are uncertainty displayed without insight.

**Signing and deal scenario modeling.** A signing carries uncertainty over a longer
horizon: the commercial outcome depends on the artist's trajectory across the term.
Core assumptions: trajectory, recoupment timing, advance sizing (calibrated to the
base case or to a more optimistic one), and term structure (whether the enterprise
can exit, renegotiate, or convert as information accumulates). The canonical write-off
is a high advance sized to a bull-case trajectory, on a long term with no exit, in a
bear-case realization. The bull scenario is the favorable resolution of trajectory
*within the term* — success after the term ends does not accrue to the enterprise.
The bear scenario must model the unrecouped position at term end and size it against
the capital structure: a small fraction of the annual budget is a manageable
portfolio event; a scale that constrains the next signing cohort is a capital threat.

**Presenting music forecasts.** The common failure is false precision — a projection
stated as a specific number with no interval or confidence. Distinguish three
components: the base-case central estimate; the scenario range (bear-low to bull-high)
with stated probability weights; and the confidence level (which assumptions are
grounded in observed comparable data vs. extrapolated). False precision is most
damaging at two junctures: advance sizing (an advance sized to a point estimate is
never tested for recoupability under the bear case) and campaign-budget approval
(marketing-driven incremental lift presented as point estimates can be approved on
returns not distinguishable from baseline within the measurement uncertainty). Every
forecast carries explicit confidence tags: evidence type, confidence level, and the
single assumption whose failure would most change the central estimate.

### Building Optionality Into Release and Deal Decisions

Three mechanisms reduce the cost of being wrong about forecast assumptions:

- **Deal-structure optionality** — shorter initial terms with extension options,
  territory- or format-limited first agreements. The option's value rises with
  trajectory uncertainty (high for early-development artists, lower for established
  ones where the cost of reduced artist certainty may exceed the benefit).
- **Release-sequencing optionality** — sequencing lower-investment, time-insensitive
  releases first to test market conditions before committing full investment to the
  peak window.
- **Campaign staging** — reserving a fraction of budget as contingent investment
  deployed only when early metrics reach defined thresholds.

Optionality requires pre-defined trigger conditions — the streaming level, engagement
rate, playlist-uptake rate, or commercial milestone that activates the option.
Without triggers, the staged structure reverts to full commitment and the option
value is forfeited without acknowledgment. Assess the *practical* operability of an
option, not just its presence in the contract: a written exit right the organization
is culturally or relationally unwilling to exercise is not a functioning option.
