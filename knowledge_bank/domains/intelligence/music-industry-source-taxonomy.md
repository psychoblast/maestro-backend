# Music Industry Source Taxonomy

A structured guide to the source categories in music intelligence and how to navigate
from a trade story to the underlying primary document. This file is the operational
companion to the source-tier system in [[source-trust-and-assessment]]: that file
defines how to *score* a source; this file defines *what types of sources to look for*
and how they are structured by category.

---

## 1. The Six Primary Source Categories

Each category groups sources by the type of organization that produces primary-tier
(Tier A) output in that domain. Within each category, the same structural features
appear across territories, so the taxonomy stays valid as specific entities change.

---

### Category 1 — Streaming Platform Official Documents

**What generates Tier A output:** an official announcement through a platform's own
channels — press room, developer portal, artist-facing documentation, or investor
relations filings — or a regulatory filing the platform has submitted to a government
body.

**What to watch for:**

- **Policy announcements:** minimum stream-length requirements, content-labeling
  requirements, explicit-content policies, playlist editorial guidelines. These are
  the most frequent source of CDM-1 and CDM-2 events because they change the rules
  every rights holder and distributor operates under.
- **Royalty model disclosures:** any change to the payout pool calculation, rate card,
  or threshold for minimum payable streams. A platform's description of its royalty
  model in its own documentation is Tier A; a trade publication's description of the
  same model is Tier B at most.
- **Platform service-tier changes:** new subscription tiers, free-tier feature changes,
  bundling arrangements. Route to Distribution & Platform; secondary route to Finance &
  Royalties for the revenue-mix implication.
- **API and technical documentation:** delivery format requirements, metadata schema
  changes, content-recognition policy updates. These are operational Tier A for Digital
  Operations.

**How to move from the trade story to the primary document:** most trade stories quote
or reference a platform's announcement. The primary document is the platform's own
press-room post, help-center article, or developer documentation — not the trade's
interpretation of it. If no direct link is present in the trade story, the primary is
accessible through the platform's official newsroom. Check the publication date on the
primary against the trade story date; a trade story published before the platform's
official announcement is speculative.

**Anti-pattern:** treating a trade story about a platform policy as Tier A because the
story includes a direct quote from the platform. A quote inside a trade story is Tier B.
The Tier A claim is the platform's own published statement, which typically includes
effective dates, scope conditions, and carve-outs that the trade story extracts
selectively.

### Category 2 — Rights Body and Collective Management Organization Documents

**What generates Tier A output:** officially published rate schedules, annual reports,
rate-setting body determinations, consent decree filings, and legislatively mandated
rate-setting decisions. Rights bodies and collective management organizations (CMOs)
publish these through their own websites and through regulatory filing systems.

**What to watch for:**

- **Rate schedule publications:** performing rights organizations (PROs) and mechanical
  licensing bodies publish rate schedules on a regulatory cycle. A rate schedule update
  is CDM-1 if it changes rates materially; a minor administrative clarification is
  CDM-4 at most. The primary document is the published schedule, not trade coverage
  of the schedule.
- **Rate-setting body determinations:** judicial or regulatory bodies periodically set
  rates for statutory licenses. These determinations are primary documents and are
  routinely underread by analysts who follow only trade coverage — the trade story
  reports the headline rate without the operational detail in the underlying ruling.
- **Merger or structural changes among rights bodies:** consolidation of PROs or CMOs
  across territories is a CDM-1 candidate. The primary is the regulatory filing or the
  bodies' own official announcement.
- **International reciprocal agreements:** bilateral or multilateral agreements between
  CMOs affect collection for international repertoire. Slow-moving but highly durable
  intelligence; a change to a reciprocal agreement is CDM-2 for the affected territories.

**Regulatory cycle awareness:** rights body rates in most territories operate on a
multi-year cycle. The cycle length varies by territory and rights type. Establish the
review calendar for each territory covered and treat cycle-open events — when a new
rate-setting proceeding begins — as watch-list triggers that warrant DEVELOPING status.

### Category 3 — Chart Authority Publications

**What generates Tier A output:** the chart authority's own published methodology
documents, rule announcements, and official methodology FAQs. In some territories the
chart authority is also the trade association for the recording industry; its
publications carry the same Tier A status for methodology-related claims.

**What to watch for:**

- **Methodology change announcements:** changes to stream-counting rules, album-
  equivalent unit definitions (TEA and SEA conversion rates), chart eligibility rules,
  or physical-sales weighting. These are CDM-1 for any specialist whose strategy depends
  on chart mechanics.
- **Chart eligibility rules:** what qualifies for the main chart vs. a genre sub-chart;
  qualification windows; bundling restrictions (when an album sold with merchandise
  counts toward chart eligibility). Changes here affect release strategy directly and
  route to Marketing and A&R.
- **Platform counting additions:** chart authorities periodically add or adjust how they
  count streams from new services or short-video platforms. Low-profile but strategically
  significant; the primary is the chart authority's own announcement.

**Regional chart authority landscape:** each major market has one or more chart
authorities, and their structural features differ sharply — some are trade-association-
operated, some are government-adjacent, some are operated by commercial data providers.
Never assume one market's chart-authority type or methodology maps to another's. The
territory-lens method in [[territory-intelligence]] identifies the structural axes;
this taxonomy identifies where the primary documents live.

### Category 4 — Label Group and Publisher Financial Disclosures

**What generates Tier A output:** quarterly earnings releases, annual reports, investor
day presentations, and regulatory filings from publicly listed label groups and
publishers. For privately held entities, output degrades to Tier B (trade coverage of
disclosed figures) unless the entity publishes official materials.

**What to watch for:**

- **Streaming revenue share disclosures:** when a major label group discloses how much
  of its recorded-music revenue comes from streaming — and how that share is changing —
  this is CDM-4 most cycles; CDM-2 if a structural shift appears (e.g., a sustained
  reversal of the streaming-growth trajectory).
- **Catalog vs. frontline revenue mix:** the ratio of catalog to frontline revenue
  signals how labels are allocating investment and promotional attention. A sustained
  shift in this ratio is a CDM-3 opportunity signal for A&R and Marketing.
- **Publishing and synchronization revenue trends:** shifts in sync, licensing, or
  publishing revenue reported by major publishers signal CDM-3 or CDM-4 opportunities
  for the sync and publishing specialists.
- **International territory performance:** major label groups often break out performance
  by region. Territory-level shifts are useful-signal to strong-trade tier depending
  on disclosure granularity.

**Anti-pattern:** treating an analyst or media summary of an earnings call as the
primary document. The earnings release, investor presentation, and call transcript
(where available) are the primary documents. Analyst notes are Tier C at best —
they interpret, select, and sometimes extrapolate from the underlying disclosure.

### Category 5 — Regulatory and Legislative Filings

**What generates Tier A output:** the text of enacted legislation, regulatory agency
determinations, judicial rulings, and official government consultation documents.
These are public records, accessible through official government publication channels.

**What to watch for:**

- **Copyright legislation:** any legislative change affecting the duration, scope, or
  enforcement mechanisms of copyright is a CDM-1 candidate for Publishing & Rights and
  Legal.
- **Competition authority decisions:** decisions on label, distributor, or platform
  mergers. CDM-1 if they resolve a pending deal; CDM-5 while proceedings are open and
  the outcome is uncertain.
- **Data protection and AI regulation:** AI-specific regulation affecting training data
  licensing, AI-generated content, and voice/likeness protection legislation routes to
  Publishing & Rights and Legal.
- **Domestic content quotas:** new or revised broadcast or streaming quotas are CDM-1
  in the affected territory; route to Marketing and Distribution together per the quota-
  supersession rule in [[triage-and-routing]].

**Navigation pattern:** legislative and regulatory filings are public but not always
easily navigable. Trade coverage of regulatory developments frequently omits effective
dates, phase-in periods, and scope carve-outs — precisely the details that determine
operational impact. Always read the primary text for CDM-1 and CDM-2 candidates.
A trade story that describes the headline ruling without the operative clause is
Tier B intelligence, not Tier A.

### Category 6 — Technology and AI Primary Documents

**What generates Tier A output:** patent filings; official regulatory consultation
responses from platforms; platform-published AI policy documents; court filings in
AI-related copyright and likeness litigation.

**What to watch for:**

- **AI training data licensing policy changes:** when a platform publishes or updates
  its policy on the use of music to train AI models, this is a CDM-1 candidate for
  Publishing & Rights and Legal.
- **AI-generated content monetization policies:** platform decisions on distributing or
  monetizing AI-generated audio. CDM-3 or CDM-2 depending on the platform's market
  position and the policy's scope.
- **Copyright litigation outcomes in AI cases:** judicial decisions route to Publishing
  & Rights, Legal, and long-term to Finance & Royalties. Decisions in one jurisdiction
  are CDM-1 for that jurisdiction; cross-territory extrapolation requires corroboration
  from other territory primary sources.
- **Voice cloning and likeness protection policies:** platform policies on AI voice
  simulation and artist likeness are CDM-1 candidates where they impose new restrictions.

**Technology source caution:** the AI policy space moves faster than any other category,
and trade coverage frequently conflates proposals, consultations, announced intentions,
and enacted policy. Apply the source tier hierarchy rigorously: an official blog post
from the platform is Tier A; a trade story summarizing that post is Tier B; a newsletter
interpreting the trade story is Tier C.

## 2. The Trade-to-Primary Navigation Protocol

The single most common intelligence failure is treating a strong-trade secondary as the
primary source. The four-step navigation pattern:

1. **Identify the claim type.** Is it a platform policy, a rate, a rule change, a
   regulatory decision, or a financial disclosure? The claim type determines the issuing
   authority.
2. **Identify the issuing authority.** Who would publish the primary document for this
   claim type? (Platform press room; PRO website; chart authority; regulatory agency;
   court system; company investor relations page.)
3. **Locate the primary.** Access the issuing authority's official channel. Most are
   publicly accessible; some regulatory filings require a filing system account.
4. **Compare primary to trade story.** Note effective dates, scope carve-outs, or
   operational details the trade story missed. If material details were omitted, the
   intelligence entry uses the primary document and notes the gap.

If the primary cannot be located, downgrade the claim one tier: a strong-trade story
citing an inaccessible primary degrades to useful-signal. If the primary contradicts
the trade story on a material point, the primary governs and the contradiction is noted
in the entry per the contradictory-source resolution protocol in
[[source-trust-and-assessment]].

## 3. Cross-Border Source Navigation

Source infrastructure varies sharply by territory. These structural patterns hold as
specific outlets change:

- **Common-law copyright territories:** litigation filings and court decisions are
  public record, accessible through official court filing systems. Regulatory agency
  determinations are published on agency websites.
- **Civil-law copyright territories:** rights body determinations are often published
  through the CMO's own official communications rather than through a centralized
  regulatory portal. Rate negotiations between CMOs and platforms are occasionally
  subject to confidentiality provisions that limit what can be publicly confirmed.
- **Territories with dominant public broadcasters:** national broadcaster license fee
  policy and content quota decisions are regulatory Tier A for broadcasting-adjacent
  intelligence; locate the communications regulator's publications as the primary source.
- **Territories where CMO and trade body are unified:** some markets have a single body
  that functions as both rights administrator and industry association; its publications
  are simultaneously Tier A (rights administration) and Tier B (trade commentary on
  broader topics).

**Coverage gap discipline:** if a territory lacks accessible Tier A or B sources in
your working language, that is a structural intelligence gap — not a quiet cycle.
Report it as NOT EVALUABLE and flag the gap for resolution through a territory-
specialist source or professional translation of available primary materials.

## 4. Source Discovery Protocol

When a new source is encountered, the watch-list decision follows three steps:

1. **Apply the STAR Protocol** from [[source-trust-and-assessment]] and assign a
   provisional tier. Record the date.
2. **Assign a provisional cadence tier** based on coverage focus and expected CDM
   relevance: daily for Tier A sources covering rule-change-prone categories; weekly for
   Tier B; monthly for Tier C.
3. **Set a six-week review date.** After six weeks of active monitoring, confirm or
   revise the tier. Provisional status is logged explicitly and is not cleared silently.

**Watch-list addition discipline:** require at least one prior example of a CDM-1/2/3
item from the source before adding it to the weekly cadence. Sources added on the basis
of reputation rather than demonstrated CDM output are the primary driver of watch-list
bloat. Fame is not authority — see the STAR Protocol anti-pattern in
[[source-trust-and-assessment]].
