# Streaming Economics Intelligence Depth

The structural mechanics of streaming royalty economics, chart methodology, and
platform policy that most frequently generate CDM-1 and CDM-2 intelligence events.
Knowing the mechanics — not just that a rule changed but *what the rule controls* — is
what separates an intelligence item that routes correctly from one that routes to the
wrong specialist with the wrong implication.

---

## 1. Royalty Pool and Per-Stream Rate Mechanics

### 1.1 How the Royalty Pool Is Constructed

Major streaming platforms calculate royalties from a pool, not a per-stream unit price.
The standard construction:

1. **Total platform revenue** from subscription fees and advertising for the accounting
   period.
2. **Non-music deductions:** platform operating costs are excluded from the pool; the
   pool is revenue, not profit; some platforms guarantee a minimum pool floor.
3. **Music allocation:** a contractually established percentage of the pool is allocated
   to music (as opposed to podcasts, audiobooks, or other content). Changes to this
   percentage — or to how "music" is defined for allocation purposes — are CDM-1 events.
4. **Rights-type split:** the music pool is further divided between master (recording)
   rights and publishing (composition) rights per the platform's licensing agreements
   and applicable law. This split is governed by contracts and law, not algorithmic
   decision.

**Intelligence implication:** when a platform announces a royalty change, the primary
document must be read to determine which layer of the pool structure changed. A change
to the music allocation percentage is a different CDM class — and routes to different
specialists — than a change to the rights-type split within the pool. Conflating the
two produces a routing failure.

### 1.2 Pro-Rata vs. User-Centric Distribution

Within the master rights portion of the pool, platforms have historically used
**pro-rata** distribution: each track's payment is proportional to its share of total
streams on the platform, regardless of which users streamed it.

An alternative — **user-centric** (or listener-centric) distribution — allocates each
subscriber's payment only to the tracks that subscriber actually streamed in the
period. This produces materially different payouts for the same stream counts: artists
with small but highly engaged audiences benefit; artists with large casual-listener
audiences benefit less. The difference is structural, not marginal.

**Intelligence trigger:** any confirmed platform shift from pro-rata to user-centric is
a CDM-1 rule-changer. It changes the payout calculation for every rights holder on the
platform. A confirmed pilot or partial rollout is CDM-2. Strong-trade speculation
without platform confirmation is CDM-5 monitoring at most.

**Source floor for this development:** Tier A (platform's own announcement). This is
among the highest-consequence structural changes a streaming platform can make; the
source discipline must be correspondingly strict.

### 1.3 Minimum Stream Length Requirements

Every major streaming platform sets a minimum stream duration before a play counts
toward royalty calculation. These requirements:

- Are not uniform across platforms.
- Have been revised upward by several platforms in recent years.
- Affect short-form content (interludes, skits, brief tracks) disproportionately; a
  track shorter than the threshold earns nothing regardless of how many times it is
  played.
- Are stated in the platform's own policy documentation — Tier A primary.

**Intelligence implication:** a change in minimum stream length is a CDM-1 rule-changer
that routes to Distribution & Platform and Finance & Royalties. The secondary implication
for A&R is that track-length conventions in certain genres (hip-hop interludes, ambient
drone tracks, classical movements) may become economically penalized under a higher
threshold — route the secondary implication to A&R with its own "so what?".

### 1.4 Payout Thresholds and Anti-Manipulation Policies

Platforms periodically adjust or publish policies on minimum annual stream counts below
which no payment is disbursed in a period. These thresholds affect small-catalog and
catalog-long-tail rights holders disproportionately and are CDM-2 when material.

Separately, anti-fraud and anti-manipulation policies — changes to what counts as a
valid stream for royalty purposes — are CDM-1 when they alter the stream-counting rule.
The primary documents are platform policy publications and, where litigation has arisen,
court filings.

## 2. Playlist Intelligence Specifics

### 2.1 Editorial vs. Algorithmic Playlist Classification

The intelligence implication of a playlist-related development depends on which type of
playlist is affected:

| Type | Mechanism | CDM classification range |
|------|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Editorial playlists** | Human curators select tracks; decisions are pitch-dependent | Personnel changes → CDM-4 (contextual); editorial-strategy restructuring → CDM-2 |
| **Algorithmic playlists** | Machine-driven: listener behavior, metadata, platform signals | Algorithm policy change → CDM-1 or CDM-2 depending on scope |
| **Personalized playlists** | Individual user history; no direct editorial input | Personalization-engine policy changes → CDM-1 if it changes the signal weighting rule |
| **Genre/mood/context playlists** | Often editorially seeded, algorithmically maintained | Hybrid — routing to both Distribution/Platform and Data & Analytics |

**Anti-pattern:** routing "a playlist editor left" as CDM-1. Personnel changes in
editorial teams are CDM-4 (contextual update) unless they represent a structural
reorganization of the editorial function — which would be CDM-2 if it materially changes
the pitch and editorial relationship for a material portion of editorial placements.

### 2.2 Pitch Window Mechanics

Editorial playlists are sourced through a pitch submission process with a defined window
— a required number of weeks before the release date. Changes to this window are CDM-2
for Distribution & Platform and Marketing: campaign calendars and distributor timelines
must be restructured.

**What constitutes a pitch window change:** the window is platform-specific and is
published in the platform's artist-facing or distributor-facing documentation. A change
in the required lead time — even by one week — is CDM-2 for any active campaign planned
against the old window.

### 2.3 Short-Video Platform Creator Fund Mechanics

Short-video platforms pay music rights through mechanisms distinct from streaming
royalties, and those mechanisms have changed more frequently than traditional streaming
royalty structures.

- **Creator fund vs. music licensing fund:** some platforms run separate pools for
  creator payments and music licensing. Changes to the music licensing fund structure
  affect rights holders regardless of creator activity; route to Publishing & Rights
  and Finance & Royalties.
- **Use-type distinctions:** how a platform distinguishes between a track used as
  background audio, a lip-sync performance, or a cover recording affects both the
  royalty calculation and the rights clearance requirement. A policy change to these
  use-type definitions is CDM-1.
- **Territory-specific licensing agreements:** a blanket licensing deal in one territory
  does not extend to another. Gaps in licensing create collection voids; route to
  Finance & Royalties and Publishing & Rights as a CDM-3 or CDM-4 depending on the
  territory's size.

## 3. Chart Methodology Intelligence

### 3.1 Album-Equivalent Unit Mechanics

Charts in most major markets no longer count only album unit sales. The standard
framework uses three components combined into Album-Equivalent Units (AEU):

- **Pure sales:** a physical or digital album purchase.
- **Track-Equivalent Album (TEA):** a defined number of individual track purchases
  equals one album equivalent. The conversion rate is a methodological rule — a change
  is CDM-1.
- **Streaming-Equivalent Album (SEA):** a defined number of streams (weighted by track)
  equals one album equivalent. This includes on-demand streams and, in some
  methodologies, programmed streams at a discounted weight.

**Intelligence trigger:** any revision to the TEA or SEA conversion rates, to the
stream-weighting methodology, or to which platforms' streams count is CDM-1 for Data &
Analytics, A&R, and Marketing. Route all three, each with its own "so what?". The
primary document is the chart authority's methodology announcement.

### 3.2 Chart Eligibility and Bundle Rules

The rules most frequently revised:

- **Bundling restrictions:** when an album is sold with merchandise, tickets, or other
  products, chart authorities may restrict or exclude those bundle units from counting.
  Changes are CDM-1 for Marketing (release campaign design) and A&R (release strategy).
- **Minimum purchase price requirements:** some charts require qualifying sales to exceed
  a minimum retail price. Promotional pricing campaigns must be designed around these
  thresholds; a change to the threshold is CDM-2 for Marketing.
- **Pre-release activity counting:** whether pre-release streams or pre-orders count
  toward first-week chart eligibility is a recurring revision point. Changes are CDM-2
  for campaigns actively using pre-order or pre-save mechanics.

### 3.3 New Platform Integration into Chart Counting

When a chart authority adds a new platform to its counting methodology — or removes one
— this is CDM-1 for any strategy relying on that chart for release planning. The primary
document is the chart authority's announcement; the decision often follows a formal
review period during which the platform is monitored as a pilot (CDM-5 until confirmed).

## 4. Reading Investor Relations Releases for Music Intelligence

### 4.1 What to Extract from Earnings Releases

A quarterly earnings release from a publicly listed streaming platform contains several
layers of music intelligence that require active extraction:

- **Subscriber and MAU numbers:** absolute numbers are context; the year-on-year
  growth rate and territory-level breakdown are the intelligence. Decelerating growth
  in a specific territory is CDM-4 unless material enough to affect revenue projections —
  then CDM-2.
- **ARPU (average revenue per user) trends:** average revenue per subscriber by tier and
  territory signals whether the platform is extracting more or less value from its user
  base. A declining ARPU with flat subscriber count is a structural signal for Finance
  & Royalties.
- **Music vs. non-music content spend disclosures:** a platform disclosing increased
  investment in podcasts, audiobooks, or other non-music content is a CDM-4 contextual
  signal about how music's share of the pool may evolve — not CDM-2 unless a direct
  music-allocation change is announced.
- **Licensing and content cost disclosures:** when a platform separately discloses
  licensing costs as a percentage of revenue, changes in that ratio are a structural
  signal about negotiating leverage between the platform and rights holders.

### 4.2 Interpreting Territory-Level Disclosures

Major streaming platforms increasingly provide territory-level metrics in earnings
materials. The intelligence value hierarchy:

1. **Subscriber count and growth rate by territory:** CDM-4 most cycles; CDM-2 if a
   territory is entering or exiting meaningful growth.
2. **ARPU by territory:** indicates local pricing power and subscription tier mix;
   material divergence between territories suggests different competitive dynamics.
3. **Management commentary on specific territories:** spoken commentary on earnings
   calls is strong-trade tier (not filed) but is the clearest signal of platform
   strategic attention. Positive commentary combined with subscriber growth is CDM-3
   (opportunity-creator) for territory specialists.

### 4.3 What Earnings Releases Cannot Tell You

Earnings releases are financial disclosures, not operational intelligence. They do not:
- Disclose per-track or per-stream payout calculations.
- Reveal upcoming policy or royalty model changes before announcement.
- Constitute a reliable proxy for the platform's position in active licensing
  negotiations.

Use earnings intelligence as contextual background (CDM-4 baseline). Any inference
about policy or royalty model direction from earnings data is CDM-5 monitoring at most
unless confirmed by a policy announcement.

## 5. AI-Generated Content and Music Economics

### 5.1 The Structural Intelligence Questions

AI-generated content in music produces intelligence across multiple specialist domains.
The questions that generate intelligence events:

- **Training data licensing:** whether platforms or AI developers are required to
  license recordings used to train generative audio models. Court decisions and
  legislative changes here are CDM-1 for Publishing & Rights and Legal.
- **Royalty eligibility for AI-generated content:** whether recordings generated by AI
  qualify for streaming royalties, chart eligibility, or PRO collection. Platform
  policies and regulatory determinations on this point are CDM-1 where they establish
  or change the rule.
- **Attribution and labeling requirements:** any platform or regulatory requirement that
  AI-generated content be labeled as such affects metadata standards and catalog
  presentation; route to Digital Operations.
- **Voice and likeness protection:** legal instruments and platform policies protecting
  artists against unauthorized AI voice simulation or likeness replication are CDM-1
  for Legal and A&R.

### 5.2 Source Discipline for AI Developments

AI-related music intelligence is the category most prone to conflating proposals,
consultations, legislative drafts, and enacted policy. The source discipline required:

- A regulatory consultation response from a platform is Tier A, but it represents the
  platform's position, not an enacted rule. Classify as CDM-5 until the rule is
  established.
- A legislative draft is not enacted legislation; classify as DEVELOPING with a
  named re-check trigger (the vote or enactment date, if known).
- A court ruling in a specific jurisdiction sets precedent for that jurisdiction only.
  Do not extrapolate globally without corroboration from other territory primary sources.
- A trade association position paper is Tier C — it represents member interests,
  not regulatory authority; cannot be used to support a CDM-1 classification.

### 5.3 Structural Watch Areas for AI Policy

These are persistent structural areas that warrant watch-list coverage:

- Statutory licensing proceedings that include or exclude AI-generated recordings.
- Platform policy pages covering AI-generated and AI-assisted content definitions.
- Legislation in major markets addressing AI in the context of copyright authorship.
- Court proceedings involving AI training data from music catalogs.
- PRO policies on collecting for AI-generated works.

Each area warrants a standing entry in the open-item log as DEVELOPING or CONFIRMED
(depending on the current policy state), with a re-check trigger keyed to the next
expected decision or policy review event.

## 6. Worked Routing: Streaming Economics Developments

A reference table for how common streaming economics developments route and classify.

| Development | CDM Class | Primary route | Secondary route |
|-------------|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Platform changes minimum stream length | CDM-1 | Distribution & Platform | Finance & Royalties, A&R |
| Platform shifts from pro-rata to user-centric payout | CDM-1 | Finance & Royalties | Distribution & Platform, Publishing & Rights |
| Platform adds new subscription tier at a higher price | CDM-2 | Finance & Royalties | Marketing |
| Chart authority revises SEA conversion rate | CDM-1 | Data & Analytics | A&R, Marketing |
| Chart authority adds a short-video platform to counting | CDM-1 | Data & Analytics | Marketing |
| Platform's subscriber growth decelerates in one territory (primary data) | CDM-2 | Data & Analytics | Marketing, Executive strategy |
| Platform's ARPU flat while subscriber count rises (earnings) | CDM-4 | Finance & Royalties | — |
| Court rules on AI training data licensing in one jurisdiction | CDM-1 (in jurisdiction) | Publishing & Rights | Legal, Finance & Royalties |
| Platform publishes AI-generated content policy | CDM-1 or CDM-2 (scope-dependent) | Publishing & Rights | Legal, Digital Operations |
| Short-video platform restructures music licensing fund | CDM-2 | Finance & Royalties | Publishing & Rights |
