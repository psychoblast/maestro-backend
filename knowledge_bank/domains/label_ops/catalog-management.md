# Catalog Management & Exploitation

Catalog is an actively managed business. The discipline is audit first, then
exploit; an unaudited catalog cannot be exploited safely because rights, metadata,
and revenue gaps are invisible until surfaced.

## The Catalog Audit Framework (Six Dimensions)

**Dimension 1 — Ownership & chain of title**
- Confirm master ownership per title: label-owned, artist-owned, licensed-in,
  co-owned.
- Confirm composition ownership: label-published, artist self-published,
  third-party publisher controlled.
- Flag titles with unclear or disputed ownership (acquisition candidates,
  terminated contracts, expired licenses) and any work-for-hire ambiguities.

**Dimension 2 — Rights registration status**
- DSP delivery confirmed live at all major platforms.
- ISRC registration confirmed for each recording.
- Performing-rights-organization registration: writer's share and publisher's
  share registered for every composition.
- Mechanical-collective registration (US streaming mechanicals).
- International mechanical-society registration in key territories.
- Content ID claims active for owned master recordings.

**Dimension 3 — Metadata quality** (per-title audit against current DSP specs):
title exactness vs. publishing registration; correct artist linking to verified
DSP pages; current and accurate genre tagging; complete producer/writer/featured
credits; highest-quality master delivered (remasters replacing inferior originals
where audio quality improved).

**Dimension 4 — Revenue yield analysis:** per-title streaming revenue over
trailing 12 and 36 months; performance-royalty receipts per title; sync revenue
per title; identify the highest-yield titles (the small fraction generating most
revenue — the exploitation priority) and zero-yield titles with latent potential.

**Dimension 5 — Sync history & placement log:** maintain an independent log of all
known placements (TV, film, ad, game, social campaign); cross-reference against
publisher sync statements — any logged placement missing from statements within 12
months is a revenue leak; identify sync-ready titles (instrumental and stems
available, sample-cleared).

**Dimension 6 — Reversion & termination risk:** US statutory termination rights
make many post-1977 copyright assignments terminable decades after assignment;
catalog with significant late-20th-century works needs documented
termination-window analysis. Contract reversion clauses (rights revert if the
label fails to release within a window) trigger automatically in many
jurisdictions — audit them proactively and maintain release cadence on
time-sensitive catalog.

## Catalog Exploitation Strategy Matrix

**Type 1 — DSP catalog optimization:** improve discoverability, metadata, and
placement of existing catalog — artist-profile optimization (current bio, photos,
verified status); catalog redelivery from best masters; metadata correction;
playlist pitching of high-performing titles in appropriate contexts; visual loops
on top titles; fan re-engagement campaigns pointing to DSP links.

**Type 2 — Re-release / remaster:** reissue with updated audio or packaging to
create a new release moment and editorial-pitch opportunity. Decision criteria: is
there genuine fidelity improvement (not just loudness)? Is there an anniversary or
press hook? Is the event sufficient to warrant an editorial pitch? A remaster that
is more than a minor loudness/EQ adjustment should receive a new ISRC to avoid
conflicts with existing streaming history and to delineate royalty attribution.

**Type 3 — Sync campaign:** proactively pitch catalog to supervisors, agencies,
platform music teams, and game developers. Sync-readiness requirements:
instrumental version available; stems available; metadata complete and verified;
all sample clearances documented; ownership chain clear and immediately
verifiable; no pending disputes. Pitch materials: a one-sheet per title (writer
credits, licensing contact, ownership, fee tier, sync history), streaming link,
instrumental link, stems on request.

**Type 4 — Catalog licensing:** license catalog in bulk for compilations, games,
platform exclusives, or adjacent creative uses. Fee structures: flat license fee
(common for games, apps, e-learning); revenue share (rare; long-tail uses);
per-use fee (advertising and broadcast).

**Type 5 — Sale or partial sale:** sell catalog or a portion to a catalog fund,
publisher, or financial buyer. Market context: catalog valuation multiples
compressed from the early-2020s peak as the interest-rate environment shifted;
buyers are more selective; trophy catalog (high sync history, evergreen appeal,
international reach) still commands the top of the range. Strategic partial sales —
selling part of the publishing rights while retaining administration — provide
liquidity without a full exit. Specific multiple ranges are NOT QUOTABLE without a
qualified valuation expert and current comparables.

## Metadata Governance Protocol

A living catalog requires ongoing governance, not a one-time audit.

**Quarterly review:** pull DSP analytics for the top tracks (trailing 90 days);
verify artist linking for all of them (any broken link or new name variant?);
cross-reference performance-registration data for new filings or amended splits;
check for DSP policy changes affecting metadata requirements.

**Triggered review events:** artist legal-name change (full re-registration at
DSPs, rights organizations, and the mechanical collective); artist death (estate
transition, rights-org notifications, profile management transfer); label
acquisition or merger (chain-of-title update across all systems); sample clearance
retroactively revoked (immediate takedown and re-delivery with a cleared version).

## Rights-Society Registration Hygiene

For each composition, registration completeness requires: the songwriter
registered with their performing-rights organization (writer's share); the
publisher registered as administrator of the publisher's share; an international
standard work code assigned; correct split percentages (summing to 100% for both
writer's and publisher's shares); alternate titles registered (covers, remixes,
interpolations).

**Mechanical-collective registration (US streaming mechanicals):** publishers must
register compositions with the mechanical collective to collect US streaming
mechanicals under the statutory blanket license. DSPs pay the collective monthly
in bulk; it matches usage to registered works, and unmatched royalties have
historically accumulated. **Practice implication:** any composition not registered
with the collective is receiving zero US streaming mechanicals regardless of how
well it streams — the single most common zero-cost revenue leak in catalog
management.

**Neighboring-rights registration** for owned masters (performance royalties for
broadcast and non-interactive streaming) is mandatory for collection but often
treated as optional. Many small-to-mid labels never register, leaving recurring
annual income uncollected. This is a systems failure: no one owns the standing
process. Assign it.

## The Catalog Reversion Clock

Many recording contracts include reversion clauses returning rights to the artist
if the label fails to exploit within a defined window. Common triggers: failure to
release within 6–24 months of delivery; failure to maintain commercial
availability; failure to account and pay within the required period. Every
recording agreement should be tracked against its reversion clock; risk is most
acute for catalog acquired in label transactions, where the acquiring label may
not know each title's clock.
