# Label Deal Structures & Recoupment

All rate ranges below are directional estimates unless tied to a named statutory
authority. Actual deal terms are private and vary significantly by artist
leverage, catalog size, and negotiating context. Any recoupability determination
for a specific deal requires review of that deal's actual contract language.

## Recording Contract Type Taxonomy

**1. Traditional recording agreement (full artist deal).** Label funds recording
costs, pays an advance, owns the master for a defined term (or in perpetuity), and
pays royalties against the advance and recording costs.
- **Advance:** an upfront recoupable payment — a loan against future royalties,
  not a gift; it must be earned back before further royalties are paid.
- **Royalty rate (new artist, directional):** roughly 13–18% of the applicable
  retail/streaming base before recoupment; may escalate via clauses tied to sales
  thresholds post-recoupment.
- **Controlled composition clause:** where the artist wrote the songs, the label
  typically pays mechanicals at 75% of statutory (the "three-quarter rate"),
  sometimes with a per-album cap on the number of controlled compositions. At
  scale this is a significant publishing-income reduction.
- **Term:** an initial period plus multiple label-held option periods (1+4, 1+5
  common). The artist is committed for all options if exercised; the label can opt
  out at each renewal.
- **Ownership:** label owns masters in perpetuity or for a license term;
  reversions are rare in major deals, more common in independent/licensing deals.
- **Delivery commitment:** a defined number of albums over the term; minimum
  delivery requirements define when label obligations are triggered.

**2. Multiple-rights ("360") deal.** A traditional deal extended to participation
in non-recording revenue — touring, merchandise, endorsements, sometimes
publishing. Directional participation ranges: touring 10–25% of net; merchandise
10–25% of net; endorsements/brand 10–20% of net; publishing participation less
common but possible for new artists with no leverage. **Decision rule:** a 360
deal is only rational if the label's marketing investment is projected to
materially increase the shared streams. If the label cannot point to specific
touring, merchandise, or brand services it provides, the participation is pure
extraction — artists with leverage should refuse or tie participation to specific
earned services.

**3. Production deal / vanity label.** A production company holds a deal with a
larger label and signs artists to itself, funding and delivering recordings while
retaining a portion of royalties. The artist's royalty flows from the production
company, which may keep 30–50% and pass through the rest; the artist typically has
no direct relationship with the larger label. Risk: an undercapitalized production
company can absorb most of an advance in overhead before any music is made.

**4. Distribution (label) deal.** An independent label licenses its releases to a
larger distribution entity for access, marketing support, and potentially
advances, while retaining masters and artist relationships. Directional revenue
share to the distributor is variable and not quotable; bundled services may
include physical distribution, DSP editorial relationships, and marketing
coordination. The most common deal for established independents wanting major
distribution infrastructure without surrendering catalog ownership.

**5. Licensing deal.** The label licenses specific recordings to another entity
for a defined term, territory, and use, retaining ownership. Appropriate for
territory-specific licensing, time-limited licenses with reversion, and
use-specific licenses (film compilation, platform exclusive).

## Royalty & Recoupment Mechanics

**The recoupment waterfall (recording-deal context):**
1. Label pays the advance — creating the recoupable balance.
2. Label pays recording costs; if recoupable (common), the balance grows.
3. Label may charge recoupable marketing costs (video, promotion, tour support)
   if the contract permits — the balance may grow further.
4. As the recording earns, the label applies royalties against the balance; the
   artist receives no cash until it reaches zero.
5. The royalty rate is applied to the label's net receipts to compute the credit
   against the balance.
6. When the balance reaches zero, the artist begins receiving royalty payments.

**Why most artists never recoup:** at a typical royalty rate and a six-figure
recoupable balance, a release must generate many multiples of that balance in
label receipts before any post-advance royalty is paid — a level most releases
never reach. This is not inherently predatory (labels carry risk on most releases
that fail commercially), but the artist must understand the math before signing.

## Cross-Collateralization — the Hidden Multiplier

Cross-collateralization offsets one album's unrecouped deficit against another
album's earned royalties. Example: Album 1 carries a $200K deficit; Album 2 earns
$300K. Without cross-collateralization the artist receives Album 2's earnings
(net of its own recoupment). With it, Album 1's deficit is applied first, leaving
far less — or nothing — for the artist. Cross-collateralization across unrelated
deals (recording vs. publishing) is especially dangerous, since publishing
royalties can be applied against recording-advance recoupment.

**Negotiation points:** limit cross-collateralization to albums within the same
deal cycle; prevent cross-deal cross-collateralization entirely; escalate any
clause that cross-collateralizes recording recoupment against publishing advances
to legal review.

## Controlled Composition Clause Mechanics

When an artist writes their own songs, the label pays mechanicals at a reduced
rate — typically 75% of statutory, sometimes capped per album regardless of track
count.

- **Statutory mechanical rate:** the US streaming mechanical is formula-based (a
  percentage of total service payments under the current statutory determination),
  not a flat per-track rate. For physical and download copies a per-copy statutory
  rate applies (cost-of-living adjusted). The three-quarter rate reduces the
  songwriter's share against either basis.
- **Impact:** across a meaningful catalog the difference between full statutory and
  the three-quarter rate compounds materially over years. The clause is
  negotiable; artists with leverage should seek full statutory or, at minimum, a
  per-album cap (e.g., up to 10 controlled compositions).

## Anti-Patterns

1. **Signing without a defined recoupment calculation basis.** If "recoupable
   costs" are undefined, the label's interpretation controls; marketing, video,
   independent radio promotion, and tour support can all become recoupable unless
   explicitly excluded. Every deal needs an explicit, comprehensive recoupable/
   non-recoupable list.
2. **Accepting cross-collateralization without understanding scope.** The common
   trap: option periods in the same deal cross-collateralized so a later album's
   royalties pay an earlier album's deficit. Limit it in the first negotiation
   round.
3. **Treating the controlled composition rate as non-negotiable.** It is
   negotiable, and at scale the difference is large.
4. **Ignoring the option-period structure.** The label holds the options; an
   unrecouped artist can be stuck delivering albums to a label that will not
   promote them. Escape clauses and guaranteed-release commitments are the
   counterweights.
5. **Accepting tour support as recoupable without a marketing quid pro quo.** Tour
   support is almost always recoupable; without a defined tour-marketing
   commitment the artist is subsidizing their own touring with their own future
   royalties.

## Practitioner-Layer Insight

- **"Most favored nations" clauses in multi-track releases:** when multiple
  rights-holders are involved (compilation, various-artists project), MFN language
  can require each party's fee to match the highest fee paid to any party. Agreeing
  a flat fee with one licensor without checking others' MFN clauses can inadvertently
  raise everyone's fees. Track MFN across all parties in any multi-artist project.
- **The "pipeline" issue at contract expiration:** recordings delivered but not yet
  released at expiration may be subject to pipeline provisions letting the label
  release them within a post-term window. Ensure the pipeline window is explicitly
  defined and time-limited (6–12 months maximum) in any recording deal.
