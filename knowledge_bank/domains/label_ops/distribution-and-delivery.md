# Distribution & Delivery

The distribution choice is strategic, not a utility decision. It carries long-term
consequences for royalty flow, advance availability, editorial relationships, and
recoupment mechanics.

## Distributor Tier Taxonomy

The landscape has three structural tiers.

**Tier 1 — DIY aggregators (self-serve, subscription or per-release pricing).**
Artists and small labels retain full control, pay upfront fees, and keep all (or
nearly all) royalties. Appropriate for emerging artists without catalog history,
labels testing markets, and releases with no editorial ambitions. Representative
services operate on annual-subscription or per-release flat-fee models. Note that
some aggregators marketed as "100% royalties" in fact take a percentage of
streaming royalties — verify the current terms before recommending any service.

**NOT QUOTABLE — distributor fee/royalty percentages.** Specific
royalty-retention or fee percentages for any named distributor are private, vary
by negotiation and date, and are not quotable as fact. Describe each distributor's
economic *model* directionally — flat annual fee, subscription, or
royalty-percentage concession — never assert a specific percentage, even when
comparing options.

**Tier 2 — Mid-tier distribution partners (applied/curated access, white-glove
service).** Companies that require application or relationship, offer DSP
editorial relationships, may advance against streaming, and take a royalty
percentage rather than a flat fee — without taking master ownership. Appropriate
for developing artists with catalog, independent labels with revenue, and artists
needing editorial access. Distinguishing features across this tier: revenue-share
economics, data-driven advance facilities, editorial relationships, and varying
A&R / marketing / playlist support. Several are owned by or affiliated with major
music groups; some remain genuinely independent.

**Tier 3 — Full-service distribution (label deal, full support, revenue share or
ownership).** Major-label distribution arms and full-service independent network
distributors. Appropriate for established artists, large catalogs, and releases
requiring major marketing infrastructure. Some are owned by the major groups;
some are independent full-service operations with their own label-services model.

## Distribution Selection Decision Matrix

**Gate 1 — Catalog volume and revenue scale**
- < 5 releases, < $10K annual streaming revenue → DIY aggregator.
- 5–30 releases, $10K–$100K → mid-tier partner evaluation warranted.
- 30+ releases, > $100K → full-service partner or major distribution deal.

**Gate 2 — Editorial ambition**
- None → any tier works; cost optimization is the primary variable.
- Moderate (editorial consideration, mid-tier curators) → mid-tier with
  editorial relationships.
- High (new-music features, platform exclusives) → mid-tier or full-service with
  documented DSP editorial relationships.

**Gate 3 — Advance needs**
- None → DIY or mid-tier; avoid advance-dependent structures.
- Advance against existing catalog → mid-tier or full-service with an advance
  facility.
- Advances are recoupable and represent a real obligation; advance sizing should
  not exceed roughly 2–3 years of current annual streaming revenue on the catalog
  securing it (directional rule; actual advance market terms are not quotable).

**Gate 4 — Control and ownership**
- Must retain all masters and royalties → DIY aggregator only.
- Willing to share a royalty percentage for services and advance → mid-tier or
  full-service.
- Open to a label relationship → full-service distribution deal or label deal.

**Practitioner rule:** the most common distribution mistake for labels in the
$50K–$300K annual streaming range is staying on DIY aggregators to "keep 100%"
while leaving editorial relationships, advance access, and marketing support on
the table. The net value of a mid-tier distributor's editorial relationships often
exceeds the royalty percentage conceded. This is a calculation, not a default.

## DSP Delivery Technical Standards

**Audio**
- WAV or FLAC, 44.1kHz or 48kHz, 24-bit depth minimum.
- Mastered for digital: integrated loudness around -14 LUFS for streaming
  normalization; true-peak ceiling ≤ -1 dBTP.
- Do not deliver lossy masters — DSPs transcode from WAV/FLAC; a lossy master
  degrades final quality and may be rejected by aggregators.

**Metadata (per track — all required)**
- ISRC (12-character) — must match exactly across all DSP registrations.
- Track title — must match publishing registration exactly (case-sensitive
  matching is enforced for royalty attribution).
- Main artist, featuring artists (separate fields) — correct DSP linking depends
  on exact name matching.
- Album/EP/single title; release date; genre (primary, secondary); explicit flag
  (E / C / blank); language; producer and songwriter credits; UPC (per release).

**Artwork**
- JPEG or PNG; sRGB color space (NOT CMYK — CMYK is print and is rejected).
- Minimum 3000×3000px, square (1:1); ~72 dpi display resolution.
- May not contain text URLs, phone numbers, misleading release dates/type labels,
  or imagery violating platform standards.

**Delivery lead times**
- Distributor-to-DSP transmission: same day to 48 hours after a complete,
  approved package is received.
- DSP processing to live: 1–3 business days on the largest platforms, longer
  (3–5 business days) on some. These are stated minimums, not guarantees;
  holiday periods, backlogs, and content flags extend them.
- Recommended operational lead time: submit at least 21 days before release; 28+
  days for editorial pitch viability; 42+ days for album-level campaigns.

## Delivery Failure Playbook

1. **Identify rejection type:** distributor-side (before DSP receipt) vs. DSP-side
   (after receipt, before live).
2. **Common distributor rejections:** missing/invalid ISRC; artwork off-spec
   (CMYK, wrong dimensions, prohibited text); wrong audio format/sample rate;
   missing required metadata; duplicate UPC already in system.
3. **Common DSP rejections:** content-policy violation (misleading title,
   unauthorized art); explicit-flag mismatch; duplicate content (same ISRC as an
   existing release); artist-name conflict with an existing DSP page.
4. **Resolution SLA:** distributor rejection — correct and resubmit, ~24–48h to
   fix, re-delivery and re-processing add 3–5 business days; DSP-side correction —
   route through distributor support, 3–7 business days.
5. **Campaign-impact mitigation:** if correction + re-delivery pushes within 7
   days of release, prefer moving the release date over releasing with incomplete
   metadata; if the editorial window is missed, pitch at delivery confirmation
   anyway — a late pitch still reads in the system; document the root cause for
   prevention.

## Structural Shift: Distribution as Financial Infrastructure

The distribution sector has repositioned from "utility pipe" to financial and
IP-management infrastructure:
- Data-driven advance facilities make catalog-backed financing a distribution
  feature rather than a separate banking relationship.
- Distributors increasingly handle tax filing, international withholding, and VAT
  compliance as part of the service.
- Rights-hygiene tooling, attribution infrastructure, and distribution are
  converging, increasingly inside major-aligned entities.

**Practice implication:** choosing a distributor today is choosing a financial
partner, not just a delivery vehicle. The advance facility, rights-management
tooling, and data access can matter as much as the royalty percentage.

## Editorial Independence Consideration

Ownership concentration means several "independent" distributors are in fact
major-owned subsidiaries. DSP editorial teams are aware of ownership structure
when interacting with major-owned distribution arms. This is not universally
disqualifying, but labels that require documented editorial independence from
major-label influence should model their choice accordingly; true editorial
independence is concentrated in a shrinking pool.
