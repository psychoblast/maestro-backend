# Radio & Broadcast Promotion

Radio promotion is a paid-service function distinct from DSP editorial pitching.
The two are frequently conflated, but they serve different distribution channels,
run on different timelines, and carry different cost structures. Radio promotion
reaches broadcast audiences; editorial pitching reaches streaming-platform
recommendation systems. Both can be in a campaign plan; neither substitutes for
the other.

## Format Taxonomy

Terrestrial radio is organized by format, and format determines the station pool
that can receive a promotion campaign. Genre-appropriate placement is the first
filter; a song that suits one format may be entirely wrong for another.

| Format | Common name | Core audience | Style notes |
|--------|-------------|---------------|-------------|
| Contemporary Hit Radio | CHR / Top 40 | Broad 18–34 | High-tempo pop and crossover; driven by current chart singles |
| Hot Adult Contemporary | Hot AC | 25–44 | Rhythmic pop and singer-songwriter; accessible crossover |
| Adult Contemporary | AC | 30–55 | Mainstream; softer; established artists; ballad-friendly |
| Adult Album Alternative | AAA / Triple A | 25–54 | Album-oriented; independent; Americana-adjacent; story-driven |
| Country | Country | Rural / suburban 18–49 | Mainstream country and country-pop crossover |
| Urban / Rhythmic | Urban | 18–35 | Hip-hop, R&B, and rhythmic pop |
| Americana | Americana | 35–55 | Roots, folk, country-adjacent; often college-radio adjacent |
| College / non-commercial | CMJ / Freeform | 18–25 | Broad; independent; format-agnostic |
| Rock | Rock | 18–44 | Active Rock, Alternative Rock, Classic Rock — distinct sub-formats |

A song can be promoted across multiple formats when genuine crossover potential
exists, but splitting campaign budget across formats dilutes momentum at any single
one. Concentrating on the primary format appropriate for the song is the default
decision; a crossover campaign is a secondary decision requiring dedicated budget
for each format separately.

## Independent Radio Promoters — Mechanics

"Independent radio promotion" (indie promo) is a paid advocacy service. An
independent promoter is not affiliated with any radio station; they maintain
sustained professional relationships with music directors (MDs) and program
directors (PDs) at the stations within their format and territory, and they
advocate for the songs assigned to them during the weekly add cycle.

**What indie promo is:**
- A weekly campaign cycle where the promoter contacts MDs/PDs to advocate for
  an "add" (the station officially adding the song to its rotation).
- A reporting mechanism: the promoter tracks station-by-station status (added,
  passed, pending, awaiting next week) and surfaces feedback from the field.
- An intelligence feed: experienced promoters surface competitive-week context —
  whether a given week is crowded with competing releases, whether a specific
  market is resistant to the genre, whether a particular MD's format priorities
  have shifted.

**What indie promo is not:**
- Payola: any quid pro quo payment to a station employee in exchange for airplay
  is prohibited under FCC regulations and unlawful. Indie promo is paid to the
  promoter for relationship-based advocacy; the station employee receives no
  undisclosed compensation.
- A guarantee: no legitimate promoter can guarantee chart positions or adds at
  specific stations. Any promoter representing a guaranteed outcome is
  misrepresenting the service.
- Interchangeable with DSP editorial pitching: a Spotify editorial pitch and a
  CHR radio campaign are different channels, different timelines, and different
  audiences. A DSP chart add does not generate radio momentum; a radio add does
  not generate DSP algorithmic signals.

## Add Dates and the Weekly Cycle

Most major formats operate on a consistent weekly add cycle. Adds registered
within the cycle's window count toward the current week's tally; late adds roll
to the next week, which affects chart position timing.

| Format | Typical add day | Add window |
|--------|----------------|------------|
| CHR / Top 40 | Monday | Monday–Tuesday |
| Hot AC | Monday | Monday–Tuesday |
| AC | Monday | Monday–Tuesday |
| AAA / Triple A | Tuesday | Tuesday–Wednesday |
| Country | Monday | Monday–Tuesday |
| Urban | Monday or Tuesday | Monday–Tuesday |
| Americana | Staggered | Varies; no single add day |

**First-week add count as a leading indicator.** The number and market weight of
adds in the first week signals campaign traction to the rest of the format. A
concentration of adds at high-audience stations (large markets; high-rated stations
in the format) drives more chart impact than the same number of adds spread across
small-market stations, because chart methodologies weight stations by audience.

## Mediabase and BDS — Airplay Monitoring

Two independent services track commercial radio airplay in the US:

**Mediabase** (owned by iHeart Media): monitors a panel of commercial stations
across all formats; the primary data source for most major music-industry radio
charts (Billboard, Luminate). Covers thousands of monitored stations.

**BDS — Broadcast Data Systems** (Nielsen / Luminate): a competing monitoring
system with a separately composed station panel. Some chart publications use BDS
exclusively; others use Mediabase; others blend data from both services.

**Practice implication — panel alignment:** for a charting campaign, confirm which
monitoring service (Mediabase, BDS, or both) is used by the target chart
publication and verify that the format's key stations are in that panel. A song
can receive significant airplay at unmonitored stations and fail to appear on the
chart. Promoter and label must confirm panel coverage before committing spend to
stations outside the tracked universe.

**Spins** are the number of times a song is played on monitored stations per week.
Adds are a one-time event at each station; spins are the cumulative ongoing
measure. A station that adds a song in Week 1 then programs it multiple times per
day contributes spins to the chart total every week the song remains in active
rotation.

## Chart Position — Methodology and Commercial Significance

Radio charts (Billboard Airplay charts, format-specific charts) rank songs by
weighted audience impressions — spins multiplied by each station's estimated
audience-per-quarter-hour. A song played 12 times at a 500,000-AQH station
generates more chart impact than 30 spins at a 30,000-AQH station.

**What chart position signals commercially:**
- A chart bullet (charting and rising week-over-week) is a verifiable commercial
  signal that is visible to retail buyers, DSP editorial teams, sync supervisors,
  booking agents, and brand partners. Unlike streaming metrics, which can be
  inflated, monitored airplay is independently verified.
- Charting at a format is a qualifying credential for follow-up records, for
  securing a mid-tier or full-service distribution upgrade, and for attracting
  co-writing and production collaborators.
- Radio chart adds and positions appear in trade publications (Billboard, Music
  Row, Mediabase charts) — providing industry visibility independent of DSP
  metrics.

**What chart position does not signal:**
- Chart position does not translate directly to a predictable streaming volume
  or ticket-sales increase; the correlation exists at the format level but is
  not reliable at the individual artist level.
- A chart position at a secondary or specialty format carries different weight
  than CHR or Urban; format, market tier, and chart level all context the signal.

## Satellite Radio — Key Distinctions

SiriusXM is a separate medium from terrestrial broadcast. Key operational
distinctions:

- **Audience:** national subscriber base; no local market geography.
- **Programming structure:** SiriusXM channels program independently under their
  own channel directors; no formal weekly add-date cycle analogous to terrestrial
  format. Promoters work the same relationship model but with SiriusXM channel
  contacts specifically.
- **Performance royalties:** SiriusXM pays performance royalties to SoundExchange
  for master recordings (at statutory rates set by the Copyright Royalty Board)
  and to PROs for compositions. Labels must be registered with SoundExchange to
  collect SiriusXM master performance royalties; many small labels miss this
  revenue stream because SoundExchange registration is never completed.
- **Chart impact:** SiriusXM airplay does not feed terrestrial radio chart panels;
  it has its own chart ecosystem. A SiriusXM add advances visibility but does not
  move a terrestrial format chart position.

## Campaign Economics and Decision Framework

A national radio campaign at a major format (CHR, AC, Urban) carries a weekly
retainer to the promoter that, when sustained over the required campaign arc, can
represent a significant share of a label's total release budget. Regional campaigns
targeting specific markets cost proportionally less. College and AAA campaigns are
lower-cost relative to CHR or AC.

**Specific promoter fees and campaign costs are NOT QUOTABLE** as they are
privately negotiated, market-variable, and promoter-specific. Get written proposals
from two or more promoters before committing; treat any quoted figure as a
starting point for negotiation.

**Decision framework:**
```
IF the song is demonstrably format-appropriate (verified by format-experienced
   professionals; not self-assessed by the artist or label owner) AND
   the artist has catalog support (prior recordings a radio listener can discover
   after hearing the promoted single) AND
   budget is available for at least 6 weeks of campaign activity:
  → radio campaign is potentially appropriate; evaluate specific budget vs. upside

IF budget covers fewer than 6 weeks at the required format:
  → do NOT start a national format campaign; a compressed run rarely generates
    sufficient add momentum to chart; hold budget for the next release or commit
    to the full arc

IF the artist has zero DSP streaming history:
  → prioritize DSP campaigns first; radio adds at stations that drive listeners
    to streaming platforms need a streaming destination that can be found easily

IF no prior format radio campaign history:
  → begin regionally; test at smaller-market stations before committing to national
    spend; a credible regional result is the basis for expanding to national

IF format campaign costs exceed 30% of total release budget:
  → flag to label head; rebalance or defer radio to a subsequent single
```

**Campaign arc — single format, regional build:**
1. **Weeks 1–2:** brief and launch with promoter; first adds from highest-
   relationship stations; confirm monitoring-service panel alignment.
2. **Weeks 3–6:** build add count; promoter submits adds for chart-monitoring
   registration; track spins weekly alongside adds; flag non-responsive stations.
3. **Weeks 7–10:** chart position consolidation if sufficient adds achieved;
   secondary adds from mid-market stations; coordinate DSP content campaign to
   capture streaming conversion from radio listeners.
4. **Week 10+:** assess chart trajectory; continue only if the chart position is
   rising or holding with meaningful weekly spins. A stalled campaign with no new
   adds and flat spins should be stopped; an experienced promoter will say this
   before the label asks.

## Practitioner-Layer Insight

- **Adds without spins are a warning sign.** A station can log an add and then
  program the song infrequently — a "passive add" that builds the add count without
  generating meaningful listener exposure. Review spins data weekly, not just the
  add count. A large add count with low aggregate spins indicates that stations are
  not actively programming the track, which limits chart impact and audience reach.
- **Promoter territory exclusivity matters.** Most indie promoters operate in
  specific format territories; engaging multiple promoters on the same territory
  creates relationship conflicts at the stations and poisons MDs against both
  promoters. Confirm territorial scope and exclusivity terms with any promoter
  before engagement.
- **Radio and sync amplify each other.** A song that charts at a major format
  gains credibility for sync pitching — sync supervisors monitor radio for
  commercially proven material. A high-profile sync placement can revive a radio
  campaign by introducing the song to new audiences. Label ops should coordinate
  radio promotion timing and sync pitching as complementary activities, not
  siloed campaigns.
- **Recoupability of indie promo costs.** Confirm in the artist deal whether
  independent radio promotion costs are classified as recoupable against the
  artist's royalty account. Some contracts class promo costs as recoupable if they
  yield a chart position; many independent deals treat them as non-recoupable label
  overhead. The classification is a contractual determination, not an industry
  default; confirm it before committing to a campaign.
