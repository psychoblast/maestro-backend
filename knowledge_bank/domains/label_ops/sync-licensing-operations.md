# Sync Licensing Operations

Sync licensing is the process of clearing music for use in audio-visual contexts:
film, television, advertising, video games, trailers, social content. It is the
highest-margin per-placement revenue source in label operations and among the most
operationally intensive — requiring fast response times, pre-cleared catalog, and
a pitch infrastructure that most small labels build reactively rather than
proactively. Labels that build the infrastructure before placement requests arrive
win meaningfully more placements than those that scramble to respond.

## The Dual-License Requirement

Every sync placement requires two independent licenses, each negotiated and
executed separately:

**1. Synchronization (sync) license** — permission to use the *musical
composition* (melody and lyrics) synchronized with picture. Granted by the
publisher or, for self-published writers, the writer/composer directly. Without
this license, no placement can proceed regardless of master status.

**2. Master use license** — permission to use the *specific recording* synchronized
with picture. Granted by the master rights holder — typically the label or, for
independently released artists, the artist themselves. Without this, the supervisor
must either use a different recording of the same composition or choose a different
song.

**Both must be independently cleared.** A placement where the publisher has agreed
but the master holder has not — or the reverse — cannot proceed. This is the most
common cause of placement failure at the clearance stage: one party agrees quickly
and the other delays or declines.

**One-stop clearance** (where a single entity controls both master and publishing)
eliminates the dual-clearance coordination problem and is significantly preferred
by music supervisors operating on production timelines. A one-stop issues both
licenses in a single negotiation at one fee, in one round of communication. Label
ops should clearly communicate one-stop status — or the publishing contact chain
when it is not one-stop — at the first point of contact with any sync inquiry.

## Placement Type Taxonomy and Directional Fee Structure

Sync fees are privately negotiated; no statutory rate exists for sync licenses.
The ranges below are directional; specific amounts for any context depend on
the artist's profile, the track's commercial history, the placement's prominence
and territory, the licensor's negotiating leverage, and current market conditions.
Specific fee amounts are NOT QUOTABLE.

| Placement type | Directional tier | Key fee variables | Notes |
|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|-------|
| Theatrical film — major studio | High | Territory, exclusivity, scene prominence | Festival holds precede theatrical deal; negotiate full-theatrical separately |
| Theatrical film — independent | Mid–low | Distribution confirmed vs. festival only; budget tier | Deferred payment on distribution deal is common |
| TV / streaming series (premium) | Mid–high | Network tier, episode vs. season option, territory | Series options (all episodes) cost more than episodic; streaming-original rates vary |
| National broadcast advertising | High | Campaign length, exclusivity, total media spend | Exclusivity period is the largest fee driver; 3-month vs. 12-month is a large spread |
| Digital-only advertising | Mid | Duration, platform scope, impression volume | Below national broadcast; above social/influencer |
| Trailer / theatrical promo | High | Feature-trailer vs. TV-spot; franchise scale | Trailers command 2–5× the underlying film sync rate in many cases |
| Video game | Mid | Prominence (featured on menu vs. background); buy-out vs. backend | Buy-out (one-time fee, in perpetuity in context) is standard for games |
| Social / influencer content | Low–gratis | Platform, creator audience, brand vs. organic | Brand-paid influencer content is a separate clearance from organic creator use |
| Non-commercial (documentary, student, festival) | Gratis–low | Distribution plans; completion guarantee | Deferred fee upon commercial distribution is an appropriate counter to gratis request |

## Gratis Use Protocol

A gratis request is when a supervisor requests use of music without a fee — for a
temp track, a low-budget project, a festival-only documentary, or "in exchange for
exposure." Gratis approval requires explicit criteria because it establishes a
precedent and consumes catalog inventory without generating revenue.

```
IF the request is for a TEMP TRACK only (will be replaced before picture lock):
  → gratis approval is appropriate; confirm in writing that temp-use approval
    does NOT bind the label to any terms for the final placement, which requires a
    separate negotiation when the scene is locked

IF the request is permanent use in a PROJECT WITH CONFIRMED DISTRIBUTION:
  → evaluate the platform and audience against the profile value
  → gratis for a confirmed major streaming platform original is potentially
    acceptable as a profile investment; assess upside (backend royalties, visibility)
  → counter with deferred fee (triggered on confirmed commercial distribution)
    rather than unconditional gratis unless the benefit is clear and specific

IF the request is "in lieu of a fee" from a COMMERCIAL ENTITY (paid broadcast,
agency, brand):
  → decline gratis; the commercial entity is generating revenue from the placement;
    a fee negotiation is appropriate regardless of "exposure" framing

IF the request is "exposure" from a small-audience channel or unconfirmed project:
  → decline gratis; route to a nominal fee with favorable escalation terms if the
    project gains distribution
```

**Practice rule:** exposure is not compensation. Any gratis approval where the
commercial case cannot be stated specifically and verifiably should default to a
minimum-fee license rather than no-fee. Setting gratis precedents with commercial
entities weakens the label's negotiating position for future placements.

## The Pitch-to-Placement Pipeline

```
Stage 1 — Incoming brief:
  Supervisor or agency sends a brief: genre, tempo, mood, scene context, budget
  range, and timeline. Respond within 24 hours; a supervisor on a deadline will
  move to the next option. A 48-hour non-response is effectively a decline.

Stage 2 — Pitch selection:
  Select 2–5 tracks that fit the brief from catalog. More than 5 dilutes focus;
  supervisors prefer a curated, considered selection over a catalog dump.
  Provide: streaming link (lossless quality), HQ download link, stems availability
  note, ISRC, one-stop status, and a one-line editorial note per track (why this
  track for this brief).

Stage 3 — Quote and terms:
  When the supervisor expresses interest, provide a fee indication or a request for
  details (territory, term, prominence, exclusivity duration). Anchor at the top of
  the appropriate tier; it is easier to negotiate down than up. Document any verbal
  agreement immediately in writing.

Stage 4 — Negotiation and clearance confirmation:
  Confirm publishing status and contact chain to the supervisor before negotiation
  concludes. If not one-stop, coordinate the publisher quote in parallel; delays
  from the publishing side will lose the placement. Agree master and sync fees
  separately; document both in writing before preparing license documents.

Stage 5 — License execution:
  Short-form sync and master use licenses are appropriate for most routine
  placements (TV, advertising, independent film). Complex placements (major
  theatrical, franchise, video game catalog deal) warrant full-form licenses
  reviewed by label counsel. Confirm: territory; term (episodic, series, in
  perpetuity in context); exclusivity and exclusivity duration; permitted use
  scope; fee and payment schedule; and credit requirements.

Stage 6 — Invoice and payment collection:
  Issue the master use license invoice on execution. Payment terms: net-30 to
  net-60 is standard for independent productions; major studios sometimes pay
  net-90 or upon confirmed delivery acceptance. Include a late payment provision
  and the direct contact for the production's clearance accounting department.

Stage 7 — Backend collection (broadcast/streaming):
  For TV and streaming-series placements, performance royalties (backend) are
  generated each time the placement airs or streams. For the composition: PRO
  (ASCAP/BMI/SESAC) payment to publisher and writer, arriving 6–18 months after
  initial air date for US; 12–36 months for international. For the master:
  SoundExchange performance royalties (non-interactive streaming) if applicable.
  Register each placement with the PRO for the performance to be credited correctly;
  PROs do not proactively attribute placements to unregistered works.
```

## Clearance Speed as Competitive Advantage

In sync licensing, the fastest credible response wins when supervisors face
production deadlines. A 24-hour non-response to an inquiry is functionally a
decline; a 48-hour response to a time-critical request is too late for many
production contexts.

**Operational requirements for fast clearance:**
- A named sync contact with decision authority, reachable during business hours
  via direct phone and email — not a general inquiries address.
- Pre-authorized fee tiers by placement type and use category, so the sync contact
  can issue a quote immediately without escalation to the label head.
- Standard short-form license templates adapted to the label's catalog context,
  ready to issue without counsel involvement for routine placements.
- Stems for all active sync catalog, accessible for same-day digital delivery.
- A current one-sheet for every sync-ready title (see format below).
- Pre-confirmed publishing chain: for every catalog title, know immediately whether
  the label is one-stop or who the publishing contact is and their direct contact
  details.

**Pre-clearance audit requirement:** before pitching any title, confirm:
(1) master ownership is clear and documented; (2) publishing status is confirmed;
(3) sample clearance status is documented — sampled titles without clearance
cannot be licensed for sync; (4) stems are available. Any title failing this check
should be excluded from the active pitch catalog until the gap is resolved.

## One-Sheet Format

A sync one-sheet is a single-page pitch document for one track (or a small themed
group). Every sync-active title should have an up-to-date one-sheet maintained as
part of standard catalog management.

**Required elements:**
1. Track title, artist name, album/single name, release year
2. Writer(s) and producer(s) credits with performing rights affiliation
3. BPM, musical key, duration (exact timing, mm:ss)
4. Mood and genre keywords — minimum 5–8, specific: "midtempo anthemic indie rock,
   major key, female vocal, builds to full band chorus, warm analog production"
   rather than just "rock, uplifting"
5. Notable prior placements, if any: show name, network, episode (if known);
   confirms commercial viability; do not fabricate
6. Master and sync availability — clear statement: "Master and publishing available
   one-stop: [label name]" or "Master: [label]; Publishing: [publisher contact]"
7. Licensing contact: full name, direct email, direct phone — not an office main line
8. Streaming link (HD quality; direct-to-track URL, not the album page)
9. HQ download link and stems note: "Stems available on request" with the stem
   types (vocal, instrumental, TV mix, acapella)
10. ISRC (facilitates monitoring and rights matching)

**One-sheet production discipline:** one page is a hard constraint. Supervisors
receive large volumes of pitches; a two-page sheet signals unfamiliarity with the
medium. Specificity in the mood and context description is the differentiator —
generic mood words ("happy," "inspiring") are useless; specific sonic and emotional
references give the supervisor a mental preview.

## Stems Management

Stems (separated mix elements: vocal, instrumental, drum-and-bass, melodic beds,
TV mix, acapella) are required for advertising, trailer, and game placements, and
increasingly requested for TV placements. Labels without stems for their active
catalog lose placements to competitors who have them.

**Stems retention policy:**
- For all new releases: require the mixing engineer to deliver a labeled stems
  package at the point of master delivery. Stems at full session resolution
  (24-bit/48kHz minimum); labeled consistently (artist_title_stems_ELEMENT.wav).
- For existing catalog without stems: commission retroactively for titles with
  active sync potential or prior placement history; retroactive stems from the
  original mix engineer (if available and willing) are significantly cheaper than
  the lost placements from not having them.
- Storage: maintain stems in a dedicated, labeled catalog storage separate from
  distribution delivery assets. Stems must be findable by the sync contact within
  minutes of a request — a 24-hour wait for stems is a de facto decline.

## Income Collection and Timing

Sync licensing generates multiple income streams with different collection timelines:

| Income type | Who collects | Typical lag |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Master use sync fee | Label invoices directly | Net-30 to net-90 from license execution |
| Composition sync fee | Publisher invoices directly | Net-30 to net-90 from license execution |
| US PRO backend (composition) | ASCAP/BMI/SESAC → publisher/writer | 6–18 months after initial air date |
| International PRO backend | Sub-publishers / PRO → via publisher | 12–36 months after air date |
| SoundExchange (non-interactive streaming, master) | SoundExchange → label | Quarterly; 6–12 month lag |

**Practice discipline:** register every sync placement with the relevant PRO
at the time of license execution. Include the production title, episode details
(for TV), and air date. PRO matching is imperfect; manual registration of known
placements improves attribution rates and ensures backend income is directed
correctly.

## Practitioner-Layer Insight

- **The sample trap in sync.** A catalog title containing an uncleared sample
  cannot be licensed for sync without also clearing the sample with the original
  rights holders — who must independently grant sync permission, usually at a fee
  that erodes or eliminates the placement's economic value. Any title with a
  sample that was not cleared for "all uses" (including sync) is effectively
  excluded from the active sync catalog until that clearance is obtained. Maintain
  a sample-status flag for every title; never pitch a sampled title without full
  disclosure of its sample-clearance status.
- **Backend royalties are the durable income; the sync fee is the immediate
  payment.** A well-placed TV cue in a long-running streaming series can generate
  PRO income for a decade as the show is re-licensed internationally and continues
  to stream. Label ops should track backend collection proactively; the fee is paid
  and done, but the backend requires registration and ongoing receipt management.
- **Music supervisor relationships are long-term assets.** There is no formal
  blacklist, but a label that provides slow response times, misrepresents
  publishing ownership, delivers stems that do not match the delivered recording,
  or fails clearance over an unresolved sample will simply stop receiving briefs
  from the supervisors who experienced those failures. A single failed placement
  over misrepresented ownership can close a relationship for years. Integrity in
  the pitch process — acknowledging what the catalog can and cannot do — is a
  commercial asset, not just an ethical standard.
- **International sub-sync and territorial fee scope.** When a production will
  distribute internationally, confirm whether the sync license is US-only or
  worldwide. A worldwide license commands materially higher fees than US-only;
  misquoting a US-only rate for a worldwide franchise placement leaves significant
  money on the table. Territorial scope should be confirmed at the brief stage,
  not at license execution.
