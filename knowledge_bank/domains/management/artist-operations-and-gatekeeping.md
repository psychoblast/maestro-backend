# Day-to-Day Artist Operations and Gatekeeping

The gap between what artists think their manager does and what management actually
requires is a primary source of conflict. The deal moments are visible; the
unglamorous coordination work that makes them possible is not. Operational
discipline — gatekeeping, triage, and a clear approval structure — is the
foundation on which strategy runs.

## 1. What Actually Fills a Manager's Day

Artists tend to assume the manager spends their time negotiating deals, taking
label and brand meetings, and calling powerful contacts. What actually dominates a
working manager's day:

- Filtering and responding to inbound inquiries (show offers, brand inquiries,
  press requests, collaboration requests) — most of which will be declined.
- Coordinating between team members to prevent decisions from falling through the
  gaps.
- Chasing information that team members or label contacts have not provided
  (royalty statements, tour settlements, marketing plans, campaign timelines).
- Managing the artist's schedule and protecting studio time, personal time, and
  creative space from professional demands.
- Handling the crisis of the week — logistical, interpersonal, or professional.
- Reviewing and routing documents for attorney sign-off.
- Communicating decisions and status to the artist.

**Practitioner insight:** a manager visible to the artist primarily during deal
moments is providing an incomplete picture. The coordination work — chasing
receipts, confirming itineraries, aligning a publicist and a label marketing team
who are not talking — is the operational foundation. Artists who don't see it
underestimate the management value-add; managers who don't do it provide only
strategic counsel, not full-service management.

## 2. Approval Funnel

Not every decision requires artist approval; a manager who routes everything to the
artist creates a bottleneck rather than protecting the artist's time. The approval
funnel defines what requires explicit sign-off versus what the manager executes
within standing authorization.

- **Artist approval required (always):** any legally binding agreement; any
  commitment above a pre-agreed fee threshold; any major career announcement; any
  media appearance of strategic significance; any financial commitment above a
  pre-agreed discretionary level; any decision affecting creative output (release
  timing, sequencing, featuring decisions with positioning implications).
- **Manager discretion within standing authorization:** show offers below the
  minimum fee (decline automatically within agreed parameters); routine press
  inquiries (decline or refer to publicist within agreed strategy); inbound
  collaborations that clearly don't fit positioning; logistical coordination within
  approved parameters; routine label check-ins (attend and report, no decisions).
- **Manager handles without artist involvement:** industry relationship
  maintenance; administrative correspondence; inbound from parties outside the
  artist's professional scope (fan mail routing, non-professional solicitations).

The funnel is calibrated with each artist at the start of the relationship and
documented in a working memo that can be updated as the relationship develops. An
artist who wants to be consulted on everything needs a different structure than one
who wants involvement only in major decisions.

## 3. Gatekeeping Protocol

The artist's time, bandwidth, and creative energy are finite; the industry's
demands on them are effectively infinite. The manager is the primary filter.

The gatekeeper controls **direct access to the artist** (who can reach the artist
and about what — an A&R executive who uses the artist's cell to short-circuit the
manager creates accumulating coordination problems), **inbound opportunity flow**
(triaging to only the opportunities that deserve artist attention), and **narrative
and information context** (presenting information with context, not raw data — "the
offer came in below our minimum, and I declined it" is better than the anxiety of
"someone offered you a show and I turned it down" or the unnecessary load of "I got
an offer, what do you think?").

### Gatekeeping Anti-Patterns

- **Under-gatekeeping (the sycophant manager):** presenting every flattering
  opportunity because the manager wants to deliver good news. The artist gets
  excited about opportunities that don't fit the strategy, and the manager's
  credibility as a filter erodes.
- **Over-gatekeeping (the controlling manager):** filtering so aggressively that
  the artist is unaware of major developments affecting them. Surprises at the
  artist level are usually management failures.
- **Inconsistent access boundaries:** tight control in some contexts and loose in
  others, confusing team members and industry contacts about how to reach the
  artist.

## 4. Inbound Request Triage Matrix

Every inbound request falls into a category that determines routing, response time,
and whether the artist is involved.

- **Legal / agreement** — immediate; artist involved before any engagement;
  respond same or next business day.
- **Major opportunity** (major label interest, major brand, major festival) —
  high; brief the artist before responding; respond within a couple of business
  days.
- **Press / media** — medium; filter by outlet tier and timing fit; involve the
  artist for major outlets only; respond within several business days.
- **Show offer** — medium; filter by fee, market, timing; involve the artist only
  above the discretionary threshold; respond within several business days.
- **Brand / endorsement** — medium-high; filter by brand tier and category fit;
  involve the artist for genuine opportunities; respond within about a week.
- **Collaboration / feature** — low-medium; filter by tier and positioning fit;
  involve the artist for career-relevant requests only; respond within about a
  week.
- **Fan / general public** — low; no artist involvement; route to the fan or
  management contact.
- **Unsolicited pitches** — lowest; no artist involvement; decline or route with a
  standard response.

**Timing discipline:** taking weeks to respond to a legitimate industry inbound
damages the artist's professional reputation. Response time is the first signal of
competence the industry receives. Declining professionally and promptly beats slow
engagement that signals disorganization. *Anti-pattern:* presenting every inbound
to the artist, which creates decision fatigue and undermines the value of triage —
the manager filters; the artist decides on filtered items that pass phase-fit and
positioning screens.

## 5. Communication Cadence

**Active periods (campaign, tour, negotiation):** daily or near-daily brief status
updates so the artist knows what is happening without being overwhelmed, plus a
weekly call covering all active matters with decisions logged.

**Quiet periods (development, hiatus, between campaigns):** a weekly status call,
with any material development triggering immediate communication.

**Escalation triggers — contact the artist immediately:** any legal document or
demand letter; any major media mention the artist should know about before
encountering it elsewhere; any team-member situation affecting active operations;
any significant industry-relationship development; any financial issue (missed
payment, unexpected cost, account concern).

**Industry communication protocols:** all material label communication flows
through the manager, who is the single point of contact for strategic matters (the
artist may speak directly with creative collaborators, with the manager copied on
anything strategic). The booking agent routes all offers through the manager; the
artist is involved only at the decision level. The manager is the attorney's
primary business contact; the artist may speak directly with the attorney on
personal legal matters.

## 6. Artist Availability Management

Operational demands shift between active touring and creative-development periods,
and the manager manages these transitions explicitly.

- **Touring cycle:** the artist's primary commitment is performance and promotion;
  creative bandwidth is limited (studio commitments during heavy touring are common
  mistakes); financial position is most visible and over-spending risk is high;
  inbound must be triaged even more aggressively; physical and mental-health
  monitoring is a legitimate management responsibility — an artist who burns out
  mid-tour is a management failure.
- **Non-touring creative cycle:** the primary commitment is creative development;
  the schedule should protect creative blocks from administrative demand; inbound is
  filtered more aggressively; team-contact frequency can reduce.
- **Vacation and personal time:** a manager who cannot enforce vacation time is not
  protecting the artist. Pre-agreed periods of full professional silence require the
  manager's active enforcement with all team members and industry contacts. The
  manager does not take personal time during the artist's active major campaigns
  unless coverage is confirmed.
