# Career Strategy and Positioning

Career strategy is the manager's most fundamental discipline. Every inbound
opportunity, investment, and team decision is evaluated against where the artist
is in their career and where they are going. An opportunity correct in one phase
can be damaging in another.

## 1. Career Phase Model

The five phases are defined by **structural signals, not chart positions or
streaming numbers**, because the relevant thresholds shift by genre, territory, and
market conditions.

### Phase 1 — Development

*Signals:* no commercial release history; identity not yet fully formed; small,
undocumented audience; the artist is still finding voice and building basic
infrastructure.

*Priority:* define the vision; build the team foundation; avoid premature deals
that foreclose development time; protect the artist's right to fail and iterate
without public consequence.

*Strategic error:* rushing to market before identity is formed. A premature release
that fails creates a public record of failure that is harder to overcome than
having no release history at all. The artist with no releases is a blank slate; the
artist with a failed release is a known quantity.

### Phase 2 — Breakthrough

*Signals:* first commercial release(s); organic audience growth; industry attention
beginning; a credible commercial story even if not yet proven at scale.

*Priority:* establish positioning; get the right deals in place before the leverage
window closes; build team competency; manage the first label/distribution
relationship; protect upside in early deals.

*Strategic error:* underselling on the first major deal. Breakthrough is the
highest-leverage negotiating moment many artists ever have — when the label wants
them most. Artists who sign badly here often spend the next decade unwinding bad
structures.

### Phase 3 — Peak

*Signals:* top-tier commercial performance; headlining major venues; media and
brand attention at scale; the artist is the most commercially active they have been
or may ever be.

*Priority:* maximize earnings during the finite peak window; protect momentum
without over-exposing; make the right catalog and rights decisions while leverage
is highest; build long-term infrastructure while income is at maximum.

*Strategic error:* treating the peak as permanent and making irreversible
commitments (major real estate, extravagant touring production, long-term personnel
contracts) that cannot be sustained when the peak ends. Every peak ends; the smart
manager builds the bridge to the sustained phase before the decline starts.

### Phase 4 — Sustained

*Signals:* activity has settled below peak but a real audience remains; touring is
still viable; catalog income is meaningful; the artist is no longer building but is
not yet a legacy act.

*Priority:* efficient operation; catalog stewardship; selective touring; maintaining
relationships without over-exposing; positioning for potential catalog transactions
or creative reinvention.

*Strategic error:* maintaining peak-phase overhead on sustained-phase income. A
touring infrastructure built for the largest venues will bankrupt a mid-room
artist. Failing to right-size operations accelerates decline.

### Phase 5 — Legacy

*Signals:* the artist is defined primarily by catalog and cultural history rather
than new commercial activity; touring may continue in selective, premium form;
estate planning may be relevant.

*Priority:* catalog stewardship; estate structure (with the legal and finance
specialists); selective re-engagement; protecting the legacy rather than chasing
new commercial cycles.

*Note:* some artists move from Phase 4 back to Phase 3 through genuine creative
reinvention. This is the exception, not the rule. The manager's job is to evaluate
whether a reinvention is genuine before committing the artist's credibility to it.

## 2. Opportunity Stack — Triage Framework

When multiple opportunities arrive at once, evaluate the stack top to bottom. An
opportunity that fails at a higher level is deprioritized regardless of how
attractive it appears lower down.

1. **Career-phase fit** — is this appropriate for the artist's current phase? An
   opportunity that locks in current-phase positioning when the artist should be
   transitioning is deprioritized regardless of economics.
2. **Positioning consistency** — does it reinforce or contradict the artist's
   positioning triangle? A contradiction requires an explicit positioning rationale
   before proceeding.
3. **Relationship quality** — who is the counterparty, and what is the relationship
   history? A deal from a reliable partner is worth more than a nominally better
   deal from an unreliable one. Relationship quality is a real economic variable.
4. **Economic terms** — only after the first three pass does economic evaluation
   dominate. Wrong phase, wrong positioning, and a bad counterparty are not saved
   by better economics.
5. **Timing and reversibility** — can this be deferred without loss? Is the
   commitment reversible? Lower-reversibility commitments require stronger evidence
   at every higher tier before proceeding.

## 3. Positioning Triangle

The three-point intersection that defines a commercially viable, distinctive artist
identity. A manager cannot evaluate opportunities or strategy without being able to
state each vertex clearly.

- **Commercial** — what commercial category does the artist occupy? What format,
  streaming genre, touring circuit? What comparable reference points does the
  industry use to understand them?
- **Creative** — what is genuinely distinctive about the artist's creative voice —
  the elements not interchangeable with the competitive set? If it cannot be said
  in a sentence, the positioning is not yet formed.
- **Cultural** — what audience identity, social context, or cultural moment does
  the artist speak to? Who are the fans, why do they care, and what does following
  this artist say about them? Cultural positioning drives organic growth and fan
  depth that commercial positioning alone cannot.

*Diagnostic rule:* if a manager cannot state each vertex in a sentence, that
manager does not yet have a strategy. A positioning that sounds like every other
artist in the genre is a category description, not a positioning.

## 4. Momentum Management

Momentum is the most finite and non-renewable resource in a career. The manager
builds it efficiently, spends it strategically, and never wastes it.

### Four Momentum States

- **Building** — audience and attention are compounding. Protect the build by not
  over-exposing or diluting the ascent narrative. A single poorly timed release can
  interrupt a build that took months to establish.
- **Spending** — the artist is actively deploying accumulated momentum (major
  release, major tour, significant announcement). Spend it only on things that
  generate significant return or build the next phase. Never spend momentum on
  anything forgotten in thirty days.
- **Preserving** — the artist has momentum but is between spend cycles. Stay
  culturally relevant without major commitments. This is where bad decisions
  happen: managers fill the space with activity that dilutes rather than preserves.
- **Rebuilding** — momentum has been lost. Do not behave as if it still exists;
  start with moves appropriate to a lower baseline, not moves that require existing
  momentum to land.

### Momentum Anti-Patterns

- **Over-touring at peak** — saturating the market before the album cycle finishes,
  so the headline tour plays half-full rooms.
- **Releasing in the wrong cycle** — dropping music while the tour is building
  (splitting focus) or after the press cycle has moved on (releasing into a void).
- **Announcing before ready** — premature announcements that cannot be delivered on
  time destroy credibility that takes multiple cycles to rebuild.
- **Letting silence become irrelevance** — a preserving state that runs too long
  without cultural touchpoints stops preserving and starts decaying.

## 5. Milestone Sequencing by Career Phase

Careers do not build linearly; each phase has a milestone order that creates the
conditions for the next. Completing milestones out of sequence — a major label deal
before commercial viability, arena tours before a breakthrough album — creates
fragile outcomes.

**Toward breakthrough:** identity defined and consistent; a core catalog of
complete songs (not demos); at least one professional-quality recording that
demonstrates commercial viability; an entertainment-attorney relationship
established before any deal engagement; a manager retained if appropriate to the
stage.

**Toward peak:** a first commercial release with documented audience response;
label or distribution infrastructure operational; a booking relationship in place;
the full professional team assembled; a management agreement on market-standard
terms.

**Into the sustained phase:** this is a market-reality transition the manager must
recognize and respond to, not force. The key signal is year-over-year decline
despite correct decisions; the priority is to right-size operations before the
income decline makes right-sizing forced.

**Practitioner insight:** the peak-to-sustained transition is where most management
relationships fail — not because the manager did anything wrong, but because the
manager's economic model (often gross commission on a declining touring base)
becomes misaligned with the artist's reality. A manager whose income halves as the
artist transitions is less likely to give honest counsel. Recognizing and
addressing that tension proactively — renegotiating commission structure, adjusting
overhead — is what distinguishes a top-tier manager from an adequate one.
