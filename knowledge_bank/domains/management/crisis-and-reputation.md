# Crisis and Reputation Management

Most crisis-management failures come from responding before assessing. The reflex
to "do something" when a crisis breaks — issuing a statement, posting a response,
calling the outlet — almost always makes the situation worse. The discipline is
triage first, deliberate response second, and a clear handoff to the legal
specialist when exposure crosses into legal territory.

## 1. Crisis Triage — Severity Classification

Misclassifying severity leads to under-response (treating a serious crisis as
minor) or over-response (mobilizing crisis machinery for something better handled
quietly). Classification is the first step in any response.

### Level 1 — Operational Crisis

A professional logistics or operational failure that does not directly involve the
artist's reputation — a cancelled show (logistical, not performance-related), a
venue or promoter conflict, a technical failure at a major performance, a booking or
scheduling error. *Response:* manager-level resolution; the legal specialist
consulted only if a contract breach is involved; no public statement unless already
public; resolution communicated directly to affected parties. **Do not
underclassify:** any operational crisis trending on social media is no longer an
operational crisis — it has become Level 2 or higher.

### Level 2 — Reputation / Press Crisis

A negative press story, social-media controversy, or public allegation affecting
reputation without legal exposure — negative coverage of a performance or statement,
a controversy over a public statement, cancellation criticism, or an allegation of
professional misconduct that is not criminal or HR-tier. *Response:* the manager
leads; the publicist is engaged immediately; no public statement without full
consultation; the response timeline is measured in hours, not days; the legal
specialist is consulted before any written public statement. *In the first hours:*
the manager assesses the story (source, claim, accuracy), consults the publicist on
strategy, consults the legal specialist if any exposure exists, issues no statements
(the artist is briefed but not authorized to respond publicly), and runs the
response decision logic before committing to any response.

### Level 3 — Legal / HR / Safety Crisis

Any situation involving potential criminal exposure, civil litigation risk, HR
violations, personal-safety concerns, or major contract disputes — a criminal
allegation or investigation, a lawsuit filed or threatened, a workplace-harassment
allegation, a credible safety threat, or a major breach-of-contract filing.
*Response:* the legal specialist leads. The manager coordinates but does not direct.
No public statements without legal authorization. All communications (text, email,
verbal) are treated as potentially discoverable, and the manager advises the artist
to limit communications. **The manager must state this scope boundary explicitly in
its own output:** once the attorney is engaged, the manager cannot advise further on
the substance of the matter — the manager's role from that point is coordination,
not direction. State this outright; do not merely imply it. *In the first hour:* the
manager calls the attorney before doing anything else, the attorney directs next
steps, and the manager issues no statement, public or private, without legal
guidance.

### Level 4 — Personal / Health Crisis

A personal crisis — mental health, addiction, personal loss, family crisis — that
requires management response to protect both the artist and the professional
situation. *Response:* the artist's wellbeing is the primary consideration, not the
professional situation; but the professional situation requires active management to
avoid compounding the personal crisis. The protocol: assess whether immediate
commitments need to be deferred or cancelled (often yes); communicate cancellations
professionally and truthfully at a minimal level of disclosure (detailed disclosure
is the artist's choice, not the manager's); coordinate with the relevant medical or
mental-health professional (not the manager's domain — the manager ensures access to
the right professional); shield the artist from professional pressure (active
gatekeeping, not passive); and consult the legal specialist if cancellations affect
legal obligations.

## 2. The First Response Protocol

**The hold rule:** before any public response to any crisis, the manager holds for a
defined cooling-off window to verify the facts (what is the actual claim, what is
the source, is it accurate?), assess the audience (who is already paying attention,
and is it reaching the artist's core audience or contained to a specific media
ecosystem?), consult by phone (not text) with the publicist and, where exposure
exists, the legal specialist, and decide whether a response is needed at all — many
crises die without a response, and a response extends a story's life.

*Anti-pattern:* responding publicly before the picture is clear. A premature
response to an inaccurate story that is later corrected creates three news cycles
instead of one. *Deadline pressure from a journalist:* when a reporter sets a
comment-window deadline, the attorney (never the manager) may contact the journalist
to request an extension — a standard, accepted practice that buys time for a proper,
legally reviewed response.

**Post-crisis remediation:** the absence of an entertainment attorney on retainer is
a management-infrastructure gap. Once the immediate crisis is resolved, establishing
a crisis-accessible attorney relationship is part of crisis preparedness and should
be flagged for remediation.

## 3. Response Decision Logic

- *Is the story accurate?*
  - **No, it contains material factual errors** — is the error consequential to the
    artist's reputation? If yes, a factual correction is appropriate and the legal
    specialist reviews it before publication. If no, monitor without response — a
    correction amplifies a story the audience may not have seen.
  - **Yes, it is accurate or largely accurate** — is there legal exposure from
    acknowledgment? If yes, the legal specialist leads and there is no public
    acknowledgment without authorization. If no exposure, assess the scope of
    attention: if contained (niche press, no fan-base attention), monitor — a
    response amplifies. If it is reaching the artist's audience or mainstream press,
    weigh the nature of the harm: a factual allegation that damages reputation may
    warrant acknowledgment or accurate context (legal-reviewed first); an allegation
    of misconduct or harm to others is never met with a defensive response before
    consulting the legal specialist — protect the artist, but never at the expense of
    denying legitimate harm to others.

## 4. Label Relationship Crisis

A label-artist relationship in crisis requires a different protocol than a press
crisis — the stakes are contractual, financial, and long-term. *Triggers:* the
label has failed to deliver significant commitments (marketing, release dates,
budget); the label has shelved the project; significant internal change has affected
the artist's team; or the artist has failed to deliver on obligations. *Protocol:*
the manager documents all relevant facts (what was committed per the agreement, what
was delivered, the timeline of divergence), consults the legal specialist before any
communication with the label about the dispute, agrees a communication strategy with
the attorney, and maintains the relationship professionally throughout — the goal is
resolution, not confrontation. *Anti-pattern:* going public with a label dispute
before the legal position is established — it gives away leverage, may breach
confidentiality provisions, and can damage the artist's ability to work with any
major label in future. The instinct to fight publicly is understandable and almost
always strategically wrong.

## 5. Artist-Manager Conflict Resolution

- **Strategic disagreement (normal, healthy):** the manager presents the full
  recommendation with rationale, risks, and alternatives; the artist decides; the
  manager documents and executes.
- **Pattern of strategic disagreement:** the artist consistently overrides
  recommendations in ways the manager believes are harmful. The manager holds a
  direct conversation acknowledging the pattern, states the concern clearly, and
  discusses whether the relationship is still effective — formalizing the concern in
  writing if it continues.
- **Trust breakdown:** either party has lost confidence in the other's competence or
  integrity. The protocol is mediation (often with attorneys present) and a
  structured process for either reestablishing or professionally ending the
  relationship, including transition terms (who holds which relationships, how
  post-term provisions apply, how team communication is handled).

The manager's obligation in a relationship ending is to make the transition as clean
as possible: hand off cleanly, honor post-term obligations, and not poison
professional relationships to protect their own position.

## 6. Internal Team Crisis

A crisis within the team — a critically underperforming member, a conflict between
members, or a key departure during a critical period — requires manager leadership.
On a **key departure during an active campaign**, the manager assesses the immediate
coverage gap, implements temporary coverage (covering directly or engaging an
interim), routes the departure agreement to the legal specialist, and communicates
to the artist clearly but not alarmingly ("X has departed; here is who is covering;
here is the replacement plan"). On a **conflict between two team members**, the
manager arbitrates — the manager is the only party with authority over both
relationships, and a manager who lets such conflicts fester until they affect the
artist's career has failed in their coordination function. *Anti-pattern:* involving
the artist in team personnel conflicts; the artist is informed of significant
changes but should never be required to manage the professional team.
