# Label and Deal Relationship Management

A label is not a single contact — it is a stack of professional relationships, each
with a distinct function, different motivations, and different information the
manager needs. Treating the label as a monolith ("the label loves us" / "the label
doesn't care") is a diagnostic failure. The manager's role in deals is strategic
positioning and intelligence, never execution — execution routes to the legal &
contracts specialist.

## 1. The Label Relationship Stack

### A&R (Artist and Repertoire)

The artist's original internal champion — the person who signed them and has
internal credibility invested in their success. The most personal and typically the
most candid relationship. The manager uses it to understand the label's creative
direction for the next cycle, to get early signal on how the label feels about
recorded material, to navigate internal politics (if A&R and marketing disagree,
A&R is the best intelligence source), and to detect when internal commitment is
declining. *Maintenance:* regular creative conversation, not just deal
conversation — an A&R relationship that becomes purely transactional loses its
candor value.

### Marketing

The team responsible for the release campaign — digital, radio, playlist pitching,
creative assets, label-funded brand partnerships. The manager uses it to ensure the
plan aligns with the artist's strategy, to understand internal resource allocation,
to hold the label accountable to marketing commitments made during deal
negotiation, and to align release timing with marketing readiness. *Maintenance:*
active contact during campaign build — a manager who engages marketing only when
something goes wrong has abdicated campaign oversight.

### Business Affairs

The label's deal-making and contract team — not a normal-operations relationship,
but critical during negotiation cycles. The manager uses it to understand the
label's contractual positions before routing to the legal specialist and to get
clarity on obligations (what does "promotional support" actually mean in the
agreement?). *Maintenance:* maintain the relationship but route all actual
negotiation to the legal specialist; never negotiate terms directly without the
attorney at least copied.

### Touring / Live

Where the label has a live division or promoter relationships, this contact
coordinates label-funded tour marketing, opening-act coordination, and festival
access. The manager uses it to access available tour-marketing support and to align
tour timing with the release campaign.

### Digital / Streaming (DSP Relations)

Labels maintain direct DSP relationships for editorial pitching and promotional
opportunities. The manager does not typically interface with DSPs at the strategic
level — the label's DSP team does — but should understand what the label is and
isn't doing on that front and hold them accountable, flagging when DSP performance
diverges from expectation without label explanation.

## 2. Label Priority Framework

On a major roster, every artist competes for finite internal priority — marketing
budget, staff time, political capital with DSPs and press. Understanding where the
artist sits in the internal priority stack is one of the manager's most important
intelligence functions.

**Signals of high priority:** A&R actively engaged and communicating frequently; a
senior marketing lead assigned; budget commitments from the deal being honored;
label leadership personally engaged; the DSP team actively pitching editorial.

**Signals of declining priority:** A&R communication becoming less frequent and
less substantive; junior staffing on the campaign; budget quietly scaled back or
deferred; release dates moving without explanation; the artist excluded from
label-organized showcases and industry moments.

**Manager's response to decline signals:** document the signals (dates, instances);
request an alignment meeting with A&R and marketing leadership (framed as campaign
planning, not confrontation); in the meeting make specific asks with specific
accountability (who is responsible for what, by when); if signals persist, route to
the legal specialist to review the label's contractual obligations against actual
behavior — now a potential breach or constructive non-performance situation.

## 3. Deal Positioning Decision Logic

The manager defines strategic parameters and provides intelligence; the attorney
executes. The decision logic before and during a deal:

- *Is this a new agreement or material amendment?* If yes — route immediately to
  the legal specialist before any response to the label. If no (admin
  clarification, accounting question) — handle directly.
- *Is the offer consistent with prior commitments?* If no — flag the discrepancy
  and route before responding. If yes — acknowledge receipt; the legal specialist
  reviews terms.
- *The manager's role before routing:* define what the artist needs (minimum
  advance, minimum marketing commitment, release-date windows, creative-control
  provisions); brief the legal specialist on the label's likely positions and
  flexibility; identify relationship dynamics affecting the approach.
- *During negotiation:* stay in coordination with the legal specialist on strategy;
  hold the artist context (what the artist will and won't accept); maintain the
  label relationship **separate** from the negotiation — the attorney negotiates
  the deal; the manager maintains the relationship.
- *After close:* ensure commitments are operationalized (marketing plan, tour
  support, release dates); hold the label accountable without creating an
  adversarial relationship; route any compliance dispute to the legal specialist.

**The most important rule:** a manager who negotiates the deal directly (without
the attorney) usually gets worse terms *and* damages the label relationship. The
label's business-affairs team is a professional negotiating unit; a personal
manager without legal training is outmatched. The manager's value in a negotiation
is strategic intelligence and relationship maintenance — not direct negotiation.

## 4. A&R Relationship Maintenance

The A&R relationship is the manager's highest-value and most fragile internal
relationship. An A&R who stops championing an artist internally is the single
biggest internal risk to a label deal.

**What maintains it:** regular creative engagement (sharing work-in-progress when
appropriate; an A&R surprised by finished album material has not been maintained);
delivering on commitments (repeated delivery delays erode the A&R's internal
credibility and damage trust); transparency about creative direction (surprises in
early form create conversations; surprises in final form create crises);
acknowledging genuine A&R contributions (an A&R who feels respected champions the
artist more actively internally).

**Warning signs of deterioration:** the A&R stops proactively reaching out
(communication becomes manager-initiated only); responses become shorter and less
substantive; the A&R starts copying business affairs or legal on communications
that were historically direct; the A&R misses meetings or sends assistants; the A&R
has moved to another label without transition communication. When these appear,
schedule a direct one-on-one — a personal conversation to understand what changed.
This is the manager's job, not the artist's.

## 5. Label Relationship Deterioration — Escalation Protocol

A deteriorating label relationship is a crisis most managers recognize too late and
respond to too slowly.

- **Stage 1 — Early warning (manager action only):** A&R communication declining;
  one or two marketing commitments deferred; the release date slipped once.
  *Response:* a direct alignment meeting with specific accountability; no legal
  involvement yet.
- **Stage 2 — Pattern of non-performance (manager + attorney):** repeated broken
  commitments; multiple delivery slips; label-side personnel changes affecting the
  campaign; formal communication with material changes to the release schedule.
  *Response:* route to the legal specialist to review contractual obligations and
  prepare a written inventory of commitments versus performance; the manager holds
  the relationship while the legal position is reviewed.
- **Stage 3 — Material breach or creative shelving:** the label actively fails to
  release material; the campaign budget is zeroed; the artist is told the label no
  longer plans to support the project. *Response:* this is now a legal-specialist-led
  situation. The manager maintains the relationship to preserve options while
  remedies, buyout options, and positioning are evaluated. The manager makes no
  public statements, ultimatums, or confrontational demands without legal guidance.

**Critical rule:** a manager who publicly calls out the label before the legal
position is established gives away negotiating leverage and may prejudice the
artist's remedies. The emotion of the situation and the urge to be seen protecting
the artist must not override strategic sequencing.
