# Management Agreement Structures and Commission

The management commission is the manager's primary economic interest in the
artist's career, and the management agreement is the document that defines it.
Getting the structure right — and recognizing the structures that quietly harm the
artist — is foundational management knowledge.

## 1. The Three Commission Structure Types

Three structures exist in practice, each defining "income" differently. The
difference compounds significantly over a multi-album career.

### Gross Commission

Commission applied to all income the artist receives before any deductions — agent
commissions, attorney fees, taxes, travel, production costs, session fees. It is
the most common structure in standard personal-management agreements and the
presumptive default when no modifier is specified.

**Artist risk:** the manager earns commission on money the artist never
effectively receives. A tour that grosses a large figure but costs most of it in
deductible expenses can leave the artist with a thin net while the manager still
earns on the full gross. Gross commission applied to touring income without a
deduction for agency fees is the most common source of manager-artist conflict in
touring-heavy careers; route disputes to the legal & contracts specialist.

### Net Commission

Commission applied only to the artist's net income after specified deductions. The
definition of "net" must be explicitly enumerated in the agreement — vague "net"
language is itself a red flag. Common enumerated deductions include artist-funded
recording costs, agency/booking commissions, tour production costs, producer
advances paid by the artist, and co-writer fees.

**Benefit:** commission tracks actual economic value received rather than top-line
revenue. **Risk:** if "net" is not precisely defined, disputes arise over what is
deductible. Agreeing to "net" without defining deductibles is accepting an
economically ambiguous contract.

### Modified Net / Adjusted Gross

Commission applied after a specified, limited set of deductions — typically agency
fees and sometimes recording costs — but not the artist's full cost structure. A
hybrid that minimizes the "gross commission on touring income" problem (the
manager should not earn on money paid directly to the booking agent) while avoiding
the ambiguity of a full net calculation.

**Practitioner insight:** modified net with clearly defined deductibles is the most
negotiated outcome in experienced agreements, because it aligns manager-artist
economics without creating definitional ambiguity. A team that understands this
structure negotiates better than one that simply accepts a standard gross
template.

## 2. Commission Rate Conventions by Artist Tier

Exact rates are negotiated and vary widely; they are not quotable as validated
market data without a verifiable source. The directional, structural pattern:

- **Emerging / developing** artists tend toward the higher end of the personal
  management range, reflecting the manager's career-building risk.
- **Breaking** artists sit in standard personal-management territory.
- **Mid-level** artists, with proven touring and charting history, see the higher
  end become increasingly hard to defend.
- **Established** artists (arena/amphitheater touring, multi-album catalog) have
  leverage to negotiate the rate down as the management value-add becomes more
  clearly scoped.
- **Legacy / catalog** artists are often on a reduced rate for passive catalog
  income, negotiated deal by deal.

Treat these as directional conventions, never as definitive benchmarks. Where a
rate is material to a decision, model it from the artist's own situation and route
specific figures to the appropriate specialist.

**Recording vs. touring commission:** some agreements carry different rates for
recording income vs. touring income vs. other streams. Any rate differentiation
must be explicitly specified; a single rate across all income is the default, and a
gross touring rate at the same level as the recording rate creates extreme
distortion on a major tour.

## 3. Term Structure

**Initial term:** most agreements specify a short initial term; emerging-artist
agreements more commonly start short with options, established-artist agreements
may run longer.

**Option periods:** the manager typically holds options to extend, usually in
yearly increments. The structure matters critically:

- *Manager-controlled options (red flag):* the manager alone decides whether to
  exercise. The artist cannot exit even after the relationship has deteriorated.
- *Mutual options:* either party can exit. More protective of the artist.
- *Performance-triggered options:* the manager's right to extend is conditioned on
  achieving specified milestones (release, touring threshold, chart position). This
  protects the artist against a manager who holds the relationship without
  delivering.

**Key-person clause:** when the artist's relationship is with a specific individual
at a firm, the agreement should state that it is personal to that individual, with
a right to exit if they leave. Absence of a key-person clause in a multi-manager
firm deal is a red flag.

## 4. Post-Term Provisions (Sunset Clauses)

The most legally consequential component from the artist's economic perspective.
The sunset provision governs whether and how much commission the manager earns on
deals negotiated during the term but paying out after it ends.

- **No sunset provision:** the manager earns full commission on all income from
  in-term deals indefinitely, even after departure. Severely artist-unfavorable;
  the artist may owe commission to a former manager for the life of a catalog.
- **Hard sunset (time-limited):** commission on post-term income from in-term deals
  declines to zero at a specified date. Artist-favorable; a clean exit.
- **Graduated / declining sunset:** the rate on post-term income from in-term deals
  declines over time (e.g., full rate, then partial, then zero). Balanced — the
  manager participates in value they created without perpetual encumbrance. The
  most commonly negotiated outcome for established-artist agreements.

**Practitioner insight:** the post-term provision is the clause that bites artists
hardest when relationships end badly. An artist who fires a manager partway through
a term — even for legitimate cause — may owe commission on every recording made
during that term for years afterward if no sunset is specified. This is not an edge
case; it is the most common source of post-management-term litigation. Route
negotiation of specific sunset provisions to the legal & contracts specialist.

## 5. Scope Definitions

A management agreement that does not define scope creates ambiguity about what the
manager is engaged to do. Scope should cover at minimum:

- **Territory:** personal management is typically worldwide; territorial limits are
  unusual but appear in regional contexts.
- **Activities:** which professional activities are in scope — recording, touring,
  endorsements, acting, speaking, licensing, other creative work.
- **Income streams:** which streams are commissionable — recording royalties, live
  fees, endorsement fees, licensing income, merchandise income.
- **Exclusions:** some agreements carve out pre-existing income streams (catalog or
  business income predating the relationship) or specific activities.

What management does **not** include by default: legal representation, booking,
public-relations execution, business management/accounting, and record-label A&R
decisions — each routes to the corresponding specialist.

## 6. Red-Flag Clause Taxonomy

The following clauses require immediate escalation to the legal & contracts
specialist before the artist signs anything. The manager identifies; the legal
specialist evaluates and advises on remedy.

- **Commission on songwriter income without limit** — the manager earns on every
  writer-royalty dollar, even on songs written before or outside the managed
  career. Watch for "all income of Artist in the entertainment industry" without a
  songwriter carve-out.
- **Commission on pre-existing income without carve-out** — watch for "all income"
  with no "from activities commenced during the term" modifier.
- **No sunset / post-term income in perpetuity** — watch for language extending
  commission to "all income derived from agreements entered into during the Term"
  with no sunset.
- **No key-person clause in a multi-manager firm** — the artist is bound to the
  firm, not the individual.
- **No reversion for non-performance** — no mechanism for the artist to exit if the
  manager does not deliver; absence of a performance trigger in option periods.
- **Options exclusively at the manager's discretion** — the manager can extend
  indefinitely without meeting any obligation.
- **Commission on non-entertainment income** — broad "all income" language with no
  limiting "entertainment industry" or "professional career" modifier.

Identification of any red-flag clause is a trigger to route the full agreement to
the legal & contracts specialist before signing.
