# Record Deal Mechanics and Structures

The manager does not negotiate recording agreements — that is the entertainment
attorney's function — but the manager must understand record deal structures deeply
enough to frame the artist's strategic position, identify what is at stake in each
term, flag provisions that require escalation, and evaluate whether a deal serves
the artist's career-phase goals. A manager who cannot read a recording agreement is
a liability at the table.

## 1. The Five Recording Deal Structures

Not all "record deals" are equivalent. The structure determines who funds recording,
who owns the masters, and how income flows back to the artist.

### Major-Label Recording Agreement

The label finances recording costs (often as a recoupable advance), funds marketing
and promotion, and handles distribution globally. In exchange, the label typically
owns the masters for a defined term or in perpetuity, with the right to license them
worldwide. The artist earns a royalty on sales and streams — but receives no cash
until royalties exceed the recoupable advance.

*Manager's lens:* major-label deals provide the largest advance and the most
marketing infrastructure; they extract the most from the artist in exchange. The
manager's job is to negotiate the terms that determine what the artist keeps when the
money flows: royalty rate, advance structure, release commitment, 360 provisions, and
reversion rights. These terms are not adjustable after signing.

### Production and Distribution (P&D) Deal

The artist or a production company funds recording independently. The label provides
manufacturing (pressing) and distribution, taking a distribution fee or percentage of
net receipts — typically paid off the top before the artist sees income. The artist
retains master ownership.

*Manager's lens:* P&D deals are appropriate for established acts with a demonstrable
market who need logistics rather than development. The economic trade-off is giving
up a lower percentage of income in exchange for financing their own recordings. An
artist who cannot fund recording independently is not in a position to use this
structure.

### Label-Services / Artist-Services Deal

The label provides a defined menu of services — radio promotion, sync pitching,
marketing campaigns, retail placement — for a fee or a percentage of income related
to those services. The artist retains masters and pays for services rendered.

*Manager's lens:* evaluating a label-services deal requires understanding exactly
what services are being provided and whether the fee reflects their market rate. A
service deal for radio promotion from a label with a meaningful independent radio
team has real value; a service deal from a company that cannot demonstrate a track
record in the format is not. Demand the specific list of deliverables and a timeline.

### Distribution Deal

Purely logistical: the distributor physically or digitally distributes the artist's
masters in exchange for a percentage of gross revenue or a per-release fee. The
artist retains full master ownership and full creative control. Most independent
digital distributors operate on this model.

*Manager's lens:* distribution deals are appropriate for self-funded artists who
need nothing from a label but logistics. The decision variable is not really which
distribution deal — it is whether the artist has the resources and team to do what
a label would otherwise handle: recording funding, marketing, radio, press.

### Self-Release via Aggregator

The artist uploads recordings to a digital aggregator (a service that distributes to
streaming platforms) for a flat annual fee or a small per-release fee. The artist
retains all income and all rights. Appropriate for development-phase artists building
an audience without label support.

*Manager's lens:* self-release via aggregator is not a "deal" — it is infrastructure.
The manager selects the aggregator and confirms the registration setup (ISRC
assignment, rights-system enrollment), but the strategic question is what the artist
is building toward with this approach.

## 2. Advance and Recoupment Mechanics

### How Advances Work

A recording advance is a cash payment to the artist that the label recoups from the
artist's royalties. It is not income — it is a loan from future royalties. The
artist does not repay the advance in cash if royalties never recoup it, but they
receive no royalty income until the advance is fully recouped.

*Practitioner insight:* the advance is the most visible number in a major-label deal
and the least important one to the manager's evaluation. The royalty rate, recoupment
base, and cross-collateralization terms determine whether the artist ever sees cash
from royalties. An artist who signs for a large advance on a low royalty rate with
cross-collateralization and a long-term contract may never receive another dollar from
the label beyond that advance.

### What Is Charged Against the Advance

Labels recoup from the artist's royalty account (not from income generally). What
commonly charges against royalties:

- **Recording costs** — costs funded by the label for studio, production, mixing,
  and mastering, typically charged at 100% against the artist's royalty account.
- **Producer advances** — advances paid directly to producers on behalf of the
  artist, charged to the artist's account.
- **Video costs** — historically 50% of video production costs; varies by agreement.
- **Independent radio promotion** — in some agreements, label-funded independent
  radio promotion is charged as a recoupable expense. This is one of the most
  significant hidden recoupment charges and must be negotiated.
- **Tour support** — advance for touring costs funded by the label; explicitly
  recoupable in most standard agreements.
- **Mechanical royalties (controlled compositions)** — see controlled composition
  provision below.

### Cross-Collateralization

Cross-collateralization means that the unrecouped deficit from one album can be
offset against royalties earned on another album before the artist receives cash.
Under cross-collateralized accounting, an artist with a successfully streaming
second album but an unrecouped first album may receive nothing because the label
nets the balances.

*Manager's position:* resist cross-collateralization categorically. Each album
should stand alone for recoupment purposes. A label that insists on
cross-collateralization is telling the artist that the business success of a later
work will subsidize the loss on an earlier one — benefiting the label, not the artist.

## 3. Royalty Rate Structure

### The All-In Rate

Recording royalties are typically stated as an all-in rate: the total percentage from
which any producer royalty points are deducted. If the all-in rate is 18% and the
producer is paid 4 royalty points (percentage points), the artist's net royalty rate
is 14%. The manager evaluates the all-in rate, the standard producer point allocation
for the genre and artist tier, and the artist's actual net rate.

Royalty rates are historically stated as a percentage of the published price to
dealers (PPD) or retail price; for digital income, most agreements now use "net
receipts" from the digital platform as the royalty base, eliminating certain
historical deductions that applied to physical formats. Confirm which base applies
to digital income in every agreement.

### Royalty Reductions

Recording agreements commonly reduce the standard royalty rate in specified
circumstances. These reductions compound across a catalog:

- **Foreign sales:** royalty rate on sales outside the home territory was
  historically reduced (sometimes to 50-75% of the base rate). Digital
  has reduced the significance of this but the clause may still appear.
- **Mid-price and budget sales:** if the label releases catalog at mid-price or
  budget retail tiers, a lower royalty rate often applies.
- **Club and mail-order sales:** historically a reduced rate for sales through
  music clubs. Less relevant in a streaming-dominant market but may persist in
  agreement templates.
- **Promotional copies:** zero royalty on copies distributed for promotional
  purposes; the promotional copy carve-out must be reasonable in scope.

The manager tracks which reductions are standard for the artist's tier and markets
and escalates anything materially more restrictive to the attorney.

### Controlled Composition Clause

Mechanical royalties are payable to songwriters and publishers for the right to
reproduce and distribute their compositions. In a major-label recording agreement,
the controlled composition clause governs what mechanical rate the label pays when
the artist has written or co-written songs on the album (i.e., "controlled"
compositions that the artist controls).

Standard practice in major-label agreements: mechanicals on controlled compositions
are paid at 75% of the statutory rate (the "three-quarter rate"). Because the
statutory mechanical rate in the US is set by the Copyright Royalty Board, this
reduction is predictable in dollar terms but reduces the songwriter income the artist
would earn as a songwriter/publisher.

*Manager's position:* resist the controlled composition clause, or negotiate a
digital carve-out (the clause applies only to physical sales, not to digital streams
and downloads where statutory rates are set separately). This is particularly
material for artists who write a high percentage of their own material and have a
significant catalog — the aggregate reduction over a career is substantial.

## 4. 360-Deal Provisions

A 360 deal (also called a multiple-rights deal) grants the label a percentage of
income streams beyond recorded-music sales: touring income, merchandise income,
endorsement income, acting income, sponsorship income, and sometimes publishing.

Labels justify 360 provisions on the basis that their marketing investment grows the
artist's brand across all these streams. The manager's role is to evaluate this claim
stream by stream.

**Negotiating framework:**

- **Enumerate each stream explicitly.** Broad "all income of Artist from entertainment
  activities" language must be replaced with a specific list with a specific rate per
  stream. Resist any undefined catch-all.
- **Distinguish streams the label actually builds.** A 360 participation in touring
  from a label with no live-music expertise or infrastructure is indefensible. An
  artist who has always had a strong live career is giving away income the label did
  not create. Route this objection through the attorney.
- **Push for a sunset on 360 provisions.** Label 360 participation should not
  survive in perpetuity on income streams the artist builds long after the recording
  relationship ends.
- **Protect merchandise and endorsement income from broad sweeps.** These are
  long-lead-time income streams the artist may be building independently of the label
  relationship.
- **Include a key-person clause for 360 provisions.** If the executive champion who
  negotiated the deal leaves the label, 360 participation triggers the artist's right
  to re-negotiate or exit those provisions.

## 5. Release and Marketing Commitments

### Release Commitment

The release commitment defines whether and when the label must release the delivered
recordings. Without an explicit release commitment:

- The label can shelve recordings indefinitely while maintaining the artist under
  contract.
- The artist cannot deliver elsewhere and cannot release independently.

Essential negotiating positions: a **delivery-then-release timeline** (the label must
release within X months of delivery and acceptance); a **failure-to-release
reversion** (if the label does not release within the window, the artist can elect
to own the recordings and release independently); and clear **delivery criteria**
(what constitutes a "delivered" recording, defined objectively — technical
specifications rather than subjective commercial-acceptability language).

*Practitioner insight:* "commercially acceptable" as a delivery criterion is one of
the most dangerous clauses in a recording agreement. It gives the label unilateral
authority to reject delivery — resetting the recoupment clock and extending the
term — without objective grounds. The attorney must replace "commercially acceptable"
with objective technical delivery criteria.

### Marketing Commitment

Labels rarely agree to specific minimum marketing spend in writing. The manager
should push for at minimum: a commitment to service the record to radio in the
artist's core format; a promotional budget commitment (stated as a minimum even if
approximate); a digital marketing budget line; and a commitment to a defined number
of press and promotional activities around release. Where a label is unwilling to
commit in writing, document the label's stated plan in the deal memo — an
undocumented marketing promise is not enforceable.

## 6. Red-Flag Clauses in Recording Agreements

The following clauses require immediate escalation to the entertainment attorney
before the artist signs anything.

- **No reversion of masters** — the label owns the recordings in perpetuity with
  no reversion provision. Even a 35-year reversion (US copyright termination
  right) may be waivable. The attorney must evaluate.
- **No release commitment with a timing window** — the artist can be held
  under contract with no obligation on the label to release.
- **"Commercially acceptable" delivery criteria** — subjective and fully
  controlled by the label.
- **Cross-collateralization of all albums** — the successful album subsidizes the
  unsuccessful one; the artist may never see royalty cash regardless of
  commercial performance.
- **Broad 360 provisions without enumeration** — undefined "all income" language
  that can sweep in any future income stream.
- **Controlled composition clause without a digital carve-out** — particularly
  harmful for songwriter-artists with large catalogs.
- **No audit rights** — without the right to inspect the label's books, royalty
  errors and underpayments cannot be identified or recovered.
- **Unilateral label options across multiple albums** — the label can hold the
  artist across many albums without triggering any performance standard.
- **Independent radio promotion as a recoupable expense** — especially damaging
  when the label does not disclose what it plans to spend.
- **No key-person clause** — the artist is bound to the label entity, not the
  individual executive who signed them, and has no exit if the champion departs.
