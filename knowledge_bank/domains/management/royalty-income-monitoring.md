# Royalty Income Monitoring and Statement Management

Understanding when money arrives, from where, and whether the amounts are correct
is a foundational management responsibility. Royalty income is not passive — it
requires active monitoring, recoupment tracking, and, when warranted, a formal
audit. A manager who only looks at the bank account rather than the statement
pipeline is managing reactively and will miss systematic underpayments.

## 1. The Income Pipeline: Streams to Artist Payment

Royalty income passes through multiple intermediaries before reaching the artist,
each adding processing delay. A manager who does not map the pipeline cannot predict
cash flow or identify which stage is causing a delay.

### Recording Income Pipeline (Label Deal)

**Streams / digital sales → DSP accounting period closes → DSP reports to label
→ label processes and accounts to artist → statement and payment issued to artist.**

Typical delay from the end of the accounting period to artist receipt of the
statement: 60–90 days. Major labels typically account semi-annually. This means
income earned in the first half of the year is accounted for in the second half,
with payment following by year-end or early in the following year. A track streamed
in January may generate a royalty statement in July and a cash payment in September.

### Recording Income Pipeline (Distribution Deal)

**Streams → DSP accounting (monthly or quarterly) → distributor processes
→ artist payment released.**

Digital distributors typically report and pay monthly, with a 30–60 day delay from
the accounting period close. This is materially faster than a major-label pipeline
and is one of the practical advantages of direct distribution deals for artists with
their own release infrastructure.

### Publishing Income Pipeline

Publishing income (mechanical royalties from recorded music, performance royalties
from broadcast and digital) follows a longer and more fragmented pipeline than
recording royalties.

- **US performance royalties** (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC): collected from broadcasters,
  venues, and digital services; distributed to members quarterly. The delay from
  performance to distribution is typically 6–9 months for domestic performances;
  international performances routed through foreign society reciprocal agreements
  can take 12–18 months.
- **US mechanical royalties (digital):** collected by the Mechanical Licensing
  Collective (MLC) from streaming services; distributed to rights-holders monthly,
  with a processing delay of several months. Unmatched royalties (income where
  ownership data is incomplete) accrue at the MLC until the data is reconciled.
- **Sync licensing income:** negotiated upfront as a master-use and sync license fee;
  collected at licensing, distributed per the publishing agreement. This is the
  fastest-flowing publishing income stream — the deal closes, the fee is paid,
  the split flows to songwriter and publisher.

*Practitioner insight:* artists who move distributors or switch publishing
administrators mid-career often discover a gap in their publishing income pipeline
that is not visible until a year later, when royalties that should have arrived are
missing. The manager must confirm that registration continuity was maintained through
any administrative transition.

## 2. Statement Frequency Standards and What to Expect

Knowing the standard accounting periods prevents the manager from mistaking a
pipeline delay for a non-payment.

- **Major label recording statements:** semi-annual, typically within 60–90 days
  of each accounting period close. An artist signed to a major can expect
  approximately two statements per year, timed around mid-year and year-end closes.
- **Independent label statements:** varies widely; semi-annual is most common,
  quarterly is not unusual for more active relationships. The management agreement
  or label agreement should specify the standard.
- **Digital distribution statements:** monthly reporting with monthly or quarterly
  payment is the current standard for major digital distributors. Annual payment
  thresholds that withhold payment until a minimum balance is reached should be
  flagged as an artist-unfavorable term in the distribution agreement.
- **PRO distributions (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC):** quarterly distributions in the US.
  International PRO distributions routed through foreign societies can arrive on
  varying schedules.
- **Publishing administrator statements:** quarterly is standard.

## 3. Recoupment Status Monitoring

For artists on a major-label recording deal with an advance, the recoupment status
is the single most important number in the income pipeline. Until recoupment, no
recording royalties flow in cash to the artist.

### What the Manager Tracks

- **Opening unrecouped balance:** the advance amount the artist received that must
  be covered by royalties before cash flows.
- **Charges posted each period:** recording costs, producer advances, tour support,
  radio promotion, video costs — each period, the label charges new expenses against
  the royalty account. The manager reviews these charges for accuracy and questions
  any that are not clearly authorized by the agreement.
- **Royalty credits earned:** the income side of the ledger — streaming royalties,
  download income, physical sales, foreign income — credited at the contractual
  rate. The manager compares the royalty credit amounts to available DSP dashboard
  data as a sanity check.
- **Closing balance:** the amount remaining unrecouped after credits and charges
  for the period. Is the artist getting closer to recoupment, or is recoupment
  moving further away?

### Cross-Collateralization's Effect on Recoupment Monitoring

If the agreement cross-collateralizes multiple albums, the manager cannot evaluate
individual album recoupment — only the combined balance. This obscures which work
is commercially successful and which is not. The manager pushes back on
cross-collateralization at the time of agreement and, when it is present, maintains
a separate shadow accounting model that tracks each album's performance independently.

*Practitioner insight:* labels are not required to provide detailed recoupment
breakdowns more frequently than the contractual accounting period. A manager who
needs more timely recoupment visibility must negotiate for it in the agreement —
a recoupment-status query right with a specified response window is a legitimate
management ask that experienced attorneys include in deal negotiation.

## 4. Royalty Statement Anatomy: What to Check

Every statement the artist receives should be reviewed against these checkpoints. The
manager is not conducting a forensic audit on each statement — but material
discrepancies must be flagged to the business manager and attorney immediately.

**Period coverage:** is the statement period correct, and does it connect without
gap to the prior statement's closing period? A gap in period coverage often means
income from that period was not accounted for.

**Opening balance consistency:** does the opening balance on this statement match
the closing balance on the last statement? Inconsistency is an immediate red flag.

**Volume sanity check:** compare the units or streams claimed on the statement to
available DSP dashboard data (Spotify for Artists, Apple Music for Artists). These
are approximate comparisons — labels have their own accounting periods and
calculation methods — but a statement showing 20% of the streams visible on the
dashboard without explanation requires inquiry.

**Rate applied:** verify the contractual royalty rate is being applied correctly.
This is especially important after rate step-ups (if the agreement includes a royalty
rate increase after the artist achieves a defined sales or streaming milestone),
after contract amendments, and after the artist's catalog moves to a catalog imprint
or budget tier.

**Deduction charges:** review each line item charged against royalties. Confirm that
each charge is authorized by the agreement (category and amount), that one-time
costs are not recurring, and that tour support advances appear only once. Duplicate
postings are a common error.

**Reserves:** labels may withhold a percentage of earned royalties as a "returns
reserve" — historically against product returned by retailers, which is less
relevant in digital formats but may still appear in agreement templates. Verify the
reserve percentage does not exceed the contractual maximum and that reserves from
prior periods are being released on the contractual schedule.

**Foreign income:** foreign territory income (licensing to foreign sub-labels or
foreign distributors) should appear for all territories where the recording is
commercially available. Missing foreign territory income requires inquiry — common
causes include delayed foreign licensee statements, foreign income being held at
the foreign sub-label level, and occasionally simple accounting error.

**Controlled composition calculations:** if the agreement contains a controlled
composition clause, verify the mechanical rate being applied to controlled
compositions matches the contractual rate (typically 75% of the applicable
statutory rate). Overpayment of mechanical royalties on controlled compositions
does not happen in practice; underpayment or incorrect rate application is the
risk to monitor.

## 5. Common Royalty Accounting Errors and Shortfalls

Royalty accounting errors are common enough that a managed artist should expect to
find discrepancies over a career. The most frequent sources of error:

- **Incorrect royalty rate applied** — wrong rate used after an amendment, after
  a milestone step-up, or due to a calculation error in the accounting system.
- **Reserves not released on schedule** — labels fail to release prior-period
  reserves by the contractual date; the manager tracks the release schedule and
  follows up when it lapses.
- **Mid-price rate applied to full-price sales** — especially relevant for catalog
  titles that move between full-price and mid-price tiers.
- **Producer royalty points calculated incorrectly** — the producer's contractual
  points deducted from the artist's all-in rate are understated or overstated.
- **Independent radio promotion charged as recoupable without prior approval** —
  significant and difficult to dispute after the fact if the agreement permits it
  without approval.
- **Foreign income missing or substantially understated** — particularly common for
  territories where the label's sub-licensee is slow to account.
- **Controlled composition clause applied to non-controlled compositions** — the
  label applies the reduced mechanical rate to songs the artist did not write and
  does not control. These should receive the full statutory mechanical rate from
  the publisher.
- **Mechanical royalties omitted entirely from statements** — in digital-era deals
  where the label is responsible for paying mechanical royalties to the publisher
  on streams and downloads, omissions are not uncommon.
- **Tour support or video costs charged twice** — duplicate postings in accounting
  system transitions or across accounting period boundaries.

## 6. When to Trigger a Royalty Audit

An audit is a formal examination of the label's books and records by a certified
royalty auditor or forensic accountant retained by the artist. Most recording
agreements include an audit right with a defined procedure, frequency, and look-back
period.

### Conditions that Warrant an Audit

- **Statement discrepancies that cannot be resolved by inquiry** — the label's
  response to questions about a specific line item is unsatisfactory, the amount
  in dispute is material, and the agreement period for informal resolution has passed.
- **Volume discrepancy with DSP dashboard data** — over multiple periods, the
  streams reported on the label's statements are consistently and significantly
  below what DSP dashboards show without adequate explanation.
- **Failure to release reserves on schedule** — a pattern of reserve non-release
  suggests a systematic accounting issue rather than an isolated error.
- **Approaching the audit look-back window** — most agreements limit the audit
  look-back to 2–3 years from the date of each statement. Delaying an audit risks
  the right to recover underpayments from older periods. The manager tracks this
  window as a calendar obligation.
- **Prior underpayments found without explanation** — if a prior informal inquiry
  resulted in a correction payment, a subsequent audit often finds additional errors
  from the same source.

### What an Audit Involves

A royalty audit is conducted by the artist's business manager or a specialized
royalty audit firm. The process: formal audit notice to the label per the agreement's
procedure; inspection of the label's books and records for the audit period;
comparison of the label's records to the artist's own data and to third-party sources;
a findings report identifying discrepancies; a demand letter from the artist's attorney
for any confirmed underpayments plus interest where applicable.

*Manager's role:* identify when conditions warrant an audit, authorize the artist to
proceed, retain the auditor (with the attorney's input on firm selection), and
coordinate the audit team's needs with the label's business affairs department. The
technical execution is the auditor's and attorney's domain.

**Audit costs:** royalty audits by specialized firms vary widely by catalog size
and agreement complexity. Some agreements require the label to reimburse audit costs
if underpayments exceed a stated threshold. Where no such provision exists, the
artist bears the cost regardless of outcome — a factor in the decision to proceed.

## 7. Income Tracking Minimum Standards for a Managed Artist

The manager is not the business manager — detailed bookkeeping routes to the finance
& royalties specialist. The manager's role is to ensure tracking systems exist and
are actively used.

**Statement receipt log:** every statement received should be logged with date
received, period covered, amount remitted, and any follow-up required. Statements
that do not arrive by the expected date trigger a follow-up inquiry before the
payment window closes.

**Recoupment tracker:** for any artist with a label advance, the manager maintains
(or reviews) a simple recoupment tracker that maps the opening balance, charges per
period, credits per period, and closing balance. This does not require complex
modeling — it requires consistent updating each statement cycle.

**Publishing registration audit:** once per album cycle, confirm that all songs on
released recordings are registered with the correct performing rights organization
and with the publisher or publishing administrator, with correct ownership splits. An
unregistered song cannot earn PRO income; a song with incorrect ownership data earns
income that may not reach the rightful owner.

**Income-expectation calendar:** the manager maintains a forward-looking calendar of
when each income type is expected — statement dates, PRO distribution dates, label
payment dates, distributor payment dates. This converts income monitoring from
reactive (waiting for something to arrive) to proactive (knowing when to expect it
and following up when it does not appear).
