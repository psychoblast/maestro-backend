# Team Assembly and Coordination

The manager is the integrator of the artist's professional team. The most common
team-building mistake is hiring in the wrong order — too early (paying for
expertise the artist cannot yet use) or too late (entering major deals without
qualified representation). The second most common is scope ambiguity between team
members, the leading operational failure in artist management.

## 1. Core Team Assembly Sequence

The core professional team, roughly in order of need:

1. Entertainment attorney
2. Personal manager
3. Booking agent
4. Business manager / accountant
5. Publicist
6. Other specialists (radio promoter, marketing director, etc.)

### Sequencing Logic: Who First?

**The entertainment attorney comes before the manager, not after.** This is the
most important sequencing rule and the most regularly violated. The management
agreement itself is a legal document with significant economic consequences; an
artist who hires a manager without first retaining an attorney has no qualified
review of the agreement they are about to sign. The attorney's first job is
reviewing the management agreement — not the label deal.

**Decision logic (default position):**

- *Is the artist considering signing any agreement (management, label,
  distribution, sync)?* If yes — retain the entertainment attorney first, before
  signing anything. If no (development stage, no deals in discussion) — manager or
  attorney can come first, but the attorney should be retained before any deal
  discussion begins.
- *Does the artist have enough live activity to warrant a booking agent?* If yes
  (consistent live income, promoter interest, or label pushing touring) — retain
  one, typically through label introductions or manager referrals. If no — no
  booking agent yet; the manager handles or declines inbound show inquiries.
- *Does income warrant a business manager / accountant?* If yes (meaningful
  irregular income or any advance received) — retain one immediately for tax
  obligations and cash-flow management. If no — a CPA for annual filing suffices.
- *Does the artist need active press for an imminent release or tour?* If yes —
  retain a publicist (the label may fund it; otherwise the artist does). If no — no
  publicist yet.

**Anti-pattern:** hiring a booking agent before the artist has an entertainment
attorney. The booking-agency agreement is a legal document the artist has no
qualified person to review.

## 2. Team Role Matrix

Who handles what — and who has authority over what.

- **Personal Manager** — career strategy, team coordination, opportunity triage,
  daily operations. Executes decisions within artist-approved strategy. The central
  hub: all team members report career decisions through the manager.
- **Entertainment Attorney** — legal review, contract drafting and negotiation,
  rights protection. The manager routes all agreements to the attorney; the
  attorney reports back to manager and artist.
- **Business Manager / Accountant** — finances, budgeting, tax compliance, royalty
  receipt. The manager provides income projections and expense forecasts; the
  business manager reports cash position.
- **Booking Agent** — live booking, routing, promoter relationships. Books within
  artist-approved routing and fee parameters; reports offers to the manager.
- **Publicist** — press, media relations, narrative management. Executes within an
  artist/manager-approved narrative; the manager approves major press pitches.
- **Label (A&R, Marketing, Business Affairs)** — recording, release strategy,
  commercial promotion. The manager maintains direct relationships with all label
  departments and filters label communications to the artist.

**Anti-pattern:** team members bypassing the manager to reach the artist directly
on strategic matters. A booking agent who pitches a tour concept to the artist
before bringing it to the manager is creating a coordination failure. All strategic
communication passes through, or is at minimum copied to, the manager.

## 3. Hiring Signal Framework

Conditions that trigger each hire:

- **Entertainment attorney** — any agreement under consideration; any legal
  document received (cease-and-desist, demand letter); any move to form a business
  entity; any co-writing or collaboration dispute.
- **Booking agent** — consistent promoter inbound; label or distribution support
  that includes touring; readiness to headline beyond the home base; live income
  that justifies agent commission. Note: agents typically take on only artists they
  can earn from immediately. If no agent will take the artist, that is a market
  signal about live readiness — not a reason to hire a boutique agent who will
  underperform.
- **Business manager / accountant** — any meaningful advance or irregular income;
  touring enough to create multi-state or international tax obligations; income that
  exceeds the artist's capacity to manage with annual filing alone. Route exact
  thresholds to the finance & royalties specialist.
- **Publicist** — a commercial release several weeks from a target press date
  (enough lead time to work media); a major touring announcement; a significant
  career announcement. A label publicity department may fund this; independent
  artists fund their own.

## 4. Internal Communication Protocol

**Cadence:** at minimum a weekly manager check-in with each active team member on
open items, via email or a shared tracker — no important decisions by text message
alone.

**Decision documentation:** any decision with economic consequence above an
artist-agreed threshold is documented in writing before execution. A phone call
that authorizes a booking is followed immediately by a confirming email.

**Memo format for major decisions:** what was decided; who authorized it; when it
takes effect; what each team member must do as a result.

**Meeting structure:** full team calls are appropriate before major moves (release
campaign, major tour, significant deal). Over-meeting is a common anti-pattern that
wastes team time. Manager-label alignment meetings run ideally monthly during an
active campaign. Artist-manager cadence is at minimum weekly in active periods; the
artist should never be surprised by a professional development they were not
consulted on. Surprise is a communication failure.

## 5. Team Conflict Resolution

- **Scope dispute (manager handles directly)** — two team members claim authority
  over the same decision. The manager arbitrates; the team member complies or
  escalates to the attorney if they believe the decision violates their agreement.
- **Performance dispute (team member not delivering)** — document specific failures
  (dates, expected vs. delivered); hold a direct conversation identifying the gap;
  give written notice with a remediation timeline; if unremedied, route to the
  legal specialist for agreement review before any termination. *Anti-pattern:*
  firing a team member by telling the artist to call and cancel. The manager
  handles personnel decisions directly; the artist is informed and consulted, not
  delegated the delivery of bad news.
- **Artist-manager conflict** — the manager provides the best professional counsel,
  documents the recommendation and the artist's decision in writing, and executes
  the artist's decision (within legal and ethical limits) even when they disagree.
  A manager who executes only the decisions they agree with is not a manager — they
  are a co-decision-maker. *Exception:* if the artist's decision would cause legal
  liability, ethical harm, or irreversible career damage, the manager formally
  states their objection in writing and, if necessary, consults the attorney before
  executing.

## 6. When to Part Ways with a Team Member

Team separation is frequently made too late (tolerating underperformance) or too
early (reacting to one bad result). Indicators that separation is warranted:
consistent, documented underperformance over multiple cycles; an undisclosable
conflict of interest; loss of the access or relationships that justified the hire;
an irreparably broken artist-team relationship; or divergence between the team
member's scope and the artist's needs (a boutique agent appropriate at small rooms
is no longer appropriate at large ones).

**Process rule:** before any separation, route to the legal specialist for
agreement review — most agreements have specific termination provisions, and
violating them creates liability.

**Practitioner insight:** the hardest personnel decision is parting ways with a
team member who was formative in the artist's career but has been outpaced by the
artist's growth. Loyalty is real and valuable, but keeping a team member who cannot
perform at the current level because of past loyalty is a tax the artist pays, not
the manager. The manager's obligation is to the artist's future, not the team
member's past contribution.
