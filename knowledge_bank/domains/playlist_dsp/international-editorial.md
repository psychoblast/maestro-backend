# Playlist and DSP — International Editorial Ecosystems

Two-layer file: GENERAL FRAMEWORKS (domain-neutral) + MUSIC MODULES (music-specific).
Chart methodologies and platform shares change; figures are ESTIMATES unless confirmed against
a primary source, and territory rules are re-verified each cycle.

---

## LAYER 1 — GENERAL FRAMEWORKS

### Territory editorial reasoning

1. **A territory is not a translation of a home market.** Editorial teams are segmented by
   language and region; a single "global push" rarely exists editorially. Each meaningful
   territory has its own gatekeepers, calendar, and audience contract.
2. **Distribution infrastructure gates access.** Several markets favor domestic distribution and
   licensing; without the right local distributor or licensing partner, the editorial door is
   structurally narrower regardless of pitch quality. Choosing the distributor is therefore a
   territory-strategy decision.
3. **Local programming moments outrank the generic calendar.** Cultural seasons and market-
   specific rituals reshape listening behavior and editorial themes; a territory pitch timed to
   the local moment beats a home-calendar pitch.
4. **Chart weight is cultural, not only economic.** In some markets chart position drives album
   optics, fan-community status, booking leverage, and press coverage far beyond streaming
   revenue — so the charting strategy differs from a pure-streaming market.

## LAYER 2 — MUSIC MODULES

### Korea

- Domestic platforms are integrated with telecoms and the local industry; chart performance
  carries cultural and commercial weight beyond streaming economics.
- The largest domestic platform weights **downloads heavily alongside streaming** with a
  unique-listener floor (one listener counts once per hour regardless of play count, as an
  anti-manipulation measure). Heavy download weighting makes individual-track purchase campaigns
  relevant for charting in a way they are not on Western platforms.
- A real-time hourly chart drives coordinated fan listening sessions on release day — the
  industry norm, calibrated against the unique-listener floor rather than banned.
- The official aggregate chart blends streaming, download, and broadcast sub-charts; exact
  inter-component weighting is not published in English (ESTIMATE). Certification tiers begin at
  the platinum level (no gold tier) and scale up to very high stream/download thresholds.
- A separate real-time physical-sales chart tracks first-week physical figures — the
  most-watched release metric for the local idol scene.
- **Access for international acts** generally requires a domestic distribution or co-release
  partnership; the largest global platforms maintain local editorial staff that are more
  accessible to Western-market acts. Artificial chart manipulation is actively detected and
  penalized, so authentic charting is itself a credibility signal.

### Japan

- The world's second-largest market by revenue, with the largest physical-CD market; streaming
  surpassed downloads later here than in the West, and physical retains structural chart weight.
- Domestic platforms include a messaging-app-integrated subscription leader (now-playing and
  playlist shares inside conversations are a native discovery mechanic with no equivalent
  elsewhere), a label-and-tech-backed service targeting younger listeners, a loyalty-points-
  integrated service, and a still-meaningful download store.
- The dominant chart authority runs both a physical-only legacy chart and a combined chart that
  includes streaming, but the stream-to-point conversion is high — physical releases retain
  chart dominance, and streaming cannot easily substitute for physical without very large volume.
- **Karaoke is a real discovery channel:** licensing tracks to the major karaoke platforms
  (requiring local licensing and publishing administration) exposes them to a very large active
  patron base; rising karaoke search/play counts are a market signal that can precede streaming
  growth.

### Southeast Asia

- Not a single editorial market. A regional Chinese-language-strong platform leads in its core
  market; a Tencent-family platform covers several markets; the largest global platform is
  dominant or near-dominant in others with dedicated regional editorial teams.
- Territory-by-territory strategy is required: English-dominant pop in one market, local-language
  dominance in others, each with distinct idol/pop scenes. There is no single regional push.

### China

- All major platforms sit under two parent entities; international music requires **domestic
  copyright licensing** through a local agent or direct agreement. Western distributors typically
  lack direct integration, so dedicated local distribution setup should be assumed for catalog
  not already present.
- The largest platform has broad demographic reach; a second major platform is known for a
  younger/indie base and a **comment-section culture** where an emotionally resonant, well-seeded
  comment can drive organic discovery independent of streaming volume. This comment mechanic is
  the single biggest local promotion differentiator — and it requires native community
  management, not just distribution.

### India

- The world's fastest-growing market by user volume: high monthly users, low ARPU (per-stream
  rates are a small fraction of US rates — ESTIMATE). The value is audience scale and catalog
  building, not per-stream revenue.
- Telecom-bundled platforms lead via mobile-data integration (a reach mechanic with no Western
  equivalent); the largest global platform grows among urban/English-speaking listeners with a
  local editorial team.
- **Language segmentation is decisive:** the national language dominates, but several regional-
  language music ecosystems are major in their own right, each with separate editorial teams. A
  national-editorial pitch is not the same as a regional-language pitch.

### MENA

- A dominant Arabic-language platform leads across the region, with the largest global platforms
  growing and running dedicated regional editorial playlists and teams covering the Gulf and
  North Africa.
- **The fasting-month season is the single most significant programming moment:** themed
  editorial, sharply shifted (late-night) listening behavior, and brand campaigns tied to the
  season. A season-timed editorial strategy is materially different from a standard-calendar one.

### Afrobeats and Sub-Saharan Africa

- A continent-dominant platform leads on-continent (Afrobeats, Amapiano, Highlife, Gospel make
  up the bulk of streams) with editorial integrated into the local industry; a second platform
  is the primary play for the diaspora abroad via a free-tier-dominant model; the largest global
  platform grows fast as smartphone and data access improve, with regional Afrobeats curators
  based locally.
- A common practitioner sequence: on-continent editorial first (home base) → diaspora organic
  seeding → global-platform Afrobeats editorial (crossover). Each stage serves a different
  audience and career objective.
