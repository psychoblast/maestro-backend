# Playlist and DSP — Pitch Theory

Two-layer file: GENERAL FRAMEWORKS (domain-neutral) + MUSIC MODULES (music-specific).

---

## LAYER 1 — GENERAL FRAMEWORKS

### Gatekeeper pitch theory

1. **The gatekeeper's economics come first.** Attention is their scarcest resource, and a bad
   yes costs them more than a missed gem (their audience's trust, their own standing). Every
   pitch is read through that asymmetry: the job is to **lower the risk of yes**, not raise the
   volume of ask.
2. **Specificity is the credibility currency.** "Fits between X and Y on your list because Z"
   is checkable in seconds; "amazing track, huge potential" is noise that costs the sender
   future reads. Superlatives are deleted; evidence stays.
3. **One ask per pitch.** A pitch with two asks has zero. The ask names the exact placement
   context so that yes is a small, concrete decision.
4. **Proof hierarchy.** Verifiable behavior the gatekeeper can check > third-party validation
   (coverage, peers) > sender claims. Lead with the highest tier available; never pad with the
   lowest.
5. **The no is information.** Rejections and silence are logged with their context; a pitch
   program that does not learn from its no's is a slot machine.
6. **The right to pitch is earned between pitches.** Cadence, conduct on rejection, and
   delivered fit determine whether the next pitch gets read at all.

### Pre-send checklist (every pitch, any domain)

- **Fit gate completed first** — the fit gate runs before any send decision, including a
  refusal. The gate result is shown. Refusing without running the gate is a named failure mode.
- **Named fit rationale present** — refuse to send without one (hard anti-pattern).
- **Weak-fit check** — if the judged playlist-fit verdict is WEAK, the send is soft-blocked by
  default; state the specific fix and proceed only on an explicit artist override. Soft blocks
  are overridable; hard blocks (failed fit gate, pay-for-play) are not.
- **Ask is single and concrete** — recipient effort to say yes is minimized.
- **Every claim evidence-tagged internally** — nothing in the pitch overstates what we can show.
- **Timing respects the gatekeeper's decision calendar**, not our excitement.
- **The send is logged as a falsifiable fit prediction** (add/no-add by window; retention).

### Pitch-construction bars (checkable quality rules)

- Subject line under ~8 words and outlet-specific.
- Body kept to at most three short paragraphs in a fixed order: **who** (artist + one defining
  quality) → **fit** (the track + why it fits THIS curator/list) → **ask** (one clear request:
  a listen, a feature, or a playlist add).
- No filler, no flattery. A fixed, honest sign-off identity.
- **Minimum proof bar before a pitch is sendable:** at least one verifiable proof point
  (a checkable metric, a named placement, or real coverage) OR an explicit, honest positioning
  of the artist as unproven-but-fitting. A pitch with zero proof and zero honest framing is not
  sent — it spends trust on a coin flip.

## LAYER 2 — MUSIC MODULES

### DSP pitch tools (the official channels)

- **Editorial pitch is tool-mediated, not relationship-mediated.** The major platforms expose
  an official pre-release pitch flow (e.g., the artist-facing portal each platform runs). The
  pitch tool *is* the channel; there is no legitimate side door to editorial. Lobbying editors
  outside the tool is a withdrawal, not a shortcut.
- **Fields gate, prose persuades.** Genre/mood/instrumentation tags and structured fields are
  gates that must be honest and precise — mis-tagging to sneak onto a list is a withdrawal
  against the *platform* account. The written pitch field carries the fit case.
- **The pitch can pay even when editorial passes.** On several platforms, completing the
  pre-release pitch also unlocks an algorithmic follower-reach surface (a new-release feed for
  followers) regardless of the editorial decision — so the pitch is worth submitting even when
  an editorial add is unlikely. Missing the pitch window can cost two surfaces, not one.

### Pitch timing and lead-time norms

- **Editorial lead time — pitch as early as the flow allows.** A documented hard floor of about
  7 days before release exists on the largest platform for guaranteed consideration, but the
  practical norm is materially longer: editorial calendars fill forward, and curators commonly
  state a two-weeks-before-release preference. Working defaults: submit at ~4 weeks when assets
  allow, treat ~2 weeks as the comfortable floor, and ~7 days as the hard documented floor —
  and **re-verify the documented floor against the platform surface each release cycle**, since
  these rules change without notice.
- **Independent curator lead time — a different calendar.** Independents program on personal
  rhythms (many weekly). The working default is pitch-on-release-or-after (finished, live
  tracks — unlike editorial's pre-release window), with a curator's own stated submission
  cadence always overriding the default (told-evidence).
- **Release-day discipline.** *Before* release day: editorial pitch submitted (it gates the
  follower-reach surface too), metadata final and honest, ISRC present, assets live. *After* is
  fine for: independent outreach, follow-ups, and post-placement reads. A release that misses
  the pre-release checklist gets a flagged read, not a silent pass.

### Pitch composition (editorial vs. independent)

- **Editorial pitch field:** fit-forward, factual, scannable — genre/mood precision, audience
  evidence, story only where it changes programming value.
- **Independent curator pitch:** shorter, named-list-specific, with the one-listen proof up
  front; it respects that a human with a backlog is reading it.
- **Anti-spam and cadence constraints (production rules):** a daily send ceiling (a product
  governor — treat ~50 sends/day as the working cap); tiered follow-up timing (A = 3 days,
  B = 5 days, C = 7 days); at most one follow-up per pitch unless the curator engages; all
  inbound free-text inputs sanitized before use; and a human-handling lane for any reply that
  needs escalation. Cadence violations are a checkable-gate failure — the pitch does not go out.
