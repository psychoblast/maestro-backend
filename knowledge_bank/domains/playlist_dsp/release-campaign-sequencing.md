# Playlist and DSP — Release Campaign Sequencing

Two-layer file: GENERAL FRAMEWORKS (domain-neutral) + MUSIC MODULES (music-specific).
Campaign calendars and timing frameworks are defaults — adapt to the specific artist context,
the platform rules in force at release time, and the observed signal behavior during the
campaign. All response-threshold figures are ESTIMATES unless confirmed against measured
outcomes.

---

## LAYER 1 — GENERAL FRAMEWORKS

### Campaign sequencing as a discipline

A playlist and DSP campaign is not a pitch event — it is a **sequenced series of moves across
multiple surfaces and economies, each timed to the signals the prior moves generate.** The
most common failure mode is treating the campaign as a single moment (submission day) rather
than an arc. The arc has three phases:

1. **Pre-release:** prepare the surfaces, submit the editorial pitches, build the demand
   signal before the track is live.
2. **Release window:** activate all surfaces in the right order within the critical early
   window when algorithmic signals are most weighted.
3. **Post-release:** respond to the signals the first window generates, route catalog plays
   to compound discovery, and close the prediction log for this campaign.

Every phase has specific inputs (things that must have happened), actions (moves made in
this phase), and outputs (what the phase produces for the next phase). Missing a phase input
collapses the phase's effectiveness — and the damage is not always recoverable. The editorial
pitch window cannot be retroactively opened; a cold-audience algorithmic surface cannot be
reseeded once the first-72-hour window closes with bad signals.

### Cross-economy sequencing logic (the default reasoning)

The three playlist economies (editorial / algorithmic / independent) have different input
requirements, lead times, and feedback speeds. The default sequencing logic is:

**Independent outreach first (weeks -8 to -1):**
Independent curators can receive pitches on live or near-live tracks. Their adds generate
real listening data and early engagement signals that inform the algorithmic seeding
environment at release. Early independent adds also build a data case (adds, engagement
patterns, curator feedback) that can be referenced in the editorial pitch.

**Editorial pitch next (weeks -4 to -1, platform-specific):**
Editorial pitch tools accept pre-release tracks within their platform windows (see
pitch-theory.md for per-platform lead times). The editorial pitch should be submitted as
early as the platform allows; the practical floor is 2 weeks before release on most
platforms. The data case from early independent traction, if available, strengthens the
pitch.

**Algorithmic seeding at and after release (week 0 and beyond):**
Algorithmic surfaces are activated by release; no pitch is involved. The pre-release work
(editorial pitch submission — which often unlocks follower-reach surfaces regardless of the
editorial decision — plus independent adds generating early listener signals) creates the
conditions for strong algorithmic seeding. The artist's job in the first 72 hours is to
drive quality organic traffic from every available channel so the algorithm's initial data
picture is strong.

**This is a default, not a rule.** The sequence is adjusted when: the artist has no
independent traction (and the editorial pitch is the first bet), or the platform's editorial
window is longer (allowing more time for independent traction data to accumulate before
pitch), or the release timeline is compressed. Document the adjusted sequence as a logged
prediction; compare outcomes to the default to improve the model.

## LAYER 2 — MUSIC MODULES

### Pre-release campaign calendar (T-minus weeks)

**T-8 weeks (8 weeks before release date):**
- Confirm release date with distributor, lock metadata (title, artist name, ISRC, UPC,
  explicit flag, genre tags, release date). Metadata changes after T-4 can delay delivery
  or break pre-save links.
- Begin independent curator target list assembly. Run the vetting playbook on all targets.
  A target identified at T-8 can be warmed (if cold state) before the pitch.
- Audit the artist's existing independent-curator relationship states. A warm relationship
  at T-8 allows a non-pitch "heads up" contact (deposit) before pitching — do not waste a
  warm relationship by leading with the ask.

**T-6 weeks:**
- Independent outreach begins for pre-established relationships (warm/opened state
  curators). Frame: "upcoming release — wanted to flag before the formal pitch comes
  through." No ask yet; this is a deposit in the trust account.
- Begin collecting any available early-engagement data (acoustic fingerprint registration,
  sync inquiry engagement, social early releases for feedback) that could serve as proof in
  the editorial pitch.

**T-4 weeks:**
- Submit the editorial pitch on platforms with a pre-release pitch tool. Target: submit at
  T-4 to give the best odds of calendar consideration. Do not wait; editorial calendars fill
  forward.
- Complete the editorial pitch checklist before submitting: metadata complete and locked,
  ISRC present, distribution confirmed, genre and mood tags accurate and honest, pitch prose
  reviewed against the quality bars (subject under 8 words, 3-paragraph structure, named fit
  rationale, single ask, no flattery).
- Set up pre-save campaign infrastructure (landing page linking to all platforms, links
  verified). Pre-save campaign goes live at T-4 or later.

**T-2 weeks (the comfortable editorial floor):**
- First formal independent pitch batch goes out (cold and opened state curators in tier A).
  Each pitch is logged as a falsifiable fit prediction with expected add/no-add window.
- Editorial pitch has been submitted; no further editorial action is possible except
  responding if the team reaches out.
- Monitor pre-save count as a leading fan-intent indicator; flag to marketing if conversion
  is materially below expectation (tells nothing about algorithmic future but signals fan
  engagement).

**T-1 week:**
- Second independent pitch batch (tier B and C curators).
- Confirm all release-day assets are live: distributor delivery confirmed, verified artist
  page links working, music-video or static-art-with-audio uploaded where applicable.
- Brief artist on release-day posting cadence — organic social traffic on release day feeds
  algorithmic seeding; uncoordinated posting is a missed opportunity.
- Check follow-up timing for tier A pitches sent at T-2: if A-tier follow-up window (3 days)
  falls within the release week, schedule it.

### Release week mechanics (T-0 to T+7)

**Release day (T+0):**
- Verify the release is live on all platforms. Check for delivery failures (missing platforms,
  metadata errors, wrong audio file). Any issue is a distributor escalation — handle
  immediately.
- Confirm the follower-reach new-release feed activated (where applicable). This requires the
  editorial pitch to have been submitted and a minimum release gap from the prior release.
  Missing this confirmation is a named checklist item.
- Artist social campaign fires: direct-to-stream links, artist commentary, fan engagement.
  The goal is diverse, organic, immediately-converting traffic in the first 24 hours — this
  is the single most algorithmically valuable traffic the artist's own team can generate.
- No additional pitches on release day; let the release find its initial signal.

**T+1 to T+3 (the algorithmic signal window):**
- Monitor: save rate from first-day listeners, completion rate vs. genre estimate, early-skip
  rate in opening seconds, follow conversion from new listeners. These signals will largely
  determine whether algorithmic push materializes. Do not intervene in the algorithm — this
  window is observational.
- If editorial placements fire in this window (the platform team made their decision),
  monitor the placement cohort's engagement separately. Poor engagement from an editorial
  cohort is a fit signal worth logging.
- Complete tier A follow-ups for any independent curators in the opened window.

**T+4 to T+7:**
- First-week signal read: compile save rate, completion, early-skip, user-playlist adds,
  follow conversion. Compare against ESTIMATE baselines and flag any dimension materially
  below expectation.
- If signals are strong: amplify organic channels that generated the best engagement (social
  posts, playlist adds, editorial coverage) to extend the window.
- If signals are weak in the early-skip or completion dimension: flag the specific signal;
  do not confuse a post-production note (weak outro) with an algorithmic intervention.
- Complete remaining tier B independent follow-ups.
- If the platform with the post-release editorial window is part of the strategy, the
  pre-release pitch was not submitted there (this platform's window opens post-release);
  submit now, supported by T+1–T+7 engagement data if positive.

### Post-release management (T+8 to T+90)

**T+8 to T+14:**
- P5 early-read window: if an editorial placement happened, read post-placement data as
  available but render P5 as NOT_EVALUABLE(window) until ≥7 days post-add. Report the
  mandatory early-read fact set (position, day-1/2 exposure) without conversion verdicts.
- Complete tier C independent outreach follow-ups.
- Log the first-week prediction resolution for any predictions made at pitch time.

**T+14 to T+30:**
- Full P5 read for editorial placements (≥7 days post-add): save rate by source, follow
  conversion from placement cohort, skip behavior, position and time-on-list.
- Independent outreach continues for any un-pitched targeted curators.
- Consider catalog enrollment programs (discovery-mode equivalents) for the track if the
  week-1 organic signal was moderate and the artist is building catalog. Enroll only after
  the primary royalty window; follow the enrollment decision framework from the algorithmic
  mechanics file.
- Report the first full post-placement read to the owner if any editorial placement
  occurred.

**T+30 to T+90:**
- Compound any algorithmic lift: catalog add to bio playlists, cross-promotion in artist
  social to catalog listeners.
- Removal post-mortems for any editorial placements that ended: distinguish list refresh
  (neutral), under-performance (log the fit miss — our call was wrong), and list death
  (their problem, not ours).
- Close all open prediction log entries from the release cycle.
- Update curator memory records with final interaction outcomes for this release.

### Fast starter vs. slow starter protocols

Not all releases behave identically in the first window. Protocol differences:

**Fast starter (strong signals in T+1 to T+3):**
- Do not pause the campaign. Amplify: more independent outreach, cross-platform social
  content, any editorial follow-up opportunities.
- Flag the strong signals to marketing and to the owner for context on ancillary decisions.
- Extend the independent pitch window — additional curators may be worth approaching while
  the momentum signal is observable. A live data case strengthens any pitch.
- Resist the temptation to enroll in discovery programs while the organic signal is strong
  and the royalty window is active. Organic momentum does the work; don't trade rate for
  discoverability you don't need.

**Slow starter (weak or mixed signals in T+1 to T+3):**
- Do not panic-pivot to paid promotion on the basis of T+1 to T+3 data alone; the window
  is too narrow to be conclusive. Complete the planned campaign.
- At T+7, run the full signal read. If save rate and completion are both below ESTIMATE
  baseline, and early-skip is above estimate, this is a fit-diagnosis signal: the initial
  audience may not be the right one, or the track has an opening-seconds problem.
- The correct response to a genuine slow-start is: (a) investigate the signal pattern for
  a specific diagnosis, (b) adjust the independent targeting to reach a more-fit audience
  cohort, (c) do NOT use paid promotion to amplify weak organic signals — that is the
  canonical "amplify signals that don't exist" error.
- Escalate the slow-start read to the owner before any spend decision.

**No data (very small artist, very few streams in T+1 to T+3):**
- The algorithmic seeding base may simply be too small to produce observable signals. This
  is not a failure mode — it is a stage-of-career reality.
- Refocus on independent outreach and direct fan engagement as the primary early-window
  strategy. Each real listen from a real listener is the signal base being built.
- Do not misread the absence of algorithmic signals as algorithmic failure; the system
  cannot signal on zero data.

### Multi-release catalog campaign management

When managing a catalog with multiple active releases:

- **Pitch capacity is a shared resource.** The daily send ceiling (~50/day) and the per-
  curator cadence rules apply across all releases, not per release. A high volume of
  simultaneous releases does not unlock proportionally more outreach capacity.
- **Prioritize releases by editorial window.** A release with an approaching editorial pitch
  deadline takes pitch capacity priority over a release in the post-window phase.
- **Stagger release dates deliberately.** Simultaneous releases compete for the same
  follower-reach surfaces, editorial attention, and curator bandwidth. A deliberate stagger
  (minimum 2–4 weeks between primary releases) allows each release to hold the editorial
  pitch window without competing with the previous one for the same curator attention.
- **Maintain per-release prediction logs.** Each release's pitch predictions, outcomes, and
  post-placement reads are tracked separately. Do not aggregate across releases; the learning
  signal is per-release.
- **Catalog releases (older than 90 days without active campaign)** are eligible for
  algorithmic enrollment programs and for independent outreach when playlist fit is
  re-discovered (a new playlist created in the relevant genre is a pitch opportunity for
  the right catalog track). Catalog campaigns do not have editorial windows on most
  platforms but can be re-pitched on platforms with post-release windows.

### Campaign completion — closing the loop

A campaign is not complete when the release day passes. It is complete when:

1. All prediction log entries from the pitch program are resolved (add/no-add confirmed,
   post-placement reads for any adds completed).
2. All curator memory records updated with final outcome evidence.
3. The campaign summary report is produced (what was pitched, what was placed, P5 reads for
   all placements, fit-call accuracy versus logged predictions, and the next-cycle
   recommendation: continue this lane / fix before next pitch / stop).
4. Any algorithmic enrollment decisions for the track have been made and documented.

Leaving a campaign open (unresolved predictions, un-updated curator records) is the
equivalent of flying the next release without navigational data from the last one. The
prediction log and curator memory are the compound-learning investment; neglecting them
zeros the return on all prior pitching work.
