Version: v1.0 — 2026-06-22

## GENERAL FRAMEWORKS

### A&R's Role in the Deal Process

A&R owns the artistic case; Business Affairs (BA) owns the deal construction. The boundary matters:

- **A&R's deal inputs:** ceiling tier, development phase, investment case estimate, deal-type fit recommendation, rights flags, team quality signals
- **Business Affairs' deal inputs:** advance sizing, royalty rate negotiation, recoupment structure, contract language, legal rights carve-outs
- **Overlap zone (requires both):** option commitment structure, delivery requirements, exclusivity scope

**Practitioner-layer insight:** the most common A&R error in deal handoff is under-specifying the investment case and leaving BA to make ceiling-tier assumptions. A vague "this artist has major potential" brief produces a deal structure that may be right for a Global Superstar ceiling when the A&R's actual assessment was National. State the ceiling tier explicitly in the handoff to BA; they cannot size advances or royalty recoupment correctly without it.

## DEAL TYPE TAXONOMY

### The Six Core Deal Structures

| Deal type | Rights scope | Who carries investment risk | Typical ceiling tier match | A&R flag |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------------|---------------------------|----------|
| **360 Deal (Multiple Rights Deal)** | Recording + some or all of: touring, publishing, sync, merchandise, brand, name/likeness | Label | Global Superstar, International | High scrutiny — broad rights capture; requires strong ceiling case and transparent revenue projections |
| **Traditional Recording Deal** | Master recording rights only (no touring/publishing/brand) | Label | National to International | Standard major-label instrument; scope is clear |
| **License Deal** | Time-limited exclusive license on existing master(s); artist retains copyright | Label (for license term) | Emerging to Developing; or established act with existing catalog | Good for artists who have leverage and want rights back; label carries distribution/promotion risk |
| **Joint Venture (JV)** | Shared ownership of masters; shared costs and revenues | Label and artist split | International to Global Superstar | Higher complexity; requires robust legal and accounting infrastructure |
| **Development Deal** | Option on future recordings; label funds development without committing to a full album release | Label | Emerging to Developing | Low commitment from label; gives label option while artist builds |
| **Production Deal** | Label-within-a-label structure; a producer or production company signs the artist and delivers to the upstream label | Production entity | Emerging to Developing | Artist has two masters: the upstream label and the production entity; negotiation complexity is higher |

**Practitioner-layer insight:** 360 deals receive significant criticism from artists and advocates, but the critique is specific to deals where revenue participation is broad without a corresponding marketing and development commitment. A 360 deal with explicit label investment commitments in touring, press, and recording is a different instrument than a 360 deal that captures revenue without committing to spend. A&R's job is to match the deal type to the artist's stage and ceiling tier — not to categorically avoid any instrument.

### Development Deal Mechanics (Common in A&R Pipeline Entry)

Development deals are the most common A&R entry point for Emerging-stage artists. Key mechanics:

- **Advance:** small or zero; the label funds development expenses (recording, content, PR) as a line item, not a personal advance
- **Option structure:** label holds option on first album/EP and X subsequent options; each option exercised only if prior-period targets are met
- **Term:** typically 12–24 months per option period; total deal length can reach 5–7 years across all options
- **Delivery requirements:** artist must deliver X commercially releasable masters per period; "commercially releasable" is a defined term in the contract — A&R flags what that standard should be
- **Exit:** if label elects not to exercise an option, artist is free; all development costs advanced may or may not be recoupable (label-specific)

**A&R flag for development deals:** the most consequential A&R input to a development deal is the delivery commitment definition. Too vague → disputes at delivery; too restrictive → artist cannot deliver within the standard; too loose → label cannot maintain quality standard. Recommend a specific standard referenced to the scoring rubric's WATCH-range minimum as the delivery floor.

## ADVANCE MECHANICS

### Advance Sizing: Inputs

A&R provides the ceiling tier and development phase; BA sizes the advance. The inputs that drive sizing:

1. **Ceiling tier** (from scoring rubric): determines the expected revenue base from which the advance will recoup
2. **Development phase at signing:** Phase 1 artists require more upfront investment; lower advance, more development budget; Phase 3 artists command higher advance because less development risk
3. **Streaming baseline:** current monthly listeners and trajectory; BA uses this to project recoupment timelines
4. **Competitive pressure:** is there a competitive situation? A second-bidder scenario compresses BA's negotiating position
5. **Deal structure:** a license deal advance sizes differently than a traditional deal advance (licensing deals have defined term limits that cap the revenue base)

### Recoupment Structure

Recoupment is how the label recoups advances and costs from the artist's royalty share before paying out:

- **Recoupable costs** (label-specific, but typically): recording advance, marketing costs (sometimes), touring support (sometimes), video production; NOT typically recoupable from artist's royalty share: radio promo, press costs, staff
- **Recoupment rate:** advances recoup at the artist's royalty rate (e.g., if the artist earns $0.10 per unit at their royalty rate and the label earns $0.40 per unit, the advance recoups at $0.10 — meaning the label has fully recouped at the label level before the artist has recouped at the artist level)
- **This asymmetry is structural:** an artist can be "unrecouped" with the label while the label has earned a profit on the recording. A&R must communicate this clearly in any artist briefing on deal terms.

### Cross-Collateralization

Cross-collateralization bundles multiple albums (or revenue streams in a 360 deal) for recoupment purposes:

- **Within-album cross-col:** all tracks on an album recoup together — a hit single cannot recoup if the album overall is unrecouped
- **Cross-album cross-col:** Album 2's advance does not begin paying royalties until Album 1's advance is also recouped; common in multi-album deals
- **A&R flag:** cross-collateralization is a significant long-term risk for developing artists; flag to BA when the ceiling tier doesn't clearly support multiple-album commercial performance. A developing artist locked in cross-collateralized multi-album deal with a declining trajectory is structurally unable to exit without the label's consent.

### Royalty Rate Reference Ranges

These are Tier B ESTIMATES from industry reporting (MBW, Music Ally, 2024–2026). Significant variance by artist leverage, deal type, and territory. Never quote in artist negotiations without BA verification.

| Artist stage | Royalty rate range (net receipts basis) | Notes |
|-------------|----------------------------------------|-------|
| Emerging (development deal) | 12%–16% | Lower range; BA negotiates; artist leverage minimal |
| Developing (first album deal) | 16%–22% | Mid-range; depends on competitive interest |
| Established (re-sign or second deal) | 20%–28% | Artist has leverage from prior catalog performance |
| Superstar (major renegotiation) | 25%–35%+ | Artist's leverage maximum; sometimes structured as JV |

**Artist-friendly vs. label-friendly deal flags for A&R awareness:**

| Term | Label-friendly | Artist-friendly | A&R note |
|------|---------------|-----------------|----------|
| Royalty rate | 12%–16% | 22%–28%+ | Flag to BA if rate seems mismatched to ceiling tier |
| Recoupable items | Broad (recording + marketing + tour support) | Narrow (recording only) | Broader recoupment = longer path to royalty payout |
| Cross-collateralization | Yes, all albums | No (per-album recoupment) | Cross-col significantly extends recoupment timelines |
| Option commitment | Label holds options with no minimum spend commitment | Label must commit X spend per option exercise | Spend commitments protect artist from option-holding without development |
| Delivery commitment | Broad discretion ("commercially releasable") | Specific quality standard with A&R sign-off | Vague delivery terms → disputes; A&R should specify the floor |
| Reversion clause | No reversion / long initial term | Reversion after N years if not commercially released | Reversion protects artist from catalog sitting un-released |

## RIGHTS FLAGS FOR A&R

### Pre-Signing IP Review (A&R Flags to Legal)

Before any offer is made, A&R must surface the following for legal review:

**Recording rights:**
- [ ] Does the artist own the masters on existing recordings, or are they encumbered by a prior distribution or label agreement?
- [ ] If existing recordings are subject to a distribution agreement: what is the term and exclusivity scope? Can it be terminated?
- [ ] Are there any co-ownership positions on the masters (producer-owned stems, co-producer points, equity shares granted to producers)?

**Composition rights:**
- [ ] Does the artist own their publishing (compositions)? Are they administered by a publisher?
- [ ] If a co-publishing deal exists: what share does the publisher hold? Is there a reversion clause?
- [ ] Are co-writers' publishing shares cleared for sync and licensing? (Missing co-writer sign-offs block sync — a common development-stage issue)
- [ ] Are there unregistered compositions (no ISWC, no PRO registration)? Flag as a royalty collection gap

**Name and likeness:**
- [ ] Is the artist name available as a trademark in relevant classes in the intended territories? (BA/legal conducts this; A&R flags the importance of confirming before deal close)
- [ ] Is there a prior professional name that could create confusion or legal risk?

**Existing obligations:**
- [ ] Does the artist have an existing management agreement? (Commission scope and term length affect the economics of a development deal)
- [ ] Does the artist have an existing booking agent agreement? (Touring commission carve-out in 360 deal)
- [ ] Is the artist a member of any union or guild (AFM, SAG-AFTRA) with governing collective bargaining agreement implications?

**A&R practitioner note:** rights flags are A&R's first line of defense, not legal's job alone. An A&R who surfaces a rights conflict early saves the deal; one who surfaces it at contract stage loses weeks and sometimes the artist. The IP review list above is the minimum A&R check before any offer is extended.

## OPTION PERIOD STRUCTURES

### Anatomy of a Standard Multi-Album Option Deal

```
CONTRACT STRUCTURE (illustrative):
│
├── Firm Period: Album 1 delivery + release
│     └── Delivery: X masters (commercially releasable)
│     └── Timeline: typically 12–18 months from signing
│     └── Release: label must release within Y months of delivery or reversion clause activates
│
├── Option Period 1: Label option to require Album 2
│     └── Exercised: within Z months of Album 1 commercial release
│     └── Advance: specified at signing or as formula (prior advance × X factor)
│     └── Delivery: same delivery standard
│
└── Option Periods 2–3 (if any): same structure as Option Period 1
```

**A&R inputs to option structure design:**
- **How many options is appropriate?** Match to the development arc. A Phase 1 artist with 3 options locks in a long relationship before market proof; 1–2 options with renegotiation rights at certain commercial milestones is more artist-protective and reduces commitment risk for the label
- **Option exercise trigger:** typically a time trigger (exercise within N days of prior album's commercial release) — but can also include commercial performance triggers ("option only exercisable if prior album has achieved X threshold")
- **Delivery commitment specificity:** A&R must define "commercially releasable" relative to the scoring rubric's minimum standard; vague language invites disputes

### Reversion Clauses: Key Term A&R Should Flag

A reversion clause returns rights to the artist if the label fails to commercially release the delivered material within a defined window:

- **Standard window:** 12–18 months from delivery of a commercially releasable master
- **Reversion trigger:** label has not commercially released the master within the window
- **Effect:** ownership of the master reverts to the artist; label loses all rights
- **A&R implication:** reversion clauses are strongly artist-protective and may face label resistance; flag for BA and note that the artist's development arc suffers most if material is shelved — reversion is a legitimate protection

## OFFER-TO-CLOSE WORKFLOW

### Stage 1: Verbal Offer and LOI

**A&R's role:**
- Present internal recommendation (scoring rubric evaluation, investment case, deal type fit) at A&R committee
- Committee approval triggers verbal offer
- Business Affairs drafts Letter of Intent (LOI) or deal memo with key terms

**LOI minimum content (A&R flags for BA):**
- Artist name and legal entity
- Deal type (recording deal, license, JV, development)
- Advance amount or advance range
- Royalty rate or rate range
- Number of firm albums/EPs + options
- Exclusivity scope
- Territory
- Any rights beyond recording (360 scope if applicable)

**Timeline:** LOI should issue within 5–7 business days of verbal offer; a delayed LOI signals internal uncertainty and damages the artist's confidence

### Stage 2: Due Diligence

**A&R's due diligence items:**
- [ ] Live performance review: has A&R seen the artist perform live? If not, schedule before close
- [ ] Streaming data pull: current Spotify for Artists, Apple Music for Artists data — save rate, monthly listener trajectory, completion rate on key tracks
- [ ] Social audit: content consistency, engagement quality (comments, not just likes), UGC creation
- [ ] Team review: confirm manager identity, track record, conflicts; confirm attorney's experience level
- [ ] Rights status: confirm IP review items above are cleared or flagged

**Legal due diligence items (BA/Legal; A&R surfaces, not conducts):**
- [ ] Prior label or distribution agreement review
- [ ] Publishing agreement review
- [ ] Management agreement review
- [ ] Trademark search on artist name

**Red flag list — A&R STOP signals during due diligence:**

| Signal | Risk level | A&R action |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Artist has signed a poorly-understood prior distribution agreement with broad exclusivity | HIGH | Surface immediately to BA; deal may be blocked until prior agreement expires or is terminated |
| Manager has a history of deal disputes or has been replaced by other clients at the same stage | HIGH | Flag to committee; consider whether to proceed without manager change |
| Live performance is structurally weaker than recordings suggest | MEDIUM-HIGH | Re-evaluate Execution pillar; consider whether this changes the ceiling tier |
| Save rate is declining on the most recent release (not flat — declining) | MEDIUM | Re-evaluate trajectory; confirm this is not an anomaly before proceeding |
| Co-writer publishing shares for key compositions are unresolved | MEDIUM | Flag to BA; sync eligibility blocked until resolved; affects Commercial Opportunity pillar |
| Artist has been through prior development deals with no release — pattern of shelved recordings | HIGH | Investigate root cause; prior shelving may indicate identity/delivery conflict that will recur |

### Stage 3: Deal Negotiation

**A&R's role in negotiation:**
- Attend or brief the key stakeholder who is negotiating (typically the label head, not A&R directly)
- Provide real-time intelligence on competing offers if a bidding situation exists (see below)
- Flag any concessions that affect the development plan (e.g., a shorter recording commitment changes the release cadence plan)
- Advocate for delivery commitment language that protects the development arc

**A&R does NOT:**
- Quote rates or advances to the artist or their attorney (this is BA's role)
- Make binding commitments on label spending (this is label head / GM role)
- Negotiate contract language (this is legal's role)

### Stage 4: Close and Handoff

**A&R's close checklist:**
- [ ] Confirm all due diligence items resolved
- [ ] Confirm rights flags cleared or documented as known risks
- [ ] Brief A&R committee on final deal terms vs. the initial investment case
- [ ] Prepare artist handoff note for the A&R-to-Ops transition (who is the A&R contact, what is the development plan, what is the release timeline, what are the first-6-month priorities)
- [ ] Log the signing to the prediction log (scoring rubric section) with the agreed deal structure, ceiling tier, and trajectory assessment

## COMPETITIVE BIDDING SITUATIONS

### The Urgency Test

When an artist's team reports competing interest, A&R must apply the urgency test before accelerating:

**Real urgency signals:**
- A named competitive label (with reputation in this genre/territory) has confirmed interest in writing
- The artist has a live offer in hand with a defined response window
- A public announcement of signing is imminent from a competing bidder

**Manufactured urgency signals:**
- "Multiple labels are interested" without names or written confirmation
- Urgency claimed by the manager without an artist-confirmed competing offer
- Artificial deadline set by the manager that does not correspond to any external event

**A&R response to confirmed real urgency:**
1. Accelerate the internal process: compress due diligence to the critical items; bypass standard waiting periods
2. Brief BA immediately with the competitive situation; BA needs to know to sharpen the advance offer
3. Escalate to the label head for real-time decision authority

**Practitioner-layer insight:** the highest-cost A&R mistake in a competitive situation is losing a correctly-evaluated GREENLIGHT artist to a competing label because the internal process moved at its default pace. The second-highest-cost mistake is overpaying for an artist because manufactured urgency bypassed due diligence. The way to avoid both is applying the urgency test rigorously before escalating.

### Bidding Discipline Principles

- **Never bid against yourself.** Once a fair offer is on the table, the competitive response is to wait for the counter, not to preemptively increase the offer.
- **The best deal for the artist is not always the highest advance.** A deal with marketing commitment, active A&R attention, and appropriate team infrastructure may serve the artist better than a higher advance from a label that will warehouse the signing. A&R should be willing to make this case to the artist's team.
- **Know your walk-away point.** Every bidding situation should have a pre-defined ceiling at which the label will decline to increase its offer. Competitive dynamics can override internal ceiling assessments if no walk-away discipline exists.

## DEAL PITFALL REFERENCE

### Most Common A&R Deal Failures (by Stage)

**Pre-offer:**
- Signing before IP review: encumbered rights discovered post-offer, forcing renegotiation or withdrawal
- Ceiling tier miscall: A&R stated International ceiling; BA sized a Superstar-level advance; artist trajectory never supports recoupment

**During negotiation:**
- Delivery commitment too vague: artist and label dispute what constitutes a "commercially releasable" master; no objective reference standard in the contract
- Manager conflict undiscovered: manager has a financial interest in a competing label; creates incentive conflict in deal negotiations
- Option exercise window not specified: label holds option without a defined exercise trigger; artist is in deal limbo

**Post-signing:**
- Development plan not documented: A&R handoff note never prepared; incoming team does not know the ceiling tier or release plan
- Rights flag not logged: known co-writer publishing dispute not documented; surfaced at sync opportunity, blocking the deal
- Trajectory stall not triggered: continue/restructure/release metrics not set at signing; label continues investment without a review trigger; sunk cost bias takes over

## CATALOG ACQUISITION VS. NEW SIGNING: DEAL STRUCTURE DIFFERENCES

When A&R is evaluating catalog acquisition (existing recorded masters) vs. a new signing (prospective recording relationship), the deal mechanics differ materially:

| Dimension | New signing | Catalog acquisition |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| Rights being acquired | Future recordings | Existing masters (already recorded) |
| Due diligence focus | Artist trajectory, team, IP clearance | Rights chain verification, royalty history, income analysis |
| Advance structure | Against future royalties | Against projected catalog income stream |
| Ceiling tier assessment | Career trajectory based | Catalog income ceiling (sync, streaming, licensing) |
| A&R's role | Artistic case + development plan | Artistic validation + catalog quality assessment |
| Timeline to value | 12–36 months (development arc) | Immediate (catalog income begins at acquisition) |
| Risk profile | Development risk dominant | Rights risk and income-decline risk dominant |

**A&R's role in catalog acquisition:** A&R validates catalog quality (does the catalog merit the projected income ceiling?), assesses the artist's ongoing creative relevance (does buying the catalog also lock in or exclude a future recording relationship?), and flags any catalog gaps that affect value (missing key tracks, rights encumbrances, streaming eligibility gaps in any territory).

Catalog acquisitions are primarily financial/legal transactions with an A&R quality-validation role — they are NOT artist development decisions. The scoring rubric composite does NOT apply; use the career stage framework (Legacy stage) and the catalog income ceiling assessment instead.
