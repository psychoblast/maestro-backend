# Deal Economics
Version: v1.0 — PLMKR Brand Connect

Note: All deal economic figures — consideration ranges, royalty rates, revenue share percentages, and advance ranges — are ESTIMATE / NOT QUOTABLE unless sourced from a specific verified comparable with stated vintage. Deal-term rates are deal-confidential by category and are never stated as fact.

---

## Economics as an Independent Assessment Dimension

Deal economics measure whether the artist captures meaningful, durable financial value from the deal under realistic assumptions — not the best-case projection, and not the theoretical maximum if every assumption goes right. The purpose of Dim-2 is to produce an honest assessment of the deal's financial quality in the base case, with the downside case understood and bounded.

The separation of Dim-2 (Economic Value) from Dim-1 (Strategic Value) is structural and deliberate. A deal's strategic leverage does not improve its economics, and strong economics do not compensate for absent leverage. Each dimension scores on its own evidence. An artist that evaluates a deal primarily on economics while treating leverage as a bonus, or that accepts thin economics because the leverage story is compelling, has collapsed a two-variable analysis into one.

## The Four Economic Structures

All brand deal economics can be analyzed through four structure types. A proposed deal may combine elements of more than one structure type; assess each independently before combining.

**Cash consideration.** The brand pays the artist a defined amount — an upfront fee, a campaign fee, or an ambassador retainer — for the association with the artist's creative or talent assets. Cash consideration is the simplest mechanism to value and the most reliable. Key questions: Is the amount material relative to the artist's opportunity cost and delivery obligations? Is payment scheduled front-loaded (at least 50% upfront) or backend-weighted? Is there a kill fee if the brand cancels?

**In-kind consideration.** The brand provides goods, services, or access in place of or in addition to cash. In-kind value requires explicit valuation at the artist's actual utility, not the brand's retail value — the artist captures in-kind value only if they can use it at a value comparable to what they would otherwise spend. Over-valuing in-kind consideration is a common artist error in brand deal assessment.

**Co-marketing investment.** The brand commits a defined marketing spend or audience reach in support of the artist's music or career. Co-marketing investment is an economic benefit to the artist equal to the value of the promotional exposure the artist would otherwise have to purchase independently. This value is ESTIMATE — it depends on the brand's actual promotional delivery, the audience's receptivity, and the attribution of measurable outcomes to the co-marketing activity.

**Revenue participation.** The artist receives a share of the revenue generated by products, events, or campaigns that use the artist's creative or talent assets. Revenue participation is the highest-upside and highest-uncertainty structure — the artist participates in commercial success they cannot fully control. Revenue participation deals must be modeled across a range of commercial outcomes, with the base case reflecting realistic brand performance, not the brand's promotional projections.

## Net Economics vs. Headline Consideration

Brand partnerships are the deal category most likely to state a headline consideration figure that does not reflect net consideration after production costs, approval delay costs, exclusivity costs, and opportunity costs. Always evaluate the net figure, not the headline.

**Production cost obligations.** A brand deal that requires the artist to deliver high-production content — professional photography, video shoots, live event appearances — carries cost obligations that reduce net consideration. A deal with a headline fee of $X and $Y in required production costs that the artist must fund nets $X minus $Y.

**Approval delay costs.** Brand deals with lengthy multi-layer approval processes (multiple departments, multiple rounds, slow turnaround) create scheduling costs and opportunity costs that are real even if they are not stated in the deal terms. An artist who locks their social calendar for brand approval windows during peak release activity pays a real opportunity cost.

**Exclusivity costs.** Category exclusivity prevents the artist from accepting competing brand deals during the exclusivity period. The value of category exclusivity received (the premium the brand paid for it) must exceed the expected value of the best alternative deal foreclosed during the exclusivity window. If it does not, the exclusivity is an unpriced concession.

**Opportunity costs.** Every brand deal consumes bandwidth — content creation time, management attention, creative alignment meetings. These are real costs that do not appear in the deal document but reduce the net value of the deal relative to its stated consideration.

## Financial Modeling Discipline

A deal's economic quality is assessed through three scenario cases, not through a single projection.

The **base case** uses the most probable set of assumptions given available evidence: the most likely payment schedule, the realistic production cost, and the most probable co-marketing delivery given the brand's track record. The base case is not pessimistic and it is not optimistic — it is the expected outcome.

The **downside case** tests whether the artist can absorb the deal's obligations if the co-marketing commitment is not fully delivered, the brand's approval process creates delays, or the kill fee is insufficient to cover production sunk costs. If the downside case produces a net-negative outcome for the artist, that is a material economic risk that must be named.

The **upside case** defines the ceiling of realistic economic value if performance is strong — brand renews, co-marketing exceeds expectations, revenue participation triggers. Upside cases are often overstated. Label upside projections as ESTIMATE and do not rely on them for the go/no-go basis.

Key assumptions driving each case must be named explicitly. The assumption that, if wrong, would move the economic outcome from base to downside is the single most important disclosure in a deal economic analysis.

## Market-Rate Assessment for Artist Brand Deals

Brand deal consideration is highly variable by artist profile — monthly listeners, engagement rate, social following, career stage, genre, and territory all affect market rate. Market-rate characterizations are ESTIMATE / NOT QUOTABLE and require a stated comparable basis and vintage. Specific deal rates are deal-confidential.

**Considerations for assessing market rate:**
- Deal type (product gifting vs. paid campaign vs. ambassador vs. equity)
- Artist reach (monthly listeners, combined social following, engagement rate)
- Brand category (global consumer goods vs. regional brand vs. emerging brand)
- Deliverable scope (number of posts, content formats, event appearances)
- Exclusivity scope (category exclusive vs. non-exclusive, exclusivity duration)
- Territory (global vs. regional vs. domestic only)

When assessing whether deal economics are above, at, or below market rate, state the comparable class (same deal type, same reach tier, same territory scope), the comparable vintage (flag if more than 18 months old), and the confidence level.

## Kill Fee Structure

A kill fee is the amount the brand pays the artist if the brand cancels the deal after signing or mid-campaign. Kill fees are standard protection in all paid brand partnerships above product-gifting level.

A kill fee should:
- Be defined as a specific percentage of the total contracted consideration or a flat amount
- Be payable within a defined timeline after brand cancellation notice
- Cover at minimum the artist's sunk production costs at the point of cancellation
- Increase proportionally if the brand cancels after specific production milestones are completed

A deal without a kill fee is a deal that places all cancellation risk on the artist. This is an economically unfavorable structure (reduces Dim-2) and a structural risk (reduces Dim-4) — the artist can complete production and receive nothing if the brand cancels without financial consequence.

---

## Payment Schedule Assessment

Payment timing determines how much of the deal's stated value the artist actually captures in the period when they need it most. Best-in-class payment structure is front-loaded: at least 50% at signing or before creative delivery begins, with the remainder due at specific defined milestones, not at the brand's discretion.

Watch for payment structures that are:
- Entirely backend-weighted (full payment at campaign end)
- Contingent on brand-defined performance metrics the artist cannot control
- Paid in installments with no kill fee for canceled installments
- Linked to milestones the brand controls the timing of

Backend-weighted payment that gives the brand effective control over whether the artist is fully compensated is economically unfavorable even when the headline number is competitive. Score it accordingly on Dim-2.

## Dim-2 Application for Artist Brand Deals

The Dim-2 scoring anchors (in the scoring rubric) apply to artist brand partnerships with the following practical mapping:

**Score 1:** Deal is economically destructive — production cost obligations exceed total consideration, no kill fee, payment is entirely contingent on brand-controlled milestones.

**Score 3:** Below-market economics — consideration is directionally unfavorable vs. comparable artist profile and deal type (ESTIMATE); payment is heavily backend-weighted; cost obligations are unclear; no kill fee or minimal kill fee.

**Score 5:** Market-rate economics — consideration is broadly in line with comparable deals (ESTIMATE, stated basis); at least partial upfront payment; kill fee is present; production cost obligations are bounded and known.

**Score 7:** Above-market economics — consideration is favorable vs. comparables (ESTIMATE); at least 50% upfront; kill fee is meaningful relative to production cost risk; upside scenario (renewal, performance bonus) is confirmed; no unbounded cost obligations.

**Score 10:** Strongly favorable economics — consideration is best-in-class for this deal type and artist profile (ESTIMATE, documented comparables); payment is fully upfront or structured with guaranteed installments; kill fee covers full production cost exposure; all obligations bounded by contract; upside scenario confirmed and negotiated.
