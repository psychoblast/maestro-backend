## Press Tier Architecture

Press credibility operates on a two-axis matrix: **cultural authority** (how much the outlet shapes the conversation) and **circulation reach** (how many people can see a placement).

| Tier | Outlets | Authority | Reach | Lead time |
|------|---------|-----------|-------|-----------|
| **T1 — Cultural authority** | Pitchfork, The New Yorker (music), NYT Arts, Rolling Stone (features), The Guardian Music, Resident Advisor | Highest | Moderate | 4–8 weeks for features |
| **T2 — Trade/industry** | Billboard, Variety Music, Music Week (UK), The Fader, NME (features), DIY Magazine | High industry | Moderate | 2–4 weeks for features |
| **T3 — Regional/specialist** | Exclaim! (Canada), KEXP (Seattle), KUTX (Austin), Clash (UK), The 405, regional alt-weeklies, music blogs | Territory authority | Niche | 1–3 weeks |
| **T4 — Consumer lifestyle** | Vogue, GQ, Highsnobiety, i-D, NYLON | Brand adjacency | Broad | 3–6 weeks; needs cultural moment |
| **T5 — Fast/newsletter** | Substack music newsletters, YouTube commentary channels, Twitter/X music critics | Speed + niche depth | Variable | 24–72h; reactive |

**Using the tier matrix:**
- An emerging artist does not have access to T1 editorial — T1 outlets cover artists who already have cultural traction. Pitching T1 before T3 is not ambition; it is inbox noise.
- T3 coverage is not a consolation prize. A strong Exclaim! feature or KEXP First Play is the documented path to T2 and T1 coverage. Editors at T1/T2 outlets monitor T3 for emerging artists.
- T4 coverage requires a hook that transcends the music — a fashion angle, a cultural identity story, a crossover moment.

## Pitch Format Mechanics

| Format | Definition | When to use | Risk |
|--------|-----------|-------------|------|
| **Exclusive** | One outlet gets the story/news/track before any other | Artist milestone events; T1/T2 targets | If the outlet delays or passes, the window closes. Require a clear time limit in the pitch (72h, 1 week) |
| **Premiere** | One outlet hosts the track/video first | Strong visual/audio asset release | Similar to exclusive; lower stakes |
| **Broad/service** | Sent simultaneously to multiple outlets | News items (tour dates, tracklist, award nominations) | Low risk; trades uniqueness for speed |

**The embargo system:**
Embargoed pitches provide journalists advance access under a time-bound non-publication agreement.
- Album pitches: 1–2 weeks advance
- Single pitches: 48–72h advance (longer for T1 features)
- Embargo requirement: explicitly state the embargo lift date/time in the pitch. Broken embargoes from the journalist's side end the PR relationship; broken embargoes from the artist's side destroy T1/T2 relationships permanently.

## Pitch Construction Framework

**Structure of an effective music press pitch:**

1. **Subject line (≤7 words):** the cultural angle in headline form. "Artist X drops new single" is zero-value. "The diaspora grief album three years in the making" earns an open.
2. **Opening sentence:** why *this story* belongs on *this writer's* beat — not "I'm reaching out about a new release," but "Your June feature on Canadian diasporic R&B connects directly to what [Artist] is building on this record."
3. **The story (50–75 words):** what makes this artist's journey or this release worth 600 words? Not what it sounds like — what it *means*, what tension it resolves, what cultural moment it crystallizes.
4. **The proof (2–3 lines):** streaming milestone, prior T3 placement, sync credit, touring evidence, award — something that signals this is not a cold pitch from an unknown act.
5. **The asset (1 link):** a streaming link pre-cued to the best 45 seconds. No downloads. No attachments. No multi-link drops. One link, one instruction: "skip to 1:23 for the hook."
6. **The close:** clear ask (review? feature? premiere? interview?), suggested response SLA, and one contact point.

**Follow-up protocol:**
- One follow-up only, sent 5 business days after the original pitch
- Message: one line acknowledging the pitch + a new hook (a streaming milestone reached, a press clip from another outlet, a newly relevant cultural tie-in)
- No further contact after the second outreach. Continued contact after no response damages the relationship for future pitches.

## PR Infrastructure Decision Framework

| Option | Cost | Access | Best for |
|--------|------|--------|---------|
| **Agency (US/Canada)** | $2,500–$10,000/month for independent artists; $10,000–$50,000/month for label campaign (ESTIMATE) | Existing T1/T2 journalist relationships | T1/T2 pitching; artists with label or management budgets |
| **In-house publicist** | $60,000–$100,000/year FTE (ESTIMATE) | Builds institutional relationships over time | Labels running 6+ T2-complexity campaigns/year |
| **DIY** | Time only | No T1/T2 relationship access; viable for T3/T4/T5 | Emerging artists with strong writing skills and existing press relationships |

**When agency PR is justified:**
Gate 1 — Is the release T2-ready? (Prior T3 coverage, measurable streaming proof, cultural hook beyond "new music")
Gate 2 — Is the budget sustainable for 3+ months? (One-month agency retainers produce press in week 4; the cycle requires sustained effort)
Gate 3 — Does the agency have documented T2+ placements for comparable artists in the last 18 months?

## The Press Pitch Decision Tree (Music)

**Step 1 — Story or announcement?**
- Story = cultural angle, human narrative, significant life event, artistic evolution
- Announcement = release date, tracklist, tour dates, award nomination, signing news
- Story → T1/T2 exclusive or premiere outreach; full feature-pitch format
- Announcement → broad service pitch to T3/T4/T5; news brief format (50 words + assets)

**Step 2 — Is the artist T2-ready?**
T2 threshold indicators (any two of the following):
- ≥2 documented T3 placements in the last 18 months
- ≥100K monthly listeners on Spotify or equivalent DSP footprint
- Touring history at 250+ cap venues with documented ticket sales
- Sync credit in a streaming-era TV or film property
- Award nomination or competition finalist (FACTOR, Polaris Prize long-list, Prism Prize, Juno nomination)
- Not T2-ready → pitch T3 and build the record

**Step 3 — Canada-specific outlets:**
- English Canada: Exclaim!, NOW Magazine (Toronto), The Georgia Straight (Vancouver), CBC Music editorial
- Québec / Francophone: Voir, Le Devoir arts, La Presse arts section, CISM campus radio (Montréal)
- Bilingual coverage requires bilingual press kit components; a PR team with Francophone relationships is non-optional for sustained Québec campaign coverage

**Step 4 — Outlet-journalist match:**
The unit of PR is the journalist relationship, not the outlet. "Which specific writer at this outlet covers this genre, and what did they write last week?" An outlet-only pitch is not a strategy. A journalist-specific pitch with a reference to their recent piece is.

## Press Calendar Alignment

| Event | Month | Coverage type | Pitch window |
|-------|-------|--------------|-------------|
| SXSW (Austin, TX) | March | Artist showcase coverage; discovery narratives | November–January (application); January–March (press pitch) |
| Canadian Music Week (Toronto) | May | Canadian industry press | February–March |
| NXNE (Toronto) | June | Canadian emerging artist coverage | March–April |
| Polaris Prize long-list | July | Canadian "album of the year" conversation | No pitch — listening only |
| TIFF (Toronto) | September | Canadian artist/music-in-film crossover | June–July |
| Year-end lists (T1/T2) | November–December | "Best albums/songs of the year" features | No pitch to year-end lists — editorial decisions. The correct action is robust T3 coverage by late summer. |

**Year-end list mechanics:** T1/T2 year-end lists are not produced from pitches — editors begin internal nominations in September/October based on what appeared in their own coverage. An artist who had T3 coverage by August is visible to this process; an artist who launches in November is not. The campaign arc for year-end consideration begins in Q1 of the same year, not Q4.

## EPK Standards

| Asset | Standard | Common failure |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| Artist bio | 150–250 words; story-first, accolade-second; hook in sentence 1 | Accolade-first ("Award-winning artist...") — editors stop reading at line 1 |
| Press photo | 2400×3600px minimum; editorial lighting | Compressed Instagram-quality crop; phone photo |
| Audio embed | SoundCloud or Spotify iFrame with pre-cue timestamp; not a download link | Dropbox download requiring authentication |
| Press quote | Third-party source (any credible prior coverage) | No quote / self-description |
| Streaming links | All active DSP links; chart/listener milestones if noteworthy | Dead links; no milestone context |
| Booking/press contact | Specific contact name + email + stated response SLA | "For inquiries" generic email |

## Anti-Patterns

- **Pitching all tiers simultaneously on release day:** T1/T2 editors learn when a story has been offered to T3 before them. Tier sequencing is operationally required.
- **Leading the pitch with the music:** editors assign stories to stories. The music is evidence for the story, not the story itself.
- **Pitching outlets that don't cover the genre:** the most common amateur error. Marks the sender as someone who doesn't read the outlet and damages the relationship for future pitches.
- **Treating a press placement as a campaign strategy:** a Pitchfork feature with no downstream conversion infrastructure (email list active, social CTAs live, DSP pre-save enabled, merch available) produces a traffic spike with zero retention.
- **Over-scheduling exclusives:** offering the same story as "exclusive" to multiple outlets sequentially without disclosing prior passes violates editorial trust.

## Practitioner-Layer Insight

The highest-performing PR campaigns in independent music are built on **the journalist relationship, not the pitch cycle.** Publicists who consistently place artists have built years-long relationships with specific writers — the writer knows the publicist has taste, doesn't pitch everything, and will bring them something genuinely interesting.

For artists and managers doing DIY press: the correct equivalent is not mass-pitching but building 3–5 genuine writer relationships over 12–18 months, before the release campaign that matters. The return on that relationship investment is disproportionate to its cost when the high-stakes campaign arrives.

A publicist hired 4 weeks before release day is solving the wrong problem. The artist needed the relationship built 12 months before. Hire early or do the relationship work yourself, earlier than feels necessary.
