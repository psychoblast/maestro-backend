# Publishing Administration & Registration — PLMKR Music Publishing

The operational layer: how publishing infrastructure is built and maintained —
publisher entity setup, step-by-step work registration workflows, the CWR and DDEX
international data standards, grand rights vs. small rights, print rights, neighboring
rights claim procedures, controlled composition detailed mechanics, and platform-specific
licensing. This is the practitioner gap between knowing the rules and being able to
execute them.

---

## 1. Publisher Entity Setup

A "publishing deal" with yourself begins before the first split sheet is signed. Every
songwriter who self-publishes must establish a publishing entity — a registered company
in the PRO system with its own IPI number — or the publisher's share of performance
royalties will remain uncollected until claimed, if ever.

**Step 1 — Entity name search.** Before forming any legal entity, search your target PRO's
publisher name database (ASCAP and BMI both allow public searches). The entity name must be
unique within that PRO's registered names. Common pitfall: forming an LLC at the Secretary
of State level with a name that is already taken in the PRO database — the legal entity is
valid but the PRO will refuse the name on enrollment, requiring a DBA or amendment.
Practical protocol: search the PRO first, then file the LLC/S-Corp.

**Step 2 — PRO publisher enrollment.** Every US PRO treats the songwriter and the publishing
entity as separate members with separate IPI numbers.
- **ASCAP:** publisher enrollment via ASCAP.com; one-time fee (confirm current schedule at
  ASCAP.com — historically ~$50, subject to revision [Tier A, ASCAP published schedule]);
  produces a publisher IPI number distinct from the songwriter's writer IPI.
- **BMI:** publisher enrollment at BMI.com; one-time fee ~$150 as of 2024 [Tier A, BMI
  published schedule]; BMI privatized in 2022 — monitor for distribution methodology changes
  post-privatization. BMI publisher IPI is distinct from the writer IPI.
- The songwriter can only affiliate with ONE US PRO; their publishing entity must affiliate
  with the SAME PRO. A songwriter at BMI cannot have their publishing entity at ASCAP.
  Cross-PRO setups require separate entities — a songwriter at ASCAP who also writes songs
  for a co-publisher affiliated at BMI will encounter the split-registration problem where
  both societies receive a portion of the performance royalty.

**Step 3 — "Writer/publisher of own works" registration.** For a self-published songwriter
who controls their own publishing, both the writer's share (50%) and the publisher's share
(50%) flow to the same person — but through two separately registered accounts. If only
the writer account is registered (no publisher entity), several PROs will pay the writer's
share directly and hold the publisher's share in an administrative pool or pay it to the
writer with a designation flag, depending on the PRO's specific policy. The safe
configuration: always enroll both the writer account (personal IPI) and the publishing
entity (separate IPI) and link them. ASCAP calls this the "affiliated publisher" structure;
BMI calls it the "publisher account" linked to the writer.

**Step 4 — IPI number management.** The IPI (Interested Party Information) number is a
9-to-11-digit permanent identifier in the CISAC (International Confederation of Societies
of Authors and Composers) network. It is assigned by the PRO on enrollment and propagated
internationally. IPIs are permanent — they are not reused or reassigned. When a songwriter
changes PRO affiliation (e.g., moves from BMI to ASCAP), they receive a new IPI in the new
PRO's system; the old IPI may remain in some databases. This IPI duplication across a
career change is one of the most common matching-failure causes in international society
databases: works registered under the old IPI do not automatically re-link to the new IPI.
Migration protocol: re-register all works at the new PRO with the new IPI, then file
corrections at all international societies where the old IPI was propagated.

**Step 5 — EIN and banking setup.** A publishing entity (LLC or S-Corp) requires an EIN
(Employer Identification Number) for IRS purposes, separate from the songwriter's personal
SSN. Both ASCAP and BMI require either an SSN (individual) or EIN (entity) at enrollment.
The publisher entity banking account must be in the entity's name. Royalty distributions
arrive in two separate checks/ACH payments: one to the writer account (personal), one to
the publisher account (entity). Both must be enrolled with banking information.

## 2. Copyright Office Registration Workflow

Registration is the step that converts the automatic copyright into an enforceable and
compensable legal right. The US Copyright Office's eCO (electronic Copyright Office)
system at copyright.gov is the primary portal.

**Form PA vs. Form SR:**
- **Form PA** (Performing Arts): for musical compositions — the underlying song (melody,
  lyrics, harmony). File this for the composition copyright.
- **Form SR** (Sound Recording): for the specific recorded performance. File this when
  you own both the composition AND the master and want to register both in one filing using
  a single form. If the composition and master have different owners, each files separately.
- Using Form SR for a composition-only registration (when the filer does not own the
  master) is a practitioner error — the resulting registration may not cover the master.

**eCO filing workflow:**
1. Create an account at copyright.gov (email + password; free).
2. Register a new claim → select Work Type → "Sound Recording" (use SR; select "Work" tab)
   or "Work of the Performing Arts" (use PA for composition only).
3. Provide: title, year of creation, year of first publication (if published), country of
   first publication, authorship (each songwriter's full legal name + % contribution to the
   work — reported as "words," "music," or "words and music" — not as split percentages).
4. Deposit copy: for published works, upload a complete copy of the work (audio file for SR;
   lead sheet, lyrics, or MIDI file for PA). For unpublished, any complete representation.
5. Claimant(s): the copyright owner — the songwriter (if self-published) or the publisher
   (if assigned). Match the claimant to the actual copyright owner at the time of filing.
6. Filing fee: currently $65 online for a single work (Standard Application) [Tier A,
   Copyright Office fee schedule at copyright.gov — confirm at time of filing, schedule
   amended periodically].
7. Receive a case number immediately; certificate arrives in weeks to months depending on
   queue depth.

**Group registration.** The Copyright Office allows group registration of:
- **Unpublished works:** up to 10 unpublished works by the same author(s) in a single
  filing under one application fee; or unlimited under the Group Registration of
  Unpublished Works (GRUW) option.
- **Published photographs, serials:** different group rules for non-musical works.
- Musical compositions do NOT have a blanket group-registration accommodation for published
  works at the same rate as individual applications — each distinct song with distinct
  co-writers or publication dates typically requires its own registration for clean records.
  Group registration of works with mixed co-authorship creates ambiguity in the certificate.

**Supplementary Registration (Form CA):** used to correct or amplify a previously filed
basic registration — e.g., to correct a misspelled author name, add an omitted co-author,
or correct a wrong first-publication date. Form CA cannot be used to change the claimant
(that requires a recordation of transfer). The CA registration must reference the
original registration number.

**Recordation of transfers:** when copyright ownership changes (assignment, co-publishing
deal, catalog acquisition), the transfer document (signed agreement) can and should be
recorded at the Copyright Office (Section 205). Recordation creates constructive notice
of the transfer to third parties and establishes priority in chain-of-title disputes.
Recordation fee: $105 per document for the first title, +$30 per each group of ten
additional titles [Tier A, Copyright Office fee schedule — confirm at filing]. Not
recording a transfer means it can be defeated by a subsequent good-faith purchaser who
does record.

**Timing rule (the 3-month window):** to be eligible for statutory damages and attorney's
fees for infringement, the work must be registered either (a) before the infringement
begins, or (b) within 3 months of first publication, for infringement that begins after
registration. Any registration after that window limits the claimant to actual damages only
(not the $30,000–$150,000 per-work statutory range). Registration before release is always
the correct posture for commercially released works.

## 3. PRO Work Registration Workflow

Registering with the Copyright Office establishes the legal right. Registering with the
PRO operationalizes the collection of performance royalties. They are separate steps.

**ASCAP work registration — Songfile / member portal:**
1. Log in at ASCAP.com with writer or publisher credentials.
2. Navigate to "My Works" → "Add a Work."
3. Required fields: work title (exactly as released — case-sensitive matching), ISWC (if
   already assigned; leave blank and ASCAP will assign one if absent), copyright notice
   date, writers (each with name + ASCAP member number or IPI + role code + % share),
   publishers (publishing entity name + IPI + PRO affiliation + % share), timing (duration
   of the work), genre, alternate titles (any significant variant title, remix designation,
   or foreign-language title used in broadcast).
4. Share total validation: writer shares must total 100%; publisher shares must correspond
   to each writer's publisher relationship. A 50/50 co-write between Writer A (published by
   Publisher A) and Writer B (self-published) should show: Writer A 50% + Writer B 50% on
   the writer side; Publisher A 50% + Writer B Publishing 50% on the publisher side.
5. Submission produces a work registration record; ISWC is assigned within days if not
   pre-existing.

**BMI work registration — BMI Works Portal:**
1. Log in at BMI.com → Songwriters/Publishers → Register Works.
2. Similar fields to ASCAP: title, co-writers (BMI IPI numbers), publishers, splits,
   alternate titles, ISWC.
3. Key BMI-specific note: BMI assigns a BMI Work Number in addition to the ISWC; both
   appear on BMI statements and must match for payment routing.
4. BMI's matching algorithm is slightly different from ASCAP's; a work title that
   resolves cleanly at BMI may not at ASCAP if metadata differs. Cross-check by
   searching both databases for each work after registration.

**Common PRO registration errors causing unmatched royalties:**
- Variant title not registered (e.g., the marketed song title differs from the title on
  broadcast logs; the PRO cannot match the log to the registered work).
- Co-writer at a different PRO with an incorrect or absent IPI: matching breaks at the
  society-to-society reconciliation step.
- Split totals that don't add to 100%: the PRO's submission engine either rejects the
  registration or holds it for manual review.
- ISWC mismatch between ASCAP and BMI for the same work (happens when each PRO assigns
  an ISWC independently without cross-checking; the Songview database now provides a
  deduplication layer, but legacy catalog has persistent conflicts).
- Producer composition credit added after initial registration without filing a revision:
  the original registration stands, and the producer's claim goes into a dispute queue.

**Registration timing:** register a work BEFORE its first public performance or DSP
delivery. For a new release: standard protocol is submission to ASCAP or BMI at least
2–4 weeks before the scheduled release date. Retroactive registration (after the release)
captures future royalties only; past performance royalties for unregistered works are
distributed on a market-share basis to already-registered works, not refunded.

## 4. The MLC Work Registration

The MLC (Mechanical Licensing Collective) collects US digital streaming mechanicals
separately from the PROs. Registration with the PRO does NOT cover The MLC.

**Publisher Portal registration (publishers only — songwriters access via their publisher):**
1. Publishers register at portal.themlc.com.
2. Each work must have: ISWC (required for matching), title, all co-writers with their IPI
   numbers, all publisher entities with their IPI numbers, split percentages, and ISRC links
   (ISRCs of the master recordings of this composition — the ISRC→ISWC link enables The MLC
   to match DSP stream reports to the correct composition).
3. DSPs submit bulk usage reports (stream counts per ISRC) to The MLC; The MLC matches ISRC
   to ISWC to find the composition; distributes monthly to publishers; publishers account to
   songwriters per their agreement.

**The ISRC→ISWC link is the critical matching step.** If a song's ISRC (assigned to the
master recording and embedded in the audio file's metadata) is not linked to its ISWC
(composition identifier) in The MLC's database, the stream generates a royalty that cannot
be matched to the composition owner and flows to the unmatched pool. The most common source
of MLC unmatched royalties is NOT missing ISWC registration — it is a broken ISRC→ISWC
linkage because the publisher did not register ISRCs in the work's metadata at The MLC,
or the ISRC in the DSP's delivery did not match the ISRC registered at The MLC.

**Unmatched royalties protocol:** The MLC provides publishers with a monthly unmatched
royalties report listing ISRCs that generated mechanical royalties but could not be matched
to a publisher account. Publishers should reconcile the unmatched ISRC list against their
catalog monthly, register the ISRC→ISWC link for any identified works, and file a claim.
As of early 2027, The MLC will begin distributing unmatched royalties pro-rata to
registered publishers (market-share distribution) — creating a financial disincentive for
competitors to remain unregistered and a structural advantage for publishers with complete
registration.

## 5. CWR — Common Works Registration

CWR (Common Works Registration) is the CISAC/BIEM international data standard for
registering musical works with collection societies worldwide. It is how publishers send
structured work-ownership data to international societies en masse and how societies
exchange work data with each other. CWR is not a public interface — it is a B2B electronic
message format used by publishers, sub-publishers, and societies. A publisher without a CWR
feed is dependent on manual registration or sub-publisher services for each territory.

**CWR versions:** CWR 2.1 (the dominant operational version as of 2025) and CWR 3.0
(JSON-based, currently in staged adoption by leading societies). Most sub-publishers and
CMS providers generate CWR 2.1 files; CWR 3.0 adoption is fastest at STIM, PRS for Music,
and SUISA [Tier C, society implementation calendars]. A CWR 2.1 file is a fixed-width flat
text file; each line is a CWR record identified by a record type prefix.

**Core CWR record types:**
| Record | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| **NWR** | New Work Registration — the core record; declares the title, ISWC, duration, language, and musical-work characteristics |
| **REV** | Revision of Work Registration — updates a previously submitted NWR; references the original ISWC |
| **ALT** | Alternate Title — registers any variant title, language version, or remix name |
| **SPU** | Controlled By You — Publisher — publisher entity record (with IPI, society affiliation, share) |
| **OPU** | Other Publisher — a publisher NOT in your network (co-publisher at a different company) |
| **SPT** | Publisher Territory — territorial scope of the SPU's rights (TIS territory code, collection share) |
| **OPT** | Other Publisher Territory — territorial scope of the OPU |
| **SWR** | Controlled By You — Writer — writer record with IPI, society affiliation, role code, share |
| **OWR** | Other Writer — a writer outside your network |
| **SWT** | Writer Territory — territorial limitations on the SWR (unusual; most writer shares are worldwide) |
| **PER** | Performing Artist — links a recording artist to the work for identification purposes |
| **REC** | Recording Detail — links the ISRC of a specific recording to the composition |

**Writer role codes (used in SWR/OWR records):**
| Code | Role |
|------|------|
| **C** | Composer (music only, no lyrics) |
| **A** | Author/Lyricist (lyrics only) |
| **CA** | Composer-Author (both music and lyrics) |
| **AR** | Arranger (of an existing work) |
| **TR** | Translator |
| **AD** | Adaptor |
| **E** | Original publisher / sub-publisher (used in the publisher record context) |
| **ES** | Original publisher, for sub-publishing arrangement |
| **SE** | Sub-publisher |

Using incorrect role codes is a common CWR error. A songwriter who writes both music
and lyrics should be coded **CA**, not two separate C and A entries. A producer who
contributed copyrightable melodic elements should be coded C or CA (as applicable) — if
coded as AR without a clear arrangement context, the society may not recognize the
contribution as original authorship. Societies will reject or hold records with unrecognized
role code combinations.

**Territory codes (TIS — Territory Identifier Standards):**
CWR uses TIS numeric codes rather than ISO 3166 country codes. Key territory codes:
- **2136** = Worldwide (rights apply globally)
- **840** = United States
- **826** = United Kingdom
- **276** = Germany
- **250** = France
- **124** = Canada
- **392** = Japan
- **036** = Australia
- **484** = Mexico
- **76** = Brazil

When a publisher registers a work with worldwide rights, a single SPT record with territory
code 2136 covers all territories. Sub-publishing splits territory-by-territory: the SPU
record for the primary publisher might cover US+Canada (840, 124), while the sub-publisher's
OPU/OPT record covers EU territories — and the shares in each territory must sum to 100%
independently.

**Society affiliation codes (in SWR/SPU records):** CISAC maintains the official list; key
US codes: ASCAP = 021, BMI = 022, SESAC = 023, GMR = 059. Each writer's and publisher's
affiliation code must match their actual PRO registration — a mismatch causes the society
to route the registration for manual reconciliation rather than auto-processing.

**Share mechanics in CWR:**
- Writer shares sum to 100% across all SWR/OWR records for a work.
- Publisher shares are expressed as each publisher's percentage of the TOTAL (not just of
  the publisher's share). For a 50/50 co-write where each writer's publisher controls 100%
  of that writer's publishing share: Publisher A's SPT share = 50% (50% of the whole work);
  Publisher B's OPT share = 50%.
- In a co-publishing structure: Writer A has a co-pub deal where their publisher (Pub A)
  controls 50% of Writer A's publisher's share (25% of total work); Writer A's self-pub
  entity controls the other 25%. In CWR: Pub A's share = 25% (worldwide), Writer A's
  self-pub = 25% (worldwide), Writer B's publisher = 50% (worldwide).
- Share totals on the publisher side must sum to the same percentage as the covered writer
  shares; CWR validates this and rejects mismatched totals.

**Common CWR errors and consequences:**
- **IPI not found:** the writer or publisher IPI in the CWR does not match any IPI in the
  society's database (wrong number, stale IPI from a PRO migration) → record rejected or
  held for manual review → delayed registration → missed distributions.
- **Share mismatch:** writer shares total ≠ 100% → rejection or manual hold.
- **Alternate title missing:** the work is performed/broadcast under a remix or alternate
  title not in the ALT records → matching failure in that territory.
- **Territory overlap:** the publisher's territorial claims overlap with a registered
  sub-publisher's existing claim on the same territory → conflict flag → distribution held.
- **ISWC collision:** two different works have been assigned the same ISWC (rare but
  occurs with poorly managed multi-PRO environments) → society flags for de-duplication.

**CWR submission logistics:** publishers with their own CMS (content management system)
generate CWR files in-house; most independent publishers use a publishing administration
platform (Songtrust, Lyricfind, Counterpoint, etc.) that generates and submits CWR on
their behalf. Submission is directly to each society's receiving server or via CISAC's
common file-exchange network. Acknowledgment (ACK) files returned by societies confirm
accepted records, rejected records (with error codes), and works requiring conflict
resolution.

## 6. DDEX Standards

DDEX (Digital Data Exchange) is the standards body whose message formats govern the
structured exchange of music metadata between the supply chain participants: distributors,
aggregators, DSPs, labels, and publishers. DDEX is not a collecting society or an
administration platform — it is a library of machine-readable schemas.

**Why DDEX matters for publishing:** incorrect or missing DDEX metadata at the distribution
layer directly causes MLC matching failures. If the ISRC embedded in an ERN delivery
to a DSP does not match the ISRC registered at The MLC, the stream generates an unmatched
mechanical royalty that cannot be attributed to the correct composition owner.

**ERN (Electronic Release Notification) — for distributors and aggregators:**
ERN is the DDEX format used to deliver a release (album, single, EP) from a distributor
to a DSP. ERN 4.3 is the current major version [Tier A, DDEX.net published standard].
Key data elements: ReleaseId (a ProprietaryId or ICPN/EAN/UPC), SoundRecording elements
(with ISRC), Contributor credits, Release date, Territory availability, DealList (territory
+ deal type + commercial conditions). Publishers typically do not generate ERN directly —
their aggregator or distributor does. But publishers must provide accurate ISRCs and ISWC
linkage to their distributor so that ERN delivery embeds correct composition metadata.

**MWN (Musical Works Notification) — for publishers:**
MWN is the DDEX format for exchanging musical work ownership and rights data between
publishers, sub-publishers, The MLC, and other collecting organizations. Where CWR is the
traditional (fixed-width, flat-file) standard for society registration, MWN is the modern
XML-based equivalent. Key MWN elements:
- `MusicalWorkId`: ISWC for the composition
- `ContributorReference`: writer name, IPI, role code, territory, right type, share
- `RightsShare`: publisher entity, IPI, territory, right type (performance / mechanical /
  synchronization), share percentage, deal type
- `RecordingReference`: ISRC(s) of recordings embodying this composition (the ISRC→ISWC
  link described in §4 above)

**RIN (Recording Information Notification) — the critical ISRC→ISWC bridge:**
RIN links a master recording (identified by ISRC) to the underlying musical works
(identified by ISWC) it embodies. This is the exact data The MLC requires to attribute
a stream (reported by DSPs with ISRC) to the correct composition (identified by ISWC)
and thus to the correct publisher.
- A complete RIN contains: ISRC, ISWC(s), work title, release information
- Missing or incorrect RIN = the stream cannot be matched = unmatched mechanical royalty
- The most common RIN error is a studio-created ISRC that was never linked in the RIN
  to its ISWC before the recording was delivered to DSPs

**Practical protocol:** when a recording is mastered and an ISRC is assigned, the
publisher's metadata workflow must (a) register the ISRC with The MLC linked to the work's
ISWC, and (b) ensure the RIN is generated correctly before aggregator delivery. This
step often falls through the gap when the publisher and the label/distributor are separate
entities that do not share a common metadata system.

## 7. Grand Rights vs. Small Rights

The distinction between grand rights (dramatic) and small rights (non-dramatic) is one of
the most consequential and most misunderstood licensing concepts in music publishing. It
determines whether a PRO blanket license covers a use — or whether direct negotiation with
the publisher is required.

**Small Rights (non-dramatic public performance):** the public performance of a musical
work in a non-dramatic context. This is what PRO blanket licenses cover: radio play,
television background/theme use, live concert performance (of a song not presented in
dramatic narrative context), streaming, background music in hotels/restaurants/retail,
satellite and internet radio. The songwriter and publisher receive PRO performance royalties
through the blanket-license mechanism; no separate direct license is required from the
publisher for each use.

**Grand Rights (dramatic performance):** the performance of a musical work as an integral,
narrative-advancing element of a staged dramatic production. The critical characteristic
is not the presence of staging, costumes, or theatricality — it is whether the song is used
to advance a story, establish character, or carry dramatic action within a narrative frame.
Grand rights are NOT covered by ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, or GMR blanket licenses. The ASCAP and
BMI consent decrees explicitly carve them out. This means:
- A Broadway musical using a licensed song as a scene-driving plot device = **grand rights** →
  direct license from the publisher required for each production
- A live concert performance of songs from a Broadway musical, presented without staging
  or narrative context = **small rights** → covered by the venue's PRO blanket
- A ballet using a song as an integral dramatic expression of character = **grand rights**
- A ballet school recital where a song plays as background accompaniment = **small rights**

The distinction is **dramatic integration**, not performance format. A cabaret show that
performs songs from a composer's catalog with no connecting narrative = small rights.
A cabaret show structured as a biographical drama about the composer, using songs to
dramatize events in their life = grand rights.

**Why it matters operationally:**
1. A venue with ASCAP/BMI/SESAC blanket licenses is covered for small rights performances
   only. If a production company performs grand-rights material under a venue's blanket
   without a direct license from the publisher, it is infringing the publisher's grand right.
2. Fees for grand rights are entirely negotiated — no statutory rate, no CRB formula.
   Typically structured as a per-performance royalty (fee per show) or as a negotiated
   advance plus per-performance. Fee levels are context- and catalog-sensitive; a
   back-catalog pop song in a regional theater production will be priced differently from
   the same song in a major touring musical.
3. Term and exclusivity: a grand-rights license typically grants rights for a specific
   production, a specific territory, and a specified run (or a fee structure for unlimited
   performances within a term). Exclusivity of the dramatic use is occasionally granted
   for premium fees.
4. Who controls: the publisher controls the grand-rights grant (not the PRO); for a co-pub
   situation, all co-publishers' consent may be required depending on the agreement.

**The print/sheet music addendum:** staged musical productions that require printed vocal
scores, orchestra parts, or libretto materials require a separate **print license** (see §8)
alongside the grand-rights license.

**Music in film (non-dramatic theatrical sync):** a film that uses a song incidentally in
the background is a synchronization license (sync), not a grand-rights issue. However, a
film that is an adapted stage musical — where the songs are integral dramatic elements of
a narrative — raises the question of whether the film use constitutes a dramatic performance
of the grand right. This distinction is an active area of licensing practice; the safest
approach for narrative-musical films is to clear both the sync right and confirm the scope
of the dramatic-use right explicitly in the license agreement.

## 8. Print Rights

Print rights are the rights to reproduce and distribute a musical composition in printed
form — sheet music, lead sheets, guitar tablature, educational arrangements, fake books,
choral scores, and their digital equivalents (licensed digital download of a printable
score). Print is a distinct right from performance, mechanical, and sync — it is one
of the bundle of exclusive rights under 17 U.S.C. § 106 (the right to prepare copies).

**Print licensing mechanics:**
- A publisher who controls print rights can license them directly or sub-license to a
  print publisher (Hal Leonard, Alfred Music, Musicnotes, SheetMusicPlus, etc.).
- Royalty structure [Tier C]: for sheet music sold through print publishers,
  the songwriter/publisher typically receives 10–15% of the marked retail price per copy
  (traditional print); for digital licensed print downloads (Musicnotes-type transactions),
  the typical publisher royalty is ~20–25% of the transaction price, often with a per-page
  floor [Tier C]. Print publishing deals often include advance and/or minimum guarantee
  components.
- Print rights for a song are NOT automatically part of a streaming mechanical or PRO
  performance license. A service that provides downloadable PDFs of copyrighted sheet music
  without a print license is infringing — even if it has mechanical and performance licenses.
- **Fake books and compilation rights:** licensing a song into a compilation songbook
  (e.g., "The Best of Classic Rock Guitar") requires both the print right for that song
  and the compilation/arrangement right. Unauthorized transcription for fake books is a
  persistent source of print infringement in the music industry.

**Educational print licensing:**
Educational arrangements (simplified piano versions, choral four-part arrangements, concert
band transcriptions) require a separate educational arrangement license from the publisher,
in addition to the base print right. The arranger of an educational version needs both
permission to arrange and a license for the resulting printed arrangement. Publishers vary
widely in their educational licensing posture: some actively seek educational print revenue;
others are restrictive to protect the integrity of their catalogs.

**Digital print (licensed printout):** a licensed digital print download (a PDF or
MusicXML the user can print at home) is subject to print rights — it is not a mechanical
royalty (which covers audio reproduction) and not a synchronization license. Platforms
that deliver these must have print licenses directly from publishers or through a print
rights administrator. Violation is common among AI-generated sheet music and unauthorized
score transcription platforms.

**Revenue context:** print is a declining revenue stream overall, but remains meaningful
for catalog evergreen titles (standards, classical-adjacent songs, songs with active
educational adoption), for songwriters with significant cover-artist ecosystems, and for
titles embedded in school music programs. Print revenue is often more resilient than
streaming for catalog titles because the per-transaction payment (several dollars per print
download) is orders of magnitude larger than a per-stream mechanical.

## 9. Neighboring Rights Registration Workflow

The theory of neighboring rights is in royalty-and-catalog-systems.md §3. The operational
question is HOW to register. Non-registration is the largest source of ongoing recoverable
income that artists and master owners fail to collect — the workflow is relatively simple
but consistently under-executed.

**SoundExchange (US — digital audio performance, non-interactive):**

SoundExchange collects for non-interactive digital audio performance (satellite radio,
internet radio, cable music channels, some podcast background) — NOT for on-demand
streaming (that is the master owner's streaming royalty paid by the DSP) and NOT for
terrestrial radio (no US neighboring right for terrestrial).

Registration workflow:
1. Create an account at soundexchange.com (separate accounts for "Artist" and "Rights
   Owner/Label" — both may be the same individual if self-releasing).
2. Artist account: enter stage name + legal name + SSN/EIN + banking (ACH preferred).
   Upload a repertoire list of recording titles and ISRCs if available; SoundExchange will
   also attempt matching by artist name alone, but ISRC provision accelerates matching
   significantly.
3. Rights Owner account (label/master owner): separate enrollment under the entity name +
   EIN + banking. List recordings with ISRCs. For a DIY artist who owns their masters,
   this is a second account under the artist's LLC or under their personal name with the
   "as master owner" designation.
4. **Dual registration is the most commonly missed step.** A self-releasing artist is
   simultaneously the featured artist (50% share, paid directly regardless of master
   ownership) AND the master owner (45% share, paid to the rights holder). Both must be
   separately enrolled. SoundExchange does NOT automatically combine them. An artist who
   only enrolls as "featured artist" forfeits the 45% master owner share; an artist who
   only enrolls as "rights owner" forfeits the 50% featured artist share. This specific
   gap costs self-releasing artists millions of dollars annually in aggregate.
5. Retroactive distributions: SoundExchange can match and distribute retroactively for
   periods up to the prior 3 years; the practical window is determined by how long
   SoundExchange has held unmatched royalties for a given artist. Enroll as early as
   possible — even before a first release — to open the matching window.

**PPL (UK — neighboring rights for performers and master owners):**

PPL collects from UK broadcast (terrestrial radio, TV, cable, background music) and from
certain digital services. UK neighboring rights cover terrestrial radio, which makes the
UK one of the highest-value NR territories globally for international artists.

Registration workflow:
1. Register at ppluk.com under the appropriate category: Performer (for vocalists and
   instrumentalists featured on recordings) or Recording Rights Owner (for the label or
   artist-as-label).
2. Performer enrollment: provide stage name, legal name, national insurance number (for
   UK residents) or tax identification (for non-residents), banking details, and a
   catalog of recordings with ISRCs. PPL processes foreign performer registrations and
   distributes via reciprocal agreements with the performer's home country performing
   rights society.
3. Recording Rights Owner (RRO) enrollment: entity registration, EIN or UK company number,
   banking, ISRC-linked catalog. Royalties are paid at PPL's published distribution
   schedules (quarterly).
4. US artists receiving PPL distributions: if enrolled at SoundExchange, royalties from
   UK broadcasts may flow through the SoundExchange–PPL reciprocal agreement. However,
   direct PPL enrollment (as performer) typically yields faster and more complete
   collection than the reciprocal pathway alone.

**Collection window and retroactive claims:**
EU member-state NR societies (PPL, GVL, SENA, IPRS, etc.) typically allow retroactive
claims for up to 3 years back from the date of registration. The practical implication:
an artist who tours or releases internationally and waits 5 years before registering has
permanently lost 2+ years of collectible income. Standard practice: register with
SoundExchange, PPL, and GVL immediately upon first international commercial release — not
when income "becomes significant."

**GVL (Germany — neighboring rights for performers and phonogram producers):**

Germany is one of the highest-value neighboring-rights territories for internationally
active artists. GVL collects from German broadcasts (public and commercial radio, TV),
background music, and online services within its scope. German collections for US artists
are distributed via the SoundExchange–GVL reciprocal agreement for most performers. For
higher-volume catalogs, direct GVL registration by a German sub-publisher or through
a direct application to GVL may yield better matching and fewer deduction layers.
GVL distributions to foreign performers lag approximately 12–18 months from the usage
period [Tier C].

## 10. Controlled Composition Mechanics (Detailed)

The fundamentals file (publishing-fundamentals.md §7) establishes the controlled
composition concept. This section provides the detailed mechanical arithmetic, the
album-cap trap, and the interaction with streaming mechanicals under the Music
Modernization Act (MMA) — which is materially non-obvious and frequently misstated.

**The rate calculation:**
- 2025 US statutory physical/download mechanical rate: 12.7¢ per copy (CRB Phonorecords IV)
  [Tier A, Federal Register].
- Standard controlled-composition clause: 3/4 of the applicable statutory rate.
- 3/4 rate for 2025: 12.7¢ × 0.75 = **9.525¢ per controlled composition per unit**.
- A 25% reduction that applies to every unit shipped/sold for the artist's own songs.
- Alternative CC formulations: some clauses fix the rate at the statutory rate in effect
  at a specific date (e.g., the date the recording contract was signed), even as the
  statutory rate rises. A songwriter who signed in 2018 at 9.1¢ may find their CC clause
  locked at a reduced rate even though the statutory rate is now 12.7¢.

**The album mechanical cap:**
Many recording contracts combine the CC rate reduction with an album cap — a limit on the
total number of compositions on an album for which mechanicals are paid. Typical clause:
"Mechanicals payable on no more than ten (10) tracks per album, even if the album contains
more tracks."

Arithmetic example: artist delivers 15-track album; all tracks are artist-controlled songs:
- Without cap: 15 × 9.525¢ = $1.42875 per unit in mechanical royalties
- With 10-track cap: 10 × 9.525¢ = $0.9525 per unit — 5 songs receive ZERO mechanicals
- The 5 uncapped songs permanently generate zero mechanical royalties for the songwriter
  from label sales — not deferred, zeroed

This cap is most damaging on albums where the songwriter is prolific or where the standard
is to deliver "album filler" (deep-cut tracks beyond the commercial core). A 10-track cap
on a 12-track album costs the songwriter mechanicals on 2 tracks for the life of the label
deal — including any streaming mechanicals captured under the CC clause's streaming
interaction.

**Streaming mechanicals and CC clauses — the MMA complication:**

This is the most misunderstood area of controlled composition mechanics. Two distinct
things are happening simultaneously:

1. **The MLC collects streaming mechanicals from DSPs at CRB rates.** Under the Music
   Modernization Act (2018), DSPs pay The MLC under a compulsory blanket license. The
   rate is set by the CRB (15.1% of adjusted revenue in 2023, rising to 15.35% by 2027
   [Tier A, CRB Phonorecords IV]). No recording contract CC clause can reduce what DSPs
   pay The MLC — the compulsory rate applies regardless of private contract terms between
   artist and label.

2. **After The MLC distributes to publishers, the publisher accounts to the songwriter
   under their publishing agreement terms.** This is where the CC clause re-enters.
   If the songwriter has a co-publishing or administration deal with the label that includes
   a CC clause, and the publishing agreement defines the songwriter's mechanical royalty
   as a percentage of the label's mechanical receipts (rather than as the full statutory
   rate), the CC clause may effectively reduce the songwriter's share of streaming
   mechanicals — not at The MLC level, but at the publisher accounting level.

3. **The specific result depends on agreement language.** Three common structures:
   - **CC clause says it does not apply to interactive streaming mechanicals:** songwriter
     receives the full pro-rata CRB-formula amount. This is the songwriter-favorable
     language; it must be explicitly negotiated because the label's form agreement will
     typically not include it.
   - **CC clause applies to streaming at 3/4 statutory:** the songwriter receives 75% of
     what they would otherwise receive from The MLC via their publisher. The label has, in
     effect, shifted a portion of the streaming mechanical from the songwriter back to
     itself via the publishing-deal accounting mechanism.
   - **CC clause has an album cap that the label applies to streaming:** some agreements
     attempt to apply the per-album track cap to streaming (capping the tracks on which
     streaming mechanicals are paid). This is contested — The MLC pays per-track per-stream
     on all registered tracks — but the accounting-to-songwriter level in a label publishing
     deal may attempt to apply the cap. This provision requires legal analysis on a
     case-by-case basis; route to qualified entertainment counsel.

**Review protocol for CC clauses:**
When analyzing any recording contract:
1. What is the CC rate (3/4 statutory, or a fixed historical rate)?
2. Is the applicable statutory rate defined as the current rate at time of sale, or a
   locked historical rate?
3. What is the per-album track cap, and does it apply per format (physical separately from
   streaming, or combined)?
4. Does the CC clause explicitly exclude interactive streaming mechanicals, or is streaming
   included in the CC-rate reduction?
5. Is there a mechanical floor (a minimum per-track mechanical rate below which the CC
   reduction cannot go)? Some well-negotiated agreements include this.
6. Does the CC clause also appear in any co-publishing or admin agreement with the label?
   If the recording contract CC clause AND the publishing agreement CC clause both apply,
   the stacking effect is compounded.

## 11. Platform-Specific Licensing Mechanics

Licensing for platforms that did not exist when most standard publishing agreements were
drafted creates specific mechanical and performance rights questions that are NOT covered
by legacy contract language or standard PRO blankets.

**YouTube and ContentID:**

YouTube operates under a licensing structure built on three layers:
1. **PRO blanket licenses:** YouTube pays ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, and GMR under negotiated
   licenses covering US-licensed performance of compositions in YouTube videos. The rates
   are confidential but flow to publishers and writers via their PRO quarterly statements.
2. **The MLC streaming mechanical license:** YouTube (as an on-demand audio platform) pays
   The MLC for composition streaming mechanicals on song-identified videos.
3. **ContentID asset-level monetization:** YouTube's Content ID system allows rightsholders
   to claim their content in user-generated videos and direct ad revenue to the claimant.
   This is separate from the PRO and MLC licensing and is managed directly within YouTube
   Studio / Content Manager.

**ContentID eligibility and access:**
ContentID is invite-only — YouTube does not grant access to individual artists or small
publishers without going through an approved partner. Eligibility criteria (as of 2025
[Tier B, YouTube published guidelines]):
- A track record of managing copyright in YouTube at scale
- A substantial catalog of original content
- No significant history of policy violations
- Most independent publishers and artists access ContentID through a distributor or CMS
  provider (such as DistroKid, TuneCore, CD Baby, Amuse, Songtrust, or BMG) that holds
  a YouTube partner agreement.

**ContentID asset structure:**
- An **Asset** is a fingerprinted reference file (audio, audiovisual, or composition)
  linked to a policy (monetize, block, or track) applied per territory.
- A claim occurs when the system detects a user-uploaded video that matches a reference file
  associated with an Asset. Claims can be: audio match (your sound recording in their video),
  video match (your video footage), or audio+video match.
- **Monetize policy:** ads run on matched videos; ad revenue flows to the claimant (not the
  uploader). This is the standard policy for most commercial recordings.
- **Block policy:** matched videos are made unavailable in the territories specified. Rarely
  used for commercially released music except in territorial exclusivity situations.
- **Track policy:** matched videos are allowed without monetization; data on views and
  geographic reach is collected. Used as a monitoring tool or for catalog too legacy to
  monetize effectively.

**The dual-claim conflict problem:**
If both the master owner and the composition owner separately submit ContentID assets
covering the same recording, YouTube may receive two competing claims on a single user
video. Resolution options: ownership conflict resolution within YouTube's CMS, or
one-stop rights management (same entity controls both master and composition ContentID
assets). Publishers without CMS coordination with the master owner regularly encounter
this conflict; the practical solution is to confirm the ContentID strategy with all
rights-holders before asserting claims.

**TikTok licensing:**

TikTok operates under licensing agreements with major publishers and some independent
publishing aggregators. The licensing structure (as of mid-2026):
- Master rights: TikTok licenses master rights from labels and distributors under a
  combination of flat-fee blanket deals and per-stream royalty structures (specific terms
  are confidential and subject to periodic renegotiation [Tier B, trade press reporting]).
- Composition rights: TikTok licenses composition rights from PROs (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, GMR
  in the US; PRS in the UK; GEMA in Germany; etc.) under negotiated blanket licenses. It
  also negotiates directly with major publishers for certain rights.
- **Coverage gap for independent publishers:** an independent publisher without a direct
  deal with TikTok or without PRO coverage of TikTok may find their compositions used in
  viral TikTok videos with no corresponding composition royalty — or receive a takedown
  notice rather than compensation. The current practice for independent publishers: ensure
  the compositions are registered with a PRO that has a TikTok blanket license; if the
  publisher has a publishing admin deal, confirm the admin deal explicitly covers TikTok
  licensing.
- Viral lift multiplier: a song that goes viral on TikTok typically experiences a streaming
  multiplier effect on DSP streams within days — composition and master royalties from
  DSPs often exceed TikTok's direct payment in total value. This does not address the
  TikTok composition licensing gap but provides context for how to weigh the platform.

**Meta Rights Manager (Facebook and Instagram):**

Meta operates a licensing structure similar to TikTok's: blanket licenses with major
publishers and PROs for composition rights; direct master licensing with labels and
distributors. The Meta Rights Manager tool allows approved rights-holders to manage
rights for their content across Facebook and Instagram, including options to monetize,
mute, or block. Access to Rights Manager is through an application process; most
independent artists access it via their distributor or publishing admin's deal with Meta.
The composition-level rights for independent publishers face the same coverage gap as
TikTok: if the composition is not covered by a Meta-licensed PRO or direct deal, usage
may go uncompensated or result in takedowns rather than revenue.

**Interactive fitness platforms (Peloton/iFIT model):**

Interactive fitness platforms that deliver on-demand classes with synchronized music
generate BOTH mechanical and performance royalties because they are interactive (on-demand)
audio services. The landmark 2022 Peloton settlements with multiple publishers established
that fitness platforms cannot operate under a non-interactive streaming license for
synchronized workout classes — the interactive, curated nature of workout playlists
requires on-demand mechanical licenses from both The MLC (composition) and direct master
licenses from labels. Publishers should confirm their admin deal explicitly covers fitness
platform mechanicals; emerging fitness platforms that have not yet negotiated direct deals
with The MLC or major publishers represent potential uncompensated usage.

**Gaming and interactive media:**

Music licensing for video games differs structurally from film/TV sync:
- **Run-of-game license (standard):** covers all platforms, all territories, and the
  commercial life of the game for a flat license fee or a advance-plus-royalty structure.
  Unlike a film sync license (which is typically territory/time/medium-specific), a
  run-of-game license is intended to be perpetual for the title.
- **Composition vs. master:** game publishers license both the composition (sync + mechanical
  for interactive use) and the master separately. For new-release clearance, both must be
  cleared before delivery of the game build.
- **Interactive mechanic distinction:** a game that lets the player control which music plays
  (interactive) has different mechanical requirements than a game with a passive background
  soundtrack. Fully interactive music systems may require additional "interactive use" license
  language beyond a standard sync/mechanical grant.
- **DLC and post-launch content:** if a song is used in downloadable content (DLC) or a
  post-launch update, the original run-of-game license must explicitly cover future updates
  or a supplemental license is required.
- **Game trailers:** a trailer for a video game is a sync use separate from the run-of-game
  license. Game trailers often command premium trailer-level sync fees (comparable to film
  trailer fees) because of their high impression counts. The trailer license must be
  separately negotiated from the in-game license.

**Social media blanket licensing — independent publisher gap:**

Major publishers (Universal Music Publishing, Sony Music Publishing, Warner Chappell) have
direct licensing relationships with Meta, TikTok, Snapchat, YouTube, and other social
platforms. These blanket deals cover compositions in their catalogs for user-generated
content on those platforms.

Independent publishers and self-published songwriters whose compositions are not under
a major-pub admin deal, and whose PRO does NOT have a blanket deal with a specific
platform, are in a licensing gap: their compositions may be used in user-generated content
on those platforms without compensation. The practical solutions:
1. **Publishing admin deal with a platform-connected admin:** Songtrust, Kobalt, BMG, and
   major-adjacent admins typically have platform blanket deals that provide coverage.
2. **PRO blanket coverage:** confirm that the composition's PRO has executed blanket deals
   with relevant social platforms for composition performance income. ASCAP and BMI have
   negotiated agreements with YouTube, Meta, and TikTok; verify coverage for the specific
   platform is current at the time of use.
3. **Direct deal:** for catalogs large enough to be worth licensing individually (typically
   above 500 commercially active tracks), direct engagement with platform licensing teams
   is possible but requires legal infrastructure.

## 12. Publishing Administration Tooling and Data Hygiene

**Content Management Systems (CMS) for publishing:**
Publishers managing catalogs of more than ~200 works operationally require a CMS to
generate CWR files, track royalty statements, manage ISRC→ISWC linkages, and produce
regulatory/society reports. Purpose-built publishing CMS platforms include: Synchtank,
OverSuite, Counterpoint/Rightsline, MediaLink, Vistex, and music-specific modules within
ERPs. For smaller independent publishers, platform-as-CMS options (Songtrust, Lyricfind
Publishing, CD Baby Pro Publishing, Amuse Publishing) handle CWR generation and society
submission on the publisher's behalf.

**Metadata consistency standard across systems:**
The single most consequential metadata discipline is ensuring the work title, ISWC, all
writer IPIs, and the ISRC linkage are IDENTICAL across:
- The PRO registration (ASCAP/BMI)
- The MLC portal
- The CWR file submitted to international societies
- The distributor/aggregator delivery metadata (ERN)
- The publisher's internal CMS
- The RIN submitted to The MLC

Any field that differs between these systems produces a matching failure at the point
of divergence. A systematic metadata audit (comparing each source against a canonical
master record for each work) should be performed at least annually for active catalogs
and before any catalog sale due diligence.

**Variant title management:**
A work released under a working title during production, then released under a different
commercial title, then remixed under a third title, can accumulate up to 3–5 title
variants in broadcast logs that need to route to the same ISWC. Each variant title
must be registered as an ALT record in CWR and as an alternate title in the PRO works
database. Failure to register variant titles accounts for a significant fraction of
black-box accumulation in electronic/remix-heavy genres where producers deliver multiple
versions with differing final titles.

**Statement reconciliation workflow:**
A systematic statement review for a publishing catalog should work through this sequence:
1. Compile all statements (PRO quarterly, The MLC monthly, sub-publisher quarterly/semi-
   annual, sync statements) into a single period summary.
2. Compare the works list on each statement against the publisher's full catalog list.
   Works appearing in the catalog but ABSENT from all statements in a period are the
   primary red flag — income missing entirely is the most common audit finding.
3. For each statement, verify that the income categories present (performance, mechanical,
   sync, neighboring rights) match the active collection infrastructure. A mechanical
   statement without a neighboring rights line when NR registration is confirmed = a
   missing collection stream.
4. Cross-reference sync placements from an independently maintained placement log against
   sync royalty line items on the publisher's sync statement. A confirmed placement
   with no corresponding royalty within 12 months = an audit trigger.
5. For sub-publisher statements, verify that the territory split in the statement matches
   the territory split in the sub-publishing agreement. Over-allocation of deductions or
   under-allocation of territories are common sub-pub accounting errors.
6. Reserve the contractual audit window — if the statement audit right window (typically
   2–3 years from statement date) is within 6 months of expiry, evaluate whether to
   engage a formal audit.
