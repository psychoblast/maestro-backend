# Publishing Fundamentals — PLMKR Music Publishing

The bedrock layer: copyright architecture, songwriter splits, publishing-deal
structures, performance royalties (PROs), and mechanical royalties. Errors here
propagate into every downstream analysis.

---

## 1. The Two-Copyright Architecture

Every recorded piece of music involves two separate, distinct copyrights:

| | Composition Copyright | Master Recording Copyright |
|---|---|---|
| **Covers** | The underlying song: melody, lyrics, harmony, arrangement | The specific recorded performance |
| **Owner (default)** | Songwriter(s) / their publisher | Label (via work-for-hire) or artist (if self-released) |
| **Duration (US)** | Life of author + 70 years (post-1978) | 95 yrs from publication / 120 from creation (pre-1972); post-1972: same as composition rule |
| **Registration** | US Copyright Office Form PA | US Copyright Office Form SR |
| **Royalties** | Performance + mechanical + sync | Neighboring rights, sync master fees |
| **Licensing body** | PRO (performance) + The MLC (US mechanical) + direct (sync) | SoundExchange (US digital performance) + direct (sync master) |

The practitioner error is treating these as interchangeable. A sync clearance that
clears only the master without the composition is legally incomplete. A catalog
acquisition that takes the master without the composition (or vice versa) acquires
half the asset.

## 2. Copyright Subsists Automatically

Under the Copyright Act of 1976 (effective 1978), copyright subsists automatically at
the moment of creation in a fixed, tangible form. Registration is NOT required for
copyright to exist, but IS required to file an infringement suit in federal court
(17 U.S.C. § 411) and to claim statutory damages (up to $150,000 per work for willful
infringement — available only if registered before infringement or within 3 months of
first publication, § 412).

The most common royalty-infrastructure failure is the gap between "copyright exists"
and "copyright is registered correctly and consistently everywhere it needs to be." An
unregistered copyright is legally protected but practically uncollectable — PROs
cannot distribute what they cannot match. The composition must exist in the PRO works
database AND the mechanical database AND the Copyright Office record, with consistent
metadata across all three, for full collection to function.

## 3. Copyright Term (US)

Works created after Jan 1, 1978: individual = life + 70; joint work = last surviving
author's life + 70; work-made-for-hire = 95 years from publication OR 120 from
creation, whichever is shorter. Pre-1978 works: complex multi-tier system based on
publication, registration, and renewal; pre-1923 works are public domain in the US.

Territorial note: the EU standard is life + 70 for compositions; EU neighboring rights
run 70 years from publication (2013 Term Directive). Japan: life + 50 for compositions
— the shortest standard term among major markets. An identical work has different
expiry dates in different territories; global licensing requires territory-by-territory
term analysis for catalog works.

## 4. Copyright Divisibility and Transfer Mechanics

A US copyright is a bundle of exclusive rights (§ 106): reproduce, prepare derivatives,
distribute, perform publicly, display publicly, and perform via digital audio
transmission. Each can be owned, transferred, and licensed independently, by territory
and time period — the legal foundation for publishing deal architecture.

- **Assignment:** permanent transfer of ownership (or a share). Irreversible without a contractual reversion right or statutory termination (§ 203).
- **Exclusive license:** one party gets exclusive control of specific rights for a specified territory/term; licensor retains ownership but cannot grant the same rights to others.
- **Non-exclusive license:** usage rights without exclusivity; the owner can grant the same rights to multiple parties.

```
Publishing deal:
   Admin → non-exclusive license to administer (ownership retained by writer)
   Co-pub → assignment of 50% of publisher's share (writer keeps writer's share + 50% pub share)
   Full publishing → assignment of 100% of publisher's share (writer keeps writer's share)
Sync deal:
   Standard sync → non-exclusive license for specific use/territory/term
   Buy-out sync → exclusive or perpetual license (composition + master cleared separately)
```

## 5. Work-Made-for-Hire Doctrine

WFH applies in two circumstances (§ 101): employee work within scope of employment; or
commissioned work under a written WFH agreement in one of nine statutory categories
(motion picture/audiovisual part, translation, supplement, compilation, instructional
text, test/test-answer material, atlas, collective-work part, periodical/anthology
contribution).

**Critical: standalone musical compositions are NOT in the nine categories.** A
standalone song commissioned from a songwriter cannot be WFH by contract alone. If the
songwriter is not an employee, the song is not WFH regardless of the contract language.
The correct structure for acquiring songwriter-created rights is assignment with a
copyright-transfer agreement, not WFH language. This is one of the most commonly misused
concepts in music publishing contracts.

## 6. Territoriality & Registration Mechanics

Copyright is territorial — there is no single "global copyright." Royalty collection
requires registration/affiliation in each territory (or a sub-publishing agreement / 
direct society affiliation that covers it). The Berne Convention establishes minimum
standards and national-treatment reciprocity, meaning a US-registered work is protected
in Berne countries under their domestic law without separate registration — but
*collection* there still requires PRO registration and/or sub-publishing. Berne creates
the legal right to collect; it does not operationalize collection.

US registration: online via copyright.gov eCO (fastest); Form PA (compositions) and
Form SR (masters). Two key global identifiers:
- **ISWC** (International Standard Musical Work Code): the global identifier for a composition, assigned in the US through ASCAP or BMI. A composition without an ISWC is systematically harder to match — a primary driver of black-box accumulation.
- **IPI** (Interested Party Information) number: the unique identifier for a rightsholder in the PRO/society system. Every songwriter and publisher must have one. Variant spellings, missing IPIs, and stale data cause matching failures.

## 7. Songwriter Splits

A split percentage determines each co-writer's share of mechanical royalties, the
publisher's share of performance royalties, sync fees (composition side), and copyright
ownership for termination/assignment/reversion. It does NOT change the writer's share
of performance royalties paid directly by the PRO — each writer registers separately and
receives their proportional share directly.

**Split sheet:** the legal document co-writers sign to record agreed percentages —
signed at or immediately after the session. Fields: song title, date written, each
co-writer's full legal name + publishing entity + PRO affiliation + IPI + ownership
percentage, producer share if applicable, all signatures. The single most expensive
dispute in publishing is the undocumented session: "we all understood the split" is the
most common preamble to a six-figure lawsuit.

**Joint works doctrine (US, § 101):** each co-author has an undivided interest in the
whole; each can grant a non-exclusive license without the others' consent but must
account to them for proportional proceeds; an exclusive license requires all
co-authors' consent. The co-authorship threshold (per the *Childress v. Taylor*
standard): each author must intend a joint work AND make a copyrightable contribution —
a title or a bare idea may not qualify.

**Producer splits — the two-track structure:** master producer royalty points
(percentage of the master's income, paid by the label, does not affect the composition
split) are distinct from a composition co-writer credit (if the producer contributed
copyrightable musical elements). Industry conventions [Tier C]: beat-only contribution
with no melodic/harmonic addition typically = 0% of the composition + a negotiated beat
fee; melodic/arrangement contributions appearing in the final composition = 10–50%
depending on contribution. A non-exclusive beat lease does not transfer composition
copyright; retroactive composition claims after a hit are not enforceable if a beat-sale
agreement existed.

**Controlled-composition clauses** appear in recording contracts, not publishing
contracts — but they reduce publishing income directly. A controlled composition is a
song written/owned/controlled by the recording artist; the clause typically pays
mechanicals at 3/4 of the statutory rate. The 2025 US statutory physical/download rate
was 12.7¢/copy [Tier A — CRB Phonorecords IV]; a 3/4 clause pays 9.525¢ — a 25%
reduction, often buried in a recording contract the publishing team never reviews.
Watch for album mechanical caps (e.g., a 10-track cap on a 15-track album zeroes out 5
tracks' mechanicals) and whether the reduction extends to streaming mechanicals. The
review must happen at the recording-contract stage, not after signing.

## 8. Publishing Deals

Every performance royalty splits at the source into two equal halves: the **writer's
share (50%)**, paid directly by the PRO to the songwriter regardless of any publishing
deal (a publisher cannot contractually take it), and the **publisher's share (50%)**.
"Co-publishing" means sharing the *publisher's* share, not the writer's share — a
standard co-pub retains 100% writer's share + 50% publisher's share = 75% of total
performance royalties. Note the asymmetry: there is no equivalent payment-level
protection for the mechanical writer's share — mechanicals flow to the publisher, who
then accounts to the songwriter per the agreement.

**Admin deal:** songwriter retains 100% copyright; publisher administers for a fee of
10–25% [Tier C]; term typically 1–3 years with reversion. Red flags: fees >25% without
justification; perpetual/open-ended terms without automatic reversion; broad
power-of-attorney without scope limits. Best for established catalogs needing
infrastructure without selling ownership.

**Co-pub:** songwriter assigns 50% of the publisher's share (= 25% of total) and keeps
75% of total. Advances usually recouped against the publisher's 50% (always check the
recoupment base). Red flags: recoupment against the full royalty pool rather than the
publisher's share (doubles the recoupment period); no reversion if unrecouped after a
set period; broad "future songs" language; admin rights that survive reversion.

**Full publishing:** songwriter assigns 100% of the publisher's share, keeping only the
writer's share. The advance is recoupable; the copyright transfer is permanent. Now
signed mainly by very early-stage writers, legacy-era writers, and writers in
full-pub-norm territories. Anti-pattern: signing as an emerging artist without
negotiating a reversion right.

**Work-for-hire / buyout:** one-time flat fee, no ongoing royalties, no writer's share —
legitimate for jingles, production-library content, some film cues. The flat fee should
reflect the expected lifetime value forfeited. Red flag: WFH language on songs that
will be commercially released and PRO-registered (the writer is still entitled to the
performance writer's share).

**Publisher-side red-flag clause table:**

| Clause | Watch for | Why it matters |
|--------|-----------|----------------|
| Controlled composition | 3/4-statutory mechanical reduction for the signed artist's songs | Cuts mechanicals ≥25%; often missed in publishing review |
| Pipeline | Publisher keeps income from works "in the pipeline" at expiry | Years of post-deal collections; define "pipeline" narrowly |
| Matching right / ROFR | Right to match any purchase/license offer | Can block a catalog sale; limit duration and scope |
| Admin rights after reversion | Publisher retains admin after copyright reverts | Reversion should be clean and full |
| Recoupment base | Publisher's share only vs. all royalties | Full-pool recoupment doubles the recoupment period |
| Reversionary timing | Trigger = calendar date, release date, or unrecouped period? | Vague triggers = no effective reversion |
| Sync approval | Unilateral (publisher) vs. shared | Unilateral publisher sync approval is a brand-integrity risk |

**Publisher types:** majors (Sony Music Publishing, Universal Music Publishing, Warner
Chappell) — global infrastructure, deep sync networks, but slower and superstar-skewed;
independents (Kobalt, Primary Wave, Reservoir) — often more transparent reporting and
better mid-tier attention (note the distinction between an admin/co-pub vehicle and a
catalog-acquisition vehicle, and that catalog multiples are interest-rate sensitive);
sub-publishers — territory collection on a primary publisher's behalf, fee layered on
top; self-publishing — maximum control, requires building PRO/MLC/sub-pub
infrastructure, realistic for established writers with regular income.

## 9. PROs & Performance Royalties

Performance royalties are collected when a song is publicly performed (radio, TV,
streaming, live venues, restaurants, retail) and flow through Performing Rights
Organizations.

**US PRO landscape** (unusually fragmented — most countries have one):

| PRO | Structure | Notes |
|-----|-----------|-------|
| **ASCAP** | Non-profit membership ($50 one-time, Tier A) | Largest by member count; member-governed |
| **BMI** | For-profit since 2022 (free for writers; $150 publishers, Tier A) | Monitor post-privatization distribution changes |
| **SESAC** | For-profit, invitation only | Selective roster; historically faster distributions |
| **GMR** | For-profit, invitation only | Premium roster; aggressive licensing stance |

A songwriter can affiliate with only ONE US PRO at a time; each publishing entity must
also affiliate. The Songview database (expanded across all four US PROs, Sep 2025 — Tier
A) provides a comprehensive ownership view for licensees.

**Global PROs:** Canada SOCAN (CMRRA for mechanicals); UK PRS for Music (merged PRS +
MCPS — performance and mechanical in one); Germany GEMA (mandatory blanket licensing);
France SACEM; Japan JASRAC (~98% of domestic music, aggressive enforcement); South Korea
KOMCA; Netherlands Buma/Stemra; Italy SIAE; Brazil ECAD; Australia APRA AMCOS; Mexico
SACM. For a US writer without direct international affiliation, foreign performance
royalties flow US PRO → foreign PRO (reciprocal) → deductions at each level → writer.

**What PROs license:** terrestrial radio, streaming (composition only — master streaming
is separate via SoundExchange), TV broadcast, live venues (blanket license), background
music in hotels/restaurants/retail, satellite radio, theatrical film performance. They
do NOT collect mechanicals (US: The MLC), sync fees (direct negotiation), or neighboring
rights.

**The PRO black box:** when a work is performed but cannot be matched to a registered
rightsholder, collected royalties go into an unmatched pool, most often distributed on a
market-share basis — meaning high-earning catalogs effectively receive a portion of
others' uncollected royalties. This is a structural incentive for precise registration.
Register BEFORE first usage — retroactive registration typically captures future
royalties only.

ASCAP and BMI operate under DOJ consent decrees (since 1941, modified over time)
requiring them to license any requester and establishing rate-setting via the CRB and
rate courts; SESAC and GMR are not subject to consent decrees and negotiate directly.

## 10. Mechanical Royalties

A mechanical royalty is owed to the composition copyright owner every time the
composition is reproduced — physical, download, or stream. In streaming, a single stream
triggers both a mechanical (to the composition owner) and a master royalty (to the master
owner), paid from different pools via different mechanisms.

**US statutory rates — CRB Phonorecords IV:** physical/download per copy: 12.0¢ (2023),
12.4¢ (2024), 12.7¢ (2025), 13.1¢ (2026) [Tier A, Federal Register]. Streaming uses a
formula, not a flat per-stream rate — the greater of a percentage of Total Payments to
Services (TPS) minus sound-recording cost, a per-subscriber minimum, or a percentage of
total content cost; headline % of adjusted revenue: 15.1% (2023) rising to 15.35% (2027)
[Tier A, CRB Final Rule]. **Caveat:** despite headline increases, effective per-stream
payouts have declined for many rightsholders because of bundling (when streaming is
bundled with audiobooks/podcasts, the revenue base subject to mechanicals is reduced).
Never quote the headline percentage as a per-stream payout.

**The Mechanical Licensing Collective (The MLC):** established by the Music Modernization
Act (2018, effective Jan 1, 2021) to administer compulsory mechanical licenses for US
digital audio. DSPs pay The MLC in bulk under a blanket compulsory license; The MLC
matches usage to registered works and distributes monthly; unmatched royalties accumulate
in a pool. **Beginning early 2027**, The MLC will distribute unmatched/unclaimed blanket
royalties monthly on a per-song pro-rata (market-share) basis — so registered
rightsholders also receive a portion of the unmatched pool. Registration requires title,
all co-writers (with IPI), all publishers (with IPI and splits), and ISWC. The MLC pays
streaming mechanicals only — not performance royalties.

Timeline from stream to songwriter check: DSP → MLC ~30–60 days after the streaming
month; MLC → publisher monthly; publisher → songwriter quarterly/semi-annually; total lag
6–18 months [Tier C].

**Physical mechanicals** (vinyl, CD, permanent downloads) still run under the § 115
compulsory license, handled by the Harry Fox Agency (now SESAC-owned), direct licensing,
or DIY compulsory license (serve Notice of Intention, pay the statutory rate — cannot be
used for the first commercially released recording).

**International mechanicals:** US DSPs pay The MLC for US streams only. Foreign-territory
streams are collected by the local mechanical society (UK MCPS via PRS, Germany GEMA,
Canada CMRRA, France SDRM via SACEM, Australia AMCOS, Japan JASRAC, and the broader
mechanical network) — but only if the work is registered there or covered by a
sub-publisher. International distributions lag 12–24 months in many territories.

**Anti-pattern:** assuming PRO registration covers mechanicals. PROs collect performance;
mechanicals require separate registration with The MLC (US streaming) and international
mechanical societies. A songwriter registered only with ASCAP or BMI is NOT covered for
mechanicals.
