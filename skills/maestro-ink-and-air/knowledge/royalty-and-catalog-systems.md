# Royalty & Catalog Systems — PLMKR Music Publishing

The advanced layer: global collection and sub-publishing, royalty accounting and audits,
neighboring rights, termination and reversion rights, catalog valuation and transactions,
sync clearance (rights side), and the AI training-rights landscape.

---

## 1. Global Collection & Sub-Publishing

Most publishing-revenue problems are global-collection problems. The difference between
capturing 40% and 80% of global royalty entitlements is almost entirely a function of
registration infrastructure, territory-by-territory society relationships, and
sub-publishing coverage.

International collection chain: foreign stream/performance → local society collects from
local broadcasters/DSPs → identifies rightsholder (direct registration → direct payment;
via sub-publisher → payment to sub-pub minus fee → original publisher; no registration →
local black box) → original publisher minus admin fee → songwriter. Each leg has its own
delay, fee, and matching process.

**Society collection timelines** [Tier C, approximate lag to primary publisher]: UK/PRS
6–12 mo; Germany/GEMA 8–14; France/SACEM 8–14; Canada/SOCAN 6–10; Australia/APRA AMCOS
8–14; Japan/JASRAC 12–24; South Korea/KOMCA 12–18; Brazil/ECAD 12–24+; most LatAm 18–36;
Scandinavia (STIM, TONO, Koda, Teosto) 6–14. A catalog with global streaming can have
25–40% of total performance income arriving on an 8–24 month delay [Tier C].

**Sub-publishing deal terms:** territory (country/regional); exclusivity (standard);
term (3–5 years); fee (10–25% [Tier C]); services (registration, collection, sync,
sheet music — define explicitly); minimum earnings guarantee (sometimes); works scope;
audit rights (always include; 2–3 year window). **Fee-layering issue:** a 15% primary
admin fee + 20% sub-pub fee leaves the songwriter ~68% of territorial collections, before
society-level deductions (10–20%) — total effective receipt can be 50–60% of gross.
Understanding the full deduction stack is essential.

**Direct society affiliation vs. sub-publishing:** sub-publishing works for catalogs of
any size at a 10–25% territory fee with reach as broad as the sub-pub's footprint; direct
affiliation avoids the sub-pub fee but is generally practical only above ~$5K–$10K/year of
territory income [Tier C] and only where direct membership is accepted. For each major
territory: an owned office is most efficient; otherwise sub-publishing for material income,
reciprocal coverage for minor income, and ensure registration while monitoring growth.

**Black box:** royalties a society cannot match, eventually distributed market-share to
registered rightsholders. Causes: variant title/spelling, missing/incorrect IPI,
co-writer share registered in one territory but not another, ISWC not propagated,
sub-publisher failed to register transferred works, society database not updated after a
transfer. Largest historical accumulations: Japan (JASRAC), Brazil (ECAD), Italy (SIAE),
LatAm. Recovery: audit registrations for IPI/title/ISWC accuracy, obtain usage reports
from key societies, file correction registrations, file retrospective claims where allowed
(most societies allow 3–5 years; some not at all).

The international registration network connects ~225 member societies in 116 territories
through a common information system; an ISWC assigned by a US PRO is theoretically
propagated to all member societies, but in practice propagation is imperfect and delayed
(6–12+ months). Register works with planned international release 3–6 months in advance.
For most catalogs the top 8 territories (US, UK, Germany, Canada, Australia, France, Japan,
plus one of Scandinavia/Netherlands) represent 80%+ of international royalties.

## 2. Royalty Accounting & Audits

A royalty statement is not a report of what was earned — it is a report of what the
publisher calculated was owed after applying the agreement's terms. These quantities are
often materially different, and the songwriter cannot discover the gap without reading and
auditing statements systematically.

Statement anatomy: accounting period; gross income by category (performance, mechanical,
sync, other); deductions (admin/sub-pub fees, advances recouped); net income; advances
applied (running recoupment balance); balance due or unrecouped; territory breakdown. The
most commonly undetected error is not a fraudulent deduction — it is **income that is
absent and therefore invisible**: a sync placement with no royalty line, a territory that
should appear but doesn't, a streaming mechanical that was never registered. The skill in
reading statements is spotting missing categories, not wrong numbers.

**Audit rights — contractual mechanics:** who can audit (songwriter, accountant, attorney);
notice (30–60 days written); frequency (usually once/year); audit window (2–3 years from
statement/payment date); statute of limitations (3–6 years for breach in the US). If the
window passes without exercise, the right to claim underpaid royalties for that period may
be permanently lost.

**When to audit:** material unexplained revenue decline (>20% YoY without catalog
explanation); a new publisher revenue stream with no corresponding royalty line;
third-party evidence of sync usage without a royalty within 12 months; audit window
approaching on a meaningful catalog; publisher acquired/merged during the period (systems
migration → high error probability); any internal-review math inconsistency. Cost/benefit:
contingency 10–25% of recovery or $5K–$50K+ flat [Tier C]; 10–30% of audits find material
underpayment, average recovery in settled disputes 5–15% of royalties audited [Tier C]; a
periodic audit is justified as a business practice above ~$50K/year in publisher payments.

**Forensic review before a formal audit:** (1) stream-to-statement reconciliation (compare
expected mechanical order-of-magnitude from stream counts to statement; >30% gap → pursue);
(2) territory-to-income mapping (major territories missing from the statement despite
streaming presence → flag); (3) sync cross-reference against an independently maintained
placement log; (4) rate verification (admin fee, sub-pub deductions, statutory vs.
CC-reduced mechanical). The single most common audit finding is not under-reporting — it is
that works were never registered with international mechanical societies, so foreign
mechanicals were never collected at all. Watch for a "cost of audit" clause that shifts
audit cost to the songwriter if findings are below a threshold; raise the threshold or
strike it.

## 3. Neighboring Rights

Neighboring rights (related rights) are held by performing artists (compensation when
their recorded performance is publicly broadcast) and master owners (compensation when the
master is publicly broadcast). They are NOT the composition copyright, NOT the same as PRO
performance royalties, and NOT included in a standard streaming license. When a song plays
on broadcast radio: the composition owner receives a PRO performance royalty; the master
owner and the artists receive neighboring-rights income — different parties, different
mechanisms.

**The US exception:** the US does not recognize neighboring rights for terrestrial (AM/FM)
radio. It DOES recognize digital audio performance for masters via **SoundExchange**
(satellite radio, internet radio, non-interactive streaming, cable music channels) — but
not terrestrial radio or on-demand streaming (those are covered by the master streaming
license). SoundExchange distributes statutorily: **50% featured artist** (paid directly,
even if a label owns the master), **45% master owner**, **5% non-featured musicians/
vocalists** (via the AFM & SAG-AFTRA fund). This split cannot be contracted away — an
artist who signed away all recording rights still receives the 50% featured-artist share
directly, but only if registered.

International NR societies: UK PPL, Germany GVL, France SCPP/SPPF (artists via
ADAMI/SPEDIDAM), Netherlands SENA/NORMA, Australia PPCA, Canada Re:Sound, Japan
RIAJ/CPRA, South Korea KAPP/KORRA, Brazil (multi-society). US artists' NR from foreign
broadcasts is collected via SoundExchange's reciprocal agreements. Note the confusion
point: SoundExchange (non-interactive digital performance, neighboring rights) is NOT The
MLC (interactive streaming, mechanical). On-demand services pay labels (master) and The
MLC (composition) directly — not SoundExchange.

**Anti-pattern:** not registering with SoundExchange because "we have a label deal." The
featured artist's 50% flows directly to the artist regardless of the deal — but only if
registered; otherwise it sits unclaimed. Most internationally active artists have
significant unclaimed NR from UK, German, French, and Scandinavian broadcasts (collection
window typically 3 years from usage in EU territories).

## 4. Termination & Reversion Rights

US **Section 203** lets an author (or statutory successors) terminate a prior copyright
grant after a waiting period, regardless of contract language — any provision purporting
to waive the right is void. It applies to grants made on/after Jan 1, 1978; it does NOT
cover works made for hire (no termination right) or pre-1978 works (governed by § 304).

**The 35-year rule:** termination can be exercised during a 5-year window beginning 35
years after the grant (or 35 years from publication if the grant covered publication
rights, or 40 years from the grant, whichever comes first). Written notice must be served
on the current grantee no earlier than 10 years and no later than 2 years before the
effective date, then recorded with the US Copyright Office before that date. Example: a
song published and assigned in January 1990 → window January 2025–January 2030; notice
must be served between January 2015 and January 2023. Songs assigned in the late
1980s–early 1990s are now in their § 203 windows — a structural consideration for any
1985–1995-era catalog acquisition.

**Effect:** the composition copyright and all rights under the original grant (including to
the publisher's licensees) revert to the author. **Derivative-works exception:** films,
recordings, and prior sync placements lawfully licensed before termination continue under
their original license; only NEW exploitations after the termination date require the
author's (now-reverted) clearance — the reversion is still economically significant. For
pre-1978 works, § 304(c) (56th–61st year) and § 304(d) (a second window at the 75th year,
added by the 1998 term-extension act) apply.

International: § 203 is uniquely US. Most jurisdictions have no direct equivalent, though
the EU 2019 Copyright Directive created a contract-adjustment mechanism and a revocation
right for non-exploited works (implementation varies); the UK, Canada, Australia, and
Japan rely on moral rights (inalienable, distinct from economic rights) rather than a
broad termination right. International portfolio management assesses each territory's
framework rather than applying US termination logic globally. Termination is an asset for
the songwriter — a reversion of their own property — not a problem to avoid.

## 5. Catalog Valuation & Transactions

Catalog M&A is a rights transaction: the buyer acquires a predicted income stream. A
publishing acquisition transfers the publisher's share of copyright, the right to future
royalties from those compositions, and administration rights — NOT the writer's share of
performance royalties (flows directly from PRO to songwriter) or the master rights
(separate transaction). No acquisition can monetize more than the publisher's share.

**NPS (Net Publisher's Share)** is the standard valuation income metric: the publisher's
total royalty income after sub-publishing deductions but before acquisition-level
overhead, usually a trailing 3-year average. It excludes writer's-share payments,
one-time/non-recurring items (a single exceptional sync year is normalized out), legal
settlements, and non-recurring advances. Acquirers normalize trailing income to remove
outliers in both directions; their normalized NPS is typically more conservative than the
seller's stated figure.

**Multiples** are expressed as a multiple of annual NPS and are highly market- and
rate-sensitive. Historical context [Tier B]: ~18–25x at the 2021 low-rate peak,
compressing to ~16x by 2023–2024, with a wider, more disciplined range thereafter — the
10-year Treasury yield is a meaningful input (the "catalog as safe annuity" narrative
compresses as yields rise). Always qualify a multiple with time window, territory, genre,
catalog vintage, and income composition. Quality signals that adjust effective value:
sync (high if recurring/evergreen, discounted if single-hit dependent), streaming
mechanical (stable, scrutinized for aging and DSP concentration), performance (radio
format risk), live (touring-dependent), digital-vs-physical split, international coverage
(poor sub-pub infrastructure discounts current NPS but is a buyer value-creation
opportunity), and income concentration (a catalog where 80% of income is in 1–3 songs is
more volatile).

**Termination-risk discount:** songs created after 1977 by freelance songwriters may be
subject to § 203 termination 35 years after assignment; a catalog concentrated in
1985–2000 hits carries active or near-term reversion risk and is structurally discounted
below the headline multiple. Due diligence (publishing side): ownership verification
(Copyright Office registrations, chain of title, co-pub agreements, controlled-composition
clauses); income verification (3-year statements, PRO/MLC registration status, sub-pub
agreements); rights integrity (§ 203 analysis, sample clearances, disputes/litigation,
WFH status); quality of income (stream decomposition, geographic distribution, streaming
trend, sync frequency/type). Always verify seller-stated NPS against third-party society
statements. Deal structures: full outright purchase, partial acquisition, advance +
participation, leaseback (seller retains admin), forward deal (catalog + future works).
Financial modeling and IRR belong to the Finance/Royalties function.

## 6. Sync Rights Clearance (Rights Side Only)

Scope boundary: this covers the *rights side* — which rights exist and how they are
cleared. Sync *pursuit strategy*, supervisor relationships, and brief-matching route to
PLMKR's Sync agent. Clearance tells you *can this be done and at what cost*; pursuit tells
you *how to get the placement*.

A synchronization license grants the right to use a composition in sync with visual
images; a separate **master use license** grants the right to use a specific recording.
**Both are required** for any use of a recorded song in a visual medium, and neither is
compulsory — both are negotiated and priced privately (no statutory sync rate exists).
Composition clearance comes from the publisher (and ALL co-publishers, unless one has
authority to sublicense for all); master clearance from the label/master owner only.

The split-rights problem: if one party can grant both composition AND master rights →
one-stop clearance (fastest); otherwise two independent clearances are required and if
either declines, the usage cannot proceed. Clearance scoping confirms medium, territory,
term, background vs. foreground use, and lyric inclusion; confirms ownership (co-pub splits,
sub-publisher coverage, master clearance authority); and assesses complexity (cover version →
composition sync still required; interpolation → original composition + new arrangement
rights; sample → underlying sample rights too; sub-publishing territory → sub-publisher may
co-approve). Fees are entirely negotiated — characterize the fee tier qualitatively; never
state a figure without a Tier A/B source. In any sync-fee or sync-licensing refusal, route
confidential terms and negotiation to counsel AND route pursuit/supervisor/placement
strategy to the Sync agent (dual routing — naming only one is insufficient).

## 7. AI & Rights Landscape (Currency-Sensitive — Verify Before Output)

This is the fastest-moving area in publishing rights. Every claim is tiered; treat
contested questions as contested.

Two separate copyright questions arise when an AI system trains on music: the
**reproduction question** (does copying works into a training dataset infringe the
reproduction right, § 106(1)?) and the **output question** (do AI-generated outputs
infringe the works they were trained on?). The publishing concern is primarily the
composition side.

**Established vs. contested (as of mid-2026):** courts have begun distinguishing training
on *legally acquired* works (more likely fair use) from training on *pirated* works (found
not protected) — a training-source distinction, not a blanket ruling. What remains
contested: whether training on legally licensed recordings is fair use; whether outputs
substantially similar to training works infringe; whether AI companies need separate
composition (mechanical) licenses for training; what "substantial similarity" means for
statistically-resembling AI outputs.

**Emerging licensing frameworks:** major-label settlements with AI music generators have
pointed toward per-generation royalties, tiered by commercial-distribution context, with
backward-looking training licensing and forward-looking "authorized AI" products. These
are bilateral settlements, NOT a statutory rate, an industry standard, or a blanket
mechanism — each AI company negotiates independently. No compulsory license for AI
training exists (would require legislation), and there is no MLC/PRO blanket for training.

**International variation:** the EU AI Act does not resolve copyright licensing; the EU
2019 Directive created a text-and-data-mining exception with a rightsholder opt-out
(Article 4(3)); the UK framework remains unsettled (more rights-protective than the EU
research exception, but no settled commercial-use rule); Japan's law has been interpreted
to permit AI training without authorization (the most permissive major jurisdiction, under
active legislative review). A global strategy cannot apply a single territorial framework.

**US Copyright Office guidance:** works created entirely by AI without human authorship
are NOT copyrightable (immediately public domain); works with sufficient human creative
control may be — the "sufficient" threshold is being decided case by case.

**Anti-fabrication rules for AI rights — do NOT state as established:** "AI training on
legally licensed music is fair use" (contested); "AI training requires an MLC mechanical
license" (publisher position, not court-established); a specific bilateral settlement's
terms apply to other AI companies (they don't); "AI-generated music cannot be copyrighted"
(nuanced — human-authorship threshold applies); "Japan permits all AI training without
limitation" (accurate as current interpretation but under review). What CAN be stated: the
facts of specific cases, reported settlement terms with Tier B sourcing, and the status of
contested questions accurately characterized as contested. Always separate the training-use
question from the output-similarity question — distinct legal issues with distinct
trajectories.
