## CONTRACT ANALYSIS

### Recording Contract Red Flags — Universal
- 360 rights without specific label commitments in return → REJECT
- More than 3 album options (5-7 = decade of career) → REJECT or negotiate hard
- No reversion clause on masters → REJECT (push for 7-10 years minimum)
- Royalty rate below 18% of SRLP → negotiate up
- Recoupable marketing costs → cap or remove
- Controlled composition clause → fight to remove
- No audit rights → deal breaker
- Cross-collateralization across albums → fight to remove
- Worldwide rights with no territory carve-outs → negotiate
- No key man clause → always include

### Publishing Deal Red Flags — Universal
- Full publishing (publisher owns 100%) → avoid unless advance is life-changing
- Existing catalog included → future songs only
- Term longer than 3 years without reversion → negotiate
- Administration fee above 20% → negotiate
- No black box royalty provisions → add

### Management Agreement Red Flags — Universal
- Commission above 20% → reject (15-20% standard)
- Commission on gross vs net → fight for net
- No sunset clause → standard 6-12 months post-term
- Vague duties and responsibilities → require specific deliverables
- No termination for cause → always include

### Brand Deal Red Flags — Universal
- Category exclusivity too broad → negotiate scope
- Unlimited name/likeness rights → define territory and term
- Morality clause with vague triggers → define specifically
- Payment too back-weighted → push for 50% upfront
- No kill fee if brand cancels → always include

## GLOBAL COPYRIGHT LAW

### USA
- Copyright Act 1976 — automatic copyright upon creation
- Registration: copyright.gov ($45-65 per work)
- Benefits of registration: ability to sue, statutory damages, attorney fees
- Duration: life + 70 years for post-1978 works
- DMCA: Digital Millennium Copyright Act — takedown procedures
- AI copyright: currently no protection for AI-generated works without human authorship

### UK
- Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988
- Automatic copyright — no registration required
- Duration: life + 70 years
- Moral rights: stronger than USA
- Post-Brexit: UK copyright law independent of EU

### CANADA
- Copyright Act (Canada) — automatic upon creation
- Registration: CIPO (cipo.ca) — optional but useful
- Duration: life + 70 years (updated 2022)
- Canadian content rules: CRTC CanCon requirements for radio

### AUSTRALIA
- Copyright Act 1968 — automatic, no registration
- Duration: life + 70 years
- ACCC oversight of music industry practices

### EUROPEAN UNION
- Directive on Copyright in the Digital Single Market (2019)
- Article 17: platforms liable for user uploads — significant for independent artists
- Duration: life + 70 years across EU
- GDPR: fan data protection requirements

### NIGERIA
- Copyright Act 2022 (updated)
- NCC (Nigerian Copyright Commission) registration recommended
- Growing enforcement as streaming economy develops
- Duration: life + 70 years

### JAMAICA
- Copyright Act Jamaica — automatic
- JIPO (Jamaica Intellectual Property Office) registration
- Duration: life + 50 years (CARICOM standard)

### SOUTH AFRICA
- Copyright Act 98 of 1978 (under review)
- CIPC registration optional
- Duration: life + 50 years (updating to 70 years)

### BRAZIL
- Lei de Direitos Autorais (Law 9.610/98)
- Automatic copyright — no registration required
- Duration: life + 70 years

### SOUTH KOREA
- Copyright Act — automatic protection
- KOCCA (Korea Creative Content Agency) resources
- Duration: life + 70 years
- K-Pop specific: exclusive contracts scrutinized by Fair Trade Commission

## VISA & IMMIGRATION — PERFORMING GLOBALLY

### PERFORMING IN USA
O-1B Visa (extraordinary ability):
- Best for established artists with significant career evidence
- Evidence required: press coverage, awards, high salary, critical role
- Processing: 3-6 months standard, 15 days premium processing ($2,500)
- Legal cost: $3,000-8,000 in attorney fees
- Petition filed by US employer/venue

P-1 Visa (internationally recognized performer):
- For groups or artists with sustained international recognition
- Must perform with culturally unique traditional art OR be internationally recognized
- Processing: similar to O-1B

P-2 Visa (reciprocal exchange):
- For Canadian and Mexican artists under CUSMA/USMCA
- Faster and less expensive than O-1B/P-1

B-1 Business Visitor:
- CANNOT be used for paid performances
- Using B-1 for paid work = deportation and potential ban

### PERFORMING IN UK
Permitted Paid Engagement (PPE):
- For short engagements (up to 1 month)
- Must be invited by UK arts organization
- No prior visa application needed for many nationalities

Creative Worker Visa:
- For longer stays
- Certificate of Sponsorship required from UK employer
- Processing: 3-8 weeks

Post-Brexit EU touring:
- Each EU country requires separate work authorization
- Germany: requires work permit
- France: requires work permit for paid performances
- Netherlands: relatively straightforward for short stays
- Spain: work authorization required
- Complex — route all EU touring visa questions through LEX-CIPHER

### PERFORMING IN CANADA
- USA/Mexico artists: covered under CUSMA/USMCA
- Other international: Temporary Work Permit required
- Apply through IRCC (ircc.canada.ca)

### PERFORMING IN AUSTRALIA
Entertainment Visa (subclass 420):
- For performers and support staff
- Sponsored by Australian promoter
- Processing: 4-8 weeks

### PERFORMING IN JAPAN
Entertainer Visa:
- Strict requirements — proof of professional status required
- Japanese promoter or agency sponsorship required
- Processing: 4-8 weeks
- Cultural sensitivity: dress and behavior standards important

### PERFORMING IN NIGERIA
- Foreign artists need work permit

- Facilitated through local promoter typically

### PERFORMING IN SOUTH AFRICA
- Work permit for foreign performers

- Facilitated through local promoter

## DISPUTE RESOLUTION — GLOBAL

MEDIATION: First step for most music industry disputes — faster and cheaper than litigation
ARBITRATION: Binding resolution without court — many contracts specify arbitration
LITIGATION: Last resort — expensive and slow in most jurisdictions

COMMON MUSIC DISPUTES:
- Label underpayment: audit then formal demand then arbitration/litigation
- Manager breach: formal termination then commission dispute resolution
- Collaborator credit/ownership: mediation first, litigation if significant value
- Venue non-payment: demand letter, small claims court for smaller amounts
- Copyright infringement: DMCA takedown, then civil action if significant
