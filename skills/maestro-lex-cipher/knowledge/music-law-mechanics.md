## Copyright Termination Rights — Section 203 and Section 304

One of the most important and non-obvious rights in US music law: the statutory right to
recapture copyright grants regardless of contract language purporting to waive it.

### Section 203 — Post-1978 Grants

Applies to grants executed on or after January 1, 1978 (17 USC § 203).

**Termination window:** the author (or statutory heirs) may terminate a grant during a
five-year window beginning 35 years after the date of execution of the grant. If the
grant covers publication rights, the window opens at the earlier of: 35 years from
publication, or 40 years from execution.

**Inalienability — critical rule:** the termination interest CANNOT be contracted away.
A clause purporting to waive or pre-assign the termination right is unenforceable under
17 USC § 203(a)(5). This applies even if the contract expressly states otherwise.

**Required notice:** written notice of termination must be served on the grantee and
recorded in the US Copyright Office. Notice must be served not less than two and not
more than ten years before the effective termination date.

**Heir priority order** (when the author is deceased):
1. Surviving spouse (in specified proportion based on surviving children)
2. Children and grandchildren in defined proportions
3. Author's executor, administrator, personal representative, or trustee — if no surviving
   spouse, children, or grandchildren

**Works-for-hire exclusion:** WFH works cannot be terminated under § 203. Record labels
historically attempted to classify sound recordings as WFH to extinguish § 203 rights
(RIAA lobbied for a 1999 addition to § 101 that was subsequently repealed in 2000). That
claim is contested and does not have clear legal support for most pre-1999 recordings.
Flag for qualified copyright counsel whenever WFH is asserted over a sound recording in
the context of termination analysis.

**Derivative works exception:** a derivative work (e.g., a film score arrangement, a
licensed remix) that was lawfully created before the termination date may continue to be
exploited under its original license after termination — but no new derivative works may
be created from the terminated grant.

**Composition vs. master — two separate analyses:** composition and master recording are
two distinct copyrights with two separate termination tracks. Each must be analyzed
independently.

### Section 304(c) and 304(d) — Pre-1978 Grants

**Section 304(c):** grants executed before January 1, 1978 covering copyrights still
subsisting in their renewal term. The five-year termination window opens 56 years after
the date copyright was originally secured.

**Section 304(d):** for copyrights already in their extended term as of October 27, 1998
(the Sonny Bono Copyright Term Extension Act effective date) where the § 304(c)
termination was not exercised — an additional five-year window opens 75 years after the
original copyright date.

**Practical identification discipline:** confirm the execution date of the grant before
applying the correct termination statute. Route specific termination-window calculations
and notice requirements to qualified copyright counsel — the calculations are
fact-specific and timeline-dependent.

## Section 115 — Compulsory Mechanical Licensing

The statutory right to record and distribute a non-dramatic musical composition that has
been previously commercially released to the public in the US, without the publisher's
express consent, at the CRB-set mechanical rate.

### Pre-MMA Framework (Harry Fox Era)

Prior to the Music Modernization Act (2018), any person could obtain a compulsory
mechanical license by serving a Notice of Intent (NOI) on the copyright owner at least
30 days before distribution, and paying at the CRB-set statutory rate per unit
distributed. The Harry Fox Agency (HFA) administered mechanical licensing and collection
for a large segment of the US publishing market.

### Music Modernization Act — Enacted October 11, 2018

**Title I: The Musical Works Modernization Act** restructured digital mechanical licensing:

- Established the **Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC)** as the designated agent to
  administer a statutory blanket license for digital on-demand streaming and permanent
  downloads (effective January 1, 2021)
- The blanket license allows DSPs to obtain a single license covering all compositions
  in the MLC database, rather than clearing compositions individually
- DSPs submit usage data and royalty payments to the MLC monthly; the MLC matches usage
  to rights-holders and distributes accordingly
- The MLC's public database (accessible at themlc.com) is the US authoritative source
  for composition registration and ownership claims

**Unmatched mechanical royalties:** when usage cannot be matched to a registered
rights-holder, the MLC holds the funds in a "black box." After a defined holding period
and matching effort, unmatched funds are distributed proportionally to MLC members based
on market share. Rights-holders who have not registered their works in the MLC database
will not receive these distributions — registration is a practical prerequisite to
collecting digital mechanicals.

**Title II: Compensating Legacy Artists** extended federal copyright protection to
sound recordings fixed before February 15, 1972 (which were previously protected only
under state law). Term runs from 95 years from first publication for pre-1972 recordings.

**Statutory mechanical rate:** set by the Copyright Royalty Board (CRB) in rulemaking
proceedings. Rate schedules are published in 37 CFR Part 385 and are currency-sensitive.
Verify current rates at copyright.gov/licensing-division/mechanical/ before any rate
reference. Do NOT state a specific percentage without a current Tier A source.

### Controlled Composition Clause — Rate Reduction Mechanics

A controlled composition clause in a recording agreement reduces the mechanical royalties
the label pays the artist-writer on compositions the artist controls. Common structures:
- Flat rate reduction to a fixed fraction of the then-current statutory rate (frequently
  75% of statutory, sometimes lower)
- Per-album cap regardless of album length (common cap: 10 compositions per album at the
  reduced rate — additional compositions on longer albums are paid at zero or require
  negotiation)
- Rate locked at a specific date rather than the rate in effect at the time of release
  (locks out future CRB rate increases)

Identify all three elements when a controlled composition clause is present. Flag as
a red-flag clause; do not characterize as acceptable or unacceptable — that routes to
the management function and qualified counsel.

## 360-Degree Deal Anatomy

A 360-degree deal (also called a multiple-rights agreement or full-access deal) is a
recording agreement in which the label captures a share of the artist's income beyond
master-recording royalties. Identify which revenue streams are captured; the business
judgment of whether to accept those provisions routes to the management function and
qualified counsel.

### Revenue Categories Captured in 360 Provisions

| Revenue Category | Capture mechanism typically used |
|-----------------|----------------------------------|
| Touring / live performance | Percentage of net (or gross) tour income after defined deductions |
| Merchandise | Percentage of net merchandise income |
| Endorsements / brand deals | Percentage of gross endorsement fee |
| Film / television / acting | Percentage of gross acting income |
| Publishing / songwriting | Direct participation in composition income (a "publishing participation" provision, distinct from mechanical) |
| Crowdfunding / fan-funding | Percentage of direct fan income |
| Podcast / speaking | Percentage of gross fees |

### Identification Protocol for 360 Provisions

When reviewing an agreement for 360 provisions:

```
Step 1 — Locate the revenue-capture provisions
  → Search for "ancillary income," "additional revenue," "multiple rights,"
    "entertainment income," "360," "all entertainment activities"
  → Note whether the provision appears in the main body or as an addendum

Step 2 — Identify the base: gross vs. net for each revenue stream
  → "Net" requires identifying what deductions are permitted and whether
    the deduction categories are defined or open-ended
  → "Gross" provides minimal deduction flexibility; identify whether it means
    gross receipts before all deductions, or a defined gross

Step 3 — Identify the covered categories with precision
  → "All income derived from the artist's entertainment career" is an
    exceptionally broad grant — flag the open-ended scope
  → Specific enumeration (only touring, merchandise, endorsements) is
    materially narrower and preferred

Step 4 — Confirm the reporting and payment mechanism
  → Is income reported quarterly? Annually? Triggered by each event?
  → Are sub-certification obligations on third-party income present?

Step 5 — Confirm audit scope covers 360 revenues
  → An audit clause limited to master-recording royalty statements does not
    cover 360 income streams — identify the audit scope explicitly

Step 6 — Identify the survival provision
  → Do 360 provisions survive termination of the recording agreement?
  → Is there a sunset on post-term 360 obligations?
```

### Red-Flag Patterns Specific to 360 Deals

- Open-ended "all entertainment income" without enumerated carve-outs (career-binding scope)
- Gross basis for live income — high severity (deductions for venue costs, agent commission,
  and production are standard; a gross-basis capture leaves almost no netting)
- 360 provisions surviving termination without a defined sunset (post-term indefinite obligation)
- Publishing-participation provisions without carve-out for pre-existing publishing agreements
  or catalog signed prior to this deal
- No audit right covering the additional revenue streams

## Sample Clearance — Mechanics and Chain Requirements

Using any portion of an existing copyrighted sound recording or composition in a new
work requires a license from each rights-holder. The chain of required clearances is
bilateral and non-waivable.

### Required Clearances

| Copyright | Owner class | What must be cleared |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|
| Sound recording (master) | Label or artist's recording entity | Master use license (no statutory rate — fully negotiated) |
| Musical composition | Publisher / songwriter | Mechanical license + synchronization right (if video) |

A license for only one side does not satisfy clearance. Both are independently required
and independently negotiated. There is no compulsory rate for sample clearance of an
existing master recording.

### Sample Clearance Workflow

```
Step 1 — Rights identification
  → Identify the sampled recording: title, recording date, apparent label of record
  → For the composition: identify publisher via ASCAP/BMI/SESAC/PRO database search
    or MLC search (for US mechanicals); search liner notes, Discogs, AllMusic
  → For the master: identify label from original release; confirm current owner
    (catalog rights change hands via acquisitions — the original label may no longer
    control the recording)

Step 2 — Locate current rights-holders and initiate clearance
  → Master: contact label licensing department; or artist's management if artist-owned
  → Composition: contact publisher's licensing department; HFA (now operating as part
    of MLC ecosystem) for mechanical-only contexts
  → Priority: identify both rights-holders before initiating either clearance —
    clearing one side without the other wastes negotiating time and budget

Step 3 — Negotiate and document each clearance independently
  → No statutory rate governs master-recording sample fees; rates are commercially
    negotiated. Common structures: flat fee, royalty participation (points on the
    new release), or flat fee plus points
  → Composition clearance: flat fee for mechanical rights plus synchronization rights
    if synchronized to video
  → Confirm scope: territory (worldwide or defined territories), term (life of copyright
    or fixed term), media (all uses or specific release)

Step 4 — Chain-of-title verification on the sampled work
  → Confirm the rights-holder's chain of title is clean (no upstream blocking defects)
  → A clearance granted by a party who does not actually own the rights is not valid

Step 5 — Recursive clearance discipline
  → If the sampled work itself contains a sample, those clearances must be current
    and in force — your license from the grantor does not flow through a broken
    upstream chain

Step 6 — Documentation before release
  → Obtain executed written clearance agreements for both master and composition
    before the new work is released or distributed
  → Wire the sample credits correctly in metadata and liner notes per clearance terms
```

### Public Domain — Complexity Note

US compositions published before a date that advances annually with the public domain
have entered the US public domain for the composition. However:
- The sound recording term is governed by a different, more complex schedule (under the
  MMA / Title II, pre-1972 recordings receive federal protection for 95 years from first
  publication)
- Public domain determination for recordings is fact-specific and jurisdiction-specific
- Route public-domain determination for specific works to qualified copyright counsel;
  do not rely on a general rule without verification

## Digital-Era Licensing Architecture

### Non-Interactive vs. Interactive Streaming

**Non-interactive digital performance (e.g., Pandora, iHeart Radio digital):**
- Master recording: governed by the statutory digital performance license under 17 USC
  § 114; royalties collected and distributed by SoundExchange
- Composition: covered by blanket licenses from ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, or GMR

**Interactive streaming (e.g., Spotify, Apple Music, Amazon Music Unlimited, YouTube Music):**
- Master recording: requires a direct license from the rights-holder (negotiated; no
  statutory rate for interactive use)
- Composition: covered by the MLC blanket license (effective January 1, 2021) with
  rates set by the CRB (Phonorecords IV proceedings)

### SoundExchange Distribution Mechanics

For non-interactive digital performance royalties, SoundExchange distributes the
statutory master-recording royalty in the following statutory allocation:
- **50%** to the label (or master rights-holder)
- **45%** to the featured recording artist(s)
- **5%** to a fund for non-featured performers (session musicians and background vocalists)

Critical identification point: the **45% featured-artist share** flows directly from
SoundExchange to the featured artist and is **not recoupable** against a label advance.
It bypasses the artist's royalty account with the label entirely. This is a material
distinction from interactive streaming royalties, which are paid to the label and flow
through the artist's recoupable royalty account.

Artists who have not registered directly with SoundExchange do not receive the 45%
direct payment — it is not automatically forwarded through the label. Registration at
soundexchange.com is a prerequisite to direct collection.

### YouTube / Content ID Architecture

YouTube operates two parallel licensing tracks:
1. **Composition (performing rights):** blanket licenses with ASCAP, BMI, SESAC,
   and direct agreements with publishers
2. **Master recording (Content ID):** a rights-management system where rights-holders
   upload reference audio files; YouTube's Content ID system identifies user uploads that
   match and routes ad revenue to the rights-holder (or triggers a block or takedown)

A Content ID claim is a platform administrative mechanism — not a copyright infringement
proceeding and not a legal finding. Disputed claims route through YouTube's rights-manager
dispute process.

Labels and distributors typically enroll masters into Content ID; independent artists
without a distributor's Content ID enrollment do not have automatic Content ID protection.

### Direct DSP Licensing and Label Agreements

Major DSPs negotiate direct license agreements with major labels separate from the MLC
blanket for compositions. These agreements may include:
- Minimum guarantee or advance structures against future royalties
- Most-favored-nations provisions
- Equity participation clauses (label receives DSP equity in exchange for catalog access)
- Preferred placement or algorithmic treatment provisions

The specific terms of major-label DSP agreements are not publicly disclosed. The agent
does not infer specific terms not in evidence; identify the category of provision
(direct license, potential equity component) and route details to qualified counsel.

### AI-Generated Music and Copyright (Currency-Sensitive)

The US Copyright Office's guidance (as of this agent's knowledge cutoff) holds that
copyright does not protect works generated entirely by AI without human authorship
contributions. The threshold for "sufficient human authorship" in AI-assisted compositions
is unsettled and actively evolving through Copyright Office guidance and early case law.

**Flag as currency-sensitive:** route any question about registrability, ownership, or
licensing of AI-assisted or AI-generated music to qualified copyright counsel with
current-guidance verification. Do not represent the doctrine as settled.

## Contract Formation Traps

### Letters of Intent and Deal Memos

A letter of intent (LOI), deal memo, or heads of agreement is frequently presented as
preliminary — but if it contains the material economic terms of a deal and is executed
by both parties, courts may treat it as a binding contract.

**Identification factors** — assess whether:
- The LOI/memo contains essential terms: parties, subject matter, economic terms, term/duration
- Clear "no binding effect until formal agreement" language is present (and whether it is
  sufficiently express to override a binding-intent reading)
- A defined expiration date is present (absence creates ongoing ambiguity)
- The LOI addresses what happens if a formal agreement is never executed (is either party
  bound to negotiate in good faith?)
- "Conditioned on execution of a formal agreement" language is present

Whether a specific LOI creates binding obligations is a legal opinion — route to qualified
counsel. The agent identifies the factors; it does not opine on enforceability.

### Oral Agreements

Under 17 USC § 204, a transfer of copyright ownership (assignment or exclusive license)
must be in writing signed by the copyright owner. Oral agreements purporting to transfer
or exclusively license copyright are unenforceable for that transfer.

Shorter-term, non-exclusive service arrangements may be enforceable as oral agreements
under general contract law depending on jurisdiction, but produce uncertain terms, disputed
facts, and litigation risk disproportionate to the convenience they provide.

Practical identification: "we agreed verbally" → identify (a) whether the agreement
involved a copyright transfer or exclusive license (writing required under § 204), and
(b) what jurisdiction's contract law would govern (Statute of Frauds analysis is
jurisdiction-specific). Route analysis to qualified counsel.

### Side Letters

A side letter is a separate written agreement that modifies or supplements the terms of a
principal agreement. Identify:
- Whether the side letter is incorporated by reference into the main agreement, or stands
  independent with a conflict-resolution provision
- Which governs in case of contradiction between the main agreement and side letter
- Whether all parties with authority have executed the side letter
- Whether the side letter affects the rights-grant scope in the main agreement (a
  modification of a copyright grant scope via side letter without proper execution can
  create a chain-of-title gap or an unregistered transfer)

A side letter that modifies a copyright grant is a "transfer of copyright ownership"
within 17 USC § 204 — it must be in writing signed by the rights-holder to be effective.

### Option Agreements and First-Look Deals

**Option agreement:** grants the optionee the right (not the obligation) to enter into a
defined deal during a specified period.

**Identification checklist:**
- The option price (what the optionee pays to hold the option open — may be recoupable
  against the exercise price, or non-recoupable)
- The exercise period (start date, end date, and how exercise is triggered — written
  notice? affirmative act?)
- The terms of the underlying deal if the option is exercised (are they fully specified
  in the option agreement, or subject to further negotiation?)
- Exclusivity (does the option prevent the grantor from dealing with others during the
  option period?)
- Automatic lapse on non-exercise (confirm there is a clear lapse mechanism — absence
  creates perpetual option ambiguity)

**First-look deal:** a separate provision requiring the artist to offer each new project
to the counterparty for a defined period (typically 30–90 days) before offering it to
third parties. Identify the covered scope (which types of projects), the offer period,
and the pass/match mechanism.

## Recoupment Accounting — Structural Mechanics

Understanding the recoupment mechanism is essential to assessing when and whether an artist
will receive royalties under a recording agreement.

### Unrecouped Position

An artist is "unrecouped" when the balance in their royalty account with the label is
negative — the label has credited advances and recoupable costs to the account faster
than royalties have accrued. Being unrecouped does NOT mean the artist owes money to the
label (absent a specific clawback or overpayment provision). It means royalties have not
yet exceeded the recoverable investment.

### Accounting Period Mechanics

Labels typically account quarterly or semi-annually, issuing a royalty statement for each
period. Key mechanics to identify:

**Reserve against royalties:** labels withhold a percentage of royalties earned each period
as a "reserve against returns" (returns of physical product) or against potential
adjustments. The reserve is released in subsequent accounting periods (typically over
2–4 periods). An open-ended reserve, a non-releasing reserve, or an extraordinarily
high holdback percentage is a red-flag clause. Identify the stated reserve percentage and
the release schedule explicitly.

**Statement dispute window:** most recording agreements require that a royalty statement
be disputed within a defined window from the statement date (commonly one to three years).
Failure to dispute within the window is sometimes treated contractually as acceptance of
the statement. Audit rights must be exercised within the audit window to surface
discrepancies — this is why audit-rights adequacy is a Dimension 5 hard gate.

### Cross-Collateralization — Structural Impact

When multiple projects are cross-collateralized, the royalty deficit from an underperforming
project is netted against earnings from a successful project before any royalties are paid.

**Identification checklist for cross-collateralization scope:**
1. Which projects or albums are cross-collateralized? (This album only? All albums under
   this agreement? All agreements with this label entity?)
2. Which rights are cross-collateralized? (Recording royalties only? Publishing
   participation from 360 provisions? Merchandise income?)
3. Is the cross-collateralization between different artists on the label's roster? (A
   joint-venture entity that cross-collateralizes one artist's unrecouped balance against
   another's royalties is a high-severity flag)
4. Is there a sunset on cross-collateralization (does it end after a defined term)?

### Identification Questions for Recoupment Review

Before assessing recoupment structure, obtain and answer these:
1. What is the stated advance? Is it all-in (including recording costs) or does it
   exclude recording costs?
2. Are recording costs separately recoupable on top of the stated advance?
3. Are marketing costs, video costs, independent promotion costs, and tour support
   separately recoupable? Is each category specifically enumerated or covered by an
   open-ended definition?
4. Is the recoupable-cost definition open-ended ("all costs incurred by the label in
   connection with the artist and recordings") or specifically enumerated (closed list)?
   Open-ended definitions are a red-flag clause.
5. What is the stated reserve percentage, and what is the release schedule?
6. Is there cross-collateralization, and what is the scope?

## Music Copyright — Registrations and Practical Steps

Copyright is automatic on fixation in the US (no registration required for the right to
exist). However, registration has material enforcement consequences:

**Registration as prerequisite to suit:** for US works, registration (or a refusal by the
Copyright Office) is required before filing an infringement lawsuit in US federal court
(17 USC § 411, as clarified by Fourth Estate Public Benefit Corp. v. Wall-Street.com, LLC,
139 S. Ct. 881 (2019)). Foreign works (non-US nationals or Berne-country authors) are
exempt from this requirement.

**Statutory damages and attorney fees:** available ONLY if the registration was made before
the infringement occurred OR within three months of first publication. Without timely
registration, only actual damages and profits are available — which are often low and
expensive to prove in music copyright cases.

**Practical registration workflow:**
```
Step 1 — Identify the copyright type
  → Composition (musical work): register on Form PA (performing arts) at copyright.gov
  → Sound recording (master): register on Form SR at copyright.gov (or Form SR for both
    composition and sound recording together if the same owner controls both)

Step 2 — Registration timing discipline
  → For commercial releases: register within 3 months of first publication to preserve
    statutory damages and attorney fees eligibility
  → Batch registration: up to 750 unpublished works or 10 published works of the same
    type may be registered as a group in some categories — route specifics to counsel

Step 3 — Supplementary registration (Form CA)
  → Used to correct or amplify an earlier registration; does NOT reset the registration
    date for statutory-damages purposes

Step 4 — Record assignment/transfer documents
  → Recordation of transfers and licenses with the Copyright Office (17 USC § 205)
    provides constructive notice and establishes priority in competing-transfer scenarios
```

**Chain-of-title maintenance:** for each owned work, confirm:
- Registration certificate matches the named owner
- All transfers and assignments from the original author to current owner are documented
  and (ideally) recorded with the Copyright Office
- Split-sheet agreements among co-writers are executed and in possession of all parties
- No pending dispute or adverse claim

## Royalty Statement Audit Protocol — Identification Only

The audit right in a recording agreement is the enforcement mechanism for royalty
obligations. Without it, there is no practical way to verify that royalty statements
are accurate.

**Standard audit-right components to identify:**
1. Who may audit (artist, artist's representative, or designated accountant)?
2. What records are within scope (the label's books and records for the specific artist/
   project; sub-licensee records; digital platform data)?
3. What is the audit window — how many years from the royalty statement date may the
   artist audit?
4. How many times per year may an audit be conducted (frequency cap)?
5. Who pays the audit cost? (Artist typically bears cost; some agreements shift costs
   to the label if a defined underpayment percentage is found)
6. What is the notice period required before commencement of an audit?

**Shortfall and recovery discipline:**
Some agreements require that if an underpayment is discovered below a defined threshold,
the artist bears audit costs. If underpayment exceeds a defined threshold (e.g., 5–10%
of royalties owed), audit costs shift to the label. Identify the threshold and the
cost-allocation trigger.

**Audit window vs. dispute window:** these are separate provisions. The audit window
governs how far back an audit may reach into the label's records. The statement-dispute
window governs how long after receiving a statement the artist may raise an objection.
Both must be identified independently; a narrow audit window combined with a narrow
dispute window can effectively eliminate audit-right enforceability.
