# PLMKR Production Fundamentals — Sourcing, Deals & Budget

Domain bedrock for PRODUCER-CONNECT: how beats are licensed and sourced, how producer/engineer compensation is structured, and how a recording budget is built and held. PLMKR-owned original. All money figures are **ESTIMATE / NOT QUOTABLE** — rates drift with market, tier, geography, and leverage; verify against current deal comparables before any commitment.

## Beat Licensing — the four structures

| Structure | Typical range (ESTIMATE) | What it grants | Where it fits |
|-----------|--------------------------|----------------|---------------|
| **Non-exclusive lease** | $20–100 | You may use the beat; the producer keeps selling it to others. Capped stream/distribution limits. No sync, no major-label use. | Emerging artists, non-priority releases, demos |
| **Exclusive lease** | $200–2,000+ | Only you use the beat going forward; higher/uncapped limits. Producer still owns the master — you license it. | Priority independent releases |
| **Exclusive buyout** | $500–10,000+ | Full ownership transfer of the master (beat); you own it outright. Required for most sync and label deals. | Artists who want catalog control |
| **Custom production** | negotiated (emerging $200–500, developing $500–2,500, established $2,500–25,000+) | A beat made specifically for you. **Always negotiate ownership upfront** — who owns the master. | Career-investment releases |

**Producer agreement essentials** (every production relationship needs these in writing): master ownership; publishing split of the composition (a producer often seeks ~50% of publishing — this is a co-write/publishing question, flag to Publishing); royalty split on commercial release; credit billing ("Produced by [Name]"); exclusivity terms; sync rights; **sample-clearance liability** (who is responsible if undisclosed samples surface); stem delivery requirements.

**Sample clearance.** If a beat contains samples: the producer must disclose all of them; uncleared samples cannot be released commercially without clearance (cost $500–10,000+ depending on the source recording's profile). Get written confirmation that all samples are cleared, and confirm stems show no uncleared content **before** paying the full production fee. Uncleared samples are a hard block on distribution — flag to legal immediately.

## Beat Sourcing — where and how

- **BeatStars** — largest global marketplace; every genre and price point; lease and exclusive options; direct producer relationships. Best for all tiers.
- **Airbit** — similar; strong hip-hop/R&B catalog; built-in licensing dashboard.
- **SoundClick** — older, large catalog; good for underground producers.
- **Direct from producers** — Instagram ([genre] + "producer"), YouTube type-beat channels, SoundCloud communities. Direct contact is preferred: better deals and a real relationship.

**Global producer community by genre (illustrative landscape — for orientation, not endorsement):** Afrobeats (Nigeria/Ghana and the UK/Canada diaspora), Amapiano (South Africa), US and Canadian hip-hop/trap, UK drill, R&B (Toronto and US schools), pop (melodic-construction schools), K-pop (label in-house teams, hard to reach directly), Latin/reggaeton, and electronic. Access is mostly through Instagram DMs, management, or the label system; match the producer's documented credits to the song's genre, sonic target, and the artist's tier — never default on name recognition alone.

## AI Beat Generation — reference-first

AI generation tools (text-to-music) are appropriate as a **starting point**: generate many reference ideas fast, sketch a structure a human producer will substantially develop, or brief a real producer with a generated reference. They are **not** finished production elements without substantial human curation and revision.

Use them to: describe the sound in detail (genre, mood, tempo, instruments), generate variations, and use the output as a reference for a producer brief or a demo skeleton. **Rights caution:** the ownership and commercial-use status of AI-generated material is a live legal/contractual question — who owns the output, whether training data creates third-party claims, what the platform's current terms allow. Always check current terms before any commercial release, and flag rights/ownership to A&R, Publishing, and legal before committing AI-assisted material to a release. Beat does not make the rights call — it flags and defers.

## Producer & Engineer Economics

### Payment for work vs. participation in outcome

Creative-talent compensation runs between two poles — the **flat fee** (defined scope, paid regardless of outcome, no residual interest) and **full participation** (no/low upfront, a share of everything the work earns). Almost every real deal is a hybrid. The central question is never "how much does this cost?" but **"who bears the risk if it underperforms, and who captures the upside if it succeeds?"**

| Structure | Bears project risk | Captures upside | Cash at production |
|-----------|--------------------|-----------------|--------------------|
| Flat fee (work-for-hire) | Commissioner | Commissioner | High |
| Advance + points | Shared | Shared (after recoupment) | Moderate |
| Pure participation (points, no advance) | Talent | Shared | Low |
| Hybrid (sub-market fee + reduced points) | Shared, unevenly | Shared, unevenly | Moderate |

### Advances and recoupment

An advance is a **pre-payment against future earnings, not extra money.** It is earned back ("recouped") from the talent's royalty share before any further royalties flow to them.

```
Royalties payable to talent = (Revenue × talent royalty rate) − Unrecouped advance balance
If negative, the talent receives nothing; the negative balance is the unrecouped advance.
```

Document the **recoupment basis** (which revenue streams; gross vs. net; which deductions), whether interest applies (usually not — state it), and whether the advance is **cross-collateralized** with other projects (must be disclosed in writing before signing). A project that never recoups is not automatically a bad deal — it is a bad deal only when the talent did not understand the recoupment condition at signing.

### Work-for-hire vs. royalty — the ownership consequence

A flat fee paid **without an explicit work-for-hire clause does not automatically transfer ownership** — one of the most common and most preventable ownership disputes. Master ownership must be explicitly assigned in every deal; payment alone is not assignment.

- Use **work-for-hire** for commodity/standardized execution where the contributor's input is craft, not artistic interpretation (clean ownership, no ongoing obligation).
- Use **royalty-bearing** where the contributor's artistic identity is central to the outcome (aligns incentives).

### Role-by-role conventions (typical, not binding — ESTIMATE)

| Role | Typical structure | Rationale |
|------|-------------------|-----------|
| Producer | Points (established) / flat fee (emerging) / hybrid (mid-tier). Established producers may negotiate ~3–5 points on a major-label deal; emerging ~1–3 — ESTIMATE. Points are paid **from** the label/commissioner's royalty share, not added on top. | Producer's creative contribution materially shapes the master's commercial value |
| Co-producer | Reduced points; split negotiated with lead producer from a **fixed** pool | Subset of the full production |
| Mixing engineer | Flat fee / work-for-hire | High-skill post-production service; not the primary commercial determinant. Specify track count, revision rounds included, and what counts as a revision |
| Mastering engineer | Flat fee / work-for-hire | Final technical prep; the last defense against a Dim-4 delivery failure. Brief it with the current platform spec and distributor format requirements |
| Session musicians | Flat fee + **work-for-hire always** | A performer without a signed WFH clause retains a potential copyright interest — a documented dispute pattern. Confirm union (scale floors, residuals, session reports) vs. non-union |
| Additional programmer / beatmaker | Flat fee if WFH; royalty if effectively a co-producer | If the beat is the foundation, they are a co-producer — and may have crossed into a co-write (flag to Publishing) |

### Casting reconciliation — rate against budget

A casting brief reconciles **credit fit, sonic fit, rate vs. budget, structure fit, availability, and collaboration history** into an auditable decision. When a preferred talent's rate exceeds the allocation, exactly three resolution paths exist — and a gap "resolved" without documenting one of them is merely deferred to invoicing:

1. **Scope reduction** — the talent does a subset at their standard rate; the rest goes to a different talent.
2. **Structure shift** — reduce the flat fee below standard in exchange for a participation element (points).
3. **Budget reallocation** — accept a higher allocation for this role and reduce another line, with the quality/schedule consequence named.

### Deal-structure failure modes (preventable, independent of tier)

Verbal-only agreement; ambiguous ownership (fee with no WFH clause); undefined recoupment basis; points not attached to a base ("3 points" of *what*?); undefined scope; undisclosed cross-collateralization; and "we'll figure out the deal after we hear how it sounds" (leverage shifts once the work is done). The **"we're friends" discount** is the most common source of undocumented deals — a deal memo between collaborators is not distrust; it is the record that lets a misremembered term be resolved without damaging the relationship.

## Studio & Recording Budget

### The budget is a constraint map, not a spending list

A budget answers four questions before a dollar moves: **the total envelope**, the **non-negotiable cost floors** (minimum to meet delivery spec), **where leverage is** (which phases return the most quality/risk-reduction per dollar), and the **contingency reserve**. It governs three resources — money, **time** (studio billing is time, so a schedule slip is a budget event), and quality (an output of spending money and time on the right things, not an independent dial).

### Phase allocation and the irreversibility principle

A recording moves through pre-production → tracking → overdubs → mix → mastering, plus contingency above the phase total. **Leverage is highest upstream:** every arrangement decision made in pre-production is one not made on a high-rate studio clock; tracking sets the quality ceiling (mix cannot exceed what was tracked; mastering cannot fix tracking problems). A dollar saved at the wrong phase costs two to fix at the next.

### Studio tiers — what a day rate reflects

A day rate pays for room acoustics, monitoring accuracy, outboard equipment, console, engineering staff, isolation, and archival — not just a room. Tiers (ESTIMATE): premier/flagship → commercial mid-tier → independent/boutique → self-build/in-the-box. The common calibration error at early/mid career is spending on **room prestige** while compressing on producer quality, pre-production depth, or mix revisions — performance and arrangement determine whether a recording succeeds, not the room.

**Studio costs by market (ESTIMATE / NOT QUOTABLE — verify current):** Toronto ~$75–300/hr, $500–1,500/day · New York ~$150–500/hr, $1,000–3,000/day · Los Angeles ~$150–500/hr, $1,000–3,000/day · London ~£100–400/hr, £800–2,500/day · Lagos ~$50–200/day · Accra ~$40–160/day. **Maximize studio time:** arrive prepared (lyrics written, references ready), warm up before arriving, work with a known engineer, and record more than you need.

### Contingency and reconciliation

Reserve contingency as a **structural line** (not "padding"), sized to uncertainty (low ~8–12%, medium ~12–18%, high ~18–25% — ESTIMATE), kept ≥10% through tracking close, and **documented when drawn**. Reconcile actuals vs. projected continuously and at each phase close, tracking **commitments as well as invoices** (a booked session is a cost before the invoice arrives). Reconciling only at project end guarantees a surprise.

### Change-order discipline

Every change to an approved project is a change order — there are no "small changes." Each is documented with the **money / time / quality** consequence named (all three evaluated even if only one applies) and approved in writing **before** the work proceeds. "We'll fit it in" without naming what it displaces is an undisclosed quality reduction. Pre-define the trigger list (arrangement decisions deferred to tracking, takes beyond the session plan, parts added beyond the locked arrangement, re-tracking, vocal re-dos after mix begins, revision rounds beyond the included count, format additions at mastering, stem-delivery non-compliance, mid-production brief revision) so a trigger is recognized, not absorbed.

### Tier-aware response depth (Playmaker)

- **GOLD** — non-exclusive leasing, BeatStars navigation, AI references for demos, a basic producer-agreement checklist. Tone: budget-conscious, first production steps.
- **PLATINUM** — exclusive licensing strategy, custom-production negotiation, producer relationships, studio-booking strategy, sample-clearance awareness. Tone: career-investment level.
- **DIAMOND** — top-producer relationships, custom production at scale, publishing-split awareness (route to Publishing), catalog production strategy, international co-production. Tone: hitmaker-level, globally connected.

**Local / National / International:** local producer community and studios → national network and showcases → global network, remote/cross-timezone collaboration, and cross-cultural production fusion.
