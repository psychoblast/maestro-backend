## Part 1 — Pre-Production Workflow

### What pre-production actually is

Pre-production is every decision that is cheaper to make before the tracking clock starts. The tracking clock bills by the hour; pre-production should cost a fraction of that because it uses a demo setup, a simple DAW sketch, or even a voice-memo pass. The standard ROI: one hour of pre-production typically prevents two to four hours of tracking-rate correction, because an arrangement decision deferred to the tracking session is made under time pressure, often with the artist fatigued, at full studio cost.

**The pre-production deliverable is an arrangement map** — a document that answers, per section (intro / verse / pre-chorus / chorus / bridge / outro), each of the following:

- **Structure:** order and bar count of every section (locked)
- **Tempo and key:** specific BPM (locked) and key verified against the vocalist's range — a vocalist pushed to the edge of their range produces physically different tone, not a performance style
- **Instrumentation:** which instruments are present and any switching between sections
- **Energy arc:** where dynamic peaks occur and *how the arrangement achieves them* (density changes, register shifts, drop-outs, re-entries — not "it builds" or "it gets big")
- **Vocal arrangement:** lead, harmonies, doubles, and ad libs by section, not "add harmonies where it feels good"
- **Production references:** per section if the sonic model changes (e.g., "Verse = sparse, reference [artist/title] verse 1; Chorus = anthemic, reference [artist/title] chorus")

A locked arrangement map converts "I'll know it when I hear it" from a session problem into a pre-production solve. It is the primary evidence for a Dim-3 (Song/Arrangement Readiness) score above 7.

### Pre-production session protocol

Three pre-production sessions typical for a single targeting studio-quality production:

| Session | Goal | Output |
|---------|------|--------|
| 1 — Reference and concept | Artist and producer listen together to ≥5 references; map the target territory; establish key and tempo range | Reference shortlist (≥3, each annotated with artist + title + specific sonic element), initial sonic territory statement |
| 2 — Structure and arrangement | Track a scratch demo at production-quality BPM; lock structure; build the arrangement map | Signed arrangement map with final BPM, key, section-by-section breakdown |
| 3 — Pre-production demo | Full-arrangement demo at tracking quality (not necessarily polished, but representative of every layer); A/B against references | Pre-pro demo signed off by artist and producer; brief finalized and signed |

Session 3 is optional but strongly recommended: it surfaces arrangement problems — a section that felt logical on paper that collapses when heard in sequence — before the tracking clock starts. An arrangement revision in pre-production costs nothing. In tracking, it is a budget and schedule event.

### "Production-ready" minimum gate before booking tracking dates

A project is not ready to book a tracking session unless all of the following are confirmed in writing:

- BPM locked and verified against the references (not estimated)
- Key verified against the vocalist's range — have them sing the top note of the chorus at tempo before booking dates; the tracking day is too late to discover the song is in the wrong key
- Arrangement map signed by artist and producer
- ≥3 annotated references in writing (artist + title + element per the brief standard)
- Beat or backing track confirmed deliverable at session BPM in 24-bit stems, with ownership documentation confirming it is custom or exclusively licensed
- Vocalist's warm-up protocol agreed (a vocalist who routinely blows out after two hours needs a morning session slot; a vocalist who takes forty minutes to peak needs warm-up time built into the session, not subtracted from it)
- Studio booked with a confirmed engineer who has reviewed the references and pre-pro demo
- Headphone mix preference confirmed with the vocalist (preferences vary: some need the lead loud in cans, some prefer a nearly dry monitor mix; asking on the day is a setup delay billed at studio rate)

## Part 2 — Tracking Session Management

### Pre-session setup (before the clock starts)

The costliest tracking waste is setup time billed at session rate. Setup that can happen before the billable session should:

- DAW session template pre-loaded: named tracks, correct sample rate, routing pre-built, talkback configured
- Pre-pro stems and backing track imported and tempo-locked in the session
- Reference mix available for playback on the room's monitoring system
- Headphone mix rough-configured per the vocalist's stated preference
- Lyric display or printed lyrics visible without the artist holding paper (holding paper limits physical performance)
- Safety-take destination track created and labeled before recording begins

### Take types and their purposes

| Take type | Definition | When to use |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **Tracking take** | Full performance from top to tail | Primary take type; full takes preserve the phrasing arc and emotional momentum that sectional punches lose. Target ≥4–6 full takes before selective punching. |
| **Safety take** | A separated, untouched copy immediately after recording | Create one before any editing begins. Export to a separate track/group; never cut into the safety. |
| **Punch take** | Targeted re-record of a specific problem line or section | Efficient for fixing isolated issues; but overdone, punches accumulate phrasing inconsistencies between sections — prefer a full take when the artist has energy. |
| **Alt-arrangement take** | A performance at a different phrasing rhythm, emphasis, or note choice on the hook | These are creative options, not corrections; label clearly ("Alt-hook-v2") so the comp session knows exactly what they are and does not confuse them with a flawed take. |

### Session sign-off protocol (before the engineer closes the session)

- Safety take confirmed, separated, and labeled
- All takes named and labeled by round and take number (not "audio001_comp3_FINAL2")
- Session saved and backed up to a second location **before the artist leaves the room** — a session that exists only on the studio drive is one power event from a full re-track
- Take notes logged immediately: the producer's verbal direction per take, preferred moments, any lyric or phrasing variants — notes written three days later are from memory, not observation, and introduce errors

### Vocal direction — the communication discipline

Give direction in the form of a feeling or an image, not a technical instruction. "Give me more emotion" is not direction — it tells the artist what you want more of but not what that feels like or where it comes from. "Give me the feeling of [specific reference moment — artist/song/section]" is direction. "Louder" is not direction; "give me the weight of someone who knows they are right" produces the volume as a by-product.

Technical direction (timing, pitch, phrasing rhythm) is the producer's internal read, translated into creative language before it reaches the artist. The exception: precise timing corrections ("come in on beat 2, not the and-of-1") are objective and should be stated technically. Aesthetic corrections should not be.

## Part 3 — Vocal Production

### Comp session structure

A **comp** (composite vocal) is the selection and assembly of the best moments from all tracking takes into a single master vocal track. Comping is a production decision, not an engineering task — the producer makes the creative calls; the engineer executes the edits.

**Comp session workflow:**
1. Listen to all takes in full before selecting anything — back-to-back without stopping, with no selection pressure from an early strong take that biases what follows
2. Identify 2–3 "keeper" takes: the ones where the emotional character is correct overall
3. Build a rough comp from those keepers, section by section, referencing the full-take performances for continuity
4. Play the rough comp through once without stopping
5. Address phrasing inconsistencies between sections — different takes have different push-pull on the grid, and these need alignment so the vocal sits on the production naturally
6. Do not over-polish: remove technical problems (timing, pitch), but leave intentional phrasing variations (breath timing, dynamics, subtle pitch inflections at phrase ends) that are part of the performance — heavy smoothing produces a vocal that passes QC but loses the thing that makes the performance interesting

### Pitch correction — approach, extent, disclosure

**Transparent pitch correction** (correcting out-of-place notes while preserving the performance's timing and formant character) is standard practice at every tier — it is an engineering tool, not an indicator of a problematic performance.

**Stylized pitch correction** (the hard-quantized, pitch-snapped effect associated with trap, hyperpop, and specific R&B sub-genres) is a creative choice, not a correction. When applied, it is a sonic signature that must be agreed in the creative brief before tracking — it cannot be reversed after the fact.

**The disclosure rule:** the artist must know the extent of pitch processing applied to their vocal and approve it before delivery. This is a practical rule, not merely an ethical one — an artist who hears their release and does not recognize their own voice is a production crisis. Pitch processing extent should be disclosed at the mix-approval stage, not discovered on the release.

**Pitch correction order of operations:**
1. Align timing first (quantize or elastic audio where needed) — pitch correction applied to a badly timed vocal locks the timing into an artifact-laden result that cannot be cleanly reversed
2. Coarse pitch correction: note-by-note to the correct pitch destination
3. Fine pitch correction: intonation within notes, vibrato management, formant preservation
4. Verification: listen at normal playback speed with the unprocessed take side-by-side — is the emotional character of the performance preserved?

The worst outcome is a vocal that passes technical QC but sounds like no one performed it.

### Layering and texture

A **double-track** (a second performance of the same line by the same vocalist) has a fundamentally different character than a pitch-shifted or time-stretched duplicate. The micro-timing variations and natural pitch micro-deviation between two real performances create a chorus effect that a single duplicated track cannot achieve — the phase relationship is never perfectly constructive, and that partial-cancellation is what creates the width sensation.

Diagnostic: if the double is audible as a separate voice, it is either too loud in the mix or the two performances differ too significantly in phrasing rhythm. A well-integrated double is not heard as two voices — it is felt as weight and width.

**Vocal stack layers by function:**

| Layer | Purpose | Mix note |
|-------|---------|----------|
| Lead | Carries the melody and lyric; all direction, correction, and attention serves this track | Only the lead gets the full pitch/timing workflow; every other layer is referenced against the lead |
| Double-track | Adds width and weight without an audible "two voices" character | Panned left and right (ESTIMATE ~±15–30% width); typically 3–6 dB below lead — adjust until the double is felt but not heard |
| Harmony | Adds harmonic richness; reinforces the emotional register | Comped and pitched with the same discipline as the lead; voiced above and below the lead rather than in unison |
| Ad libs | Textural and emotional fills; improvised or arranged between phrases | Often 2–3 stacked performances; group-bussed for their own reverb and level treatment, kept low enough to support but not compete with the lead |
| Spoken word / rap feature | Different articulation pattern and usually different proximity character | Brief the engineer on the performance before the session; a rapper and a singer typically need different headphone mix setups and different mic placement |

## Part 4 — Mixing Workflow and Discipline

### Mix session setup checklist

A mix session does not start without:

- **Mix reference** loaded in the session and calibrated to the room level — the reference from the creative brief, confirmed mono-compatible, confirmed at playback level-matched to the rough mix
- **Gain structure verified** — every individual track reaching the mix bus at a healthy pre-fader level (ESTIMATE: input averaging around −18 to −14 dBFS before fader rides); every bus summing with headroom to avoid clipping before the master bus
- **Phase relationships checked** — any parallel-recorded instrument (two microphones on a guitar amp, DI and amp on bass, multiple room mics on drums) phase-checked against the primary mic before the mix begins. Phase anomalies embedded at the start of a mix become invisible with familiarity; a mono fold button in the monitoring chain, checked on the full sum on day one, prevents that
- **Mono compatibility verified** — a mono fold checked immediately for the full mix sum; the goal is to identify elements that lose significant energy or intelligibility in mono before any mix decisions are made around them

### Low-end management discipline

Low-end frequency management (fundamentals below ~250 Hz, sub-bass below ~80 Hz) is the most common source of mix failures and the dimension most exposed by inferior monitoring environments. The discipline:

- **High-pass every element that does not require low-end content.** Vocals, hi-hats, synth pads, room reverbs: high-pass. The energy below the cut is not warmth — it is frequency-domain clutter that accumulates into an opaque low-mid and inflated peak levels.
- **Bass and kick define the low-end character** — the relationship between them (which element occupies the sub register, which occupies the upper-bass punch register, how much space each has, whether they share sub-bass or divide it by frequency) is the single most consequential mix decision for low-end clarity. Make it deliberately; do not let it happen by default.
- **Sub frequencies are essentially mono.** Below approximately 80–100 Hz, stereo information is predominantly phase anomaly, not perceivable width. Apply an M/S low-pass to the side channel below ~80 Hz to keep the low end in the center, which translates to all playback systems including mono.
- **Headphone check on low-end.** A well-mixed low end holds together on consumer headphones as well as on studio monitors. If the low end falls apart on headphones, there is a phase or balance issue upstream, not a headphone limitation.

### Bus structure

Professional mix sessions use a bus architecture that groups signals before the master fader. This enables group-level processing (a single compressor across the entire drum bus rather than individual compressors per drum element), group-level rides (reducing all backing vocals simultaneously without adjusting individual tracks), and parallel processing (blending a heavily compressed copy of a drum bus beneath the dry signal for punch and density without sacrificing macro-dynamic character).

Typical architecture (adapt per genre, production style, and session):

| Bus | What feeds it | Common processing |
|-----|--------------|------------------|
| Drum bus | Kick, snare, hi-hat, overhead, room mics | Bus compression, saturation (optional), EQ |
| Percussion bus | Shakers, toms, additional percussion | EQ, parallel compression if genre-appropriate |
| Bass bus | Bass guitar DI, bass DI, 808 sub | Bass-specific compression, sidechain from kick (optional) |
| Instrumental bus | Keys, synths, guitars, pads | Aggregate level control, spatial treatment |
| Lead vocal bus | Lead vocal comp | De-essing, compression, reverb/delay send bus |
| Backing vocal bus | Doubles, harmonies, ad libs | Aggregate level, separate reverb character from lead |
| Master bus | Sum of all buses | Stereo glue processing only; should not be asked to compensate for upstream problems |

The master bus is the last creative stage, not the problem-solving stage. A master bus compressor that is working hard to control peaks is a signal that the individual bus stages are not managing gain adequately.

### Revision discipline

Revision rounds are a budget and scope item, not a creative variable. Industry convention for a single (ESTIMATE):
- 2–3 rounds of artist revisions with the artist present or signed-off in writing per round
- 1 additional technical-only round after delivery QC

A **revision** is a documented change to an approved mix after artist sign-off. "Add a little more reverb on the vocal" expressed informally after a listening session is not a revision until confirmed in writing as a requested change. Every revision request should be logged in writing before any mix change is started — this protects both the engineer and the artist.

"Final version" naming discipline: never name a file "FINAL" — use versioned naming with a date and context marker (e.g., `SongTitle_Mix_v4_ArtistApproval_20260618.wav`). "FINAL" is a name that is always wrong by the time it matters.

## Part 5 — Mastering Essentials

### What mastering does and does not do

Mastering is the **final stage of audio preparation before distribution** — it is not a second mix and cannot fix mixing problems.

**What a mastering engineer does (in order of typical priority):**
1. **Tonal balance correction** — a calibrated final evaluation on a trusted monitoring system, addressing broad-spectrum balance issues that emerged from the mix environment (every mixing room imparts a character; mastering is the correction layer)
2. **Loudness management** — achieving the delivery target integrated loudness and true-peak ceiling with the best possible dynamic character at that level. A louder master at the same LUFS is a more limited, more compressed master — loudness at mastering is a tradeoff, not a free gain
3. **Stereo field finalization** — confirming the stereo image is correct and mono-compatible across multiple playback references
4. **Format preparation** — dithering (exactly once, at the final bit-depth reduction; for 24-bit delivery it is not required), sample-rate conversion if required, format encoding and file naming per spec
5. **Identifier embedding** — ISRC embedded in the file metadata, confirmed correct against the delivery brief
6. **Issuing the delivery package** — confirmed deliverable set with format verification and QC sign-off

**What mastering cannot fix:**
- A mix with a fundamentally broken frequency balance (vocal buried under reverb, bass energy masking everything below 200 Hz)
- Phase cancellations baked in at tracking or mix
- A mix delivered with 0 dBFS peaks and no headroom — a pre-limited mix gives the mastering engineer nothing to work with and produces a more compromised master, not a louder one
- Distortion introduced at tracking (a saturated preamp, an over-driven interface input) — distortion at the source is permanent
- Fundamental creative-direction errors (the wrong instrumentation palette for the genre, a vocal character mismatch) — mastering has no creative scope

### The mastering brief

The document sent to the mastering engineer with the mix file should contain:
- Target delivery format and platform spec (state as TARGET; use ESTIMATE figures for current loudness target and ceiling — the mastering engineer has current spec intelligence)
- Reference tracks for tonal character and loudness register (same references from the mix brief)
- Any existing processing on the mix bus (transparency prevents the mastering engineer duplicating compression that is already there)
- Whether stems are required and their naming spec
- The delivery deadline and version number
- Any artist-specific preference (e.g., "the artist prefers the high end relatively open rather than polished" — these are valid inputs to the mastering engineer, not overrides)

**Mix headroom for mastering handoff (ESTIMATE — confirm with the mastering engineer):** mix-bus peak levels approximately −3 to −6 dBFS integrated, −18 to −14 LUFS integrated, true-peak approximately −3 dBTP or lower — this preserves the mastering engineer's dynamic range to work with. These are pre-mastering targets, not delivery targets.

### Stem mastering vs. full-mix mastering

**Full-mix mastering** is the standard path: the mastering engineer receives a single stereo mix file. This is the correct approach for most projects — it preserves the mix engineer's balance decisions and gives the mastering engineer a clear, complete picture.

**Stem mastering** gives the mastering engineer grouped stem buses (typically: drums/percussion, bass, instrumentation, vocals) rather than a single stereo file. This is appropriate when the mix is close but one element — a vocal that is slightly too forward in one frequency range, a low-end that needs surgical adjustment — requires a targeted fix that cannot be achieved from the stereo sum without impacting everything. Stem mastering requires:

- Stems that sum to the stereo mix within a negligible tolerance (verify before sending — a stem package that does not sum correctly was edited after export and will not behave predictably)
- All stems at the same format and sample rate as the mix file
- Naming per the spec confirmed with the mastering engineer before delivery
- Stems labeled per a documented standard (not custom naming per session)

Stem mastering costs more and is not warranted for every project. Reserve it for cases where the mix has a specific issue that stems uniquely solve.

### Spatial audio and Dolby Atmos production

Spatial audio delivery (object-based immersive audio) requires a **separate production and mastering workflow** from the stereo master — the stereo master is not automatically correct for binaural rendering.

**Production-side requirements for an Atmos deliverable:**
- The mixing session must be built for object-based mixing from the start; adapting a stereo session after the fact produces a technically compliant file that does not use the format's spatial capabilities meaningfully
- Loudness target is different for immersive — ESTIMATE approximately −18 LUFS integrated for immersive versus approximately −14 to −16 LUFS for stereo streaming; verify current platform spec
- The binaural render output must be QC'd on headphones specifically; the spatial mix character is distinct from the stereo mix character and must be evaluated as a separate deliverable
- Not every project warrants an Atmos master — assess whether the genre, release profile, and production scope justify the additional cost; for emerging artists and catalog, the stereo master is the priority deliverable

## Part 6 — Genre Production Conventions

Genre conventions are the shared production language of a sonic community — the decisions that make a track legible to its audience as belonging to a genre. These are not constraints; they are the grammar. Breaking them intentionally is a production statement. Breaking them accidentally is a production error. The producer's job is to know the grammar well enough to know which one they are doing.

**All specific technical figures in this section are ESTIMATE / NOT QUOTABLE and drift with production trends — verify against recent releases in the target genre before any technical assertion.**

### Hip-hop / Trap

**BPM:** trap typically 120–150 BPM in double-time feel; effective kick/snare grid at 60–75 BPM half-time. Classic boom-bap 80–100 BPM.

**Rhythmic foundation:** the 808 sub-bass is a *melodic instrument*, not a texture — the note choice of the 808 creates harmonic content in the sub register, and the specific pitch interacts with every harmonic layer above it. The 808 and the kick are typically separate elements: the kick provides attack transient, the 808 provides sub-bass sustain. A common construction is a short transient kick layered with an 808 that slides or sustains through the phrase.

**Vocal placement:** frequently slightly behind the beat (laid back), which creates a relaxed pocket over an energetic production. Auto-pitch processing is both a correction tool (transparent) and a stylized harmonic element — both are genre-standard; the distinction must be agreed in the brief before tracking.

**Mix priorities:** 808 sub clarity in mono (below 80 Hz, the 808 must translate in mono sum); hi-hat definition with subtle velocity variation for groove; lead vocal presence above the 808 in the 1–3 kHz midrange without clouding the upper midrange.

**Common production pitfalls:** uncleared samples discovered after distribution submission (trap production has a high sample-clearance surface area); 808 note choice that creates harmonic tension with the melodic elements; over-quantized hi-hats that remove the groove that gives the beat its character.

### R&B (contemporary)

**BPM:** 70–100 BPM; often *feels* slower due to half-time rhythmic patterns and rubato vocal phrasing.

**Rhythmic foundation:** hi-hat programming is typically humanized rather than loop-locked (bar-to-bar variation is a signature of the genre); kick and snare are frequently multi-layered (two or three samples per hit — a sub layer for weight, a mid layer for snap, an outer layer for presence).

**Vocal prominence:** the vocal is the production's primary element and is typically mixed first, with the production built around the vocal's frequency range and dynamics rather than the vocal being fitted into an existing production shape.

**Mix priorities:** sub-bass present and warm but not dominant over the vocal; midrange space preserved for the vocal's fundamental range (approximately 200–2000 Hz kept relatively open); reverb character that is longer and more present than trap but controlled enough that the vocal retains intelligibility; pad layers beneath the vocal in the chorus for warmth without adding density.

**Common production pitfalls:** chord voicings that crowd the vocal's frequency range (extended voicings, 9ths and 11ths, are harmonically rich but can conflict with a dense vocal arrangement); insufficient pre-production leading to arrangement decisions made in the tracking session.

### Afrobeats / Afropop

**BPM:** 90–115 BPM typical. The rhythmic feel is often derived from a three-over-two (clave-derived) pattern: the production's groove is polyrhythmic, not a straightforward 4/4 subdivision.

**Rhythmic foundation:** percussion is a co-equal production element, not a background filler. Shaker, cowbell, talking drum samples, and toms define the genre's rhythmic identity as much as the kick and bass. Getting the percussion layer right — the note choices, the velocity variation, the interaction between individual percussion elements — is often the longest part of the beat construction.

**Vocal placement:** precise on the downbeat with melismatic extensions between downbeats; ad libs in the gaps between main phrases are a genre signature. The vocal must translate across multiple playback environments — club, mobile phone speaker, and car stereo all matter in the primary markets.

**Mix priorities:** percussion brightness and presence (shaker and cowbell need presence without inducing high-frequency fatigue over a full-length track); bass warmth without heavy sub (Afropop is a mid-first genre by production convention, not a sub-heavy genre); midrange that is open, not crowded.

**Production note:** amapiano (South African subgenre) has distinct production conventions: log-drum sub-bass figure, slower BPM (typically 112–118), more spacious arrangement. It is distinct from traditional Afropop and should be referenced as its own genre context.

### UK Drill / NY Drill

**BPM:** UK drill 138–145 BPM standard; NY drill similar range with slight rhythmic emphasis variations.

**Rhythmic foundation:** the sliding 808 glide is the genre's signature — the pitch of the 808 slides between successive notes (over 1/8 or 1/16 transitions), creating a melodic sub-bass figure that is as important as any melodic instrument. Hi-hat rolls are very present; the kick pattern is typically sparse, primarily on beats 1 and 3 with the snare on 2 and 4 and frequent snare rolls.

**Vocal placement:** monotone delivery, rhythmically dense, with close-mic proximity character. The vocal is typically drier than hip-hop — minimal reverb to maintain intelligibility in dense lyrical delivery.

**Mix priorities:** 808 slide audibility in the sub and low-mid registers; hi-hat clarity without harshness (the hi-hat presence is high but should not fatigue); vocal intelligibility in a performance that is rhythmically dense with little melodic variation.

**Common production pitfalls:** 808 slides that are too subtle to be audible on consumer speakers; hi-hat velocity patterns that are too uniform to have the characteristic swing; minor-key or ambiguous tonal melodic layers that do not complement the 808's pitch path.

### Pop (mainstream / commercial)

**BPM:** wide range, 90–130 BPM; varies significantly by sub-genre and era.

**Song structure priority:** pop production is built around the song, not the beat. Every arrangement decision is evaluated against whether it serves the hook. The verse's job is to make the chorus feel inevitable; the chorus's job is to be the reason the track exists.

**Chorus differentiation:** the chorus must sound and feel different from the verse, typically through a combination of: frequency register change (add energy in the 100–500 Hz range, which makes the chorus feel bigger on small speakers); density change (additional instrumentation or layers entering at the chorus); or rhythmic change (a shift in the rhythmic anchor pattern). Volume alone is not differentiation.

**Mix priorities:** clarity and transient definition (pop is heard on small speakers at normalize-equalized playback levels); nothing in the 100–500 Hz range that obscures the vocal in the chorus; stereo width managed so the chorus does not collapse in mono or on single-driver speakers.

**Radio edit discipline:** the radio edit (typically under 3:30, no extended intros, hook within the first 30 seconds) is usually the primary commercial deliverable for pop. The radio edit is not a shortened version of the album track — it is the primary version, built to spec. Produce it as the default; the extended version extends from it.

### Reggaeton / Latin trap

**BPM:** reggaeton 90–100 BPM. The **dembow** rhythmic pattern (kick on beat 1, snare on the and-of-2, kick on beat 3, snare on beat 4) is the genre-defining signature. Variations in the arrangement build around the dembow but the dembow itself is the floor — departing from it is a genre departure, not an arrangement variation.

**Vocal convention:** typically a hybrid of sung hook and rapped verses in a specific cadence tied to the dembow rhythm. The vocal ad lib culture (multiple layers of vocal commentary beneath the main phrase) is a production element in its own right, not an afterthought.

**Mix priorities:** kick clarity within the dembow (the pattern depends on kick definition and placement); bass that reinforces the dembow's energy without masking the kick; vocal presence in a rhythmically dense arrangement.

**Regional production note:** Latin music has strong regional production conventions — Mexican norteño, cumbia, Caribbean reggaeton, and urban Latin each have distinct sonic signatures and audience expectations. A production intended for a specific regional market should reference that market's current production conventions explicitly, not default to a generic "Latin" template.

## Part 7 — Multi-Song Production Arc (EP and Album)

### How multi-song production differs from a single

Single production optimizes one deliverable. EP and album production must achieve **cohesion** — the collection sounds intentional, not like unrelated singles assembled after the fact. Cohesion is not uniformity; tracks can differ significantly in tempo, energy, and tone. Cohesion is the sense that the tracks inhabit the same world, made by the same creative mind, for the same listener.

**Cohesion tools:**

- **Sonic thread:** at least one production element that appears consistently across the project — a specific synthesizer texture, a recurring rhythmic motif, a particular drum sound signature, the same reverb space on the lead vocal throughout. A sonic thread does not limit creative variety; it provides the connective tissue that makes variety feel intentional.
- **Reference pool consistency:** if tracks in a project were produced to entirely different reference pools with no shared target, the collection will lack cohesion regardless of individual track quality. The project-level reference session (identifying the sonic world of the project before individual-track production begins) is the key pre-production investment for a cohesive EP or album.
- **Sequencing decisions:** the first track sets the listener's expectation for the project; the transition between tracks determines whether the energy flows or interrupts; the closing track determines what the listener is left with. Sequencing is a production decision made after all tracks are produced, with the mastered collection available for evaluation — it cannot be pre-decided from an arrangement map alone.

### EP vs. album scope

| Dimension | EP (3–6 tracks) | Album (7–15+ tracks) |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Pre-production | 1–2 sessions per track + 1 project-level cohesion session | 1–2 sessions per track + 2–3 project-level sessions (world-definition, cohesion checkpoint, sequence review) |
| Budget structure | Per-track + sequencing/mastering addition | Per-track + project budget for cohesion-mixing adjustments and mastering the collection |
| Mastering deliverable | Per-track QC + inter-track level matching (the EP is a single listening experience) | Same as EP + sequencing plan + cross-track level consistency analysis + gap/segue decisions |
| Schedule risk | 2–3× the single production timeline; delays compound across tracks | Higher compounding risk; each tracking delay extends into every downstream phase; mastering slot books relative to the last track's mix approval |

### Sequencing and collection mastering

A collection mastered as individual tracks — each optimized for its own loudness without reference to the others — produces level inconsistencies between tracks. The listener experiences a volume jump or drop between songs that breaks the experience even if every individual track is technically excellent.

**Inter-track level matching** is a specific mastering deliverable for any multi-track project: the mastering engineer adjusts relative perceived loudness across the collection so that the track-to-track transitions feel consistent. State this explicitly in the mastering brief — it is not assumed.

**Gap vs. continuous play:** the collection can have silence (typically 2–4 seconds) between tracks, or tracks can segue directly (a crossfade or a continuous sonic through-line). Continuous segues require crossfade decisions made during mastering, not added after the fact. State the gap or segue intent in the mastering brief.

## Part 8 — Sync-Ready Production

### What "sync-ready" means from the production side

A track placed in sync (film, TV, advertising, games) faces requirements that go beyond the standard distribution spec. A track that is commercially releasable may still fail a sync brief if the production was not prepared for it. The sync-readiness questions are resolved at production and mix close — not at the point of the brief, which is too late.

**Sync readiness from the production side requires:**

1. **A clean session from the start.** All elements are original or fully cleared samples, confirmed in writing before the session ends. The sync editor cannot clear a sample on your behalf; the brief either clears or rejects the track based on what is in it. See the sample-clearance protocol in production-fundamentals.

2. **A split mix exported at mix close.** Most sync briefs require alternate mixes (vocal up, instrumental, dialogue-friendly, TV track). A split mix is built from the open session at mix close — it cannot be reliably reconstructed from a bounced stereo master, and the attempt to do so typically produces artifacts. The split mix decision must be made before the mix session is closed, not after.

3. **Format and duration flexibility prepared at mix stage.** Sync editors frequently need alternate edits: 15-second, 30-second, 60-second, underscore/loop versions. Re-editing from a stereo master introduces artifacts that require re-mixing; preparing the alternate-duration exports at mix stage costs an hour and prevents a full re-engagement with the session weeks or months later.

### Split mix specification

| Version | Description | Primary use |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| Full (vocal + instrumental) | Standard stereo mix | Film/TV placement, advertising |
| Instrumental | All vocals removed; no audible vocal artifacts | Background underscore, foreign-language dubbing, fitness platform licensing |
| A cappella | Vocal only, no instrumentation | Archival; rare requirement, but archive at mix close while the session is open |
| Vocal up (+3 dB) | Full mix with vocal level raised approximately 3 dB | Placements requiring vocal intelligibility above ambient noise or competing dialogue |
| Dialogue-friendly (TV track) | Mid-frequency reduced in the 200 Hz–4 kHz range; stereo width increased to push the mix to the edges | Broadcast TV placements; dialogue and narration added overtop; the center channel cleared for speech |
| Loop version | A section (typically verse or chorus, 8 or 16 bars) that loops seamlessly | Underscore use, game placements, interactive media |

The split mix set is archived with the stereo master at project close. A sync opportunity that arrives after the session is closed without alternates requires reopening the session — a budget event. For any project with sync potential, the alternate-mix cost should be in the change-order trigger list at project start.

### Music-to-picture timing considerations

Sync placements are frequently edited to picture timing after placement — the music is cut to hit specific visual moments (a scene cut, an emotional beat, a sound effect). Production decisions that aid this:

- **Clear structural boundaries.** The chorus starts on a downbeat; transitions occur at 8- or 16-bar boundaries rather than odd-length bars. Clean structure makes music-to-picture editing possible without artifacts.
- **Sections that can loop.** A verse or a chorus section that loops without audible discontinuity extends the placement without requiring a different license. Build loops with clean phrase endings and no transitional production material that breaks at the loop point.
- **No hard tempo changes** within a piece intended for linear sync placement. A tempo change inside a cue requires an edit point precisely at the change, which limits placement flexibility significantly.
- **Tail management.** The track's ending should be clean: a dedicated fade with a predictable decay window, or a hard stop with any reverb tail confirmed to silence within 2–3 seconds. A track that never definitively ends creates a practical problem for the picture editor. Export tails confirmed to silence before archiving.
