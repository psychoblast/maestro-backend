# Neighboring Rights & International Collection Mechanics

Neighboring rights are legally distinct from copyright and represent one of the most
consistently uncollected income streams in the music business. International
collection adds a second layer of complexity: money earned in other countries flows
through a chain of reciprocal society agreements before reaching the artist, with
each hand-off adding lag. This file gives the practitioner the mechanics needed to
diagnose whether the money is in transit, stuck, or simply never claimed.

---

## 1. What Neighboring Rights Are

Copyright in a musical work belongs to the songwriter and publisher (the
composition copyright). **Neighboring rights** — also called related rights or
rights related to copyright — belong to the performers and producers of the
*recording itself* (the master recording). They are legally independent of the
composition copyright and require separate registration with separate collectors.

A single play on a digital radio station therefore generates:
- Performance royalties on the **composition** → paid through PROs to publishers
  and writers
- Neighboring-rights royalties on the **master recording** → paid through
  neighboring-rights societies to the sound recording owner (label or independent
  artist) and the featured performer

Both streams are generated simultaneously by the same play. Missing the neighboring-
rights registration leaves the second stream entirely uncollected.

---

## 2. The US Neighboring-Rights Regime

**Key fact:** The United States does not recognize neighboring rights for
terrestrial (AM/FM) broadcast. US-registered sound recordings played on terrestrial
radio in the US generate no neighboring-rights royalties for the performer or
label — a unique gap not present in any other major music market.

**Where US neighboring rights DO apply:**
- **Digital audio transmissions** (satellite radio, internet radio, non-interactive
  streaming services): governed by Section 114 of the US Copyright Act; the
  designated collector is **SoundExchange**.
- **Interactive streaming services** (on-demand) compensate master rights through
  direct label/distributor deals — these are master-use royalties, not neighboring
  rights in the strict sense, but the result is the same: income on the master
  recording.

### SoundExchange — The US Neighboring-Rights Collector

SoundExchange collects and distributes digital performance royalties under the
Section 114 statutory license. The split is statutory:

| Recipient | Share |
|-----------|-------|
| Sound recording copyright owner (label/independent artist who owns the master) | 50% |
| Featured artist | 45% |
| Non-featured musicians and vocalists (administered through AFM & SAG-AFTRA funds) | 5% |

**Critical registration mechanic — two separate accounts required:**
An independent artist who both owns their masters and performed on them must
register twice with SoundExchange:
1. As a **featured artist** (to receive the 45% performer share).
2. As a **sound recording copyright owner** (to receive the 50% owner share).

A single-account registration captures only one of the two shares. Missing the
owner account leaves the 50% owner share in the holding pool. Missing the artist
account leaves the 45% performer share in the holding pool. Both amounts are
held only for a limited period before redistribution.

**Distribution lag:** SoundExchange distributes quarterly; income commonly reaches
registrants 6–9 months after usage (Tier C). Retroactive claims for periods with
completed registrations are accepted, but unclaimed money eventually ages out —
SoundExchange policy governs the specific retention window (Tier A — confirm
current policy before advising on window deadline).

**Direct licensing bypass:** Major labels commonly have direct licensing agreements
with Pandora and similar services that supersede the Section 114 statutory rate.
Independent artists without direct deals receive Section 114 statutory rates via
SoundExchange. The distinction matters for recovery analysis: an independent
artist's SoundExchange statement is the authoritative source for digital
performance income; it is not supplemented by a separate label accounting.

## 3. International Neighboring-Rights Regime

Outside the US, neighboring rights commonly extend to **terrestrial broadcast**
(not just digital). A US recording played on UK, French, or German radio generates
neighboring-rights income that the performer and label can collect — income that
does not exist for the same play on US terrestrial radio.

### Major International Collecting Societies (not exhaustive)

| Territory | Sound Recording Owner Society | Performer Society |
|-----------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| United Kingdom | PPL (combined owner/performer) | PPL |
| Germany | GVL | GVL |
| France | SCPP (labels/producers) | SPEDIDAM (session musicians), ADAMI (performers) |
| Netherlands | SENA | SENA |
| Canada | Re:Sound | Re:Sound |
| Japan | CPRA | CPRA |
| Australia | PPCA | APRA (for composers) / PPCA for masters |
| Scandinavia | Gramex (territory-specific variants) | Gramex |

**All the above are illustrative of the territory landscape; confirm current society
structures before advising on specific territory collections (Tier A).**

### Territory Split Mechanics

International neighboring-rights splits vary by territory but commonly follow an
approximate 50% (sound recording owner) / 50% (performer) structure, with the
performer portion then subdivided between featured and non-featured performers per
territory-specific rules. The US 50/45/5 statutory structure is US-specific; do
not assume it applies internationally.

### The US Artist's International Neighboring-Rights Position

A US artist who releases recordings that receive airplay internationally is
entitled to neighboring-rights income in those territories (subject to
reciprocal-agreement coverage). Collecting it requires either:

1. **Direct registration** with each territory's society (practical only for very
   active catalogs in specific markets).
2. **Reciprocal-agreement collection** through a US-affiliated or home-country
   representative who has agreements with foreign societies.
3. **International neighboring-rights aggregator** — specialist firms that register
   with multiple societies and consolidate collection for a service fee (commonly
   15–25% of collected income, Tier C).

**The gap most often missed:** Many US independent artists and smaller labels
have never registered with any international neighboring-rights society, leaving
terrestrial radio income in those territories entirely uncollected. Because there
is no US terrestrial neighboring right, the concept does not arise domestically,
and the international right goes unnoticed.

## 4. Reciprocal Society Agreement Pipeline (International Performance Royalties)

International performance royalties on the **composition** (not the master) flow
through a "double waterfall" via reciprocal PRO agreements:

```
FOREIGN USAGE (radio play / streaming in Territory X)
        ↓
TERRITORY X PERFORMING-RIGHTS SOCIETY
    collects blanket license fees from local broadcasters/services
        ↓
DISTRIBUTION CALCULATION IN TERRITORY X
    (local survey/sampling + digital usage reporting)
        ↓
REMITTANCE TO HOME-COUNTRY PRO VIA RECIPROCAL AGREEMENT
    (money sent from Territory X society to the artist's home PRO — commonly ASCAP or BMI for US writers)
        ↓
HOME PRO DISTRIBUTION CYCLE
    (home PRO distributes to the publisher, who pays the writer per agreement)
```

Each waterfall level adds lag:
- Territory collection and distribution lag: commonly 6–12 months after usage (Tier C)
- Remittance to home PRO and distribution there: additional 6–12 months (Tier C)
- **Combined typical window:** 12–24 months from usage to US artist receipt (Tier C)

**Recovery implication:** an artist who first had meaningful international usage
2 years ago may just now be receiving the first distributions from those plays.
Income within this window is in transit. Asserting underpayment before the full
lag window elapses is the HG-3 error for international performance income.

### PRO Reciprocal Agreement Coverage Gaps

Not every territory has a full reciprocal agreement with US PROs. Territories with
limited or no reciprocal agreement produce income that either:
- Accumulates in the foreign society as unmatched (recoverable only via direct
  registration).
- Gets distributed on a market-share basis to locally registered publishers, as
  unmatched pool.

**Recovery action:** for any territory showing confirmed activity but no
corresponding international performance-royalty line, check whether the home PRO
has a reciprocal agreement with that territory's society. Absent an agreement,
direct territory registration may be required to capture the income.

## 5. Sub-Publishing Mechanics

**Sub-publishing** is the arrangement by which a publisher in one territory (the
"originating publisher") appoints a local publisher in another territory (the
"sub-publisher") to administer the catalog in that market.

### Sub-Publisher Deal Types

| Type | What the sub-publisher does | Typical fee range (Tier C) |
|------|-----------------------------|---------------------------|
| Collection-only / at-source admin | Collects local royalties; no promotion | 10–20% of collections |
| Active sub-publishing | Collects AND pursues local sync, covers, placements | 15–25% of collections |
| Full sub-publishing | Collects, promotes, and may grant local licenses | 20–30% of collections |

**"At source" vs. "at receipt" accounting:** the originating publisher wants
income calculated "at source" (on the gross collected in-territory before the
sub-publisher takes their fee). The sub-publisher prefers "at receipt" (the
originating publisher's share is calculated on what arrives after the sub-
publisher's deduction). This distinction materially affects the writer's net
income. Review the sub-publishing agreement for the applicable accounting basis
before any statement analysis.

### Sub-Publishing Collection Lag Addition

Beyond the reciprocal-society lag, sub-publishing adds a pipeline step:

```
TERRITORY COLLECTION → SUB-PUBLISHER ACCOUNTING PERIOD (quarterly, semi-annual)
        → SUB-PUBLISHER REMITTANCE TO ORIGINATING PUBLISHER (30–90 days post period)
```

Total additional lag: commonly 3–6 months on top of the territory's own collection
and distribution cycle (Tier C). For recovery analysis, the combined lag for income
flowing through a sub-publisher can reach 18–30 months from usage to the originating
publisher (Tier C). This is in-transit, not missing.

## 6. International Collection Lag — Summary Reference (all Tier C)

| Income type | Territory type | Expected lag from usage to US-artist receipt |
|-------------|----------------|----------------------------------------------|
| US digital performance (SoundExchange) | US domestic | 6–9 months |
| International performance royalties | Major territory with reciprocal PRO agreement | 12–24 months |
| International performance royalties | Territory via sub-publisher | 15–30 months |
| International performance royalties | Territory without full reciprocal agreement | Potentially indefinite without direct registration |
| International neighboring rights via direct registration | Major territory | 12–18 months |
| International neighboring rights via aggregator | Major territory | 15–24 months |
| International mechanical royalties via sub-publisher | Major territory | 12–24 months |

Use the longer end of each range when deciding whether income is overdue (HG-3
discipline).

## 7. Registration Workflow Checklist

### A. SoundExchange (US Digital Performance)

1. Confirm whether the artist owns the master recording. If yes, two registrations
   are required: (a) Featured Artist account and (b) Sound Recording Copyright
   Owner account.
2. Collect required documents: legal name, stage name, ISRC codes for recordings,
   master ownership documentation (copyright certificate, label agreement, or
   independent ownership declaration).
3. Register on the SoundExchange portal. Create separate logins for the performer
   account and the owner account if the same individual holds both.
4. Submit catalog: for each registered account, submit recording titles with
   ISRCs. Missing ISRCs cap registration coverage — any recording without an ISRC
   cannot be accurately matched to usage data.
5. Set up EFT payment instructions separately for each account.
6. File retroactive claims for past usage periods: SoundExchange accepts
   retroactive registrations; confirm the current retention window and file before
   it closes.

**Common error:** Registering only one account and missing the second. Confirm
both accounts exist and have catalog loaded.

### B. International Neighboring Rights (via Aggregator or Direct Registration)

1. Determine which territories have confirmed airplay activity (use DSP territory
   data, PRO statements showing international performance income, or distributor
   territory reports).
2. Assess whether direct registration with territory societies or an aggregator
   is appropriate (catalog depth, market activity, and administrative cost are the
   factors).
3. For aggregator route: confirm fee structure (at-source vs. at-receipt) and
   which territories are covered by the aggregator's network.
4. Gather required documentation: proof of performer credit (album liner notes,
   session contracts), sound recording ownership documentation, ISRC codes per
   recording, IPI numbers if applicable.
5. Submit catalog per aggregator or society requirements. International societies
   often require ISRCs to match recordings to their logging data — missing ISRCs
   create unmatched income at the territory level.
6. Track retroactive claim windows by territory: most territories allow 3-year
   retroactive claims from registration date; some apply shorter windows. File
   claims for all qualifying periods before any window closes.

## 8. Practitioner Insights

**The US terrestrial blind spot.** US artists are conditioned to think of
neighboring rights as not applying to them because of the US terrestrial radio
gap. This creates systematic under-registration for international neighboring
rights — income that exists for every US recording played on radio in territories
that do recognize the right. A recovery review for any artist with confirmed
international radio activity should always check whether neighboring-rights
registration exists in those territories.

**SoundExchange unclaimed-fund discipline.** SoundExchange holds unclaimed
funds for performers and owners who have earned but not registered. The published
unclaimed list is searchable and updated regularly. A recovery intake should
always include a name search against this list — it is a documented, time-limited
recovery opportunity requiring only proof-of-identity and registration to trigger
disbursement.

**ISRC as the international matching key.** Both SoundExchange and international
neighboring-rights societies use the ISRC (International Standard Recording Code)
as the primary matching key. Recordings without ISRCs cannot be matched to
territory logging data. Every release in an active catalog should carry a proper
ISRC assigned at release time. Retroactive ISRC assignment is possible but adds
delay to matching. An ISRC gap is therefore both an income-stream gap today and
an impediment to retroactive recovery.

**Sub-publishing retention carve-outs.** Some sub-publishing deals include
"black box" carve-outs where the sub-publisher retains unmatched or unidentifiable
income rather than remitting it. Review the sub-publishing agreement's carve-out
language; an unfavorable carve-out can permanently siphon income that would
otherwise flow to the originating publisher and ultimately the songwriter.

**International royalties withheld at source — tax intersect.** Many territories
withhold tax on royalties paid to non-residents (commonly 15–30% at source,
Tier C). The net royalty after withholding is what arrives at the sub-publisher
or home PRO. Some of this withholding is reducible under US tax treaty provisions.
Route territory-specific withholding questions to qualified tax counsel — this is
outside the recovery specialist's scope but should be flagged so the correct
function can recover or credit the withheld amount.
