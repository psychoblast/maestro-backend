# Royalty Pipeline Mechanics

How royalty money is generated, who collects it, in what order it flows, and where
it gets stuck. This is the map the recovery specialist uses to distinguish income
in transit from income that is genuinely missing. All timelines are industry
convention (Tier C) and labeled as ranges, not guarantees.

---

## 1. US Streaming Royalty Flow — Two Separate Pipelines

A single stream generates two legally distinct royalty pipelines that flow through
different organizations on different timelines. Both must be tracked; missing
either leaves earned income uncollected.

```
STREAMING ACTIVITY (a play on a DSP)
        |
        |── MASTER RECORDING ROYALTY ───────────────────────────────────────>
        |    DSP → Label (or distributor) → Artist
        |    Mechanism: master-use license fee paid by the DSP to the rights holder
        |    Rate: negotiated (label deals) or distributor terms (independent)
        |    Timeline: per the accounting period — commonly quarterly with a 45–90 day lag
        |
        └── COMPOSITION ROYALTIES ──────────────────────────────────────────>
              |
              |── PERFORMANCE ROYALTIES ────────────────────────────────────>
              |    DSP → PRO → Publisher → Songwriter
              |    Mechanism: performance-license fee paid by the DSP to the PRO
              |    Timeline: commonly 6–12 months after usage (Tier C)
              |
              └── MECHANICAL ROYALTIES ────────────────────────────────────>
                   DSP → mechanical collector → Publisher/Administrator → Songwriter
                   Mechanism: compulsory mechanical license at the statutory basis
                   Rate basis: statutory percentage of service revenue (Tier A — confirm current rate)
                   Timeline: commonly 6–18 months after usage (Tier C)
```

**Recovery implication:** an artist streaming at any meaningful level is earning
from three pipelines simultaneously (master, performance, mechanical). A recovery
audit that checks only the master royalty misses the two composition streams that
the same play generated.

## 2. US Performance Royalty Collection Pipeline

Performance royalties (composition side) flow through PROs, not the mechanical
collector:

```
BROADCAST / STREAMING / LIVE PERFORMANCE
        ↓
BLANKET-LICENSE FEES collected by the PRO
        ↓
DISTRIBUTION CALCULATION (survey/sampling + digital usage reporting)
        ↓
PUBLISHER PAYMENT (periodic distributions; timing varies by PRO)
        ↓
WRITER PAYMENT from the publisher per the writer's share in the agreement
```

**Key mechanic — writer's share vs. publisher's share.** Most PROs pay the
writer's share directly to the songwriter and the publisher's share to the
publisher. In a co-publishing deal the songwriter receives the writer's share plus
their percentage of the publisher's share; in a full publishing deal the songwriter
receives only the writer's share during the term.

**Collection lag.** Performance distributions commonly lag usage by 6–12 months
(Tier C). International performance royalties via reciprocal society agreements lag
an additional 6–12 months beyond home-territory distributions (total commonly
12–24 months from usage to receipt) (Tier C). This lag is the most common thing
mistaken for "missing" international money.

## 3. Label Royalty Accounting Cycle

The standard recording-agreement royalty cycle:

```
ACCOUNTING PERIOD (quarterly or semi-annual per contract)
        ↓
ROYALTY COMPUTATION
    · gross sales/streams × royalty base × royalty rate
    · less applicable deductions
        ↓
RESERVE DEDUCTION
    · label withholds a reserve against future returns (commonly 15–35% of gross, Tier C; contract ceiling applies)
    · reserve is liquidated over subsequent periods (commonly 2–4 periods)
        ↓
RECOUPMENT OFFSET
    · while unrecouped: net royalties reduce the recoupable balance (nothing payable)
    · once recouped: net royalties paid after deductions
        ↓
STATEMENT ISSUANCE
    · commonly 45–90 days after the period closes (Tier C)
```

**The recoupment reality.** An artist whose record performs well can receive no
royalty check for years if the advance plus recoupable costs exceed net royalties
earned. This is contractual and common — not evidence of underpayment. An
unrecouped balance is a financial-modeling fact, not a recovery claim. Recovery
analysis must not mistake "unrecouped" for "money owed."

**Reserve accounting.** Reserves legally defer payment of earned royalties for a
few accounting periods. A recovery review checks whether reserves are being held
beyond the contractual release schedule or above the contractual ceiling — that is
recoverable, correctable through statement analysis and a written demand.

## 4. Collection Timelines Map (all Tier C, industry convention)

| Income Stream | Earning Point | Expected Receipt | Typical Lag |
|---------------|---------------|------------------|-------------|
| US streaming mechanicals | Monthly usage | Periodic collector distribution | 6–18 months |
| US performance royalties | Usage date | Periodic PRO distribution | 6–12 months |
| US recording royalties (label) | Sales/stream period | 45–90 days post period-close | 3–12 months |
| International performance royalties | Usage date | Via reciprocal society agreement | 12–24 months |
| International mechanical royalties | Usage date | Via mechanical society / sub-publisher | 12–24 months |
| Sync fee (one-time, composition side) | License grant | Net 30–90 from license | Near-term |
| Neighboring rights (US) | Performance date | Periodic | 6–18 months |
| Label tour support (recoupable advance) | Disbursement | Applied to the unrecouped balance; not paid out | N/A |

**Recovery rule:** always use the conservative (longer) end of the lag range when
deciding whether income is overdue. Income within the normal window is in transit,
not missing — asserting a loss inside the window is the HG-3 error.

## 5. Pipeline Failure Taxonomy — Where Money Goes Missing

| Failure Point | Mechanism | Financial Consequence |
|---------------|-----------|----------------------|
| Missing mechanical registration | Work not registered; collector cannot match the royalty to the owner | Mechanicals accumulate in the unmatched pool; redistributed pro-rata to registered publishers after a retention period |
| Missing PRO registration | No performance-royalty claim can be matched | Performance royalties lost or paid as black-box to registered members |
| Missing neighboring-rights registration | Featured-performer / owner share not claimed | Neighboring-rights income uncollected in applicable territories |
| Label statement computational error | Addition errors, wrong rate, wrong deduction math | Systematic underpayment; detectable by statement reconstruction |
| Controlled-composition underpayment | CC clause reduces the mechanical rate below statutory; artist often unaware | Mechanicals paid below statutory; the difference forfeited |
| Cross-collateralization | Multiple advances pooled into one recoupment balance | Artist never recoups even when an individual release performs |
| Reserve over-retention | Reserve held beyond the contractual schedule or ceiling | Earned income withheld; correctable through analysis and demand |
| Currency-conversion shortfall | International royalties converted at an unfavorable rate without authority | Small per-transaction loss; material in aggregate on active international catalogs |
| Lag mischaracterized as underpayment | Normal in-transit income treated as missing | Premature, credibility-damaging audit demands |

**Anti-pattern (the core recovery discipline):** WRONG — concluding underpayment
before mapping the pipeline and ruling out timing lag. CORRECT — step one is always
to map the expected collection timeline for the stream in question. Income within
normal lag is in transit, not missing.

## 6. Practitioner Insight

**Unmatched-pool mechanics.** Mechanical money that cannot be matched to a
songwriter because of missing or incomplete registration accumulates in an unmatched
pool. Under prevailing policy (Tier A — confirm currency before advising), unmatched
royalties are subject to distribution on a market-share basis after a retention
period — meaning income earned by one writer's unregistered work is distributed to
other registered publishers in proportion to their overall share. The recovery
implication: an artist with streaming activity but incomplete registration is not
merely missing their own mechanicals — those mechanicals are being paid to others.
Registration is a time-sensitive financial action, not paperwork.

**The reserve release cycle.** Reserves are commonly set at 15–35% of gross
royalties (Tier C) and released over 2–4 subsequent periods. If the contract does
not specify a release schedule and ceiling, the payer has discretion over timing —
a recoverable pressure point a recovery review surfaces from the reserve section of
the statement, not the net-payable line.
