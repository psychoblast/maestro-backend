## The Sync Opportunity

A single sync placement can generate more revenue than years of streaming:
- National TV commercial: $50k–300k+ (per side, ESTIMATE — not quotable to buyers)
- Major film (featured/end-title): $35k–75k+ per side (ESTIMATE)
- TV show (scene use, cable/streamer): $1.5k–7.5k per side (ESTIMATE)
- Trailer: $25k–150k+ per side — premium class (ESTIMATE)
- Indie film (distributed): $2.5k–15k per side (ESTIMATE)
- Video game (usually buyout): $2.5k–25k per side (ESTIMATE)
- Web/digital content: variable

> All fee ranges are ESTIMATE — indicative US-market bands only. Never quote these numbers directly to a buyer. Every quote cites real closed-deal comparables or is explicitly framed as exploratory. Real deals logged from day one replace these estimates at the quarterly calibration pass.

Every sync requires TWO licenses:
1. **SYNC LICENSE:** Permission to use the composition (from publisher/songwriter)
2. **MASTER LICENSE:** Permission to use the specific recording (from master owner)

Independent artists who own both masters and publishing (**one-stop**) can negotiate both sides in one signature — this is a major structural advantage. Supervisors pay a premium for one-stop and some briefs require it.

## The Six Dials of Every License

Every license is six dials, and value moves only when a dial moves:
1. **Scope** — which rights, for what use
2. **Term** — how long
3. **Territory** — where
4. **Media** — on what channels/formats
5. **Exclusivity** — who else can license the same asset
6. **Price** — what it costs

**Never negotiate price before the other five are fixed.** A "high" or "low" number is meaningless until the dials it buys are defined.

## Clearance Status (Mandatory Vocabulary)

Every asset carries exactly one status — "probably fine" is not a status:
- **CLEARED** — all parties papered for this use
- **CLEARABLE — known parties** — chain fully mapped, all parties identified and reachable, no quotes yet
- **PENDING** — quotes/permissions out; name the outstanding party and date
- **BLOCKED** — a named party has refused or is asserting a dispute
- **UNKNOWN** — chain not fully mapped; treat as unpitchable as cleared, full stop

The chain rule: **one unresolved 5% interest blocks the whole use exactly as hard as a 95% holdout.** Confidence about clearability is capped by the least-verified link — and that link is named.

## Sync-Ready Music Checklist

For a song to be sync-ready:
- Clean version available (no explicit content for most TV/advertising)
- Stems available (separated vocal, instrumental, drums, bass)
- Metadata complete: ISRC, ISWC, songwriter splits, publisher info
- Both master and sync rights cleared (no uncleared samples, no UNKNOWN chain)
- High quality WAV file (24-bit, 48kHz minimum)
- Artist registered with PRO and distributor
- Clearance status documented and current

## Brief Anatomy (How to Decode One)

A brief has seven layers; the skill is reading what's load-bearing:
1. **Project + media** — sets the fee tier and clearance scope
2. **Scene/emotional function** — what the music must *do*, the true spec
3. **Reference tracks** — the real brief. Decode what the references share: tempo, era, production density, vocal type, lyric theme, energy arc. Pitch to the shared property, not to any one reference.
4. **Hard constraints** — budget ceiling, lyric restrictions, no-sound-alike warnings, exclusivity needs
5. **Timeline** — drives whether anything not-yet-cleared is even pitchable
6. **Clearance requirement** — one-stop preferred/required is a deadline signal, not a preference
7. **What's missing** — absent budget or references each mean something; ask once or infer and state the inference

When prose and references conflict: trust the references. The prose was written by committee; the references were chosen by the person who decides.

### Funded-vs-Fishing Doctrine
Score the brief **real** when ≥2 of: named project with an air/release date · specific scene description · stated budget or range · deadline inside ~2 weeks · addressed directly (not mass-bcc). Score **fishing/coverage** when: no project name, no deadline, generic language, mass-bcc.

- Real brief → full pitch (up to 3 options, one-line fit cases, clearance status, fee expectations)
- Fishing from a known contact → one safe, pre-cleared option with a one-line note
- Fishing from unknown mass sender → optional, lowest priority; never zero-respond a known contact
- Override: a "fishing" brief from a top-target supervisor is a relationship door — overinvest deliberately

## Music Supervisor Pitching

### How Supervisors Think
- Time-starved, several projects at once, hundreds of pitches per brief
- Judged by: (a) director/showrunner saying yes, (b) staying inside budget, (c) zero clearance problems after air
- Professional fear list: clearance blowup post-air · budget overage they own · creative misfit they championed
- **They remember senders, not songs.** The unit of marketing is the relationship, not the pitch.

### What Gets a Pitch Opened
- Sender's clearance reliability history
- Subject lines carrying the answer: "3 one-stops for the diner scene — all under $10k"
- Pitch counts respecting the slot (≤3 unless invited)

### What Gets a Sender Blacklisted
- Uncleared works pitched as cleared
- Mass blasts ignoring the brief
- Re-pitching rejected tracks unchanged
- Fee bait-and-switch after creative approval

### Response Discipline
- Speed is a competence signal: same-day beats thorough-but-late for almost every brief class
- Every pitched track states: clearance status · fee expectation vs. their budget · why it's on-brief (one line each)
- An honest fast "nothing on-brief in our catalog" is a trust credit, not a missed chance

### Honest-Pass Discipline
When no catalog track clears the brief-fit floor: lead with the honest pass. Do NOT proactively pitch below-fit alternates. Offer stretches ONLY if the buyer explicitly asks, and frame them explicitly as below the fit bar — never as on-brief.

## Turnaround Feasibility by Buyer Class

Only one-stop and known-responsive-indie catalog is pitchable into trailer and rush-ad briefs. Major-label / major-publisher masters and estate chains are structurally ineligible for short-deadline buyer classes regardless of creative fit.

| Buyer class | Expected quote turnaround (ESTIMATE) |
|---|---|
| Trailer | Same-day to 24h |
| National ad (rush) | 24–48h |
| TV episodic | 2–5 business days |
| Film (non-festival) | 1–2 weeks |
| Games | 2–4 weeks |

Rule of thumb: the quote must arrive while the scene is still yours to lose. Quote ≠ license — track both dates, because the gap between them is where deals die silently.

## One-Stop Doctrine

One-stop is a deadline guarantee, not a discount opportunity. Build one-stop by construction:
1. All new catalog: sync representation across both sides included at signing, or pre-agreed approval + split letters on file
2. Existing catalog: consolidate the top decile by sync potential via split confirmations and pre-cleared approval letters
3. Re-records: only when a specific buyer class is demonstrably blocked by master-side latency on a track with live demand — never speculative

## Quote Posture Doctrine

Open at the top of the comparable-supported band (~10–20% above expected close). Never anchor above what a stated comparable justifies — the real cost of over-anchoring in sync is silent disqualification by a time-poor buyer.

Switch to quote-to-close when:
- The buyer disclosed a budget (quote inside it; treating disclosed budgets as anchors insults the buyer)
- Repeat buyers with known patterns
- Speed-is-the-product deals (trailers, rush ads) — a clean instant number closes the slot

MFN-bound deals demand precision, not anchoring — the number propagates across all tied parties.

## Scoring Rubric (Brief-Fit Assessment)

When assessing a track against a brief, use the four-dimension rubric:

| Dimension | Weight | What it measures |
|---|---|---|
| Brief fit | 40% | How well the track matches the references and scene function |
| Clearance complexity | 25% | How fast and clean the chain can close |
| Turnaround feasibility | 20% | Can the quote arrive before the scene is gone |
| Fee tier | 15% | Does buyer budget align with our band for this use |

**Composite = Σ(weight × score × 20) → 0–100, PROVISIONAL** until ≥30 outcome-checked assessments exist.

Hard gates:
- Clearance UNKNOWN → cap composite at 40 — clearance work order, not a discard
- Turnaround feasibility = 1 → cap at 25 — a yes that lands after the air date is a no
- Brief fit ≤ 2 → do not pitch — protects sender score

Verdict: PITCH (≥60, all gates clear) · HOLD (40–59 or clearance/turnaround needs work) · PASS (any gate triggered)

## Fee-Floor Schedule (Strategic Exceptions)

Below-floor deals are accepted only when the deal creates a **documented future obligation or relationship** — a pre-papered step-up on distribution, a first-look on the buyer's next project, or entry to a key supervisor relationship. Every strategic exception is logged with its intended payoff and a check-by date. Free that produces only a credit is value-eroding — the $0 becomes the comparable for the next deal.
