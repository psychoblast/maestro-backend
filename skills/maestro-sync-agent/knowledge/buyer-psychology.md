# PLMKR Sync Agent — Buyer Psychology & Brief Anatomy

---

## LAYER 1 — GENERAL FRAMEWORKS (gatekeeper buying psychology)

### What professional buyers actually buy
A professional gatekeeper's needs rank, in order:
1. **Don't embarrass me** — no downstream blowups attributable to their choice
2. **Solve my deadline** — their constraint is almost always time, not options
3. **Make me look good** — to the person they answer to
4. **Save me money** — real, but fourth

Price arguments aimed at a buyer stuck on #1 or #2 miss. Diagnose which level the buyer is on before pitching anything.

### Cognitive-load economics
Gatekeepers reward vendors who reduce their thinking: pre-filtered options, stated trade-offs, instant availability answers, decisions framed for forwarding ("here's the one-paragraph case you can send your boss"). Every pitch that forces the buyer to do the filtering trains them to open your emails last.

### The trust ledger
Every interaction credits or debits a running account. Credits accrue slowly (on-brief pitches, honest "we don't have it," deadlines met). One misrepresentation — one asset that wasn't what it claimed to be — wipes the ledger and often the relationship. Asymmetry is the point: behave as if a single debit is unaffordable, because it usually is.

## LAYER 2 — MUSIC SUPERVISOR PSYCHOLOGY & BRIEF ANATOMY

### Music-supervisor psychology
- **Operating reality:** time-starved, several projects at once, hundreds of pitches per brief, judged by (a) the director/showrunner saying yes, (b) staying inside the music budget, (c) zero clearance problems after air. Their professional fear list, in order: a clearance blowup post-air, a budget overage they own, a creative misfit they championed.
- **They remember senders, not songs.** Supervisors triage by sender reputation before content. The unit of marketing is the relationship, not the pitch.
- **What gets a pitch opened:** sender's clearance reliability history, subject lines carrying the answer ("3 one-stops for the diner scene — all under $10k"), pitch counts respecting the slot (≤3 unless invited).
- **What gets a sender blacklisted:** uncleared works pitched as cleared, mass blasts ignoring the brief, re-pitching rejected tracks unchanged, fee bait-and-switch after creative approval.

### Brief anatomy (how to decode one)
A brief decomposes into seven parts; the skill is reading what's load-bearing:
1. **Project + media** — sets the fee tier and clearance scope before any creative reading
2. **Scene/emotional function** — what the music must *do*, the true spec
3. **Reference tracks** — the real brief. Decode what the references share: tempo, era, production density, vocal type, lyric theme, energy arc. Pitch to the shared property, not to any one reference.
4. **Hard constraints** — budget ceiling, lyric restrictions (no profanity / no brand names), no-sound-alike warnings, exclusivity needs
5. **Timeline** — drives whether anything not-yet-cleared is even pitchable
6. **Clearance requirement** — one-stop preferred/required is a budget-and-deadline signal, not a preference
7. **What's missing** — absent budget or absent references each mean something; ask once, precisely, or infer and state the inference

- **Prose vs. references conflict:** trust the references. The prose is often written by committee; the references were chosen by the person who decides.

### Funded-vs-fishing doctrine
Score the brief **real** when ≥2 of: named project with an air/release date · specific scene description (not mood-board prose) · stated budget or range · deadline inside ~2 weeks · addressed directly rather than visible mass distribution. Score **fishing/coverage** when: no project name, no deadline, generic reference language, mass-bcc.

Effort rule: real → full pitch (up to 3 options, one-line fit cases, clearance status, fee expectations); fishing from a known contact → one safe, pre-cleared option with a one-line note (presence without spend); fishing from an unknown mass sender → optional, lowest priority. Never zero-respond a known contact.

Override: a "fishing" brief from a top-target supervisor is a relationship door — overinvest deliberately and say so in the deal memo. If logged outcomes show "fishing"-classified briefs placing at a material rate, the threshold is wrong — retune on that evidence.

### Response norms
- Speed of response is read as a competence signal: same-day beats thorough-but-late for almost every brief class.
- Every pitched track states: clearance status, fee expectation vs. their budget, why it's on-brief (one line each).
- An honest fast "nothing on-brief in our catalog" is a trust credit, not a missed chance.

### Honest-pass discipline (infeasible briefs)
When no catalog track clears the brief-fit floor within the buyer's window, the buyer-facing response leads with the honest pass. Do NOT proactively pitch below-fit alternates. Below-fit / stretch options are offered ONLY if the buyer explicitly asks, and then framed explicitly as below the fit bar — never as on-brief or "ready to go today."

Confidence about pitchability is capped by the least-verified link in the clearance chain. When any part of the chain is UNKNOWN, pitching as cleared is an anti-fabrication violation — name the gap instead.
