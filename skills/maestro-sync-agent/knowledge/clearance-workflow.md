# PLMKR Sync Agent — Clearance Workflow & Chains

---

## LAYER 1 — GENERAL FRAMEWORKS (clearance workflow logic)

### What clearance is
Clearance = establishing **who controls each right the intended use requires**, obtaining **documented permission from every controller**, at an agreed price, before the buyer's deadline. Three failure modes: missed party (didn't know they existed), wrong party (claimed control they didn't have), late party (real, found, but slower than the deadline).

### The chain rule
A clearance chain is only as strong as its weakest link — one unresolved 5% interest blocks the whole use exactly as hard as a 95% holdout. Any output's confidence about clearability is capped by the least-verified link, and that link is named.

### Canonical workflow
1. Identify the rights the intended use requires (driven by media/term/territory, not by the asset)
2. Map ownership — every party, every percentage
3. Verify authority — does each party actually control what they're being asked to grant?
4. Obtain quotes from each controller
5. Confirm agreement in writing (quote acceptance ≠ license)
6. Paper the license
7. Archive the proof — the chain documentation is itself an asset

### Complexity drivers (what makes a clearance hard)
Number of parties · unknown or disputed ownership · estates and successors · embedded third-party material (recursive chains) · territorial ownership splits · any party with approval rights and a history of using them.

### Status taxonomy (mandatory vocabulary)
Every asset carries exactly one status — "probably fine" is not a status:
- **CLEARED** — all parties papered for this use
- **CLEARABLE — known parties** — chain fully mapped, all parties identified and reachable, no quotes yet
- **PENDING** — quotes/permissions out; name the outstanding party and date
- **BLOCKED** — a named party has refused or is asserting a dispute
- **UNKNOWN** — chain not fully mapped; treat as unpitchable as cleared, full stop

## LAYER 2 — MUSIC MODULES (clearance chains, fee mechanics, turnaround norms)

### Two chains per song — always
1. **Master side** (the recording): controlled by the label or the artist (if independent/reverted). One license: master use.
2. **Publishing side** (the composition): controlled by the writer(s)/publisher(s) — routinely split among several parties, each of whom can have approval rights. One license per controlling party: synchronization.

100% of **both** sides is required for the use. Common trap: the artist enthusiastically "approves" a sync while controlling only the master — the publishing side is a separate negotiation with parties who may not care about the artist's enthusiasm.

### One-stop doctrine
**One-stop** = a single party controls 100% of both master and publishing. Supervisors pay a premium for it and some briefs require it: it collapses steps 2–6 of the workflow into one negotiation, which is a deadline guarantee, not a discount opportunity. A catalog's one-stop share is a structural revenue lever.

One-stop doctrine:
1. New deals are one-stop **by construction** — every new signing/production agreement includes sync representation across both sides, or pre-agreed sync approval plus split letters on file at signing; deviations are named exceptions.
2. Existing catalog: consolidate the **top decile by sync potential** (rubric-scored) via split confirmations and pre-cleared approval letters — papering the whole catalog is cost without revenue.
3. Re-records are justified only when a specific buyer class is demonstrably blocked by master-side latency on a track with live demand — re-record on observed demand, never speculation.

### Recursive chains
- **Samples:** a sampled recording embeds someone else's master AND publishing — the chain recurses into the sampled work, with every layer needing clearance for the new use. Sample-heavy works are the canonical UNKNOWN-status trap.
- **Interpolations** (re-played melody/lyric, no recording used): publishing side of the interpolated work only — still a full publishing chain.
- **Covers:** a new master clears the recording side cheaply (you own the new master), but the composition's publishing chain is unchanged and fully required.

### Turnaround norms
> **ALL FIGURES ESTIMATE — NOT QUOTABLE** until owner comparables replace them. Use these as order-of-magnitude guides only; real turnarounds are logged and retune these tables quarterly.

Buyer-side expectation (the quote must land within):

| Buyer class | Expected quote turnaround |
|---|---|
| Trailer | Same-day to 24h — ESTIMATE |
| Ad (rush/national) | 24–48h — ESTIMATE |
| TV episodic | 2–5 business days — ESTIMATE |
| Film (non-festival-deadline) | 1–2 weeks — ESTIMATE |
| Games | 2–4 weeks — ESTIMATE |

Rights-holder-side reality (time to get a full chain answered):

| Rights-holder type | Realistic full-chain clearance |
|---|---|
| Own one-stop catalog | Same-day — by construction |
| Indie label / self-administered writer | 1–5 business days — ESTIMATE |
| Major label / major publisher | 1–4 weeks — ESTIMATE |
| Estate / disputed / sample-bearing | Weeks to never — ESTIMATE |

**The operative rule is the subtraction:** pitchability per brief = buyer deadline minus chain latency. **Only one-stop and known-responsive-indie catalog is pitchable into trailer/rush-ad briefs**; major-dependent or estate chains are structurally ineligible for short-deadline buyer classes no matter how good the fit — the turnaround-feasibility gate enforces this.

A major-label / major-publisher master, or an estate / disputed / sample-bearing chain, against a short-deadline buyer class (trailer, rush-ad) is **structurally ineligible by default** — T=1 score. A theoretical or unsourced "they might expedite" does NOT lift the score; only a confirmed rush quote already in hand changes structural status. Flag any rush possibility as an explicit low-confidence note, never as a feasible pathway.

- Rule of thumb: the quote must arrive while the scene is still yours to lose.
- **Quote ≠ license.** A confirmed quote holds the deal; paper follows. Track both dates — the gap is where deals die silently.
