# PLMKR Sync Agent — Pitch Strategy & Brief Decoding

## LAYER 1 — BRIEF DECODING TRADECRAFT

### Reference-track analysis method
The brief's reference tracks are the real specification. The correct method is to extract what the references share, not what any single reference contains.

**Extraction protocol:**
1. List BPM range across all references — if ≥80% fall within a 20-BPM window, that window is the tempo spec.
2. Identify production era per reference (decade). A decade cited by 2 of 3 references is the era target; a decade cited by only 1 is probably coincidental.
3. Classify vocal type: male / female / processed-or-pitched / no vocal (instrumental brief). All three references using the same vocal type = hard constraint, not preference.
4. Identify energy arc profile: does the energy build (verse-to-chorus lift), stay constant, or resolve down? Scene function drives this — a climactic scene wants a build; a montage typically wants sustained energy.
5. Extract lyric theme: positive/aspirational, melancholic, anthemic, ironic. "No profanity" and "no brand names" are hard constraints, not just preferences.
6. Note production density: sparse (live room, few instruments) vs. dense (layered production, heavy processing). Supervisors who include a sparse reference alongside two dense ones usually want sparse-with-energy, not dense-with-restraint.

**Prose vs. reference conflict rule:** When the brief's written description contradicts the references, trust the references. Prose is often written by committee after the references were already chosen by the decision-maker.

**The missing reference case:** When a brief has no reference tracks, ask for one targeted reference before pitching. "Can you point me to one track that's directionally right?" is a legitimate single question. Pitching blind into a no-reference brief produces scatter, not precision.

**The "timeless" trap:** "Timeless" in a brief almost always means "nothing that will date in 5 years." Avoid heavy era-defining production signatures (specific 808 presets associated with one year, trend-driven vocal processing, dated side-chain compression stylistics). If the references are all 2+ decades old, "timeless" means pre-millennium; if the references are recent, it means "production-neutral."

## LAYER 2 — PITCH EXECUTION

### Pitch count discipline
Maximum 3 tracks per brief slot unless the supervisor explicitly invited more. Over-pitching signals either poor curation or a mass-blast strategy — both damage sender reputation. If fewer than 3 fully on-brief, pre-cleared options exist in catalog: pitch what qualifies, not what fills the count.

**Per-track pitch structure (one pitch block):**
- Track title
- Clearance status (verbatim from status taxonomy — no "should be fine" language)
- One-sentence fit case: why this specific track against this specific brief (not generic "great energy and mood")
- Fee expectation vs. stated budget (or "within stated budget" / "above stated budget — would need step-down discussion")
- Stems availability (YES / NO / partial — supervisors often need to re-cut)

**Subject line doctrine:** The subject line carries the answer. "3 one-stops for the coffee shop scene — all cleared, all under $8k" closes faster than "Music for your brief." A supervisor triaging 300 emails picks the subject that gives them what they need to forward to their director.

### Honest-pass discipline (expanded)
An honest "we don't have the right track for this brief" is a relationship credit when it arrives fast. The alternative — pitching a below-fit track with aspirational framing — is a trust debit that may not manifest until the supervisor plays it for the director and gets a no.

**Execution:** A fast honest pass reads: "Nothing in our current cleared catalog fits [specific brief spec]. We'll flag this slot for future one-stop additions in [genre/era]. If you open a second round, we'll be there." Do not apologize. Do not offer alternates the buyer didn't ask for.

**Override for top-target supervisors:** A "fishing" brief from a priority supervisor relationship is a relationship door — overinvest, pitch one safe on-brief option, and use the response to open a dialogue. This is a logged exception, not a policy change.

## LAYER 3 — BUYER ECOSYSTEMS

### Television vs. film vs. advertising vs. trailers
Each buyer class operates differently. A pitch strategy optimized for TV episodic fails in trailer pitching.

**Television episodic:**
- Decision chain: music supervisor recommends → showrunner/director approves → network/streamer signs off (for main title or recurrings). Background cues may need only supervisor approval.
- Speed: episodic shoots on a weekly cycle; a cue needed for an episode airing in 10 days has an effective 5-day approval window.
- Volume: a drama series can place 20–40 tracks per episode. Volume makes catalog relationships more valuable than one-off pitching.
- Budget: episodic TV budgets are structured and relatively predictable; streaming originals trend higher than cable or broadcast.

**Film (non-trailer):**
- Decision chain: music supervisor → director → studio music clearance (if major) → final music budget review at picture lock.
- Timeline: picture lock to delivery can be 3–6 months for a studio film; indie films are less predictable.
- Strategic priority: the end-title slot. End titles play over credits, are featured prominently, and generate streaming/purchase activity post-release. End-title fees are in a higher tier than background cues and carry more catalog exposure.

**Advertising:**
- The brand approval layer is decisive. A music supervisor may love a track; the brand's legal and marketing teams may reject it for reasons that have nothing to do with the music (brand association, competitor adjacency, lyric risk). Pitching into an ad brief without accounting for brand-approval latency underestimates the clearance chain.
- National vs. regional vs. online-only: fee tiers differ by 5–10x. Always clarify which before quoting.
- Campaign term: ads run on defined cycles (13 weeks, 26 weeks, 52 weeks). A perpetuity fee for an ad license is unusual and commands a very large multiple.

**Trailers:**
- Trailer houses are not music supervisors. They function as creative production companies who pitch trailers to film studios. The music decision is made by the trailer editor and the trailer house creative director, sometimes with studio marketing approval.
- Spec pitching: trailer houses often work on spec (not yet hired; competing for the trailer contract). Licensing for an unawarded spec trailer is pre-commercial use — a rights-holder may grant spec rights for free or nominal fee against a negotiated escalator if the trailer is adopted.
- Temp track displacement: trailer editors cut with temp tracks from existing trailer music libraries. Getting selected over temp requires the pitch to arrive before the editor gets attached to the temp. The practical window is the first 72 hours of a brief being circulated.
- Trailer music libraries: production music houses that pre-clear catalogs specifically for trailer use. A placement in one of these libraries generates licensing volume but at lower per-use fees than a direct deal. The tradeoff is discovery volume vs. per-deal fee.

### Music library mechanics
A music library is an intermediary that pre-licenses music for use by its subscriber base. Library deals vary substantially:

**Non-exclusive library deal:** Music remains pitchable outside the library. The library gets a commission (typically 35–50%) on placements it generates. The artist/label retains direct-licensing rights. Most indie-friendly structure.

**Exclusive library deal:** The library controls all sync licensing for the contracted works (or a defined subset). Eliminates conflicting licenses but removes the artist's ability to pitch directly. Justified when the library's buyer relationships are demonstrably superior to direct-pitch volume, or when the advance from the library warrants the exclusivity.

**Re-titling:** Some libraries re-title a track under a pseudonym in their catalog to protect their buyer relationships from competitive pricing. Re-titled tracks can create PRO registration conflicts — a re-titled version registered by the library may conflict with the original registration. The original writer/publisher retains rights; the re-titled version is a non-exclusive license vehicle, not a rights transfer. Do not authorize re-titling without explicit understanding of the PRO registration implications.

**Blanket license libraries:** When a buyer (e.g., a post-production house, a streaming platform) has a blanket license with a library, individual track negotiations do not apply — any track in the library can be licensed under the blanket terms. This generates passive placement volume but at blanket-rate fees that may be below direct-negotiation rates. Know which libraries have blanket deals with which buyer classes before deciding on library placement.

## LAYER 4 — SUPERVISOR RELATIONSHIP MANAGEMENT

### The relationship unit of measurement
Music supervisor relationships operate on a trust ledger that accumulates over years and can be destroyed in one interaction. The unit of investment is the relationship, not the individual pitch.

**Relationship tier model:**
- **Tier 1:** Supervisors with volume (multiple shows/films simultaneously) and budget (streamer or studio level). Maximum investment — take every meeting, attend their panels, respond same-day.
- **Tier 2:** Supervisors with moderate volume or a single high-value project. Invest proportionally; maintain contact without over-pitching.
- **Tier 3:** Entry-level or infrequent supervisors. Respond to briefs; don't initiate outreach outside brief cycles.

**Relationship-building without a brief:**
- Quarterly "catalog update" emails to Tier 1–2 supervisors: 3–5 new cleared tracks, no ask, no pitch. Presence maintenance only.
- Festival introductions: music supervisors attend SXSW, Sundance, and Music Biz conference. A direct introduction, especially through a mutual, is more effective than 12 months of cold emails.
- Music houses as intermediaries: established music houses have existing supervisor relationships. A placement through a house introduces the catalog to a supervisor's rotation; the house relationship expands over time.

**Blacklist avoidance (permanent relationship killers):**
1. Pitching an asset with UNKNOWN clearance status as if it's cleared.
2. Mass-blast pitching (BCC to 200 supervisors — every supervisor on the list can usually tell).
3. Re-pitching a track that was already passed on without material change (clearance improvement or new fit context).
4. Fee bait-and-switch: quoting a price for creative approval, then raising it at the license stage.

**Post-placement protocol:**
- Thank-you email within 48 hours of placement confirmation (not after airing — at deal close).
- Track cue sheet submission: confirm with the production company that the cue sheet has been filed with the relevant PRO. Don't assume — supervisors don't control cue sheet filing and often don't follow up.
- Post-air: send a congratulatory note when the episode/film airs. Attach a streaming link. This is not a pitch — it is relationship maintenance.
- Ask for the credit once: a single tasteful request for a credit or placement acknowledgment in a follow-up note is appropriate. Do not ask twice.
