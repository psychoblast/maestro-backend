# PLMKR Tour & Live — Live Business Ecosystem

The commercial and relationship layer of touring: booking-agency dynamics, the
venue and promoter ecosystem, offer/deal evaluation, ticketing strategy, tour
merchandise, festivals and special events, and international touring. Quantified
figures are Tier B (trade reporting; currency-verify) or Tier C
(practitioner-consensus ranges) unless labeled otherwise.

---

## 1. Booking-Agency Dynamics

### Agency Tier Structure

- **Tier 1 — Major (global):** the dominant agencies control most headline
  touring acts; full international routing, senior promoter relationships,
  top-tier festival buying, genre/stage specialization. Structural limitation:
  artists below a certain draw receive less senior attention.
- **Tier 2 — Mid-tier / boutique:** genre- or region-specialized; higher
  agent-to-artist attention ratio; strong relationships in specific venue and
  festival tiers; often the correct home for mid-career artists with growing
  national profile. International capability varies — verify specifically.
- **Tier 3 — Regional / independent:** local and genre-specific; correct home for
  early-career artists building regional draw before national routing.

Agency-tier decision (default): draw <250 at home → Tier 3 or self-booking;
250–1,000 with national momentum → Tier 2 boutique appropriate (Tier 1 may sign
but attention risk is real); 1,000+ with tour history → Tier 1 engagement begins
to make sense; evaluate the attention structure before signing.

### Commission Structure (Tier C — verify per agreement)

- 10% of the gross performance fee is the common standard; 15% appears in some
  boutique/early-career deals. Commission is on the gross guarantee.
- Packaging fees (agency packages multiple acts on a tour it controls) are a red
  flag without explicit disclosure and artist consent.
- Standard agreements do not commission merch income; language attempting to
  commission "all income from live performances" is a red-flag clause — route to
  LEX-CIPHER for specific language.

### Exclusive vs. Non-Exclusive

Exclusive with Tier 1 is appropriate when the artist has national draw in 5+
markets, an active release campaign, a touring history of 30+ shows, and a named
agent committed in writing. Anything less: negotiate term length and performance
clauses before signing exclusive; consider non-exclusive or a short initial term
with benchmarks.

### Agency Pitch Readiness

Agents assess: streaming footprint (geographic spread), live-draw history (they
verify ticket sales), show/production quality, recording momentum, professional
management, and press/profile. Practitioner insight: agents sign artists they can
put on tour in the next 90–180 days for a specific near-term opportunity — not
artists they hope to develop. The agent's currency is relationships, not
information, and those relationships are specific to territory, venue tier, and
genre.

## 2. Venue Ecosystem & Promoters

### Venue Tier Ladder by Capacity

| Tier | Common Name | Capacity | Typical Deal |
|------|-------------|----------|--------------|
| 1 | DIY / rehearsal space | <100 | Flat or door deal |
| 2 | Club (small) | 100–350 | Door deal or small guarantee |
| 3 | Club (mid) | 350–750 | Guarantee + backend |
| 4 | Club (large) | 750–1,500 | Guarantee + backend |
| 5 | Small theatre / large club | 1,500–2,500 | Guarantee + backend |
| 6 | Theatre | 2,500–5,000 | Guarantee + backend |
| 7 | Large theatre / small amphitheater | 5,000–10,000 | Guarantee + backend |
| 8 | Amphitheater / shed | 10,000–20,000 | Guarantee + backend; complex |
| 9 | Arena | 10,000–25,000 | Complex structures |
| 10 | Stadium | 40,000+ | Full production; complex |

Venue-tier advancement signal (default): an artist is ready to move up when
consistently selling 85%+ capacity at the current tier in their top 5 markets.
Advancing earlier typically produces under-attendance and trajectory damage.

### Promoter Landscape

The promotion landscape is dominated by two global, vertically integrated
players (promotion + venue ownership + ticketing) plus significant independent
competition at regional and local levels. Independents often have deeper local
market knowledge, media relationships, and fan-community insight than the
nationals, and agent-promoter relationships with regional independents are
sometimes stronger at the emerging level.

Promoter selection (default): club tiers (1–5) → prefer an independent regional
promoter with local knowledge; theatre tiers (5–7) → mix of independent and
national regional offices, evaluate track record at the specific venue tier;
amphitheater/arena (7+) → a major operator is typically required for venue
access; confirm which operator controls the target venue before routing.

Practitioner insight: above ~5,000 capacity, routing means routing within the
major operators' venue portfolios, and an artist tied primarily to one operator
may find limited options in markets the other controls. Understand which operator
controls which venue in each target market before building the routing.

### Settlement Process

Who is present: the promoter's accounting rep (box-office + expense
documentation), the artist's tour manager (reviews, disputes, signs), sometimes
the venue box-office manager and production manager. Review sequence: box-office
statement → comp-ticket count vs. contracted allotment → every expense line vs.
the deal memo (marketing requires receipts) → adjusted gross → split-point check
→ final payment confirmed → sign only when all figures are confirmed.

Dispute protocol: raise every discrepancy before signing. If unresolved on the
night, note the dispute in writing, sign "under protest" with the disputed amount
noted, and escalate to the agent and counsel the next day. Never sign a
settlement with unexplained expense lines and expect to recover later.

### Promoter Relationship Maintenance

Promoter relationships are long-term career assets. Promoter-friendly artists:
complete and timely advance, professional on-time load-in, professional conduct
with venue staff, disputes handled professionally, public thanks to the local
promoter. Relationship-damaging behavior: cancelling without compensation or
communication, excessive hospitality demands relative to the deal, show-day
conflict with venue staff, and disputes raised after signing.

## 3. Offer & Deal Evaluation

### Deal Structure Taxonomy

- **Type 1 — Flat guarantee:** fixed fee regardless of sales; artist bears zero
  box-office risk. Common at club level, support slots, corporate/private events.
- **Type 2 — Guarantee vs. backend:** guarantee floor + % of profits above a
  split point ("$X vs. Y% of adjusted gross above the split point, whichever is
  greater"). The most common contemporary structure at mid-level and above.
- **Type 3 — Straight percentage (no guarantee):** maximum upside, no protection;
  rare; only for established acts with documented sell-out history.
- **Type 4 — Co-headliner partnership:** two comparable-draw acts share a tour;
  complex; route legal structure to LEX-CIPHER.
- **Type 5 — Four-wall rental:** artist rents the venue and takes 100% of box
  office while bearing all risk; for established artists in proven markets.

### Split-Point Calculation

```
Split Point = (Promoter's costs + risk buffer) ÷ average ticket price
Promoter costs typically include: venue rental/guarantee, marketing spend,
promoter-provided production, insurance/permits/staffing, box-office and card
fees, ticketing-platform fees, promoter profit margin (~10–15% of gross, Tier C).
```

Evaluation question: is the split point reachable at the set ticket prices given
realistic attendance? If reaching it requires ≥85% capacity at average price
(default trigger), flag the backend as LOW-PROBABILITY and treat the guarantee as
the effective total compensation. A strong guarantee may be the correct
structure — the error is accepting a low guarantee on the assumption of a backend
the split-point math makes unreachable.

### Offer Evaluation Checklist

Market size · venue capacity vs. expected draw · ticket-price ceiling · guarantee
vs. break-even · backend reachability · production provisions (undefined scope =
undefined cost exposure) · marketing commitment (unspecified = under-promotion
risk) · settlement terms (capped deductions?) · exclusivity/radius implications ·
offer expiry (a tight expiry on a sub-standard offer is a pressure tactic, not a
reason to rush).

### Red-Flag Clause Table

| Clause | Risk | Approach |
|--------|------|----------|
| Production deductions without cap | Unlimited deductions before split | Negotiate a cap; route language to LEX-CIPHER |
| Marketing withholding without receipts | Undocumented "marketing" deduction | Require itemized receipts; specify in deal memo |
| Post-settlement clawback | Promoter recovers funds after settlement | Refuse; settlements are final on the night |
| Comp tickets: undefined quantity | Unlimited comps shrink the split base | Cap comps in the deal memo (typically 1–5% of capacity, Tier C) |
| Undefined settlement timing | Indefinite payment delay | Specify timing (night-of advance; 5–10 business days final, Tier C) |
| "All other costs" catch-all | Any expense added under vague language | Define exhaustively or remove |

Practitioner insight: the settlement is the only moment in the show cycle where
every dollar is traceable — before the show, costs are projections; after, they
are facts. Mastery of settlement mechanics is revenue recovery, not
administration.

## 4. Ticketing Strategy

### Primary Platforms

Most venues have exclusive ticketing contracts, so the artist usually inherits
the platform based on the venue chosen — **venue choice is ticketing choice.**
The landscape spans the major integrated platforms (arena/amphitheater
dominance), AEG-affiliated ticketing at its venues, and independent/mobile-first
platforms strong at club and mid-size level, plus tour-discovery/notification
services that drive consumers to the primary platforms.

### Dynamic Pricing

Adjusts price in real time to demand signals. Serves the artist in proven-demand
markets where secondary resale far exceeds primary, and at large venues with a
wide floor-to-ceiling spread. Does not serve the artist for fan-relationship-
critical acts where accessibility is a brand value, in markets with uncertain
demand, when building a new market, or at club level. Default criteria: recommend
only for artists with demonstrated sell-out history in the specific market;
require an accessible floor price; require a ceiling cap unless the artist opts
into uncapped pricing understanding the brand risk; never for new-market entry.
Fan-registration/verified-fan programs reduce bulk bot buying and are recommended
for high-demand shows where available.

### Price-Point Selection

```
Step 1 — Market capacity ceiling (last show gross/price; comparable artists;
         market income level; venue tier). No data → NOT EVALUABLE; set conservatively.
Step 2 — Tier structure: GA floor (accessible; high price sensitivity) ·
         mid/premium 25–50% above floor (default) · VIP 2x–5x floor ·
         platinum/dynamic tier (if applicable).
Step 3 — VIP bundle: meet-and-greet (highest value; supply-limited), early entry,
         exclusive merch, lounge/catering, priority soundcheck viewing.
         VIP at 3x–5x floor is standard for packages including a meet-and-greet.
Step 4 — Capacity scaling: if the venue is scalable, soft-cap at 75–80% of max;
         open to full once selling ≥80% of the initial cap within ~30 days;
         re-evaluate pricing/marketing if below 50% after ~45 days.
```

### Secondary-Market Management

Secondary pricing is a signal, not just a problem. Resale/primary ratio
interpretation (default): 1.0–1.5x normal; 1.5–2.5x meaningful demand, consider a
next-cycle increase; 2.5–4x significantly underpriced, implement a dynamic/VIP
tier and revise; 4x+ high-demand event, immediate review plus fan-communication
strategy. Practitioner insight: the secondary market does not create demand — it
reveals demand the primary market failed to price.

## 5. Tour Merchandise

### Operating Structures

- **Artist-owned (direct):** artist produces and sells; retains 100% of gross
  less hall fees; requires upfront inventory investment and merch staff; maximum
  upside.
- **Third-party merch company:** artist licenses name/likeness/designs; company
  produces, owns, and sells; artist receives a royalty (often 30–40% of gross,
  Tier C), sometimes against a recoupable advance; no inventory investment.
- **Venue-operated:** a subset of venue deal terms; less common.
- **Fan-direct hybrid:** online (own store) before/after the tour + at-venue
  physical sales; the most sophisticated model.

### Hall Fees

The venue's percentage of gross merch sales as the cost of selling in their
facility — typically on **gross**, not net. Ranges (Tier C; negotiable):
club <1,500 cap 0–10%; theatre 1,500–5,000 15–25%; amphitheater/shed 25–35%;
arena 25–40%. Always model hall fees in the P&L — at 30% on a $500,000 merch
gross, the venue takes $150,000 before the artist sees any production cost.

### Merch Timing & Online/Tour Balance

Drop sequence: pre-release limited/exclusive items → release-week primary drop →
tour-announcement line with ticket bundles → during tour, city/show-specific
limited items (highest premium) and online drops timed to dates → post-tour
clearance and archival pieces. Maintain an evergreen online store; reserve
tour-exclusives for at-venue to protect scarcity; avoid cannibalizing at-venue
merch with identical online items before the tour date.

Practitioner insight: per-fan merch spend is one of the few live-business
variables an artist can raise with almost no structural cost — what is offered,
how it's presented and priced, and whether limited-edition urgency drives the
purchase. Over a full tour this is often the difference between a profitable and a
break-even tour.

## 6. Festivals & Special Events

### Festival Ecosystem (Tier B; currency-verify)

The festival landscape is stratified by size, booking-relationship type, lead
time, and strategic value, from career-setting top-tier festivals (9–15 month
lead) through major and regional festivals (6–10 months) to industry
showcase-conference events essential for emerging artists (3–6 months).
International marquee festivals can serve as the primary vehicle for establishing
profile in a territory without a full tour. Verify current festival status,
ownership, and booking relationships before citing specifics.

### Festival Slot Decision Framework

Match the offered stage to the artist's current draw profile: discovery/emerging
stage for a regional-draw artist building national profile; second-stage direct
support for a 500–2,000 headliner-level act; main-stage mid-day for 2,000–5,000;
main-stage headliner for arena/stadium draw or a negotiated breakthrough moment.
Assess release-campaign alignment and audience-demographic fit. A prestigious
festival name does not justify an underslotted position.

### Exclusivity Window Management

Festival radius/exclusivity clauses are among the most commercially significant
provisions in live deals. Standard structure (Tier C; verify per contract): a
time window of 30–90 days before and after the festival, and a geographic radius
of 50–150 miles (or DMA-defined). Evaluate before accepting: the exact territory
and window; any committed shows inside them; whether the window blocks a headline
tour through key markets; and whether the fee justifies the exclusion. Default
rule: if exclusivity blocks headline touring in 2+ top-5 markets during a 90-day
window, explicitly calculate the strategic cost (headline net income forgone ×
excluded shows) before accepting.

### Festival as Marketing

For emerging and mid-career artists the primary value is exposure, not the fee:
discovery (converting non-fans), peer positioning (billing as a market signal),
concentrated press access, and international breakthrough. It is the wrong frame
when the artist could headline a larger venue in the same market (the festival
caps direct-to-fan income) or is past the discovery phase there.

## 7. International Touring

### Territory Readiness Framework

International touring before domestic establishment is a trajectory and financial
error. Prerequisite signals (default): streaming concentration in the target
territory (≥15% of monthly streams, or 2+ territory cities in the top-10);
territory editorial-playlist presence; organic social following in territory;
territory press coverage; a booking agent with territory relationships; a
confirmed work-permit pathway; and a break-even model that passes for the
international leg. Most common mistake: touring because a promoter expressed
interest, without verifying that streaming/social data shows an existing audience.

### Work Permits & Visas (framework only — execution routes to LEX-CIPHER + qualified immigration counsel)

Immigration law is jurisdiction-specific and changes with regulation. Most major
touring territories require a performer-specific work authorization for paid
performances; a tourist/visitor entry is generally insufficient. Lead times
commonly run 4–8+ weeks and backlogs exist. Treat each territory as separate —
neighboring or formerly-aligned territories now frequently require independent
authorization, which has materially increased the administrative burden and cost
of multi-territory runs. Default protocol: identify target territories → confirm
the permit category and lead time → begin applications 8–12 weeks before tour
start → escalate every application to LEX-CIPHER and qualified immigration counsel.
Never provide definitive immigration advice; always escalate specifics.

### Withholding Tax (structural overview only — execution routes to the finance function + qualified tax counsel)

Many territories withhold tax at source on non-resident performance income
(commonly in the 15–30% range, Tier C — verify current rates and treaties).
Treaty relief is often available but requires a filing/election process.
Gross-up provisions (the promoter compensates for withholding) are a negotiating
point, not a standard provision — route to LEX-CIPHER for negotiation. Anti-pattern:
modeling an international P&L without withholding — a $50,000 guarantee may net
$35,000–$40,000 after withholding, which materially affects break-even.

### Currency & Settlement Risk

Local-currency settlements expose the artist to FX movement between the agreement
date and the settlement date; 5–10% movement can be a meaningful variance on a
large tour. Mitigation (execution routes to the finance function + counsel):
USD-denominated contracts (shifts risk to the promoter), a forward FX contract
(lock the rate in advance), or accepting the risk (acceptable for small legs
only). Always model an international P&L in both best- and worst-case FX scenarios.
