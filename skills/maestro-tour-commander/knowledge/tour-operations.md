# PLMKR Tour & Live — Tour Operations

Routing and logistics, tour economics and P&L, and production management — the
operational core of executing a tour. All quantified figures are Tier C
practitioner ranges or PLMKR default conventions unless labeled otherwise;
currency-verify before citing in client-facing work.

---

## 1. Routing & Logistics

### Anchor-First Routing Protocol

The foundational routing method. Violation is the most common cause of
economically inefficient tours.

1. **Identify anchor markets** — the top 3–5 cities where the artist has
   documented draw, key promoter relationships, or release-campaign priority.
2. **Secure anchor offers before routing between them** — anchors are confirmed
   offers, not aspirational holds. Routing between unconfirmed anchors produces a
   calendar that collapses when one falls through.
3. **Route outward from anchors** — secondary markets fill the travel corridor
   between anchors; they are not independently justified.
4. **Apply the dead-leg test to every secondary market** — does adding this date
   generate more net revenue than it adds in travel and production cost? If not,
   it is a P&L drag, not an addition.
5. **Confirm the full routing before announcing** — the first public
   announcement should reflect a confirmed calendar. Announcing dates that later
   pull damages artist-fan trust and promoter relationships.

Anti-pattern: building a routing by listing every desired city and then chasing
offers. Correct: anchor-first, then fill — more markets served efficiently with
fewer dead legs.

### Routing Efficiency Tests

- **Routing efficiency ratio** = show days ÷ total tour days; target ≥0.65 for an
  efficient schedule (default threshold).
- **Travel-distance ratio** = total miles traveled ÷ linear first-to-last
  distance; a ratio above 2.5x signals significant routing inefficiency.

### Distance-vs-Revenue Trade-Off (Fill-Date Test)

```
Net contribution = Net guarantee
                 − (transportation delta + accommodation delta + per-diem delta)

Net contribution > 0 → date may be viable
Net contribution ≤ 0 → P&L drag; do not add unless a strategic reason exists
                        (new-market development, key promoter relationship, support slot)
```

Transportation cost reference (Tier C; highly variable; currency-verify):
- Tour bus lease: ~$800–$1,500/day (driver and fuel additional)
- Drive (sprinter/van): ~$200–$500/day in fuel and vehicle for a typical load
- Fly dates: varies widely by distance and lead time; budget air is not always
  available in touring corridors

### Travel Mode Decision Tree

```
Party size (band + crew)?
→ ≤5 → distance <200 mi: drive · 200–500 mi: drive or fly · >500 mi: fly
→ 6–12 → <14 sporadic shows: fly/drive hybrid · 14+ dense shows: tour bus more
         efficient · mixed: bus for dense regional stretches, fly for long jumps
→ 12+ (full production) → tour bus (≈one bus per 10–12 people; truck for equipment)
```

Bus economics: efficient when dates are dense (5–7/week), distances moderate
(200–600 mi/jump), and the party is large enough to spread per-person cost; the
bus doubles as overnight accommodation. Not efficient for low-density tours,
coast-to-coast jumps, or very small parties.

### Day-Off Optimization

Rest days are operational necessities on any tour over 14 days, not gaps to fill.

- Max run without a day off: ~7 consecutive show days for smaller productions; ~5
  for full-production tours (default).
- Place day-offs after the highest-energy or most logistically complex shows.
- A 10-hour overnight bus ride is not a rest day; schedule a genuine day-off
  after any travel exceeding 8 hours.

Anti-pattern: eliminating day-offs to maximize date count. Performance-quality
degradation and injury risk on a fatigued crew cost more than the day-off saves —
the 10th show in a 10-day run typically underperforms, a cost the booking
spreadsheet does not show.

### Dead-Leg Analysis

A dead leg is a travel day without a show — economically negative and physically
taxing. Classify each as **necessary** (no viable fill market in the corridor) or
**unnecessary** (a fill date exists; the dead leg is a decision, not a
constraint). For unnecessary dead legs, run the fill-market net-contribution
test; for necessary ones, evaluate overnight travel to avoid burning a full day.

## 2. Tour Economics & P&L

### Tour P&L Structure

Both sides must exist before any routing commitment.

**Revenue:** gross guarantees (the floor) · backend receipts (model
conservatively — assume zero until ticket sales validate) · VIP/meet-and-greet
packages (artist-controlled; not commissioned by the promoter) · merch net of
hall fees (highest margin; tracked separately) · sponsorship/brand activation
(negotiated separately; not available to most emerging artists).

**Expenses:** agent commission (10–15% of gross guarantee) · management
commission (15–20% of gross) · production (backline, lights, sound) ·
transportation · accommodation · crew wages · tour-manager fee · per diems
(~$50–$100/day per person) · catering/hospitality · insurance · miscellaneous
buffer (5–10% above the itemized total). All ranges Tier C.

Modeling rule: model expenses at actual or conservative before routing any date;
model backend at zero or minimum until sales support a higher assumption.

### Break-Even Calculation

```
Step 1: Total expense budget (sum of all expense categories)
Step 2: Gross guarantee floor = Total expenses ÷ (1 − combined commission rate)
        combined rate = agent % + management %
        e.g. $100,000 expenses ÷ (1 − 0.25) = $133,333 gross-guarantee floor
Step 3: Per-show floor = total floor ÷ number of shows
Step 4: Compare the per-show floor against offers received
```

Sensitivity (default): model at 70% / 85% / 100% capacity.
- 70% breaks even → financially resilient; acceptable risk
- 85% breaks even but 70% negative → viable but exposed; requires marketing confidence
- 100% barely breaks even → structural loss leader; require explicit strategic justification

### Artist-to-Gross Ratio (Tier C — highly variable)

| Career Stage | Typical Artist-to-Gross | Note |
|--------------|--------------------------|------|
| Emerging (club) | 20–40% | High relative cost; low guarantee floor |
| Mid-career (small mid-size) | 35–55% | Margin improves as production cost spreads |
| Established (theatre/mid-size) | 45–65% | Production scale + more frequent backend |
| Arena/stadium | Variable; complex | Production cost massive; ratio less meaningful |

Anti-pattern: using the ratio as a benchmark without adjusting for career stage,
structure, and production level. 30% at club level is normal; 30% at theatre
level signals expense-management failure.

### Tour Support as Advance

Label tour support is, in most agreements, a **recoupable advance against future
royalties** — not a gift or grant. Confirm whether it is recoupable; whether
expected royalty income can repay it; whether the tour serves a release goal; and
whether a non-recoupable option exists (always prefer it). Detailed advance
recoupment timelines route to the finance function and qualified counsel.

### Profit-Center Ranking (typical mid-level headline tour; Tier C)

1. **Merch** (artist-owned, hall fees managed) — highest margin when inventory
   is managed; hall fee 15–30% of gross is the primary cost.
2. **VIP/premium packages** — high margin; no venue split; 2x–5x ticket price.
3. **Backend participation** — when the split point is met, can exceed guarantee.
4. **Guarantee** — the certain floor; not the highest margin once backend is met.
5. **Sponsorship/brand** — high margin when available; not universally accessible.

Practitioner insight: the gap between a tour that generates meaningful artist
income and one that merely breaks even is almost always merch P&L management, not
the guarantee structure. The guarantee is negotiated once; merch is managed for
the entire tour.

## 3. Production Management

### Rider Architecture

A performance rider is a contractual addendum to the deal memo — binding, not a
wishlist. Two documents:

**Technical (production) rider** — stage dimensions; sound-system spec; lighting
rig spec; backline (artist-carried vs. promoter-provided); stage plot; labeled
input list; power requirements; production-crew count; load-in schedule; advance
contact.

**Hospitality rider** — dressing-room count/requirements; catering by time;
specific F&B; towels/laundry/ice; backstage security.

Responsibility split: artist bears the cost of production elements they carry;
the promoter bears venue elements specified as "provided by promoter." Riders
must match the deal level — a $2,000 hospitality rider against a $3,000 guarantee
is economically irrational.

### Production Advance Protocol

The process of communicating all production requirements to a venue before show
day — one of the most important risk-reduction activities in touring.

| Activity | Timeline before show | Owner |
|----------|----------------------|-------|
| Send technical + hospitality rider to promoter | 4–6 weeks | Tour/production manager |
| Promoter acknowledges; notes shortfalls | within 1 week of receipt | Promoter production contact |
| Technical advance: resolve all discrepancies | 2–3 weeks | TM + venue technical director |
| Confirm load-in time, crew count, access | 1 week | Tour manager |
| Confirm hospitality substitutions | 1 week | Tour manager |
| Day-of confirmation on site | show day, load-in | TM/production manager |

Anti-pattern: arriving on show day without a completed advance and expecting the
venue to accommodate unconfirmed requirements — the single most common cause of
delayed load-ins, reduced production quality, and show-day disputes.

### Stage Plot & Input List

A stage plot diagrams the physical position of every person and piece of
equipment on stage, with equipment labels, monitor positioning and mix
assignments, stage-dimension reference, and standardized directional orientation.
Send the input list alongside it. Always send PDF — never a proprietary format.
Common failures: a plot drawn for a larger stage than the venue's; an input list
that doesn't match the equipment; missing monitor-mix assignments; an outdated
plot sent after a lineup change.

### Sound/Lighting Provision Tiers

- **Tier 1** — full backline + production provided by promoter/venue (major venue
  / festival level); artist carries personal instruments and in-ears only.
- **Tier 2** — partial provision; artist carries critical backline (mid-level touring; hybrid).
- **Tier 3** — bare-bones (club): PA + some lighting only; artist carries all backline.
- **Tier 4** — artist carries full production (arena/stadium, self-promoted);
  requires a production truck and crew absorbed into the P&L.

### Venue Production Evaluation Checklist

PA spec (brand/wattage/configuration), stage dimensions, load-in access (truck
height, door width, dock vs. street), power availability (single vs. three-phase;
amperage), monitor capability (wedge vs. in-ear), dressing-room security and
size, venue-provided crew count, and the day-of technical-director contact.

Practitioner insight: the production advance is the difference between a show
that runs on time and one that doesn't. Every delay at load-in compresses the
schedule downstream. The advance is risk management, not paperwork — every
discrepancy resolved in advance is a crisis that didn't happen on show day.
