# PLMKR Tour & Live — Contracts, Crew Operations & On-Sale Strategy

Deep-reference material on the contract mechanics behind touring, the crew
structure that executes it, and the on-sale and announcement workflows that
drive ticket revenue. Quantified figures are Tier C (practitioner-consensus
ranges) unless labeled otherwise; currency-verify before citing in
client-facing work.

---

## 1. Deal Memo Anatomy — Every Required Clause

A deal memo (performance contract) is the binding document between artist and
promoter. It must contain all of the following; absence of any clause leaves
the artist exposed.

### Core Compensation Terms

**Guarantee:** The dollar amount owed regardless of attendance. Must be stated
gross (before commission), not net. Specifies the currency — internationally,
a USD-denominated guarantee is non-negotiable; never accept a local-currency
guarantee without explicit FX language.

**Backend / overage:** The percentage split and the split point. A complete
clause reads: "Artist shall receive the greater of the Guarantee or X% of
Adjusted Gross after a split point of $Y." "Adjusted Gross" must be defined in
the same document — the most common dispute in settlement arises from
undefined or loosely defined "Adjusted Gross." Require: "Adjusted Gross equals
gross ticket revenue net of applicable sales tax, credit card fees (capped at
[%] of gross), and facility fee passed through to venue — no other deductions
apply unless separately listed and capped in this document."

**Commission language:** The deal memo governs the artist-promoter relationship
only. Commission to the agent and manager flows through separate agreements.
Any language in the deal memo that references "net to artist" is not the
relevant figure for P&L purposes — model on gross guarantee, then deduct
commissions per the agent and management agreements.

### Payment Schedule

Standard structure (Tier C): 50% deposit at least 30 days before the show;
50% balance at settlement night-of-show (before the artist performs). The
deposit is the most critical negotiating point — without it, the promoter can
cancel with no liability. A 30-day deposit protects the artist's ability to
redirect the date.

Red flags: no deposit specified; "balance due within 30 days of show" (shifts
the risk to the artist for the balance); "deposit refundable upon cancellation"
without a defined cause threshold.

Settlement payment method: cash or certified check only. Bank transfer requires
explicit settlement-timing language (wire cleared to artist's account within 2
business days). Never accept "check in the mail after settlement" — once the
show has played, the artist's leverage is gone.

### Production Provisions

State explicitly what the promoter provides: PA specification (brand, wattage,
system configuration), monitor specification (wedges vs. in-ear racks + mix
requirements), lighting rig (description or minimum specification), backline
(what the promoter provides, what the artist provides), production crew
(soundperson, lighting operator, load-in crew count, runner). "Standard house
system" is not a specification — require a technical rider acknowledgment that
the promoter has reviewed and can meet the rider or has listed shortfalls in
writing.

**Critical:** undefined production provisions are undefined costs. A clause
that says "promoter provides production as mutually agreed" creates no
obligation — what was mutually agreed must be in the document.

### Marketing Obligations

A standard marketing clause specifies: confirmed marketing budget (as a dollar
amount, not a percentage of gross); approved channels (digital/social,
print/street, radio, press); required creative approval process (artist must
approve all materials before public release); co-promotion credit language;
and a marketing plan deadline (typically 6–8 weeks before the show).

Anti-pattern: a marketing clause that reads "promoter shall conduct customary
marketing" is an unenforceable platitude. Require a dollar commitment, a
channel list, and a plan delivery deadline. If the promoter under-promotes —
whether intentionally or by resource constraint — the artist has no remedy
without specific commitments in the document.

Practitioner insight: marketing deductions in settlement are one of the most
disputed line items. Require that only pre-approved marketing spend (from the
approved plan, with receipts) is deductible. "All marketing costs" as a
settlement deduction with no cap and no receipts is an open-ended liability —
negotiate a deduction cap as a percentage of gross or a fixed-dollar maximum.

### Exclusivity and Radius Clauses

**Radius clause:** prohibits the artist from performing within a defined
geographic radius for a defined window before and after the show. Standard
form: 100-mile radius, 30 days before and 30 days after. Evaluate before
signing: does the radius block committed dates; does it conflict with any
festival window; does it prevent routing through adjacent markets in the same
campaign leg.

**Type I exclusivity:** radius applies to all live performances.
**Type II exclusivity:** narrower — applies only to public ticketed performances
(allows private events, corporate shows, or benefit performances). Prefer Type
II; negotiate from Type I if presented.

Negotiating radius reduction: the artist's historical markets are the leverage
point. If the artist regularly plays the adjacent market (within the radius)
the promoter has no business rationale for excluding it — push for a named-market
carve-out or a shorter radius.

### Cancellation and Force Majeure

**Artist cancellation:** The promoter's remedy should be limited to the deposit
(already paid) as liquidated damages. Never accept "all damages including
anticipated profits" as the cancellation remedy — that is an open-ended
exposure that cannot be modeled. A clause that allows the artist to re-route
the cancelled date with the same promoter within 12 months is artist-favorable
and often acceptable to promoters.

**Promoter cancellation:** The artist's remedy should include: full guarantee +
any out-of-pocket production costs incurred before cancellation (non-refundable
flights, pre-paid crew, non-refundable deposits). Require this be stated
explicitly — the default legal remedy may be less.

**Force majeure:** Should be narrow and exhaustive. Events that qualify: act of
God (natural disaster), pandemic with government-ordered closure, terrorist
event, venue destruction, or government prohibition on the specific event.
Events that do not qualify (and should not appear): poor ticket sales,
promoter financial difficulty, inclement weather (for indoor venues), and
travel inconvenience. A force majeure clause that excuses the promoter from
payment for "circumstances beyond reasonable control" without enumeration is a
red flag. Route all force majeure language to LEX-CIPHER for review.

Rescheduling protocol when force majeure is invoked: the artist's contract
should require that any rescheduled date be offered to the artist on the same
financial terms before the promoter has the right to treat the event as
cancelled. Without this language, a promoter can invoke force majeure, cancel,
and then rebook with a different artist.

### Comp Tickets and Box Office Controls

Comp allocation: specify the total number of comps (combined artist allotment
and promoter allotment) as a fixed number or percentage of house capacity.
Industry default (Tier C): 3–5% of house capacity total. Any comp above this
allotment requires the artist's written approval.

Box-office access: the artist has the right to a tour-manager representative
in the box office during the sellable window on show day, to verify walk-up
sales, comp count, and the final accounting basis before settlement. Request
this in the deal memo — verbal agreement is unenforceable.

Independent audit right: the deal memo should include a clause allowing the
artist's representative to audit the box-office records for up to 90 days post-
show. This is the remedy for disputes discovered after the settlement is signed.
Without an audit right, post-settlement disputes have no contractual basis.

## 2. Support Slot Mechanics and Economics

### What a Support Slot Actually Is

A support (opening) slot is a paid performance engagement with the headliner's
tour, with specific terms that differ materially from a headline deal. Key
distinctions: the support artist performs on the headliner's production (rarely
their own), the set length is fixed and short (typically 20–45 minutes, Tier
C), and the financial structure is either a flat guarantee or a "joint
guarantee" where the support guarantee is embedded in the promoter's deal with
the headliner's team.

### The Support-Fee Pipeline

The support guarantee flows through the headliner's booking agency in most
deals, not directly from the promoter to the support artist. This creates a
structural information gap: the support artist's team often does not see the
underlying promoter deal and cannot verify what portion of the total package
the support fee represents. Best practice: require direct deal memos between
the promoter and the support artist's agent — avoid accepting a support slot
with only verbal confirmation of a fee from the headliner's camp.

Commission: the support artist's booking agent commissions the support
guarantee. The headliner's agent does not commission the support artist's fee
unless there is a specific packaging arrangement (a red flag requiring explicit
disclosure). Packaging fees in support deals are an abuse of the agency
relationship — route any packaging-fee language to LEX-CIPHER immediately.

### Support Slot Financial Modeling

Support fees at the mid-level (Tier C): $500–$2,500 per show for club-level
support; $2,500–$10,000+ for mid-size; higher for arenas and above. The fee
rarely covers full expenses for the support artist — the correct frame is:
does this slot generate enough strategic exposure to justify the net cost?

P&L modeling for support: total artist expenses for the leg ÷ number of shows =
per-show cost floor. Subtract the support fee. The remainder is the per-show
strategic investment cost for the slot. Evaluate: does the audience exposure,
market-development value, and potential future headline draw justify that
per-show investment? This is a strategic expense, not a profit center.

Practitioner insight: many support slots are P&L negative. A tour team that
treats a support slot as break-even and does not model the net cost is
mispricing the slot's value. The investment is in audience conversion and
market development — quantify it before committing.

### Production on a Support Slot

The support artist typically performs on the headliner's production — meaning
the venue's PA system as configured by the headliner's production manager, and
the headliner's lighting rig in its house configuration. The support artist's
FOH engineer may not have access to the main console (particularly at larger
shows where the headliner's FOH runs the system for the whole night).

Advance requirements for support: the support artist's tour manager must
conduct an advance with the headliner's production manager (not the venue)
at least 2 weeks before the first date of the leg. Confirm: input-list
compatibility, monitor requirements (wedges or in-ear — confirm the system is
compatible), set length and change-over time, backline sharing arrangement if
any, and stage time slot.

Anti-pattern: showing up to a support date without a completed advance with
the headliner's production manager. The headliner's production manager controls
every minute of the day — arriving without a completed advance means the
support artist is at the mercy of that manager's goodwill.

### Advancing the Relationship with the Headliner's Camp

A support slot is a relationship opportunity as much as a performance
opportunity. Professionalism benchmarks that headliner camps observe: on-time
load-in (the support artist should be the most punctual crew in the building),
clean and respectful use of shared spaces, stage left promptly at set-end (never
allow a set to run long without the headliner's explicit advance approval),
no contact with the headliner without introduction from management, and zero
hospitality complaints.

Practitioner insight: headliner camps discuss support artists extensively. One
support leg done well creates future opportunities at the next level. One
professionalism failure — running long, overfilling the stage plot, or creating
conflict with venue staff — removes the artist from consideration across the
agency's roster for years.

## 3. Nightly Settlement — Deep Mechanics

### Pre-Settlement Preparation (Show Day)

Before the show plays:

- The tour manager has the executed deal memo and a printed settlement
  worksheet derived from it.
- Walk-up sales begin after doors; the TM checks with the box-office at 30
  minutes after doors for a running count.
- Track comps: confirm the artist allotment was not exceeded; confirm the
  promoter allotment was not exceeded. Each unauthorized comp above the
  contracted allotment reduces the sellable capacity.
- If walk-up sales are below expectations, flag the possibility that the
  split-point will not be reached — the guarantee becomes the settlement
  total, which simplifies the settlement but eliminates backend.

### Settlement Sequence (Show Night)

1. **Box-office statement:** The promoter's accountant produces a box-office
   report showing: gross ticket revenue by price tier, ticket count by tier,
   comps by allotment (promoter and artist), total comps, and adjusted gross
   ticket revenue (net of applicable taxes, card fees, facility fee pass-through
   if applicable). Verify: total tickets sold + comps = advertised capacity (or
   hard cap if the venue is not full capacity). Any discrepancy is a red flag.

2. **Verify every expense line:** The promoter's settlement sheet should list
   every deducted expense with a receipt, an amount, and a reference to the
   deal-memo clause that authorizes the deduction. Compare every expense line
   against the deal memo. Common over-deductions: unauthorized marketing
   expenses (above the agreed cap or without receipts), production costs for
   elements the promoter agreed to provide (now being back-charged), phantom
   staffing charges, and inflated venue fees.

3. **Calculate adjusted gross:** Gross ticket revenue minus authorized
   deductions as defined in the deal memo. This is the number from which the
   split-point is calculated.

4. **Check the split point:** Is the adjusted gross above the contractual split
   point? If yes: calculate the overage (adjusted gross minus split point);
   multiply by the artist's backend percentage; add to the guarantee; that is
   the settlement total. If no: the guarantee is the settlement total (confirm
   the promoter is paying the full guarantee, not the attendance-based
   percentage).

5. **Confirm payment method and timing:** Cash or certified check, paid before
   the performance (deposit) and before the artist leaves the venue (balance).
   If a balance is outstanding, do not allow the artist to depart until
   payment is confirmed or a written acknowledgment of outstanding balance is
   signed by the promoter.

6. **Sign and receipt:** The tour manager signs the settlement and retains a
   copy. Note any disputed items before signing — never sign a clean settlement
   if items are disputed; use "under protest" language with the dispute noted.

### Common Settlement Disputes and Resolution

| Dispute | Root cause | Resolution |
|---------|-----------|------------|
| Marketing deductions without receipts | No receipt requirement in deal memo | Refuse deduction without receipts; negotiate a cap in next deal |
| Comp count above allotment | Promoter over-comped | Deduct excess comps from the promoter's allotment at average ticket price; add back to artist's adjusted gross |
| Unauthorized expense lines | Vague "all costs" language | Reference the deal memo; refuse any deduction not explicitly authorized |
| Split-point disputed (definition of "adjusted gross") | Ambiguous definition in the deal memo | Reference the signed deal-memo definition; if undefined, escalate to the agent |
| Payment delayed (check not available night-of) | Promoter cash-flow issue | Do not perform until deposit is confirmed; for balance, require a written acknowledgment with a specific wire date before artist departs |

Post-settlement audit: flag any settlement with unexplained variances above
$500 for a follow-up audit within 30 days while documentation is available.
Beyond 30 days, box-office records become harder to obtain.

## 4. On-Sale Strategy and Announcement Windows

### Announcement-to-On-Sale Sequencing

The gap between the public announcement of a tour and the on-sale date is one
of the most consequential decisions in the tour-marketing cycle. Too short: not
enough time for press pickup and social amplification before tickets are
available, resulting in a slow first-hour sales figure (which affects algorithmic
placement on discovery platforms and creates public perception of weak demand).
Too long: momentum dissipates and competing events capture the calendar
commitment before tickets are available.

Recommended windows (Tier C; varies by artist level and market):
- Club / small mid-size (<1,500): 2–4 weeks announcement to on-sale
- Mid-size theatre (1,500–5,000): 4–8 weeks
- Large venue / amphitheater: 8–12 weeks
- Arena / stadium: 12–20 weeks

The anchor-market on-sale should not be used as the indicator of tour demand
until the full routing is announced — a presale in one market before the full
tour is announced creates fragmented momentum.

### Pre-Sale Sequencing

The pre-sale period before the general on-sale is a demand-management tool,
not a loyalty reward. Treat it as structured demand capture.

Standard pre-sale ladder (Tier C):

1. **Artist-to-fan presale (48 hours before general on-sale):** Fan club or
   email-list exclusive. Reserved inventory: 10–15% of house capacity. This is
   the highest-value pre-sale for artist-fan relationship and for capturing
   the most committed buyers before bots. Require verified-fan registration
   for high-demand shows.

2. **Credit-card / bank presale (24–48 hours before general on-sale):**
   Platform-sponsored; often a venue/ticketer condition. Reserved inventory:
   negotiate a cap (typically 10–20%); uncapped credit-card presales can
   capture too large a share and leave the general on-sale looking thin.

3. **Radio / media presale (optional; 24–48 hours before general on-sale):**
   Bundled with promotional partnership; useful for market entry; negotiate
   a tight inventory cap (5–10%).

4. **General on-sale:** All remaining inventory released simultaneously.

Practitioner insight: the sequence exists to manage velocity. A strong first
hour of the general on-sale (indicated by near-sellout or a sell-through rate
above 30% of remaining inventory within 60 minutes) drives algorithmic
amplification on ticketing platforms and social media pickup. Pre-sales should
not consume so much inventory that the general on-sale looks weak by
comparison. If total presale allocation exceeds 40% of house, recalibrate.

### Sales-Velocity Monitoring and Intervention Triggers

Monitor ticket sales by daily pull of the box-office report (available from the
ticketing platform for confirmed shows). Velocity benchmarks and intervention
points (Tier C — directional only):

| Days after on-sale | Sell-through | Signal | Action |
|-------------------|-------------|--------|--------|
| Day 1 | >30% | Strong demand | Confirm production advance; open capacity if scalable |
| Day 7 | >50% | Healthy | Hold current strategy |
| Day 14 | 25–50% | Normal pace | No action |
| Day 14 | <25% | Slow | Evaluate: is the market undersaturated or overpriced? Assess secondary signal |
| Day 30 | <40% | At-risk | Marketing intervention; promoter consultation; consider venue change |
| Day 45 | <50% | Structural concern | Break-even re-model; venue right-size decision required |

Venue right-sizing: if a show is tracking to sell 60% or below and the venue
has a scalable section (a "soft cap"), reduce the capacity to create a sell-out
and shift the narrative. A 600-person sell-out is better for the artist's
trajectory than a 1,000-person show at 60% capacity — the same number of
tickets, but dramatically different audience perception and promoter
relationship outcome.

### Luminate (SoundScan) and Chart Implications of Ticket-Bundle Sales

Ticket/album bundles (a ticket includes an album download or streaming credit)
can impact chart eligibility under Luminate reporting rules. Understand the
current rules before structuring a bundle: historically, a bundle where the
album is physically shipped and the primary offer is the ticket (not the album)
counted toward album chart consumption, but these rules change. Route specifics
to the distribution and digital-ops function.

Anti-pattern: assuming ticket bundles will chart impact without verifying
current Luminate methodology. The rules are regularly updated and chart
eligibility requires specific structuring — never assume.

## 5. Crew Classification and Operations

### Touring Crew Hierarchy

Understanding the reporting structure prevents show-day conflicts over authority
and responsibility.

**Tour Manager (TM):** The artist's on-the-road representative. Sole authority
on the artist's behalf for all show-day decisions including settlement
negotiation and dispute resolution. Manages the full touring party's logistics
(travel, accommodation, per diem). The production manager reports to the TM on
logistics; both report to management and the artist.

**Production Manager (PM):** Technical authority for all production elements:
stage plot execution, rider advance, venue-technical relationships, crew
management, load-in/load-out scheduling. The PM owns the relationship with the
venue's technical director and stage manager.

**FOH Engineer (Front-of-House Mix):** Responsible for the audience mix.
Controls the main PA system from the front-of-house position. Artist-side
employee, not venue staff — the FOH engineer is not interchangeable with the
venue's house engineer (the house engineer runs the house for support, if needed).

**Monitor Engineer:** Responsible for the stage monitor mix — what the artists
and band hear on stage. Typically more consequential to performance quality than
the FOH mix; an artist performing without a reliable monitor mix cannot deliver
a consistent performance.

**Stage Manager:** Coordinates stage activity during the show: cue timing,
stage-left/right crew positioning, change-over management (for multi-act shows),
and communication between the TM (house) and the PM (stage).

**Lighting Director (LD):** Programs and operates the lighting rig. Carries a
complete lighting plot for each show; advances the rig with the venue's lighting
technician.

**Backline Technician (Tech):** Instrument maintenance and stage-setup for one
or more instruments. Responsible for tuning, string changes, amp settings, DI
signal routing, and equipment substitution if a failure occurs mid-show.

### Union Considerations (IATSE and Teamsters)

Many large venues are operated under IATSE (stagehands, electricians, riggers)
or Teamsters (drivers) union contracts. In a union venue:

- **Local crew requirements:** Union venues typically require that all load-in,
  load-out, and show-day labor be done by union stagehands from the local. The
  artist's crew directs but cannot perform the physical labor of the local's
  members (e.g., the PM designs the rig, but an IATSE rigger hangs it).
- **Minimum call requirements:** Most IATSE locals have minimum call lengths
  (often 4 or 8 hours) that apply regardless of the actual work time. Over-time
  provisions kick in at specified thresholds. These costs flow through the
  promoter's production budget and appear in the settlement — verify they match
  the contracted labor call before signing.
- **Work rules:** Rules vary by local. Common rules: department separations
  (electricians do not handle carpenter work); specific equipment operators
  (only a licensed rigger can fly truss); meal-break requirements (30-minute
  meal breaks at specific intervals, or substantial penalty payments). Violating
  local work rules triggers grievance processes that can shut down a load-in.
- **Carry-through exception:** Some IATSE agreements allow "carry-through" for
  touring crew on specific work — the touring crew may be permitted to operate
  instruments or equipment they carried in, even in a union house. The PM must
  confirm carry-through permissions in the advance.

Practitioner insight: production overruns at union venues almost always trace
to unanticipated union labor call costs. Every production advance in a union
venue must confirm the labor-call structure, minimum hours, and any applicable
overtime provisions — this is the PM's first advance question, not an afterthought.

### Work-for-Hire vs. Crew Employee Classification

Touring crew are almost universally engaged as independent contractors or work-
for-hire in markets without specific regulations requiring employment status.
However, jurisdiction-specific rules (California AB5 is the most prominent U.S.
example) may reclassify certain crew as employees based on the nature and
duration of the engagement, creating payroll-tax, workers'-comp, and benefits
obligations. International touring further complicates this — many territories
treat any paid worker on tour as a local employee if the engagement exceeds a
minimum duration (commonly 15–30 days in a 12-month period).

Route classification analysis to the finance function and qualified employment
counsel; do not assume independent-contractor status is valid for multi-month
touring engagements across jurisdictions.

**Per diem as a non-compensation element:** Per diem (the daily cash allowance
for meals and incidentals, Tier C range: $50–$100/day for crew) is not a wage
and is typically not subject to payroll tax up to IRS/government-specified
thresholds. Amounts above the threshold become taxable compensation. Track per
diem separately from wages in the tour budget and confirm the applicable
threshold before issuing per diem on a long tour.

### Road Book and Day Sheet

**Road book:** The master operational document for the tour. Contains: full
routing with confirmed venue addresses, venue-specific contacts (venue
manager, production manager, box-office manager), deal-memo summary for each
date, rider-acceptance notes, confirmed production provisions, hotel block
information, ground-transport arrangements, emergency contacts, and travel
itineraries. Every member of the touring party receives the relevant sections.
The road book is updated after every advance is completed and is the TM's
operational bible.

**Day sheet:** The show-day schedule, issued by the TM for each show day.
Minimum content: hotel check-out time; ground transport departure time; venue
arrival time; load-in time; soundcheck time (by act — support first, headliner
last); production hold/change-over windows; doors time; show time (support set
time and length; headliner set time); curfew (hard venue curfew with overtime
penalty language if applicable); settlement time; load-out completion target;
hotel check-in (next city).

Practitioner insight: the day sheet is the primary tool for preventing every
type of show-day crisis — late arrivals, missed soundchecks, set-time conflicts.
A TM who does not issue a day sheet forces every crew member to carry the
schedule in their head; the first time it fails, it costs more than the time it
would have taken to write.

### Venue Curfew and Overtime Mechanics

Most venues have a hard curfew — a time at which all amplified performance must
end (usually driven by local ordinance or union shift-end). Common structure:
a hard curfew with an optional overtime window (typically 30-minute increments
at a fixed cost per increment, Tier C: $500–$2,000+ per increment depending on
venue size and local labor costs).

The headliner's production manager negotiates the curfew and overtime terms
with the venue in the advance. Incorporate into the day sheet: allow a 10-minute
buffer before the hard curfew. Do not rely on the overtime option as a planned
extension — it is an emergency tool. A set that routinely runs to the overtime
window signals production-management failure, not a venue limitation.

Set-length enforcement: the TM has authority to signal the stage manager to
wrap the set at the hard-curfew safety point regardless of where the artist is
in the setlist. An overtime penalty at a 2,000-capacity venue can run $1,000–
$3,000 for a 30-minute extension — more than the margin on several shows.

## 6. Tour Insurance: Cancellation, Non-Appearance, and Liability

### Cancellation and Non-Appearance Insurance

Tour cancellation insurance indemnifies the artist and/or promoter against
financial losses from a tour that cannot proceed due to a covered event —
typically illness or injury of a key performer, venue destruction, or a
government-ordered closure. This is not the same as force majeure in the
contract: insurance pays claims; the contract defines legal liability.

**Who should carry it:** Both parties often carry separate policies. The artist
should carry non-appearance insurance covering their guarantee obligation in the
event of illness, injury, or a covered event. The promoter carries event-
cancellation insurance covering their marketing investment, facility, and
overage liability. These are complementary, not redundant.

**Underwriting requirements (Tier C):**
- Medical history of key performers is reviewed for pre-existing conditions
  (a pre-existing illness not disclosed can void coverage for a related
  cancellation).
- The insured value is based on the total guarantee exposure, not the gross.
- Premium range (Tier C): 1–3% of the insured value for a standard tour, more
  for tours with a history of cancellations or performers with known health risks.

**What is typically NOT covered:** poor ticket sales, voluntary cancellation
(even for strategic reasons), travel delays not caused by a covered event,
and cancellations within a specific look-back period (often 30 days pre-show)
for a pre-existing condition.

### General Liability

The artist is a "performer" under the promoter's general-liability insurance
but is not the primary insured party. The artist's management should maintain
a separate general-liability policy covering the artist and touring entity —
particularly for touring with physical set elements, pyrotechnics, or crowd-
interaction staging that could create a liability exposure beyond the venue's
coverage.

Touring entities that carry their own production equipment (especially pyro,
water effects, or aerial rigging) require separate equipment insurance
(inland marine) and must add the venue as an additional insured on show day.
Verify this is included in production-advance paperwork, not discovered at
load-in.

### Weather and Outdoor Events

Outdoor shows carry weather cancellation risk that indoor shows do not.
Weather insurance for outdoor events is available; the insured-weather trigger
(wind speed, precipitation volume) must be specifically defined. Anti-pattern:
assuming the venue's general-liability policy covers weather cancellations —
most do not. Outdoor shows require a separate weather endorsement.

Lightning protocols: OSHA and venue-insurance requirements typically mandate a
lightning-safety plan for outdoor events including a defined minimum distance at
which weather monitoring triggers a hold and an evacuation protocol. The
production manager is responsible for the lightning protocol; confirm it exists
and is documented in every outdoor advance.

## 7. Artist Advancement Signals Across Venue Tiers

Tier advancement is one of the most consequential decisions in a touring
career — advance too early and attendance underperforms, damaging the artist-
promoter relationship and public perception of trajectory; hold too long and
miss the window to expand the market.

### Signal-Based Advancement Framework

The correct trigger for moving from one venue tier to the next is behavioral
(ticket-sales data), not aspirational (the artist "feels ready" or a manager
"thinks the moment is right").

**Primary advancement signal:** Consistently selling 85%+ of the current tier's
capacity in the artist's top 5 markets across 2+ consecutive runs. Not in one
market — in the top 5. Not on one tour — across multiple runs.

**Secondary confirmation signals:**
- Secondary-market velocity: the current tier sells through to 85%+ in
  secondary markets (not just anchor markets) within 30 days of on-sale.
- Streaming-to-draw correlation: per-capita streaming in a market correlates
  with per-capita ticket sales — the audience the artist has online is
  converting to live attendance.
- Hold requests: promoters for the next-tier venues are requesting holds before
  the artist has confirmed availability — demand signal from the supply side.
- Supportable demand: the gap between the current tier's capacity and the next
  tier is demonstrably bridgeable — advancing from 700 to 1,500 with $5–$8/head
  in average ticket price increase is bridgeable; advancing from 700 to 5,000
  is a trajectory jump that requires extraordinary demand signals.

**Contra-indicators (do not advance when any of these are present):**
- Current-tier shows in anchor markets are not selling to 85%+ consistently.
- Sell-through in secondary markets is below 60%.
- The proposed next-tier venue is 3x or more the capacity of the current tier.
- Promoter hold requests are aspirational (based on momentum perception) rather
  than based on ticket-velocity data.

Practitioner insight: a premature venue-tier jump is one of the most damaging
moves in a touring career. Advancing a 500-capacity act to a 1,500-capacity
venue — appearing 33% attended — sends a stronger negative signal to promoters,
media, and the industry than staying in the 500 capacity for one more tour. The
